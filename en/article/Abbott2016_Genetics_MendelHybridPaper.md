# Experiments on Plant Hybrids by Gregor Mendel

## Citation (NLM)

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.195198](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.195198)

## Summary

This paper presents a new English translation of Gregor Mendel's original 1866 article "Versuche über Pflanzen-Hybriden," republished in *Genetics* to mark the journal's 150th anniversary. Translators Scott Abbott and Daniel J. Fairbanks aimed to produce a translation that is both more accurate and more accessible than previous versions.

Mendel systematically explained his choice of the garden pea (Pisum sativum) as the experimental plant. Pisum satisfies three requirements: (1) clearly distinguishable constant traits, (2) protection from foreign pollen during the flowering period, and (3) no noticeable disturbance in fertility of the hybrids. After testing 34 varieties over two trial years, he selected 22 for cross-fertilization, studying seven pairs of contrasting traits.

The seven traits studied were: (1) seed shape (round vs. angular/wrinkled), (2) albumen color (yellow vs. green), (3) seed coat color (grey-brown vs. white), (4) pod form (inflated vs. constricted), (5) color of the unripe pod (green vs. yellow), (6) position of flowers (axial along stem vs. terminal), and (7) stem length (tall vs. dwarf).

In F1 hybrids, for all seven traits, only one parental character appeared — the dominant character. The character hidden in the hybrid was termed recessive. Crucially, dominance was independent of whether the character came from the seed or pollen parent.

In the F2 generation (from F1 self-fertilization), recessive characters reappeared in a consistent ratio averaging 2.98:1, or approximately 3:1. Specific results: seed shape 7,324 seeds (2.96:1), albumen color 8,023 seeds (3.01:1), seed coat 929 plants (3.16:1), pod form 1,181 plants (2.95:1), pod color 580 plants (2.82:1), flower position 868 plants (3.14:1), stem length 1,064 plants (2.84:1).

Among F2 plants showing the dominant character, two-thirds again produced offspring in a 3:1 ratio (behaving as hybrids), while one-third bred true. The true ratio was therefore 1:2:1, expressed as A + 2Aa + a.

When two pairs of differentiating characters were combined, the 556 seeds produced ratios of 315 (round+yellow): 101 (wrinkled+yellow): 108 (round+green): 32 (wrinkled+green), approximating 9:3:3:1. For three combined traits, a 27-class developmental series emerged. The general law: for n pairs of differing characters, 3ⁿ classes, 4ⁿ individuals, and 2ⁿ constant combinations. This is the Law of Independent Assortment.

Mendel concluded by proposing that hybrid plants produce equal numbers of different types of germ and pollen cells corresponding to all possible constant combinations. He verified this through backcross experiments in which hybrids were crossed to parental lines, confirming the proposed equal-frequency gamete hypothesis.

## Significance in Introduction Context

Why this paper matters at the introduction to genetics:
- Provides the primary source for the Law of Segregation and the Law of Independent Assortment, the foundational principles of genetics
- Establishes the revolutionary concept that hereditary information is particulate and non-blending — each trait is transmitted intact across generations
- Essential course reading because it is the origin of modern genetics, a model of hypothesis-driven quantitative science in biology, and the first application of statistical reasoning to heredity

## Future Research Directions

Unresolved questions and future research directions from this paper:

- What are the molecular identities of the seven traits Mendel studied — later answered by Reid & Ross (2011) and Feng et al. (2025)
- Do the same segregation laws apply in species other than Pisum — Mendel himself tested Hieracium and Phaseolus, finding both confirmations and apparent exceptions
- Can continuously varying (quantitative) traits be explained by the same particulate inheritance principles — later resolved by polygenic/quantitative genetics theory
