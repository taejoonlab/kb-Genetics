# De Novo Gene Birth: Origin of New Genes from Noncoding Sequences

## Citation (NLM)
Abrusan G. Turning junk into gold: domestication of transposable elements and the evolution of gene regulatory networks. Genetics. 2013;195(4):1407-1417. doi:10.1534/genetics.113.152256

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.113.152256](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.113.152256)

**Educational primer:** [[en/review/Abrusan2013_Genetics_DeNovoGeneBirth-Frietze2014primer]]

## Background

The question of how new genes originate is a fundamental problem in evolutionary genetics. While gene duplication followed by sequence divergence has long been the canonical mechanism, recent evidence shows that entirely new genes can arise de novo from noncoding DNA sequences. This paper examines de novo gene birth in yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae), using proto-genes (translated ORFs without clear function) as an intermediate stage model, and quantitatively analyzes how new genes integrate into cellular networks over evolutionary time.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Phylogenetic profiling to assign evolutionary ages to yeast genes, from ancient to recently born
2. Measurement of β-strand content in proteins across evolutionary age groups using structural prediction tools (PSIPRED)
3. Measurement of aggregation propensity across evolutionary age groups using TANGO and AGGRESCAN algorithms
4. Analysis of protein-protein interaction (PPI) data to correlate gene age with number of interaction partners
5. Comparison of proto-genes to annotated genes for transcription level, translation level, and structural properties

---

## Results

- **β-strand content and aggregation propensity**: Younger genes have higher β-strand content and aggregation propensity; both decrease as genes age, indicating progressive structural optimization
- **Regulatory network integration**: New genes integrate into transcriptional regulatory networks relatively rapidly, within approximately 14 million years
- **PPI acquisition rate**: Protein-protein interactions are acquired far more slowly at approximately 2–2.25 × 10⁻⁸ interactions/year, orders of magnitude slower than regulatory integration
- **Proto-gene properties**: Proto-genes show higher β-strand content and aggregation propensity than annotated genes, consistent with being at an early, structurally unoptimized stage of gene birth
- **Evolutionary selection**: Genes that successfully integrate into cellular networks are selectively retained, with gradual structural improvement over time

---

## Perspective

This study establishes a temporal framework for de novo gene birth: transcriptional regulation is acquired early, while stable protein interactions are built slowly over geological time. The asymmetry between these two timescales suggests that new genes first gain regulatory control, then are gradually refined at the protein level. Future work should examine whether these patterns hold in multicellular eukaryotes and identify the molecular chaperone mechanisms that allow high-aggregation proto-proteins to persist without toxicity.
