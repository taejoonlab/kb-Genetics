# Transcriptome Divergence in Field Cricket Accessory Glands: Testing the Seminal Fluid Protein Hypothesis

## Citation (NLM)
Andres JA, Larson EL, Bogdanowicz SM, Harrison RG. Patterns of transcriptome divergence in the accessory gland of two closely related species of field crickets. Genetics. 2013;193(2):501-513. doi:10.1534/genetics.112.142299

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.112.142299](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.112.142299)

**Educational primer:** [[en/review/Andres2013_Genetics_FieldCricket-Wittkopp2013primer]]

---

## Background

Seminal fluid proteins (SFPs) have been proposed to evolve rapidly due to sexual conflict and coevolution, potentially contributing to speciation through reproductive isolation. This study tests that hypothesis by comparing the accessory gland transcriptomes of two closely related field cricket species — *Gryllus firmus* and *Gryllus pennsylvanicus* — which partially overlap in habitat and occasionally hybridize, making them an ideal model for studying speciation with incomplete reproductive isolation.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. RNA-seq of accessory gland tissue from *G. firmus* and *G. pennsylvanicus* males
2. SNP analysis to estimate fixed differences between species
3. Identification of SFP candidates based on homology with *Drosophila* SFPs
4. Comparison of species divergence levels between SFP candidates and non-SFP transcripts
5. Hybridization experiments to assess reproductive isolation

---

## Results

- **Fixed SNP differences**: Approximately 10% fixed SNP differences observed between species in accessory gland transcriptomes, indicating substantial divergence despite recent speciation
- **SFP divergence rate**: Contrary to the hypothesis, seminal fluid protein candidates did not diverge significantly faster than non-SFP accessory gland genes
- **Overall transcriptome divergence**: Considerable divergence observed across the entire accessory gland transcriptome, including expression level differences
- **Reproductive isolation**: Accessory gland proteins may contribute to reproductive isolation, but no evidence that SFPs alone play a disproportionately large role

---

## Perspective

This study provides important counter-evidence against simple models where SFPs drive speciation through accelerated evolution. The finding that SFPs are not especially fast-evolving relative to other accessory gland genes suggests that cumulative divergence across many reproductive genes may be more important than any single gene class. This challenges single-gene-class explanations for reproductive isolation. Future work should quantify the relationship between genome-wide transcriptome divergence and reproductive isolation strength.
