# Aneuploidy Causes Non-genetic Individuality

## Citation (NLM)
Beach RR, Ricci-Tam C, Brennan CM, Moomau CA, Hsu PH, Hua B, Silberman RE, Springer M, Amon A. Aneuploidy Causes Non-genetic Individuality. Cell. 2017;169(2):229–242. doi:10.1016/j.cell.2017.03.021

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2017.03.021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2017.03.021)

---

## Summary

This study from the Amon laboratory at MIT systematically demonstrates that cells sharing an identical aneuploid karyotype nonetheless exhibit dramatically elevated cell-to-cell phenotypic variability in cell-cycle progression, responses to environmental stimuli, and gene expression. The authors developed an inducible chromosome mis-segregation system in budding yeast (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae*) using conditional centromeres controlled by the *GAL1* promoter, enabling acute generation of defined monosomies, disomies, or trisomies. Time-lapse microscopy of individual cells revealed that the standard deviation of G1 duration and S+early M phase duration was significantly elevated in aneuploid populations relative to euploid controls, with variability scaling positively with the degree of aneuploidy (number of chromosomes and total open reading frames affected).

Two lines of evidence establish that this variability is non-genetic in origin. First, cell-cycle durations in consecutive divisions of a single cell showed no correlation (r² ≤ 0.019), ruling out heritable genetic or epigenetic causes. Second, deletion of *RAD9*, a component of the DNA damage checkpoint, dramatically reduced S+early M phase variance in both monosomic and disomic strains, identifying stochastic DNA damage as the principal driver of variability in this cell-cycle window. Analysis of gene expression responses using fluorescent reporters (GAL1pr-YFP, P4xHSE-YFP, P4xUPRE-GFP) under galactose, heat shock, and DTT conditions revealed uniformly elevated standard deviations in all aneuploid strains tested — with both intrinsic (pathway-specific) and extrinsic (global) noise components contributing.

Crucially, the authors extended these findings to mammalian models: inbred trisomy 19 and trisomy 13 mouse embryos (C57BL/J6 background) showed marked phenotypic variability in facial morphology, nuchal edema thickness, and hemorrhaging relative to isogenic euploid littermates. This demonstrates that non-genetic individuality is a universal property of the aneuploid state, not an artifact of yeast biology. The findings provide a new mechanistic framework for understanding the striking variability in clinical presentation and treatment response observed in aneuploidy-associated diseases, including Down syndrome and cancer, beyond what allelic variance alone can explain.

## Significance in Introduction Context
- Challenges the prevailing assumption that phenotypic heterogeneity in aneuploid diseases is explained solely by allelic variation among individuals; introduces non-genetic individuality as an additional, intrinsic source of variability.
- Provides direct experimental evidence that chromosome-scale gene dosage imbalances erode the robustness of multiple biological networks, with severity proportional to the degree of aneuploidy.
- Essential background for students learning the biology of aneuploidy, chromosome biology, and the molecular basis of diseases such as Down syndrome and cancer, where patient-to-patient variability is a defining clinical challenge.

## Future Research Directions
- Identify molecular mechanisms driving G1 length variability in aneuploid cells beyond DNA damage — candidates include stochastic protein synthesis rates, metabolic stress, and protein folding imbalances
- Determine which specific gene dosage changes most strongly destabilize biological network robustness
- Assess whether non-genetic individuality in human cancer aneuploids contributes to clonal heterogeneity and drug resistance
- Elucidate adaptive mechanisms by which chronic aneuploid strains partially attenuate G1 variability over time
- Investigate whether aneuploidy-driven phenotypic diversification functions as a bet-hedging strategy in evolutionary or therapeutic contexts
