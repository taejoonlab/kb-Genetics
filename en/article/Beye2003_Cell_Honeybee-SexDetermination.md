# The Gene csd Is the Primary Signal for Sexual Development in the Honeybee and Encodes an SR-Type Protein

## Citation (NLM)
Beye M, Hasselmann M, Fondrk MK, Page RE Jr, Omholt SW. The gene csd is the primary signal for sexual development in the honeybee and encodes an SR-type protein. Cell. 2003;114(4):419–429.

**DOI:** DOI not identified in extraction. Cell 114:419–429 (2003).

## Summary

The honeybee (Apis mellifera) employs a haplodiploid reproductive system in which males develop from unfertilized (haploid) eggs and females from fertilized (diploid) eggs. However, ploidy alone does not determine sex: inbred crosses frequently yield diploid males, a phenomenon explained by the "complementary sex determination" hypothesis—the allelic composition at a single sex-determining locus governs sex. This landmark study by Beye et al. (2003) identifies, positionally clones, and functionally characterizes the molecular primary signal: the **complementary sex determiner (csd)** gene.

### Background
Following Whiting's (1943) observations in the parasitic wasp Bracon hebetor, a model was proposed in which a single multi-allelic locus dictates sex in Hymenoptera: heterozygous individuals develop as females, hemizygous haploids as males, and homozygous diploids as sterile diploid males. In honeybee populations, an estimated 11–19 csd alleles segregate. Despite the genetic clarity of the model, its molecular basis had remained unknown until this study.

### Key Experiments and Results

**1. Positional Cloning of csd**
- Using genetic markers (Q and Z) flanking the sex locus, the authors performed a chromosome walk assembling over 70 kb of contiguous genomic DNA.
- Fine-scale recombinant mapping across 1000 meioses localized the csd locus to a 13 kb interval between markers GWQFMb and MKFHi-52 that is always heterozygous in females.
- Shotgun sequencing of a 24 kb region identified two embryonically expressed transcripts: **csd** (within the zero-recombinant interval) and **Ex4.8–5.8** (in the recombinant zone).

**2. CSD Encodes an SR-Related Protein**
- The csd transcript is 1453 bp and encodes a 385-amino-acid protein with nine exons spanning 9279 bp of genomic sequence.
- Sequence analysis reveals an arginine/serine-rich (RS) domain and a C-terminal proline-rich region, structurally similar to the Drosophila sex-determination protein TRA (transformer).
- Unlike TRA, CSD lacks an RNA recognition motif (RRM) and the conserved 11-amino-acid TRA-specific motif, indicating that CSD is an independently evolved member of the SR-related protein family rather than a TRA ortholog.

**3. csd Transcription Is Identical in Both Sexes**
- Northern blot confirms a single ~1500 bp transcript in female embryos.
- RT-PCR throughout development shows csd is transcribed from blastoderm stage onward in both sexes, with no detectable sex-specific differences in transcript level or splicing.
- This establishes that sex-specific information is encoded at the protein level (allelic composition), not at the transcript level.

**4. Extreme Allelic Amino Acid Variation**
- Sequencing four csd allelic proteins (CSD A, C, b1-4, d1-18) reveals that the N-terminal region (positions 1–229) is 70.3% identical across alleles, while the RS domain and proline-rich C-terminus share only 40.7% identity.
- A **hypervariable region** (positions 339–383) composed mainly of Asn (N) and Tyr (Y) repeats distinguishes alleles most dramatically.
- Pairwise identity between the most divergent alleles drops to 59–61%, yet all predicted isoelectric points are very similar (~10.1–10.8), preserving similar physicochemical properties.

**5. RNAi Functional Analysis**
- Injection of csd dsRNA into genetic female (heterozygous csd) embryos caused 92% (36/39) to develop fully male gonads (testes), with no intersex phenotypes.
- Non-injected (n=23) and non-csd dsRNA controls (n=33) all developed normal ovaries.
- Injection of csd dsRNA into genetic (haploid) males had no phenotypic effect—all developed normal testes.
- Conclusion: csd encodes a functional product only when transcripts from two different alleles are present (heterozygous); a single allele product is non-functional, leading to default male gonad development.

### Proposed Model
The authors propose that two CSD proteins from distinct alleles cooperatively form a functional heteromeric complex that recruits an RNA-binding SR protein (likely a TRA-2 homolog) to drive female-specific splicing of the Apis doublesex (Am-dsx) pre-mRNA, producing female-determining DSX protein. In hemizygous or homozygous males, the single CSD protein is non-functional and male-specific dsx splicing occurs by default.

## Significance in Introduction Context

Why this paper matters at the beginning of the honeybee sex determination story:
- **First molecular primary signal in a haplodiploid**: This is the first positional cloning of a sex-determining gene in any haplodiploid organism, providing molecular grounding to decades of genetic inference.
- **Allelic composition as signal**: Unlike the X:A dosage system (Drosophila) or SRY (mammals), sex here is determined by the combination of alleles rather than the presence/absence of a chromosome or gene—a conceptually distinct mechanism.
- **Structural link to the tra/dsx cascade**: Despite independent evolution, CSD's SR-domain architecture hints at a conserved downstream pathway (dsx regulation), which Hasselmann et al. (2008) and Gempe et al. (2009) later trace to the ancestral fem gene.
- **Foundation for the full series**: This paper establishes csd as the primary signal and provides the experimental toolkit (inbred crosses, RNAi, allele genotyping) used by all subsequent papers in the series.

## Future Research Directions

Open questions raised by this paper:

- What is the molecular mechanism by which two different CSD allelic proteins form a functional heteromeric complex while two identical copies do not? Crystal structure or co-immunoprecipitation studies are needed.
- Which RNA-binding co-factor (TRA-2 homolog or equivalent) cooperates with CSD to splice Am-dsx pre-mRNA? Identifying this partner is essential for completing the pathway.
- Is heterozygous csd activity required throughout development to maintain female fate, or only during a critical embryonic window? Clone analysis in heterozygous females could resolve this.
- What maintains the unusually high allelic diversity (balancing selection) at the csd locus, and how rapidly do new alleles arise and spread in natural populations?
- Do csd-like complementary sex determination mechanisms exist in other Hymenoptera, or is the csd gene itself unique to honeybees?
