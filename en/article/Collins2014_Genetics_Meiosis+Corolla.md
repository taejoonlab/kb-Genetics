## Summary

This research article from R. Scott Hawley's laboratory at the Stowers Institute for Medical Research identifies and characterizes Corolla, a novel component of the central region of the Drosophila melanogaster synaptonemal complex (SC).

**Scientific context:** The laws of Mendelian inheritance are, as the authors state in their opening sentence, "little more than a statistical restatement of the events that underlie the proper segregation of homologous chromosomes at the first meiotic division." The SC is essential for this segregation—it links paired homologs during prophase I and is required for crossover formation. Before this study, the only known transverse filament (TF) protein in Drosophila was C(3)G.

**Key experimental strategy and results:**

*Nondisjunction screen:* Over 120,000 EMS-induced mutants were screened for elevated nondisjunction. Three mutants mapping to a single complementation group (mei-391, mei-39129, mei-39166) were selected for characterization.

*Gene identification by NGS:* Whole-genome sequencing of two mutant strains compared to the parental wild-type identified mutations exclusively in CG8316, later named corolla. All three EMS alleles carry stop-codon-generating mutations in exon 3 (at amino acids 173, 199, and 188), and the gene was renamed corolla after its cytological phenotype.

*Phenotypic characterization:* corolla mutants showed X chromosome nondisjunction approaching 50% (vs. ~0.1% in wild type), 4th chromosome nondisjunction, severe centromere clustering defects (average 3.97 CID foci vs. 1.80 in wild type), a 63% reduction in programmed DSBs, and near-complete elimination of third chromosome meiotic recombination. All phenotypes were rescued by a single genomic copy of corolla.

*Structured illumination microscopy (SIM):* Using antibody to the C-terminal globular domain of C(3)G—which localizes to the lateral edges of the SC by immuno-EM—two parallel C(3)G tracks were resolved with an inter-track distance of 137 ± 3 nm. A Corolla signal appeared as a single peak between the two C(3)G tracks in 33/34 analyzed SC segments, establishing Corolla as a central region protein.

*Yeast two-hybrid:* Strong physical interaction between Corolla and Corona (Cona), the central element protein, was demonstrated using both ADE2 and MEL1 reporter assays. This provides the first evidence of a direct physical linkage between any two Drosophila inner-SC proteins.

**Model:** Corolla possesses multiple predicted coiled-coil domains and depends on C(3)G for chromosomal localization. In c(3)G mutants, Corolla fails to associate with chromosomes; when C(3)G cannot attach to the lateral elements, both Corolla and Cona assemble into polycomplexes. The authors propose that all three central region proteins (C(3)G, Cona, Corolla) function as an interdependent unit, with Cona and Corolla stabilizing antiparallel C(3)G interactions needed to form the SC.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Demonstrates the power of phenotype-first (forward) genetic screening in Drosophila for discovering new molecular players in essential processes
- Shows how super-resolution microscopy (SIM) transformed the analysis of protein sub-localization within cytological structures like the SC
- Raises the question of SC protein conservation: Corolla shares partial amino acid similarity with C. elegans SYP-4, suggesting that the central region proteins of the SC may be more evolutionarily conserved than previously recognized
