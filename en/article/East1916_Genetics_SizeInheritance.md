# Studies on Size Inheritance in Nicotiana

## Citation (NLM)
East EM. Studies on size inheritance in Nicotiana. Genetics. 1916 Mar;1(2):164–176.

**DOI:** https://www.genetics.org/content/1/2/164 (original 1916, no DOI assigned)

---

## Summary

This paper by Edward M. East (Harvard University, Bussey Institution) was published in the inaugural issue of *Genetics* in 1916 and constitutes a landmark demonstration that continuous quantitative variation is governed by Mendelian inheritance of multiple factors.

**The conceptual dispute:** At the time, some geneticists argued that size characters—continuous traits—could not be described by Mendelian notation without additional assumptions. East counters this view directly, arguing that the criticisms leveled against the Mendelian interpretation of quantitative traits "come no nearer the mark" because they would apply equally to Mendelism as a whole.

**Experimental system:** Two highly inbred varieties of Nicotiana longiflora were crossed: variety 330 (mean corolla length 40.54 mm, variance 3.53 mm²) and variety 383 (mean 93.30 mm, variance 5.11 mm²). Corolla length was measured for individual plants across multiple generations (P, F1, F2, F3).

**Mathematical requirements:** East lists eight conditions that pedigree data must satisfy for a Mendelian interpretation of size inheritance, including: uniform F1 with intermediate means, greatly increased F2 variance, recovery of grandparental types in large F2, and divergent means and variances among F3 families derived from different F2 individuals.

**Key results:**

*F1:* Mean corolla length (63.53 mm) was intermediate between parental means (average 66.92 mm). F1 variance (8.53 mm²) was somewhat larger than parentals—possibly reflecting incomplete parental homozygosity.

*F2:* The 244 F2 plants showed a mean (68.65 mm) close to the parental average but greatly increased variance (40.52 mm²). No F2 plant recovered either parental mean—expected if ≥5 loci contribute, since each parental genotype would occur with frequency <1/1000 in the F2.

*F3:* Families derived from F2 plants selected from different points on the phenotypic distribution showed markedly different means and variances, demonstrating that the F2 variation was heritable and segregating according to Mendelian principles.

**Legacy:** Sewall Wright as a graduate student used data like East's to develop the Wright-Castle estimator of the minimum number of loci contributing to a quantitative trait difference. Applied to East's data, the estimator yields n ≈ 9.6 loci. This study, together with Johannsen's pure-line experiments and Nilsson-Ehle's wheat kernel color work, provided the empirical foundation for Fisher's (1918) mathematical synthesis of Mendelian and biometrical approaches.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A founding paper of quantitative genetics—demonstrates that the continuous phenotypic distributions studied by Galton and biometricians are produced by segregation and assortment of multiple Mendelian factors
- Provides the empirical basis for the Wright-Castle estimator, a key historical tool for estimating the number of QTLs from crossing experiments
- Published in the inaugural issue of *Genetics*—a historical marker for when genetics became a formal discipline

## Future Research Directions

- Apply modern QTL mapping to the same N. longiflora variety 330 × 383 cross to identify and characterize the actual loci contributing to corolla length difference, testing the Wright-Castle estimate of ~10 loci
- Develop improved estimators of QTL number that account for dominance and epistasis, building on the limitations of the Wright-Castle formula that East's data have historically been used to illustrate
