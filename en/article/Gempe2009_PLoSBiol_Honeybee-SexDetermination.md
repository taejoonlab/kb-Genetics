# Sex Determination in Honeybees: Two Separate Mechanisms Induce and Maintain the Female Pathway

## Citation (NLM)
Gempe T, Hasselmann M, Schiøtt M, Hause G, Otte M, Beye M. Sex determination in honeybees: two separate mechanisms induce and maintain the female pathway. PLoS Biol. 2009;7(10):e1000222. doi:10.1371/journal.pbio.1000222

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.1000222](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.1000222)

---

## Summary

Following the discovery that csd is the primary sex-determining signal (Beye 2003) and that fem is a second switch gene activated downstream of csd (Hasselmann 2008), three central questions remained: (1) How is the female-determined state maintained throughout development after the initial csd signal? (2) Is csd activity required continuously, or only during a brief embryonic window? (3) Do fem and csd regulate germ cell fate in addition to somatic differentiation? Gempe et al. (2009) systematically answers these questions, establishing the complete regulatory architecture of honeybee sex determination.

### Background
The full SDL had been assembled into five protein-coding genes (GB11211, GB13727, fem, csd, and GB30480). While Hasselmann et al. (2008) established fem and csd as binary switch genes based on head morphology, detailed analysis of the full spectrum of sexual differentiation—including internal reproductive organs, germ cells, and external appendages—through late pupal stage was still needed. The regulatory relationship between fem and csd (including the question of maintenance) had also not been fully dissected.

### Key Experiments and Results

**1. Systematic Functional Analysis of All Five SDL Genes**
- dsRNA knockdown of GB11211, GB13727, and GB30480 produced no sex-converting phenotype in either sex; knockdown was confirmed by real-time RT-PCR.
- fem siRNA: 74% of females showed complete gonadal sex reversal to testes; no effect in males.
- csd siRNA: 88% of females showed complete gonadal sex reversal to normal-sized testes; no effect in males.
- Distinguishing finding: fem-repressed testes are smaller and contain fewer, shorter testioles compared to csd-repressed or wild-type male testes, suggesting a subtle timing difference in how fem and csd control gonad differentiation.

**2. Complete Sexual Reversal: Soma and Germ Line**
- Analysis extended to late pupae (P3) to assess full morphological differentiation.
- fem or csd siRNA-treated females (74–85%) display all aspects of male differentiation in internal organs: fully formed testes, mucus glands, and endophallus.
- Germ cell fate: cross-sections of testicular tubules from fem-repressed females reveal spermatids, demonstrating that **fem controls germ cell sexual identity**—in contrast to Drosophila tra, which is restricted to the soma.
- Hind leg appendage: pollen brush, pollen comb, and lobe (female-specific structures) are replaced by male structures in fem- and csd-repressed females.
- Mosaic intersexuals (7% of fem siRNA-treated females from non-inbred crosses): adjacent male and female structures on a single first tarsal segment, consistent with **cell-autonomous sex determination** and providing evidence for a cell-stabilizing positive feedback mechanism.

**3. Regulatory Hierarchy: csd → fem → Am-dsx**
- In csd-silenced pseudomales: both fem and Am-dsx transcripts predominantly exhibit male splice variants.
- In fem-silenced pseudomales: both fem and Am-dsx transcripts also predominantly show male splice variants.
- Interpretation: Active Csd protein directs fem pre-mRNA into the female splice form → Fem protein directs Am-dsx pre-mRNA into the female splice form. Male splicing is the **default regulatory state** that requires no sex-specifying signal.
- The male variant of fem reappears when fem activity is experimentally suppressed and then resumes in late larvae, demonstrating that **csd cannot restore female fem splicing once its critical window has passed**.

**4. Positive Feedback Loop of fem: Maintenance Mechanism**
- Developmental profile of fem mRNA: female fem transcripts appear at late blastoderm (~25–35 h post-oviposition) and persist throughout embryogenesis, larval stages, and pupal development.
- When fem is transiently suppressed in early embryos, male fem mRNA is produced even when endogenous fem transcription resumes in late larvae—because csd is no longer competent to switch fem splicing—demonstrating that csd acts only in a **critical developmental window**.
- **Key experiment**: Injection of in vitro-synthesized fem mRNA into haploid (male) embryos induces a partial shift of endogenous fem pre-mRNA from male to female splicing. This trans-activation demonstrates that **Fem protein promotes its own synthesis by directing female splicing of its own pre-mRNA**—a positive autoregulatory feedback loop.
- This feedback loop: (i) generates a stably determined female state following the initial csd signal; (ii) maintains it throughout development; and (iii) produces binary (all-male or all-female), not intermediate, cell fates (evidenced by the mosaic intersex phenotype).

**5. Two Separate Mechanisms: Induction and Maintenance**
- Induction: Heterozygous CSD proteins in early embryogenesis direct female splicing of fem → Fem protein produced.
- Maintenance: Fem protein directs female splicing of its own transcript via a positive feedback loop, independent of ongoing csd activity.
- These are mechanistically separate: csd is only needed for initial induction; fem feedback maintains the decision.

### Comparison with Drosophila and Ceratitis
- In Drosophila: Sxl protein maintains itself by splicing its own transcript (positive autoregulatory loop), operating upstream of tra → dsx.
- In Ceratitis: Cc-Tra protein similarly maintains an autoregulatory loop at the tra level.
- In honeybees: Fem protein maintains the female state at the fem level—using the same strategy (positive splicing feedback) at the same hierarchical level as Cc-tra in Ceratitis, but different molecules from Drosophila Sxl.
- Evolutionary inference: The maintenance of sexual fate via a self-splicing loop at the fem/tra gene level is likely ancestral; Drosophila's Sxl-based maintenance is a co-opted, derived feature.

## Significance in Introduction Context

Why this paper completes the foundational understanding of honeybee sex determination:
- **Two-mechanism paradigm**: Separating induction (csd) from maintenance (fem feedback) is a conceptually powerful framework that generalizes beyond honeybees to other systems where primary signals act transiently.
- **Direct experimental proof of autoregulatory feedback**: The gain-of-function fem mRNA injection experiment is a clean demonstration that Fem protein drives its own female-splice form, closing a key mechanistic gap.
- **Germ cells controlled by the same pathway**: fem governs the sexual identity of both soma and germ line, indicating that the honeybee pathway is more broadly deployed than the Drosophila tra gene (soma-only).
- **Teaching value**: Completes the Beye 2003 → Hasselmann 2008 → Gempe 2009 series; highlights how a small cascade of two switch genes (csd + fem) achieves robust, stable, binary sex determination across the entire organism.

## Future Research Directions

Open questions raised by this paper:

- What RNA-binding co-factor(s) does Fem protein use to recognize and splice fem and Am-dsx pre-mRNAs, given that Fem has no RRM? (Directly addressed by Nissen 2012, identifying Am-Tra2.)
- What are the exact cis-regulatory elements in fem pre-mRNA that the autoregulatory loop targets, and how are they distinct from the csd-responsive elements?
- What is the precise molecular nature of the "critical window" for csd—is it the dilution of Csd protein, chromatin remodeling, or competition from newly expressed inhibitors?
- How does the male default state reset in early embryos before the female pathway is induced? What prevents maternal Fem mRNA from locking eggs into female fate?
- Does the Fem-mediated germ cell sex determination operate through the same Am-dsx pathway as the soma, or via germ-cell-specific mechanisms?
