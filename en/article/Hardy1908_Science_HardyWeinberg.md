# Mendelian Proportions in a Mixed Population

## Citation (NLM)

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1126/science.28.706.49](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.28.706.49)

## Summary

This short letter is the original paper that mathematically proved one of the most fundamental principles in population genetics — Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium. It was written on 5 April 1908 by G. H. Hardy, a mathematician at Trinity College, Cambridge, and submitted to *Science*.

The paper's starting point is a rebuttal of a claim by Udny Yule, who had suggested that "if brachydactyly (short-fingeredness) is dominant, then over time the population should come to have three brachydactylous people for every one normal." Hardy showed, using nothing more than multiplication-table mathematics, that this expectation is entirely unfounded.

Hardy's argument runs as follows. Given a pair of Mendelian characters *Aa* with *A* dominant, let the numbers of pure dominants (*AA*), heterozygotes (*Aa*), and pure recessives (*aa*) in any generation stand in the ratio *p* : 2*q* : *r*. Under random mating, equal sex ratios, and equal fertility, the next generation's ratio is (*p*+*q*)² : 2(*p*+*q*)(*q*+*r*) : (*q*+*r*)². The condition for this distribution to persist unchanged is *q*² = *pr*, and from the second generation on this "stable distribution" is maintained.

As a concrete example, in an initial population where the brachydactyly:normal ratio is 1:10,000, if brachydactyly is dominant then in the second generation the ratio becomes 20,001:100,020,001 — i.e. roughly 2:10,000. It merely doubles and does not increase thereafter. Conversely, if brachydactyly is recessive, the ratio drops sharply to about 1:100,000,000 in the second generation and does not decrease further. There is therefore no basis whatever for the idea that "a dominant character tends to spread through the whole population, or a recessive character to die out."

Hardy also notes that small deviations (chance events) in real populations produce distributions slightly off the stable one, but these too converge to a new stable distribution in the following generation, so stability is preserved. At the end of the paper Hardy shows intellectual honesty by noting that Karl Pearson had already recognized the "stability" of the 1:2:1 ratio.

This principle was discovered independently the same year in Germany by Wilhelm Weinberg, and so is named the "Hardy-Weinberg law." The equilibrium holds under five assumptions — random mating, no natural selection, no mutation, no migration, and infinitely large population size — and provides the framework for analyzing how far a population departs from equilibrium when these assumptions are not met.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- This is the historical original that first mathematically proved Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, the foundational principle of population genetics.
- It provides the context of the 1908 dispute between Mendelian genetics and biometry — directly refuting the misconception that a dominant allele should come to dominate a population.
- In class this paper is the starting point for all of population genetics, essential for understanding the generational stability of allele frequencies and the null model against which evolution is measured.
