# Evidence for the Evolutionary Nascence of a Novel Sex Determination Pathway in Honeybees

## Citation (NLM)
Hasselmann M, Gempe T, Schiøtt M, Nunes-Silva CG, Otte M, Beye M. Evidence for the evolutionary nascence of a novel sex determination pathway in honeybees. Nature. 2008;454(7203):519–522. doi:10.1038/nature07052

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nature07052](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature07052)

---

## Summary

Beye et al. (2003) established that the csd gene is the primary sex-determining signal in the honeybee, with heterozygosity at csd directing female development. The molecular link between CSD activity and downstream sexual differentiation remained unknown. Hasselmann et al. (2008) resolved this by discovering a second binary switch gene, **fem (feminizer)**, located 12 kb upstream of csd within the Sex Determination Locus (SDL). Crucially, they demonstrate that csd arose from a gene duplication of fem within the honeybee lineage and was subsequently shaped by strong positive selection—providing a mechanistic account of how a novel sex-determining pathway can emerge evolutionarily.

### Background
Prior analysis of the SDL had covered ~21 kb, but the AT-rich nature of this region caused its underrepresentation in cloning and sequencing strategies. In this study, the authors assembled an additional 26 kb, bringing the total SDL coverage to five protein-coding genes. Only csd and the newly identified fem were shown by RNAi knockdown to have sex-determining functions.

### Key Experiments and Results

**1. Discovery of fem as the Second SDL Switch Gene**
- The fem gene lies 12 kb upstream of csd within the SDL; it encodes an SR-type protein with >70% amino acid identity to CSD in the C-terminal Arg/Ser-rich and Pro-rich domains.
- Unlike CSD, Fem lacks the hypervariable region and carries an additional Arg/Ser domain at its N-terminus; both are paralogs.
- RNAi knockdown of fem in female embryos converted 78% to entirely male head morphology; knockdown in males had no effect.
- RNAi of the three remaining SDL genes (GB11211, GB13727, GB30480) produced no sex-determining phenotype.

**2. Sex-Specific Splicing of the fem Transcript**
- Male fem transcripts retain all of exon 3, which contains an in-frame stop codon → premature translation termination → non-functional protein.
- Female fem transcripts splice out part of exon 3 plus exons 4 and 5 → intact open reading frame → 403-amino-acid functional Fem protein.
- When csd is knocked down in females by RNAi, fourth-instar larvae predominantly display male-type fem splicing, demonstrating that **csd activity directs female splicing of fem**.
- Conversely, fem knockdown does not alter csd mRNA levels, confirming a unidirectional regulatory relationship (csd → fem, not vice versa).

**3. csd Is Unique to the Honeybee Lineage: Gene Duplication**
- fem orthologs are found in multiple corbiculate bees (Apis spp., Melipona compressipes, Bombus terrestris) and the jewel wasp (Nasonia vitripennis).
- csd sequences are found only in Apis; in gene trees based on synonymous and amino acid substitutions, honeybee csd and fem cluster together as a single clade.
- Relative rate tests confirm that the stingless bee fem sequence has a lower substitution rate than honeybees, ruling out an accelerated rate in a non-Apis lineage as explanation for clustering.
- The duplication event is placed within the honeybee lineage: after the split from stingless bees and bumble bees (~70 Myr ago) but before Apis species divergence (~10 Myr ago).
- Southern blot of B. terrestris genomic DNA with fem probe shows a single-copy locus, confirming that the duplication is restricted to the Apis lineage.

**4. Positive Selection Drove csd Divergence from fem**
- Branch i→ii (immediately post-duplication): significant excess of non-synonymous (N) over synonymous (S) substitutions (P=10⁻⁴, Fisher's exact test), indicating strong positive Darwinian selection.
- Branch ii→iii (A. mellifera lineage): excess of N over S is also significant but significantly less than the early post-duplication branch (P=0.016), suggesting stronger selection during the initial emergence of csd.
- Six amino acid substitutions fixed in csd form part of a coiled-coil motif—a protein–protein interaction interface essential for the allelic discrimination mechanism.
- fem branches show excess of S over N substitutions (purifying selection, P<0.05), consistent with functional conservation of the ancestral sex-determining role.

### Proposed Model
csd arose by gene duplication of fem, and positive selection drove the acquisition of an allelic (heterodimeric) mode of activation. Heteroallelic CSD proteins (from two distinct alleles) direct female splicing of fem pre-mRNA, producing functional Fem protein that implements the female developmental program. The csd-based system thus controls sex-specific splicing of its own progenitor transcript. This provides direct molecular evidence that gene duplication followed by positive selection on one duplicate can generate a novel upstream signal in a developmental pathway.

## Significance in Introduction Context

Why this paper is central to the honeybee sex determination series:
- **Missing link between csd and dsx**: fem fills the regulatory gap between the primary signal (csd) and the final effector (Am-dsx); the pathway architecture is now csd → fem → Am-dsx.
- **Evolutionary origin of a new primary signal**: The duplication–divergence model explains how a novel sex-determining mechanism can arise from an existing gene—consistent with the "bottom-up" cascade growth theory of Wilkins (1995), providing one of the clearest empirical examples.
- **Why csd has a hypervariable region and fem does not**: Positive selection on csd drove acquisition of the allele-diversifying hypervariable region; purifying selection maintained the conserved functional architecture of fem.
- **Teaching context**: This paper answers the logical question following Beye 2003—"how does heterozygous CSD actually trigger female development?"—and sets up Gempe 2009 (which dissects the feedback maintenance of the female state) and Nissen 2012 (which identifies Am-Tra2 as the RNA-binding co-factor).

## Future Research Directions

Open questions raised by this paper:

- What is the biochemical mechanism by which heteroallelic CSD proteins—but not homoallelic ones—direct female splicing of fem? Structural studies of the CSD coiled-coil interface are needed.
- Does the fem protein directly bind fem pre-mRNA to direct its own female splicing (autoregulation), and if so, what are the cis-elements and co-factors involved? (Addressed partially by Gempe 2009.)
- What RNA-binding protein serves as the co-factor for CSD/Fem in the splicing machinery, given that both CSD and Fem lack an RRM? (Addressed by Nissen 2012 identifying Am-Tra2.)
- How do the 15+ csd alleles in natural populations differ structurally, and what prevents new alleles from being recognized as 'self' by existing alleles?
- Are there other Hymenoptera outside Apis where a recent gene duplication is similarly co-opted for sex determination, or is this a unique evolutionary event?
