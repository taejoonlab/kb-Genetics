# Anterior Pituitary Transcriptome Suggests Differences in ACTH Release in Tame and Aggressive Foxes

## Citation (NLM)

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/g3.117.300508](https://doi.org/10.1534/g3.117.300508)

## Summary

This study performs RNA-seq transcriptomic analysis on anterior pituitary tissue from 6 tame and 6 aggressive foxes selected through the long-running Belyaev/Trut domestication experiment (1959–present). The anterior pituitary is the central hub of the HPA (hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal) axis, releasing ACTH to regulate the stress response. The authors hypothesized that decades of behavioral selection would produce detectable differences in pituitary gene expression between tame and aggressive lines.

RNA-seq analysis identified **346 differentially expressed genes (DEGs)** at FDR < 0.05 between the two behavioral groups. Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analysis highlighted pseudopodium organization, JAK-STAT signaling, and cAMP regulation pathways as most significantly enriched. Notably, POMC (proopiomelanocortin)—the precursor protein for ACTH—was not differentially expressed at the transcript level, suggesting that differences in ACTH output arise downstream through post-translational processing or secretion efficiency rather than transcription.

Weighted gene co-expression network analysis (WGCNA) identified the **Antique White 4** and **Pale Turquoise** modules as more active in aggressive foxes. These modules contained genes associated with neuroendocrine function and intracellular signal transduction.

Alternative splicing analysis found significant differential splicing in 36 genes between the behavioral groups, with several key regulators of JAK-STAT signaling among the differentially spliced transcripts.

The study represents the first quantitative transcriptomic characterization of the molecular consequences of multi-generational behavioral selection. Despite the small sample size (n=6 per group), the results provide important molecular leads linking the domestication syndrome to HPA axis function at the gene expression level.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides a modern example of forward genetic reasoning applied to a complex behavioral trait, using transcriptomics as the readout
- Directly addresses the molecular mechanisms underlying the Belyaev/Trut fox experiment—among the most famous domestication studies in behavioral genetics
- Teaches RNA-seq analysis, WGCNA co-expression modules, and DEG interpretation in a concrete biological context
- Essential for this course because it connects HPA axis biology, behavioral genetics, and modern genomic methods in a single study

## Future Research Directions

- Replicate RNA-seq with larger sample sizes to confirm reproducibility of identified DEGs
- Measure protein expression of key DEGs in pituitary tissue to validate transcriptional findings
- Determine whether differential activity of POMC processing enzymes (PC1/3, PC2) accounts for ACTH output differences between behavioral lines
