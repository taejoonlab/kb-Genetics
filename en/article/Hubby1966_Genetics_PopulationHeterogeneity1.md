# A Molecular Approach to the Study of Genic Heterozygosity in Natural Populations. I. The Number of Alleles at Different Loci in Drosophila pseudoobscura

## Citation (NLM)
Hubby JL, Lewontin RC. A molecular approach to the study of genic heterozygosity in natural populations. I. The number of alleles at different loci in Drosophila pseudoobscura. Genetics. 1966;54(2):577-594.

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1093/genetics/54.2.577](https://doi.org/10.1093/genetics/54.2.577)

---

## Summary

This paper is the first of two companion papers published by Hubby and Lewontin in 1966, establishing protein electrophoresis as a systematic approach to measuring genetic variation in natural populations.

The researchers collected 43 strains of *Drosophila pseudoobscura* from five geographic locations in western and central North America (Flagstaff, Mather, Wildrose, Cimarron, Strawberry Canyon) and from Bogota, Colombia. Using acrylamide gel electrophoresis, they detected allelic variation based on differences in net charge that produce different migration velocities in an electric field.

Of 21 loci examined, allelic variation was detected at 9. The most polymorphic locus was esterase-5 with 6 alleles, followed by malic dehydrogenase (4 alleles), leucine aminopeptidase (4 alleles), three alkaline phosphatases (2 alleles each), and larval proteins Pt-7, Pt-8, Pt-10 (3-4 alleles). The remaining 12 loci showed no detectable variation.

Methodologically, the paper demonstrated that electrophoretic phenotypes follow simple Mendelian inheritance, allowing unambiguous identification of homozygotes and heterozygotes. Alleles were named by their relative electrophoretic mobility, a convention that became standard in subsequent population genetics research.

A key methodological contribution was the inclusion of larval proteins detected by general protein staining alongside specific enzyme assays. This approach minimized selection bias toward inherently variable loci. The similar polymorphism rates observed in both gene classes (3/10 larval proteins, 4/8 enzymes) validated the random sampling approach.

The paper also explicitly recognized that electrophoresis detects only approximately half of amino acid substitutions — those affecting net charge — meaning actual variation levels were higher than observed. This acknowledgment of bias toward underestimation, combined with conservative interpretations, established a methodological standard for subsequent decades of population genetics research.

Together with its companion paper (Lewontin & Hubby 1966), this work provided the first systematic molecular evidence that natural populations harbor unexpectedly high levels of genetic variation, offering direct empirical support for the "balance hypothesis" in the classical-versus-balance debate about polymorphism maintenance.

---

## Significance in Introduction Context

This paper marks a molecular turning point in population genetics. Traditional approaches relied on phenotypic observation or special marker genes, but this research demonstrated that biochemical methods could provide an unbiased random sample of variation across the genome. It enabled the debate about genetic variation levels in natural populations to be grounded for the first time in direct molecular data.

## Future Research Directions

DNA sequencing technology, which followed protein electrophoresis, enabled measurement of synonymous substitutions that electrophoresis cannot detect, yielding more accurate estimates of variation in natural populations. Modern whole-genome sequencing has overcome all the methodological limitations acknowledged in this paper, allowing simultaneous analysis of millions of loci and making the question of variation levels tractable at the genomic scale.
