# Rapid Identification of Paternal-Effect Lethal Mutations in C. elegans Using Hawaiian SNP Mapping

## Citation (NLM)
Jaramillo-Lambert A, Hao J, Xiao H, Xue Y, Bhatt A, Allen AK. Rapid identification of paternal-effect lethal mutations using a Hawaiian mapping panel in Caenorhabditis elegans. Genetics. 2016;204(4):1407-1422.

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.191551](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.191551)

---

## Summary

This study developed a methodology for rapid identification of paternal-effect embryonic lethal (Pel) mutations in *Caenorhabditis elegans* and used it to identify a temperature-sensitive allele of the *top-2* gene.

The researchers initially identified the *mel-15(it7)* allele as a paternal-effect embryonic lethal mutation. The defining feature of Pel mutations is that progeny die when a mutant male contributes sperm, but survive when wild-type sperm are used — the defect resides in the sperm rather than the egg.

To identify the mutant gene, the team combined Hawaiian SNP mapping with whole-genome sequencing (WGS). The Hawaiian strain (CB4856) carries numerous SNPs throughout the genome compared to the N2 Bristol reference strain, making it an ideal mapping tool to track mutation-linked regions. Cross experiments and SNP analysis of segregating individuals narrowed the candidate locus to a specific region on chromosome III.

WGS analysis identified a C2977T substitution in the *top-2* gene (encoding topoisomerase II, the *C. elegans* homolog of human TOP2A/2B). This change causes a missense mutation, Arg828Cys. The identification was confirmed by complementation tests and by a precise CRISPR-Cas9 reversion experiment restoring the arginine codon.

Characterization of the temperature-sensitive phenotype by fluorescence microscopy revealed that all 953 spermatids from top-2(it7ts) males produced at the restrictive temperature (25°C) were anucleate — completely lacking chromatin — while all 733 spermatids from wild-type N2 males contained normal chromatin. This demonstrates that TOP-2 is essential for chromosome segregation during meiosis.

Examination of anaphase I revealed chromatin bridges in top-2(it7ts) mutants, indicating failure to resolve chromosome catenation during homolog separation.

Importantly, double mutant analysis with *spo-11(ok79)* showed that loss of SPO-11 (which catalyzes meiotic double-strand breaks) did not suppress the top-2 defect. This means TOP-2's function in resolving chromosome entanglement is independent of DSB-dependent crossover formation.

Immunofluorescence confirmed that TOP-2 protein localizes to meiotic prophase I chromosomes, suggesting TOP-2 functions on meiotic chromosomes prior to synapsis and recombination.

---

## Significance in Introduction Context

This study is an exemplary demonstration of combining Hawaiian SNP mapping and WGS to rapidly identify a mutation discovered by genetic screening in *C. elegans*. It also established that Topoisomerase II is essential for meiotic chromosome segregation, deepening understanding of topoisomerase roles in cell division beyond mitosis.

## Future Research Directions

Future work should determine exactly at which meiotic prophase I substage TOP-2 resolves catenation, and how it cooperates with centromeric cohesin complexes. Because TOP2 inhibitors (e.g., doxorubicin, etoposide) are widely used anti-cancer drugs, understanding meiosis-specific TOP2 functions may have applications for infertility research related to germline development.
