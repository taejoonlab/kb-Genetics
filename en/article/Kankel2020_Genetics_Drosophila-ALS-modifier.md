# Genome-wide Drosophila Screen for ALS Disease Modifiers

## Citation (NLM)
Kankel MW, Sen A, Lu L, Theodorou M, et al. Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis modifiers in Drosophila reveal the phospholipase D pathway as a potential therapeutic target. Genetics. 2020;215(3):747-766.

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.120.303234](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.120.303234)

**Educational primer:** [[en/review/Kankel2020_Genetics_Drosophila-ALS-modifier.Banerjee2020primer]]

## Summary

This study performed a large-scale genome-wide modifier screen in *Drosophila melanogaster* to identify genetic modifiers of amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) disease models, identifying the phospholipase D (PLD) pathway as a potential therapeutic target.

ALS is a fatal neurodegenerative disease characterized by selective motor neuron death. TDP-43 pathological aggregation is observed in 97% of ALS patients. Major causative genes for familial ALS include FUS, TDP-43, C9orf72, and SOD1, yet effective treatments remain absent due to incomplete understanding of molecular pathways.

The researchers screened 15,500 Exelixis transposon insertions in a genome-wide modifier screen. ALS models were generated using the GAL4-UAS system to drive eye-specific overexpression of human mutant proteins (hFUSR521C or hTDP-43M337V), inducing eye degeneration phenotypes. Insertions were scored for their ability to suppress or enhance eye degeneration.

The screen identified 637 FUS modifiers, 553 TDP-43 modifiers, and 432 modifiers shared between both screens. Shared modifiers between two independent ALS gene models are enriched for components of shared molecular pathways.

Gene ontology and pathway analysis identified 6 components of the PLD pathway as suppressors in both screens: dPld (phospholipase D), ArfGAP3, Plc21C (phospholipase C), Rho1, Ras85D, and Rgl. RNAi knockdown of dPld suppressed eye degeneration in both FUS and TDP-43 models, and also suppressed a C9orf72 overexpression ALS model.

For mammalian validation, PLD pathway inhibition in SOD1 mutant mice produced modest motor function improvements, suggesting PLD pathway suppression may confer neuroprotective effects across multiple ALS etiologies.

A methodological strength of this study is the use of two independent ALS gene models to identify common modifiers, distinguishing disease-model-specific effects from general neurodegeneration pathways. It also demonstrates that traditional forward genetic screens remain powerful tools for therapeutic target discovery.

---

## Significance in Introduction Context

This study exemplifies how *Drosophila* genetic screens remain one of the most powerful approaches for exploring molecular pathways underlying complex human neurodegenerative diseases. The combination of GAL4-UAS controlled expression with large-scale transposon insertion collections provides an unbiased platform for therapeutic target discovery that complements biochemical and cell-based approaches.

## Future Research Directions

The exact molecular mechanism by which PLD pathway suppression provides neuroprotection in ALS models remains to be elucidated. Whether PLD-derived phosphatidic acid modulates mTOR signaling or autophagy to reduce pathological protein aggregation is an important open question. Functional analysis of the many non-PLD modifiers identified in this screen may reveal additional therapeutic targets.
