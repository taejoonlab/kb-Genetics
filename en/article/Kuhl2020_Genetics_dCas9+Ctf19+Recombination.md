# A dCas9-Based System Identifies a Central Role for Ctf19 in Kinetochore-Derived Suppression of Meiotic Recombination

## Citation (NLM)

## Summary

This study develops and applies a dCas9-based ectopic recruitment system in budding yeast (*S. cerevisiae*) to identify the specific kinetochore components responsible for suppressing meiotic crossovers (COs) in the pericentromeric region.

**Background**: Meiotic recombination is required for accurate chromosome segregation and genetic diversity, but COs are strongly suppressed near centromeres. COs close to the kinetochore impair biorientation tension sensing, leading to chromosome nondisjunction. The identity of the kinetochore components mediating this suppression was not known.

**Experimental approach**: The authors fused dCas9 to individual subunits of the Ctf19 kinetochore complex (Ctf19, Iml3, Wip1, Ctf3, Ndc10) and targeted these fusions to ectopic non-centromeric loci using guide RNAs. This allowed independent assessment of each component's ability to suppress COs in an artificial pericentromeric-like context.

**Key findings**:
- Only **Ctf19** significantly reduced CO frequency at the ectopic locus; Iml3, Wip1, Ctf3, and Ndc10 had no effect
- The CO-suppressive activity mapped to the N-terminal 30 amino acids of Ctf19 (ctf19^1-30)
- This activity depended on DDK (Dbf4-Dependent Kinase) phosphorylation of 9 serine/threonine residues within this region
- The mechanism involves Ctf19 recruiting the **Scc2-Scc4 cohesin loader** to the ectopic locus
- DSB (double-strand break) frequencies were unchanged, indicating that Ctf19 acts by **repair pathway channeling**—directing repair toward non-crossover (NCO) pathways rather than reducing initiation events

This work establishes the molecular pathway: Ctf19 → DDK phosphorylation → Scc2-Scc4 recruitment → CO suppression via NCO repair channeling.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- First identification of the specific kinetochore component mediating pericentromeric meiotic recombination suppression
- Demonstrates a novel application of CRISPR/dCas9 technology: not for genome editing but as a programmable protein recruitment platform to interrogate position-dependent biology
- DDK kinase and cohesin loader function are core concepts in cell cycle control, providing important conceptual connections within the course
- Essential for this course because it integrates meiosis, recombination, kinetochore biology, and CRISPR technology in a single elegant experimental framework

## Future Research Directions

- Test whether human orthologs of Ctf19 (CENP-P and CENP-O) perform the same CO suppression function in mammalian cells
- Identify the molecular mechanism by which Scc2-Scc4-loaded cohesin biases repair toward NCO pathways
- Investigate whether dysregulation of pericentromeric CO suppression contributes to human infertility or recurrent pregnancy loss
