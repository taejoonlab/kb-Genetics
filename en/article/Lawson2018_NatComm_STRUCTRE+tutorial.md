# A Tutorial on How Not to Over-Interpret STRUCTURE and ADMIXTURE Bar Plots

## Citation (NLM)
Lawson DJ, van Dorp L, Falush D. A tutorial on how not to over-interpret STRUCTURE and ADMIXTURE bar plots. Nat Commun. 2018;9(1):3258. doi:10.1038/s41467-018-05257-7

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-018-05257-7](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-018-05257-7)

---

## Summary

STRUCTURE and ADMIXTURE are widely used model-based clustering algorithms that characterize individuals and populations from genetic data. Both programs employ an "admixture model" assuming each individual derives ancestry from K genetically distinct source populations, simultaneously estimating ancestral allele frequencies and individual admixture proportions. When model assumptions are satisfied — as in the case of African Americans reflecting admixture between West African and European ancestral populations — the results are biologically interpretable.

However, the authors demonstrate through simulations that three demographically distinct scenarios — "Recent Admixture," "Ghost Admixture," and "Recent Bottleneck" — can produce nearly identical ADMIXTURE bar plots. Following the standard interpretation protocol literally for the latter two scenarios leads to inference of false population histories.

To address this, the authors developed badMIXTURE, a tool that evaluates the goodness of fit of STRUCTURE/ADMIXTURE models using chromosome painting palettes generated by CHROMOPAINTER. The core principle is that under a genuine recent admixture scenario, the palette of each admixed individual should be predictable as a mixture of ancestral population palettes in the corresponding admixture proportions. Systematic residual patterns indicate model misfit.

Four real case studies validate the approach. For the Ari of Ethiopia, previous ADMIXTURE-based interpretations concluding that Blacksmith and Cultivator groups derived from different ancestral sources were shown by badMIXTURE to reflect a bottleneck in the Blacksmiths rather than admixture. Analysis of worldwide human data demonstrated how sample composition biases can produce highly misleading results from K=2 through K=9 — for example, inferring the French as an admixture between East Asians and Papuan highlanders. For Indian data, high residuals in ASI populations were identified, and after correcting for recent genetic drift, evidence for four ancestry components was confirmed.

The authors conclude that STRUCTURE/ADMIXTURE are useful data summary tools but should never be the end of an analysis; badMIXTURE goodness-of-fit testing should be combined with additional hypothesis-testing approaches.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides a systematic critique of fundamental limitations and misuse of STRUCTURE/ADMIXTURE, among the most widely applied tools in population genetics, teaching methodological critical thinking
- Demonstrates empirically the problem of "model non-identifiability" — that identical visual outputs can arise from completely different population history scenarios
- Why this paper must be read in class: to develop the ability to critically evaluate population structure analysis results and understand the statistical assumptions underlying ancestry estimation that is frequently presented as background in GWAS and population genomics studies

## Future Research Directions

- Improve badMIXTURE to function more robustly without linkage information, enabling application to smaller marker sets
- Develop bias-correction methodology for ancient DNA ADMIXTURE analyses, where ancient samples tend to be inferred as mixtures of modern populations
