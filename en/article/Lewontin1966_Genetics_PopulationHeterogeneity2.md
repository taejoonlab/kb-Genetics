# A Molecular Approach to the Study of Genic Heterozygosity in Natural Populations. II. Amount of Variation and Degree of Heterozygosity in Natural Populations of Drosophila pseudoobscura

## Citation (NLM)
Lewontin RC, Hubby JL. A molecular approach to the study of genic heterozygosity in natural populations. II. Amount of variation and degree of heterozygosity in natural populations of Drosophila pseudoobscura. Genetics. 1966;54(2):595-609.

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1093/genetics/54.2.595](https://doi.org/10.1093/genetics/54.2.595)

---

## Summary

This paper is the second of two companion papers by Hubby and Lewontin (1966), applying the protein electrophoresis methodology established in the first paper to *Drosophila pseudoobscura* natural populations to quantitatively analyze the amount of genetic polymorphism and degree of heterozygosity.

The study examined 18 loci in strains from five U.S. natural populations (Strawberry Canyon, Wildrose, Cimarron, Mather, Flagstaff) and a single strain from Bogota, Colombia. At the species level, 7 of 18 loci were clearly polymorphic in more than one population, and 2 had rare local variants. Using conservative criteria, 39% of loci were polymorphic across the species. At the individual population level, an average of 30% of loci were polymorphic.

The more striking finding was the proportion of loci heterozygous in the average individual: 8-15% depending on population, with an overall average of approximately 12%. This was calculated directly from observed allele frequencies assuming Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.

The paper's central contribution was a systematic evaluation of three hypotheses to explain this variation. First, complete neutrality (isoalleles) is challenged by high heterozygosity observed even in small populations like Cimarron and Wildrose, and by the complete absence of "local pure races." Second, mutation-selection balance would require implausibly high mutation rates or extremely weak selection to explain the observed allele frequencies. Third, heterozygote advantage (heterosis) is biologically coherent but generates the "genetic load paradox" — balancing selection at thousands of loci would exponentially reduce population fitness to effectively zero.

Lewontin and Hubby proposed that at any given time only a fraction (perhaps less than 10%) of all polymorphisms are actually under selection, with most being relics of past selection combined with minimal migration to retard genetic drift. This interpretation directly foreshadowed the subsequent neutral theory vs. selectionist debate.

Among the most notable loci: esterase-5 harbored 6 alleles with no single dominant frequency across populations; Pt-8 showed a stable ~50:50 polymorphism of alleles .81 and .83 in every population; the Bogota strain had a unique .80 allele at Pt-8 not found elsewhere, illustrating geographically isolated populations developing private alleles.

---

## Significance in Introduction Context

This paper is one of the founding studies of molecular population genetics, providing the first systematic demonstration that genetic variation in natural populations far exceeds what classical population geneticists expected. The findings triggered the genetic load debate and directly catalyzed Kimura's neutral theory (1968), as well as the broader modern debate about the fraction of the genome under selection that drives contemporary population genomics.

## Future Research Directions

The genetic load paradox raised by this paper triggered the development of the neutral theory of molecular evolution. Modern whole-genome studies can now analyze millions of SNPs to quantitatively assess the relative contributions of neutral drift versus selection, and genome-wide scans for signatures of selection (selective sweeps, balancing selection) directly address the questions first posed by Lewontin and Hubby.
