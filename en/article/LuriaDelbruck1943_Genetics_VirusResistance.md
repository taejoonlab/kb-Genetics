# Mutations of Bacteria from Virus Sensitivity to Virus Resistance

## Citation (NLM)
Luria SE, Delbrück M. Mutations of bacteria from virus sensitivity to virus resistance. Genetics. 1943;28(6):491–511.

**DOI:** No DOI assigned (1943 publication)

---

## Summary

This landmark paper introduces the **fluctuation test**, one of the most important experiments in the history of genetics. Salvador Luria and Max Delbrück quantitatively demonstrated that bacteria acquire virus resistance through spontaneous mutations that occur before any exposure to the virus.

**Background:** When phages are added to a bacterial culture, almost all bacteria lyse, but a few survive and form resistant strains. Two competing hypotheses existed: (1) **Acquired immunity hypothesis** (Lamarckian view): virus contact directly induces mutations conferring resistance; (2) **Mutation hypothesis** (Darwinian view): resistance arises from spontaneous mutations independent of virus presence, and the virus merely selects pre-existing resistant mutants.

**Key insight:** Luria, while watching slot machines at a faculty mixer in 1942, realized that if the mutation hypothesis were correct, the number of resistant bacteria across independent cultures would show enormous variance — early mutations would produce large "jackpot" clones. Under the acquired immunity hypothesis, the count would follow a Poisson distribution (variance = mean).

**Theoretical framework:** Delbrück derived the probability distributions for resistant bacteria under each hypothesis. Under the mutation model, variance greatly exceeds the mean due to a long tail of high-count cultures; under acquired immunity, the distribution is Poisson. This mathematical distinction is the key experimental test.

**Experimental design:** Using E. coli B and phage α (later renamed T1), two types of experiments were performed: (1) **Control experiment**: multiple samples from the same culture — verifies Poisson sampling; (2) **Key experiment**: parallel independent cultures grown from small inocula, then each challenged with phage separately.

**Results:** Resistance counts varied enormously across independent cultures (variance >> mean). Some cultures had zero resistant bacteria while others had hundreds — the "jackpot" effect. In Experiment 16, the mean was 11.35, but 11 of 20 cultures had zero and 3 had 35–107 resistant colonies. This is wholly inconsistent with Poisson statistics expected under acquired immunity.

**Mutation rate:** Two methods yielded the mutation rate to resistance: (1) from the fraction of cultures with no resistant bacteria (Poisson method), and (2) from the average count. Across ten experiments, the mutation rate averaged 2.45 × 10⁻⁸ mutations per bacterium per division cycle — consistent across different media and culture volumes.

**Conclusion:** Results strongly support the mutation hypothesis. Resistant bacteria descend from rare clones arising from pre-existing spontaneous mutations; the virus merely selects them. This was the first rigorous experimental proof that **natural selection reveals pre-existing variation rather than inducing it** — a cornerstone of evolutionary theory demonstrated at the level of bacteria.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- First quantitative proof that mutations occur randomly with respect to selective pressure — cornerstone of the neo-Darwinian synthesis
- Resolved the Lamarck vs. Darwin debate experimentally, enabling the Modern Synthesis of genetics and evolution
- Demonstrates the power of statistical reasoning (Poisson distribution) in genetics — teaches quantitative thinking about rare events
- Foundational for understanding antibiotic resistance: resistance arises by selection of pre-existing spontaneous mutants, not drug-induced adaptation
