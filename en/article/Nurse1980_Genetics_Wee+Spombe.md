# Regulatory Genes Controlling Mitosis in the Fission Yeast Schizosaccharomyces pombe

## Citation (NLM)
Nurse P, Thuriaux P. Regulatory genes controlling mitosis in the fission yeast Schizosaccharomyces pombe. Genetics. 1980;96(3):627-637.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/96/3/627](https://genetics.org/content/96/3/627)

---

## Summary

This landmark paper by Paul Nurse (Department of Zoology, University of Edinburgh) and Pierre Thuriaux (Institute of Microbiology, University of Bern), submitted in October 1979 and published in November 1980, systematically characterizes the genetic basis of cell size control at mitosis in fission yeast.

Background: Temperature-sensitive cdc (cell division cycle) mutants that arrest at specific cell cycle stages had been isolated in many organisms. While these mutants identify genes necessary for completing cell cycle events, they cannot reveal rate-limiting functions — because any mutant blocked in a required function shows the same phenotype regardless of whether that function is normally limiting. Rate-limiting functions can, however, be identified using the opposite type of mutation: one that causes the function to complete faster than normal, thereby advancing the cell cycle. In S. pombe, these are the wee mutants, which divide at roughly half the normal cell size. Since wee cells traverse the cell cycle faster than wild type immediately after a temperature shift, the affected genes are indeed rate-limiting for mitotic entry.

Key experimental methods: Twenty-six new wee mutants were isolated and genetically analyzed alongside 26 previously identified ones, yielding a total of 52 wee mutants. Cell length at septation and protein content per cell were used as quantitative measures of cell size at mitosis. Heterozygous diploid analysis was used to determine dominance and dosage relationships. A fine-structure genetic map of the cdc2 locus was constructed by random-spore analysis of heteroallelic crosses.

Key results: Fifty-one of the 52 wee mutants mapped to the wee1 locus; only one (cdc2-1w) mapped within cdc2. Evidence that wee1 is a negative element (inhibitor): (1) the wee1-112 allele was suppressible by an opal nonsense suppressor, proving that loss of wee1 protein function causes the wee phenotype; (2) wee1 is unlinked to all 26 known cdc genes; (3) the high frequency of independent wee1 mutations at diverse intragenic sites is consistent with a loss-of-function mechanism. Evidence that cdc2 is a positive element (activator): previously identified cdc2 alleles were temperature-sensitive loss-of-function alleles that blocked mitosis (cdc phenotype); the new cdc2-1w allele, mapping within the same gene, produces a wee phenotype — premature mitosis. The two opposite phenotypes from the same gene are most simply explained if cdc2 encodes an activator: complete loss prevents mitosis, while certain altered-activity mutations produce hyperactive or abnormally behaving protein that advances mitotic entry. Gene dosage analysis: halving wee1+ dosage (heterozygous diploid) reduced division size to an intermediate level, confirming dosage-sensitive inhibitory activity. Halving cdc2+ dosage had almost no effect on division size, suggesting that the total quantity of Cdc2 does not determine mitotic timing — rather, a specific aspect of Cdc2 activity (later shown to be post-translational regulation by phosphorylation) is the critical determinant.

Conclusion: wee1+ encodes a dosage-sensitive negative element (inhibitor) of mitosis; cdc2+ encodes a dosage-insensitive positive element (activator) whose activity — not quantity — determines the timing of mitosis.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- The founding paper establishing the antagonistic relationship between the mitotic inhibitor Wee1 and the activator Cdc2, which forms the molecular basis for our understanding of CDK1 regulation during the cell cycle
- Demonstrates that the same gene (cdc2) can be identified by both loss-of-function alleles (arresting mitosis) and altered-activity alleles (advancing mitosis), illustrating the power of combining phenotypically opposite alleles to dissect a regulatory circuit
- Why this paper must be read in class: to learn, from a primary research article, how forward genetic screens can reveal the genetic logic of a fundamental cellular process — the regulation of when a cell divides

## Future Research Directions

- Determine the biochemical mechanism by which Wee1 inhibits Cdc2 — the subsequent discovery that Wee1 phosphorylates Tyr15 of Cdc2/CDK1 and that Cdc25 phosphatase removes this inhibitory phosphate explains the dosage-insensitivity of cdc2+ genetically
- Identify functional homologues of Wee1 and Cdc2/CDK1 in mammalian cells and characterize how dysregulation of these interactions contributes to cancer
