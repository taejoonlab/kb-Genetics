# Cancer-Independent Somatic Mutation of the Wild-Type NF1 Allele in Normal Tissues in Neurofibromatosis Type 1

## Citation (NLM)
Oliver TRW, Lawson ARJ, Lee-Six H, et al. Cancer-independent somatic mutation of the wild-type NF1 allele in normal tissues in neurofibromatosis type 1. Nat Genet. 2025;57(3):515–521. doi:10.1038/s41588-025-02097-2

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41588-025-02097-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41588-025-02097-2)

## Summary

In recessive tumor predisposition syndromes, one allele is inactivated in the germline; tumors arise when the remaining wild-type allele sustains a somatic second hit. A central, rarely tested assumption has been that second hits cause neoplastic transformation. This study directly demonstrates — for the first time at scale in normal tissues — that second hits in NF1 pervade histologically normal tissues in children with neurofibromatosis type 1 independently of any tumor lineage, and that these variants are under positive selection.

**Study design**: A postmortem study of three children who died from high-grade midline gliomas: one (PD51122) with neurofibromatosis type 1 carrying a pathogenic truncating germline NF1 mutation (c.3113+1G>A), and two (PD50297, PD51123) with sporadic tumors. The team performed whole-genome sequencing (WGS) on 838 laser capture microdissected (LCM) cell groups (median 28× coverage), supplemented by bulk tissue WGS (n = 71) and WES of microdissected tissues (n = 180). Tissues sampled spanned the central nervous system, peripheral nervous system, skin, gastrointestinal tract, and mesodermal organs. A validation cohort of nine adults with NF1 undergoing surgical resection was also studied by duplex sequencing.

**Key finding in normal tissues**: Multiple independent somatic NF1 driver variants were identified in histologically normal CNS tissues of the predisposed child — but not in 610 biopsies from the two unaffected children. Variants included truncating mutations (p.R304*, p.K233fs*48, p.I679fs*21) and a likely oncogenic missense mutation (p.Y489C) in anatomically distant regions (parietal cortex, cerebellum, brainstem, spinal cord). Clone sizes ranged from 2% to 56% by variant allele frequency in microdissections, and up to 19% in bulk tissue biopsies. Tumor contamination was rigorously excluded.

**LOH analysis**: Haplotype-resolved copy number calling revealed six instances of LOH at the NF1 locus (chromosome 17q) in normal tissues of the predisposed child, with at least two represented by distinct breakpoints indicating independent events. No second NF1 hit was found in non-neuroectodermal lineages from this expanded analysis.

**Evidence for positive selection**: Duplex sequencing of the NF1 gene in 60 normal tissue samples (21 from the predisposed child, 39 from unaffected children) yielded 29 nonsynonymous (21 truncating) and 2 synonymous NF1 mutations in the predisposed child, versus 9 nonsynonymous and 5 synonymous in unaffected children. dN/dS ratio analysis provided statistically significant evidence of positive selection for truncating NF1 variants in neuroectodermal normal tissues of the predisposed child. The spleen had the highest dN/dS, consistent with the predisposition of NF1 patients to juvenile myelomonocytic leukemia. In the adult validation cohort, duplex sequencing of peripheral nerves confirmed a marked excess of truncating NF1 mutations, indicating the selective pressure operates throughout life.

**Mechanistic implications**: NF1 encodes neurofibromin, a negative regulator of the RAS/MAPK signaling pathway. Loss-of-function mutations in NF1 in a neuroectodermal cell confer a growth or survival advantage in the context of monoallelic NF1 loss, explaining the tissue-restricted pattern of second hits. However, biallelic NF1 loss is insufficient on its own for most cells to undergo neoplastic transformation — tumor formation requires additional cooperating events or specific cellular contexts.

## Significance in Introduction Context

This paper's significance at the introduction of the field:
- [Extends Knudson's two-hit hypothesis by demonstrating that second hits are a widespread normal-tissue phenomenon in carriers of recessive tumor suppressor mutations — not rare stochastic events confined to neoplastic precursors]
- [Establishes the concept that positive selection operates in histologically normal tissue: NF1 null cells are clonally expanded in normal brain and peripheral nerve, challenging the classical view that two-hit inactivation invariably leads to cancer]
- [Essential reading for a genetics course because it combines classical cancer genetics (tumor suppressor two-hit model), population genetics (selection measured by dN/dS), and cutting-edge normal-tissue genomics into a clinically grounded narrative]

## Future Research Directions

Unresolved questions and future research ideas raised by this paper:

- [Can the quantitative burden of NF1 null clones in normal tissues (clone size, density, affected tissue types) serve as a measurable biomarker predicting individual cancer risk in NF1 carriers?]
- [Is cancer-independent second-hit accumulation a general principle of recessive cancer predisposition syndromes (e.g., BRCA1/2, RB1, TP53), or does it reflect a specific property of NF1 biology?]
- [The spleen's exceptionally high dN/dS for NF1 truncating variants suggests clonal hematopoiesis as an early event in NF1-associated juvenile myelomonocytic leukemia — can longitudinal monitoring of NF1 clonal dynamics in blood predict leukemia risk?]
