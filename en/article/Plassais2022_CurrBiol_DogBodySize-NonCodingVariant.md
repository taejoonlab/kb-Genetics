# Natural and Human-Driven Selection of a Single Non-Coding Body Size Variant in Ancient and Modern Canids

## Summary

This paper reports the first identification of a functional mutation at the IGF1 locus controlling body size across ancient and modern canids. Domestic dogs display a ~40-fold range in body mass — the largest variation among mammalian species — and while IGF1 had been established as the primary body size locus (~15% of variance), a functional causal variant had remained elusive for over 15 years.

**Dataset:** 1,431 total genome sequences analyzed: 1,156 modern breed dogs (230 breeds), 140 indigenous/village dogs, 1 dingo; 33 published ancient dog genomes (oldest ~10,930 years before present); 68 modern and 9 ancient gray wolves; 24 additional canids from 11 species (coyotes, jackals, African golden wolves, dholes, foxes). Total: 64.92 million biallelic variants identified.

**Variant discovery:** GWAS of the IGF1 locus on CFA15 (41.20–41.27 Mb) in 456 dogs from 179 breeds identified a novel candidate SNP, rs22397284 (chr15:41219654, T>C, p = 10^-29). This SNP is located in the last exon of a predicted 1,204 bp antisense lncRNA (IGF1-AS) that overlaps the IGF1 gene by 182 bp, forming an RNA duplex with IGF1 mRNA.

**Genotype-phenotype associations:**
- C allele ("small allele"): 75% of dogs homozygous CC have breed body mass average (BMA) <15 kg
- T allele ("large allele"): 75% of dogs homozygous TT have BMA >25 kg
- Validation in three schnauzer breeds: miniature schnauzer (C allele frequency >0.95) vs. giant schnauzer (fixed for T allele), representing a 6-fold size difference
- Significant correlation between IGF1-AS genotype, body mass, and serum IGF-1 protein levels (p < 0.001, r = 0.6 Spearman test, n = 51 dogs)

**Evolutionary history:**
- T (large) allele identified in a 53,000-year-old Pleistocene Siberian wolf (heterozygous) — demonstrating the allele predates dog domestication by tens of thousands of years
- Both alleles present in a 9,500-year-old Siberian sled dog (heterozygous); both alleles have co-existed for at least 9,500 years
- Latitudinal gradient in ancient dogs: northern sites (>55°N) had T allele frequency of 0.75; southern sites (<45°N) had C allele frequency of 0.79 — consistent with Bergmann's rule
- Comparative genomics: ferret, panda, and cat all carry the C allele, supporting C as the ancestral state; the Canidae ancestor was likely small-bodied

**Modern coyote validation:**
- West coast coyotes: C allele frequency 0.93, mean body mass 9.18 kg
- East coast coyotes: C allele frequency 0.47, mean body mass 16.03 kg
- East coast T allele increase explained by recent wolf introgression (hybridization), not local adaptation

**Evolutionary model:** Canidae ancestor was small and carried the ancestral C allele → T (large) allele arose >53,000 years ago in wolves → natural selection during the Pleistocene (lower temperatures at high latitudes) drove T allele to high frequency in northern wolves → ~15,000 years ago, domestication began with large, wolf-like dogs → human selection subsequently amplified the ancestral C allele to produce the small breeds dominant in modern dog populations.

## Significance in Introduction Context

The significance of this paper in the context of course introduction:
- **Single non-coding variant controlling a complex quantitative trait:** A SNP within a lncRNA antisense to IGF1 is a primary determinant of body size across the entire Canidae family, providing a compelling example of non-coding regulatory variation controlling complex phenotypes
- **Separating natural from artificial selection:** The same allele shows geographic distribution consistent with Bergmann's rule (natural selection) AND was specifically targeted by human breeders (artificial selection); both forces act on the same standing variation that existed for >53,000 years before domestication
- **Why read this in class:** Integrates ancient DNA analysis, population genetics statistics (D-statistics for introgression detection, generalized linear models for geographic effects), lncRNA biology, and evolutionary thinking across natural and artificial selection contexts

## Future Research Directions

Unresolved questions and future research ideas raised by this paper:

- Determining the precise molecular mechanism by which the IGF1-AS lncRNA duplex modulates IGF1 mRNA translation rate without affecting mRNA levels — the SINEUP class of lncRNAs is a candidate mechanism but direct evidence is needed
- Understanding why standard poodles carry both alleles at intermediate frequency, while miniature/toy poodles are fixed for C and giant schnauzers for T — what maintains size-allele polymorphism in intermediate-sized breeds?
- Obtaining more ancient genomes from the critical domestication window (15,000–10,000 years ago) to track allele frequency changes during the initial domestication process
- Testing whether IGF1-AS-like regulatory variants at the IGF1 locus contribute to body size variation in other domesticated mammals (cats, horses, cattle)
- Distinguishing true shared ancestry of the C allele haplotype between Middle Eastern wolves and small domestic breeds from convergent evolution
