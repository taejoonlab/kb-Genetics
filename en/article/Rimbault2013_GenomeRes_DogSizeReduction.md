# Derived Variants at Six Genes Explain Nearly Half of Size Reduction in Dog Breeds

## Citation (NLM)
Rimbault M, Beale HC, Schoenebeck JJ, Hoopes BC, Allen JJ, Kilroy-Glynn P, Wayne RK, Sutter NB, Ostrander EA. Derived variants at six genes explain nearly half of size reduction in dog breeds. Genome Res. 2013;23(12):1985-1995. doi:10.1101/gr.157339.113

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1101/gr.157339.113](https://doi.org/10.1101/gr.157339.113)

---

## Summary

This paper characterizes the genetic basis of body size variation in domestic dog breeds by showing that derived alleles at just six genetic loci explain approximately half of the variance in standard breed weight across 93 breeds. Domestic dogs exhibit the greatest body size diversity of any land mammal — mastiffs can be 50 times heavier than Chihuahuas — and this study defines the combinatorial genetic logic underlying this extraordinary range.

**Study design:** Four body size QTLs from the CanMap GWAS (915 dogs, 60,968 SNPs) were fine-mapped to identify candidate genes. The most associated variants at these loci, plus previously described variants at IGF1 and IGF1R, were genotyped in 500 dogs representing 93 AKC-recognized breeds spanning the full range of canine body sizes. American Kennel Club (AKC) standard breed weight (SBW) was used as the phenotypic measure.

**Fine-mapping results — six key loci:**
- **GHR (CFA4:67 Mb):** Two nonsynonymous SNPs in exon 5: GHR(1)(E191K) and GHR(2)(P177L). The syntenic human exon carries three disease-associated SNPs linked to Laron Syndrome (growth hormone insensitivity dwarfism)
- **HMGA2 (CFA10:8 Mb):** SNP in the 5' UTR; Hmga2 knockout mice display a "pygmy" phenotype; D/D mean SBW = 4.7 kg (±2)
- **STC2 (CFA4:39 Mb):** SNP 20 kb downstream of the protein-coding region; STC2 suppresses growth in mice independently of the GH/IGF1 pathway
- **SMAD2 (CFA7:43 Mb):** 9.9 kb deletion 24 kb downstream (in complete LD with an adjacent 5.7 kb deletion); TGF-β signaling transcription factor
- **IGF1 (CFA15):** Previously described intronic SNP and SINE insertion (complete LD); D/D mean SBW = 9.8 kg (±9) — higher than other markers
- **IGF1R (CFA3):** R204H missense variant; D/D mean SBW = 4.6 kg (±4)

**Ancestral allele determination:** 30 wild canids (26 gray wolves, 2 red wolves, 2 coyotes) were genotyped; all markers showed ancestral alleles predominating in wild canids, confirming that derived alleles represent size-reducing variants selected during breed development.

**Allele frequency patterns:**
- Step-like pattern: as body size decreases, derived allele frequency increases at progressively more loci
- Dogs <11.3 kg: 90% carry derived alleles at 3+ markers; 98% carry IGF1 derived allele
- Large breeds (≥40.8 kg): homozygous for ancestral alleles at nearly all markers
- Exception: Rottweilers and mastiff-related breeds carry IGF1 derived allele despite large size — may reflect LD tagging or epistasis with unknown loci

**Statistical model:**
- Linear model using breed-averaged allele frequencies at seven markers: 86% of SBW variance explained without population structure correction
- After correction for population structure (PCA-based): 46.0%–52.5% of variance explained
- For breeds with SBW <40.8 kg: 64.3% explained; for breeds ≥40.8 kg: only 8.4% explained — indicating distinct genetic mechanisms operate in giant breeds

**Comparison with human height genetics:** In humans, 180+ GWAS loci explain only ~10% of height variance. By contrast, 6 loci explain 46–52% of interbreed weight variance in dogs. This contrast reflects intense artificial selection creating a simplified genetic architecture in dogs, superimposed on a subtler size regulation system inherited from wolves.

## Significance in Introduction Context

The significance of this paper in the context of course introduction:
- **"Few genes of large effect" concept:** Demonstrates that a quantitative complex trait can be explained by a small number of loci when artificial selection is intense — a key principle distinguishing artificially selected vs. naturally evolved populations
- **Ancestral vs. derived allele framework:** Introduces the method of using wild relatives to determine allele direction, enabling inference of the direction of selection
- **Why read this in class:** Provides a detailed worked example of QTL fine-mapping methodology, genotype combination analysis for complex traits, the use of breed standard phenotypes in genetic studies, and direct comparison between dog and human genetics that illuminates study design differences

## Future Research Directions

Unresolved questions and future research ideas raised by this paper:

- Finding additional loci that explain the ~92% of body size variance in giant breeds (≥40.8 kg): X-chromosome intervals span megabases of LD encompassing dozens of genes, requiring new methodological approaches
- Functional validation of identified variants: particularly whether the SMAD2 downstream deletion affects TGF-β transcription factor binding sites and expression in growth plate chondrocytes
- Within-breed (intrabreed) body size variation genetics: current study targets between-breed differences only; individual dog weight measurements needed for finer-scale mapping
- Understanding why the IGF1 derived allele appears in some giant breeds (Rottweilers, mastiffs): LD tagging vs. epistasis with an undiscovered large-effect locus
- Translational application: GHR variants identified here overlap with human Laron Syndrome mutations, suggesting dog-to-human translation for understanding GH insensitivity and related growth disorders
