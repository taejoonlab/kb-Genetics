# Phenylthiocarbamide: A 75-Year Adventure in Genetics and Natural Selection

## Citation (NLM)
Wooding S. Phenylthiocarbamide: a 75-year adventure in genetics and natural selection. Genetics. 2006;172(4):2015-2023.

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.104.027490](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.104.027490)

---

## Summary

Since Arthur Fox accidentally discovered in 1930 that the ability to taste phenylthiocarbamide (PTC) varies among people, PTC tasting has served as a classic model trait in human genetics for 75 years. Wooding (2006) surveys this history and examines molecular evidence that variation in the bitter taste receptor gene TAS2R38 is the primary cause of variation in PTC tasting, while also evaluating population genetic evidence for natural selection maintaining this polymorphism.

PTC tasting was long understood as a simple dominant trait — tasters versus non-tasters. However, PTC taste sensitivity is actually continuously distributed, with some "non-tasters" responding to very high concentrations, suggesting that a single locus cannot fully explain this trait.

TAS2R38 encodes a bitter taste receptor and shows polymorphism at three key amino acid positions (49, 262, and 296). Combinations of variants at these positions yield two major haplotypes: PAV (Pro-Ala-Val), associated with PTC tasting ability, and AVI (Ala-Val-Ile), associated with non-tasting. Minor haplotypes also exist, and haplotype diversity distributions differ among human populations.

Wooding applies population genetic analyses to assess whether balancing selection maintains the TAS2R38 polymorphism. Balancing selection maintains multiple alleles at intermediate frequencies over long evolutionary timescales, which can arise through heterozygote advantage or frequency-dependent selection.

Using Tajima's D statistics and phylogenetic analysis, the study demonstrates that the taster and non-taster haplotypes trace to a very ancient common ancestor (approximately 3 million years ago), and that both the tasting and non-tasting polymorphism is shared between humans and chimpanzees. This finding validates the hypothesis that Fisher, Ford, and Huxley advanced in 1939 when they reported that the PTC tasting polymorphism is shared between humans and chimpanzees — long interpreted as evidence for balancing selection.

Cross-population comparisons also show that both major TAS2R38 haplotypes coexist at appreciable frequencies in most human populations studied, a pattern that is difficult to explain by genetic drift or neutral evolution alone. However, what selective pressure specifically maintains both the tasting and non-tasting phenotypes remains debated: proposed mechanisms include detection of toxic compounds (e.g., glucosinolates in cruciferous vegetables), links to thyroid function, or dietary preference effects.

The author also notes that genetic factors beyond TAS2R38 may contribute to PTC taste variation, and discusses potential contributions from other bitter receptor genes and gene-environment interactions.

This paper illustrates how a trait that appears simple — taste or no taste — has a complex evolutionary history detectable through population genetics. It enables exploration of natural selection, neutral evolution, population history, and gene-environment interaction through a single well-characterized locus.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Uses the classic human PTC taste trait as a window into core population genetics topics — balancing selection, phylogenetic analysis, and cross-population comparison — all within a single locus
- Demonstrates that a trait classified as simple dominant/recessive by classical genetics reveals considerable molecular complexity at the haplotype level, illustrating the integration of classical and molecular genetics
- Why read this paper in class: it shows how to detect signatures of natural selection in real human data using Tajima's D and phylogenetic approaches, providing hands-on context for interpreting these statistics

## Key References

1. **Fox AL (1932)** — Classic report: first systematic documentation of PTC taste polymorphism in human populations
2. **Fisher RA, Ford EB, Huxley J (1939)** — Discovery that PTC tasting polymorphism is shared between humans and chimpanzees; founding evidence for balancing selection hypothesis
3. **Kim UK et al. (2003)** — [https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1080190](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1080190) — First identification of TAS2R38 as the primary determinant of PTC taste variation

## Future Research Directions

- Conduct genome-wide association studies (GWAS) to quantify contributions of additional bitter taste receptor gene variants to PTC sensitivity beyond TAS2R38
- Epidemiological studies to clarify the actual selective pressure maintaining the polymorphism: links between TAS2R38 genotype, cruciferous vegetable consumption (glucosinolates, goitrogens), thyroid disease risk, or bitter toxin avoidance
- Survey TAS2R38 polymorphism distribution across additional primates beyond humans and chimpanzees to refine the evolutionary timeline of balancing selection at this locus
