# Chromosomes & Sex Determination

**Course:** BME333 / BIO333 Genetics (UNIST, 2026 Fall) · Lecture 05 · ~60 min
**Syllabus:** [← Course schedule](../../lectures/2026.BME333-BIO333-Syllabus.md) — Week 03 Mon, 09-14

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:
- Explain the chromosome theory of heredity and cite the observational and genetic evidence that established it (Sutton, Boveri, Bridges).
- Relate meiotic chromosome behavior (segregation, independent assortment) to Mendel's laws.
- Describe how nondisjunction produces aneuploidy and how Bridges used it to prove genes reside on chromosomes.
- Compare the major sex-determination systems (XX/XY, ZZ/ZW, haplodiploidy, environmental) and explain why the mechanism is evolutionarily labile.
- Outline how sex chromosomes originate, degenerate, and drive dosage-compensation and gene-regulation adaptations.

## Lecture Outline (~60 min)
1. **From Mendel to chromosomes** (~8 min) — Recap Mendel's factors; introduce mitosis vs. meiosis and how homolog segregation and independent assortment physically embody the two Mendelian laws.
2. **The chromosome theory of heredity** (~12 min) — Sutton (1902/1903) and Boveri independently linked chromosome behavior to inheritance; discuss the parallelism argument and why it was initially resisted.
3. **Genetic proof: nondisjunction** (~12 min) — Bridges' 1916 *Drosophila* work on X-chromosome nondisjunction gave the "exception that proves the rule," tying a specific gene to a specific chromosome. Morgan's white-eye linkage as the sex-linked precedent.
4. **Human chromosome number and karyotype** (~6 min) — Brief history of getting the human count right (46, not 48) and why technique matters for cytogenetics.
5. **Sex-determination systems** (~12 min) — XX/XY (mammals, SRY), ZZ/ZW (birds), haplodiploidy (Hymenoptera, e.g., honeybee *csd* locus), and environmental sex determination; emphasize the diversity and turnover of mechanisms.
6. **Sex-chromosome evolution & dosage** (~10 min) — Recombination suppression, Y degeneration, dosage compensation, and gene-regulation consequences; heterogametic sex and Haldane's rule as a bridge to speciation.
