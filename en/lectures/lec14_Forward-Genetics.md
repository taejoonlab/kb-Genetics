# Forward Genetics

**Course:** BME333 / BIO333 Genetics (UNIST, 2026 Fall) · Lecture 14 · ~60 min
**Syllabus:** [← Course schedule](../../lectures/2026.BME333-BIO333-Syllabus.md) — Week 09, 2026-10-28 (Wed)

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:
- Define forward genetics ("phenotype → gene") and contrast it with reverse genetics.
- Design a mutagenesis screen: mutagen choice, generations, and phenotype scoring.
- Explain complementation and how mutants are grouped into genes.
- Describe how a mutation is mapped and identified, from classical linkage to whole-genome sequencing.
- Recognize the role of model organisms and modifier/suppressor screens in dissecting biological pathways.

## Lecture Outline (~60 min)
1. **What is forward genetics?** (~7 min) — The phenotype-first logic; start with a variant of interest and work back to the causal gene. Historical importance in defining gene function.
2. **Mutagenesis** (~10 min) — Chemical (EMS/ENU), physical (X-ray), and insertional (transposon) mutagens; mutation spectra; dosage vs. survival; nature of induced lesions (point mutations vs. rearrangements).
3. **Designing a screen** (~10 min) — Choosing a scorable phenotype; F1/F2 schemes; saturation; forward screens for essential vs. viable genes; conditional (temperature-sensitive) alleles for essential genes.
4. **Complementation & allelic series** (~8 min) — Complementation tests to count genes; dominance/recessivity of alleles; how the honeybee/yeast/worm/fly systems illustrate the logic.
5. **From mutant to gene: mapping** (~12 min) — Recombination mapping and positional cloning; the shift to whole-genome sequencing of mutants for rapid causal-variant identification.
6. **Modifier and suppressor screens** (~8 min) — Sensitized backgrounds to find pathway components and disease modifiers; connect to model-organism disease work.
7. **Strengths, limits & wrap-up** (~5 min) — Unbiased discovery vs. redundancy/lethality pitfalls; bridge to reverse genetics next session.
