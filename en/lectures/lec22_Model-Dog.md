# Genetic Model — Dog

**Course:** BME333 / BIO333 Genetics (UNIST, 2026 Fall) · Lecture 22 · ~60 min
**Syllabus:** [← Course schedule](../../lectures/2026.BME333-BIO333-Syllabus.md) — Week 13 Wed, 2026-11-25

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:
- Explain why the domestic dog (*Canis familiaris*) is a powerful natural model for the genetics of variation and selection: strong artificial selection, breed structure, and extreme morphological diversity within one species.
- Describe how breed formation creates long linkage blocks and reduced within-breed diversity that make GWAS and mapping of trait loci unusually tractable.
- Connect dog domestication and breeding to the textbook themes of variation and selection in populations.
- Summarize the genetic architecture of a classic dog trait — body size — and the role of a small number of large-effect loci (e.g., *IGF1* and related variants).
- Discuss how dog genetics informs both dog biology and comparable human traits and diseases.

## Lecture Outline (~60 min)
1. **The dog as a natural experiment in selection** (~10 min) — Domestication from wolves and intensive artificial selection into hundreds of breeds; enormous variation in size, shape, coat, and behavior within a single species. Tie back to Darwin's use of domestication as evidence for selection (Lecture 01).
2. **Breed structure & why dog genetics is tractable** (~12 min) — Population bottlenecks and closed breeding produce reduced within-breed diversity, extended linkage disequilibrium, and shared haplotypes — so few markers/samples are needed to map traits. Contrast with human GWAS.
3. **Whole-genome variation & across-breed GWAS** (~13 min) — Using genome sequences from many breeds and village/free-ranging dogs to catalogue variation and map morphological and disease traits; the power of combining across-breed association with within-breed mapping.
4. **Case study: the genetics of body size** (~13 min) — Body size as a model quantitative trait largely controlled by a handful of large-effect loci; *IGF1* and a nearby non-coding regulatory variant associated with small size, and how such variants segregate across breeds. Relate to QTL/complex-trait concepts (Lecture 12).
5. **From dog traits to human biology; wrap-up** (~12 min) — Behavior and morphology genetics (e.g., links between candidate genes and behavioral syndromes); dogs as comparative models for human disease. Preview: human cancer genetics (Lecture 23).
