# Human Genetics — Cancer

**Course:** BME333 / BIO333 Genetics (UNIST, 2026 Fall) · Lecture 23 · ~60 min
**Syllabus:** [← Course schedule](../../lectures/2026.BME333-BIO333-Syllabus.md) — Week 14, 2026-11-30 (Mon)

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:
- Explain cancer as a genetic disease of somatic cells that arises through the stepwise accumulation of mutations (the multistep/clonal-evolution model).
- Distinguish oncogenes (gain-of-function, dominant) from tumor-suppressor genes (loss-of-function, recessive at the cell level), and apply Knudson's two-hit hypothesis to inherited vs. sporadic cancer.
- Describe how driver mutations are separated from passenger mutations and why somatic mutations accumulate even in normal tissue.
- Interpret a tumor as an evolving population of cells and explain how phylogenetic/population-genetic principles apply to intratumor heterogeneity.
- Connect inherited cancer-predisposition syndromes (e.g., hereditary breast cancer, retinoblastoma) to the same genes disrupted in sporadic tumors.

## Lecture Outline (~60 min)
1. **Cancer as a genetic disease of somatic cells** (~8 min) — Hallmarks of cancer; clonal origin; cancer results from mutations in the cell's own DNA, most of them somatic rather than inherited. Frame cancer as loss of normal growth control.
2. **Oncogenes and proto-oncogenes** (~10 min) — Gain-of-function mutations (point mutations, amplification, translocation) that act dominantly; examples such as RAS and MYC; how a single altered allele drives proliferation.
3. **Tumor-suppressor genes and the two-hit hypothesis** (~10 min) — Loss-of-function, recessive at the cellular level; Knudson's two-hit model using retinoblastoma (RB1) to contrast inherited (one hit inherited) vs. sporadic (two somatic hits) disease; loss of heterozygosity.
4. **Multistep tumorigenesis and genome instability** (~8 min) — Accumulation of driver mutations over time; caretaker genes (DNA repair, mismatch repair, checkpoint control) and how their loss accelerates mutation; distinguishing driver from passenger mutations.
5. **Cancer as an evolving cell population** (~10 min) — Tumors as Darwinian populations: mutation + selection + clonal expansion produce intratumor heterogeneity; phylogenetic reconstruction of tumor lineages and why this matters for therapy resistance.
6. **Inherited cancer predisposition** (~8 min) — Familial syndromes (hereditary breast/ovarian cancer via BRCA1/2, Li-Fraumeni via TP53, Lynch syndrome); comparative and cross-species genetics of cancer risk; genetic counseling implications.
7. **Somatic mutation beyond cancer & wrap-up** (~6 min) — Somatic mutations accumulate in normal, non-cancerous tissue with age; implications for aging and for interpreting "driver" mutations. Synthesis and clinical outlook.
