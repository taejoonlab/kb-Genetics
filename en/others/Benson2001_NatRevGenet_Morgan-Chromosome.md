# T. H. Morgan's Resistance to the Chromosome Theory

## Citation (NLM)
Benson KR. T. H. Morgan's resistance to the chromosome theory. Nat Rev Genet. 2001;2(6):469-474.

**DOI:** Not available (Nature Reviews Genetics, June 2001)

---

## Summary

This paper is a historical Perspectives article by science historian Keith R. Benson that reexamines Thomas Hunt Morgan's long resistance to the chromosomal theory of heredity before his famous conversion in 1910. Standard accounts credit Morgan with establishing the chromosome theory through his Drosophila work, but Benson argues that this narrative obscures decades of principled opposition. The dominant nineteenth-century paradigm of "blending inheritance" — the idea that parental traits blend uniformly in offspring — harmonized well with Darwin's observations of continuous variation and fostered deep skepticism toward particulate inheritance. Early proposals by Weismann (germplasm theory) and de Vries (pangenes) were largely dismissed by American experimentalists for lacking empirical support.

Benson situates Morgan's resistance within the tradition of American experimental biology shaped at Johns Hopkins under William Keith Brooks and at European laboratories, particularly the Stazione Zoologica in Naples, where a new philosophy of "Entwicklungsmechanik" (developmental mechanics) insisted that biology must be built on observable, experimentally testable phenomena — not speculative particles. Morgan's close colleague Edmund Beecher Wilson was already moving toward chromosomal explanations by 1902–1905, and Walter S. Sutton, Wilson's student, proposed the chromosome-character parallel in his 1903 Biological Bulletin paper. Yet Morgan continued until at least 1909 to argue that inheritance and variation were products of cytoplasmic blending, writing to Hans Driesch that he was "not in sympathy with all this modern way of referring everything to the chromosomes" and that he "lived in an atmosphere saturated with chromosomic acid and blue dyes."

The turning point came in 1910 when Morgan himself identified the white-eye mutation in Drosophila, whose inheritance pattern could only be explained if the responsible gene resided on the accessory (sex) chromosome. This provided direct physical evidence linking phenotype to the chromosome as the carrier of heritable units. By 1912 Morgan had fully converted, and in 1915 he, Sturtevant, Muller, and Bridges published The Mechanism of Mendelian Heredity, which systematically mapped genes onto chromosomes. Benson concludes that appreciating this history reveals how powerfully theoretical frameworks constrain scientific practice, and that Morgan's conversion required accumulating decisive experimental evidence — not a simple moment of insight — before blending inheritance could be displaced.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the essential historical backdrop to understanding how the material basis of heredity — chromosomes — came to be established, bridging nineteenth-century conceptual confusion and twentieth-century genetics.
- Demonstrates a critical lesson in science: even abundant circumstantial cytological evidence (chromatin, mitosis/meiosis behavior, sex chromosomes) was insufficient to overturn an entrenched theoretical framework; only direct experimental linkage of phenotype to a specific chromosome achieved this.
- The paper explicitly contextualizes Mendel's rediscovery (1900) and Morgan's Drosophila work (1910), explaining the conceptual gap between them — essential for students to understand why "Mendelian genetics" and "chromosome theory" were not immediately unified.
- For BME333/BIO333, this historical narrative is ideal for the introductory lecture asking "What is the physical basis of heredity?" and for motivating why chromosomes, not cytoplasm, carry genetic information.
- Illustrates how blending inheritance's apparent compatibility with continuous variation was its greatest strength — and how sex-linked inheritance provided the decisive discontinuous result that broke the paradigm.

## Future Research Directions

- Morgan's resistance and conversion remain a paradigmatic case study in the history and philosophy of science, informing how scientific communities process anomalies under Kuhnian framework analysis.
- The tension between blending (continuous) and particulate (discontinuous) inheritance resurfaces in modern quantitative genetics and GWAS, where polygenic architectures of continuous traits are explained by discrete genetic variants.
- Wilson and Morgan's early skepticism about nuclear-exclusive control anticipates the modern renaissance of cytoplasmic/epigenetic inheritance, providing historical perspective on why non-Mendelian inheritance took so long to be systematically studied.
- The Entwicklungsmechanik tradition that shaped Morgan's empiricist philosophy is a historical precursor to evo-devo (evolutionary developmental biology), whose integration with genetics Morgan himself initially resisted.
- The role of institutions like the Stazione Zoologica in shaping international scientific trajectories remains a rich subject for the sociology of science and the study of how research environments constrain or enable paradigm shifts.
