# The advantages and disadvantages of being polyploid

## Citation (NLM)
Comai L. The advantages and disadvantages of being polyploid. Nat Rev Genet. 2005;6(11):836-846. doi:10.1038/nrg1711

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg1711](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg1711)

---

## Summary

Polyploidy is the state of genetically possessing more than two sets of chromosomes, and is extremely common in plants and some animals (fish, amphibians). Analysis of eukaryotic genomes shows that many species — including humans — have polyploid ancestors, evidence that polyploidy confers long-term evolutionary flexibility. In this review, Comai presents a balanced examination of the advantages and disadvantages of polyploidy and their molecular bases.

Polyploidy arises when abnormalities in mitosis or meiosis produce gametes with more chromosome sets than normal. Polyploids are broadly classified as autopolyploids (genome doubling within a single species) and allopolyploids (a combination of interspecific hybridisation and genome doubling). In flowering plants, polyploids form at a frequency of approximately 1 in 100,000, and stable polyploids are common in species with high polyploidy tolerance. By contrast, higher vertebrates have low polyploidy tolerance, and approximately 10% of spontaneous abortions in humans are caused by polyploidy.

Three major advantages of polyploidy are presented. First, heterosis (hybrid vigour). In allopolyploids, diverged parental genomes are permanently fixed, maintaining heterozygosity across generations. In diploid F1 hybrids, by contrast, half of heterozygous loci become homozygous each generation, progressively eroding heterosis. Second, gene redundancy. Polyploidy provides a buffering effect that masks recessive deleterious mutations with dominant wild-type alleles, especially reducing the impact of lethal or deleterious mutations during the haploid gamete stage. Duplicated genes also provide opportunities for mutational functional change, enabling subfunctionalization and neofunctionalization and thereby facilitating long-term evolutionary innovation. Third, loss of self-incompatibility and vegetative reproduction. Polyploidy disrupts some self-incompatibility systems, enabling self-fertilisation, and can be associated with apomixis (asexual reproduction), allowing propagation without a sexual partner.

Three main disadvantages of polyploidy are also identified. First, cellular geometry changes and their regulatory implications. Genome doubling increases cell volume, but nuclear envelope surface area increases only in proportion to the 3/2 power of volume. This geometric mismatch can cause stoichiometric imbalances among nuclear envelope proteins and heterochromatin components. Second, difficulties in mitosis and meiosis. Autotetraploid yeast show increased chromosome missegregation, and in animal cells tetraploidy activates the G1-tetraploidy checkpoint (p53-mediated). Bypassing this checkpoint leads to multipolar spindle formation and production of aneuploid cells. Studies finding that 30–40% of offspring from autotetraploid plants are aneuploid strongly suggest a causal relationship between polyploidy and aneuploidy. Third, regulatory changes in gene expression and epigenetic instability. Polyploidy can alter gene expression in unexpected ways; in allopolyploid Arabidopsis, approximately 5% of the transcriptome showed non-additive expression deviating from the expected mid-parent value, with suppression predominating — confirming that most expression changes from hybridisation and polyploidisation occur in the direction of silencing.

The evolutionary potential of polyploids is especially pronounced in allopolyploids. Spartina anglica, an invasive plant, is a recently formed allopolyploid whose rapid spread exemplifies its adaptive potential. When polyploids successfully adapt, they undergo diploidisation: some duplicated genes are lost, and others are subfunctionalised or neofunctionalised. Through this process the genome eventually functions like a stable "diploid," but genomic analysis can still recover its polyploid ancestry.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- **Evolutionary ubiquity of polyploidy:** The fact that the genomes of many eukaryotes — including plants, fish, and amphibians — have polyploid ancestors demonstrates that polyploidy is not an evolutionary dead end but a source of new genetic variation and adaptation.
- **Molecular understanding of heterosis:** The mechanism of permanent heterozygosity maintenance in allopolyploids explains one of the genetic bases of hybrid vigour, with direct implications for agricultural applications (particularly hybrid seed production).
- **Linking gene duplication to evolutionary innovation:** Whole-genome duplication (WGD) driven by polyploidy enables diversification of gene function through subfunctionalization and neofunctionalization. This principle is recognised as a central mechanism explaining increases in genome complexity across diverse lineages, including vertebrate evolution.
- **Polyploidy, aneuploidy, and cancer biology:** The observation that polyploidy increases the risk of aneuploidy has implications for understanding the evolutionary origins of chromosomal instability and aneuploidy commonly observed in cancer.
- **Evolutionary significance of epigenetic remodelling:** The discovery that widespread epigenetic remodelling occurs in allopolyploids demonstrates that evolutionary innovation can be achieved not only through DNA sequence changes but also through epigenetic alterations.

## Future Research Directions

- Real-time tracking of diploidisation following recent polyploidisation using high-quality long-read sequencing — and elucidation of the rules governing which genes are preserved, lost, subfunctionalised, or neofunctionalised — is needed.
- Analysis of the rate, pattern, and genetic determinants of epigenetic remodelling that occurs in early polyploidy at single-cell resolution, and understanding of how these changes are selectively filtered or maintained over the long term by natural selection, requires further research.
- The cell-biological mechanisms by which aneuploidy arises after polyploidisation (centrosome amplification, spindle abnormalities, meiotic errors) must be precisely analysed in model organisms.
- The molecular mechanisms underlying heterosis in the allopolyploid forms of major crops — wheat, cotton, rapeseed — should be elucidated to contribute to the development of new varieties with enhanced hybrid vigour.
- The molecular basis of the contrasting polyploidy tolerance between higher vertebrates and fish/amphibians must be addressed through comparative genomics.
