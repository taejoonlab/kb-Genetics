# The chromosome number in humans: a brief history

## Citation (NLM)
Gartler SM. The chromosome number in humans: a brief history. Nat Rev Genet. 2006;7(8):655-660. doi:10.1038/nrg1917

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg1917](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg1917)

---

## Summary

By 1956, genetics as a discipline had matured considerably through work in model organisms such as Drosophila and Neurospora, and the structure of DNA had already been published. Human genetics, however, lagged conspicuously behind — particularly in cytogenetics. The correct human chromosome number was not known, no human gene had been mapped to a specific autosome, and no chromosomal abnormality had ever been identified. This article by Stanley Gartler traces the history of how a fundamental biological fact — the diploid number of human chromosomes — remained incorrect for more than three decades, and how a single paper published in 1956 by Joe Hin Tjio and Albert Levan finally corrected it.

The prevailing count of 48 human chromosomes was established largely through the work of Theophilus S. Painter in the early 1920s. Painter was a skilled insect cytologist who examined human spermatogonial cells using paraffin-section preparations. Despite acknowledging uncertainty in his counts — his clearest preparations showed only 46 — he concluded the correct number was 48, a figure that went unchallenged for over thirty years. Gartler attributes this persistence partly to the poor quality of cytological preparations available at the time, which made exact counts essentially impossible. Limited access to human specimens (cytologists reportedly waited at gallows to obtain fresh testicular material) compounded the problem, as did the paraffin-sectioning method that caused chromosomes to overlap and split across sections.

Technical advances in the 1940s and 1950s progressively enabled better chromosome preparations. The squash technique, introduced by John Belling in 1921 for plant material, spread chromosomes more effectively than paraffin sections. More importantly, Eleanor Slifer's hypotonic shock method — which swells cells and disperses chromosomes before fixation — was independently rediscovered by multiple mammalian cytology groups in 1952. The use of colchicine, which arrests cells in metaphase and enhances chromosome condensation, was another critical tool. Levan himself had pioneered the use of colchicine in plant cytogenetics. The combination of hypotonic shock and colchicine treatment allowed Tjio and Levan to obtain what Gartler describes as "picture-perfect" human metaphase spreads in December 1955, from fetal cell cultures at the virus laboratory in Lund, Sweden.

Tjio and Levan published their finding of 46 chromosomes in the first issue of Hereditas in 1956, and the work was presented as a demonstration at the First International Congress of Human Genetics in Copenhagen that same year. The discovery was initially met with skepticism — at least one senior scientist reportedly asked "very interesting, but what difference will it make?" The answer came rapidly: by 1959, numerical chromosomal abnormalities had been identified in Down syndrome (trisomy 21), Turner syndrome (45,X), and Klinefelter syndrome (47,XXY). The field of medical cytogenetics was born. Subsequent advances included the discovery by Nowell that phytohaemagglutinin stimulates blood cell division (enabling easy karyotyping from peripheral blood), the identification of the Philadelphia chromosome in chronic myelogenous leukemia, and the introduction of chromosome banding by Caspersson's group in the late 1960s, which allowed precise identification of individual chromosomes and chromosomal rearrangements.

Gartler concludes that the prolonged error in human chromosome counting was due to a combination of genuine technical inadequacy and cognitive bias — "preconception." The evidence was present earlier: the Melander group had seen cells with only 46 chromosomes in 1955 and shown their results to Levan before his Sloan Kettering stint, but Levan himself did not question Painter's count. Only Tjio, whose unusual personal history may have made him more willing to question established authority, pushed forward with preparations of sufficient quality to leave no room for doubt. The Tjio-Levan paper not only corrected a factual error but catalyzed the development of human cytogenetics, gene mapping, and clinical genetics.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Demonstrates how a foundational biological fact can remain incorrect for decades when poor technical methods combine with cognitive anchoring to an established figure
- Illustrates the critical role of methodological innovation (hypotonic shock, colchicine, cell culture) in enabling discoveries that conceptual advances alone cannot achieve
- Marks the origin of human medical cytogenetics: the 1956 correction of chromosome number directly enabled identification of chromosomal syndromes (Down, Turner, Klinefelter) within three years
- Highlights the importance of human material availability and specimen quality in human genetics research, a constraint that did not apply to model organism genetics
- Raises the epistemology of scientific error: the persistence of Painter's incorrect count despite ambiguous original data illustrates how authority and preconception can suppress legitimate challenge in the scientific community

## Key References

1. **Tjio JH & Levan A (1956)** — https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1601-5223.1956.tb03010.x — The classic paper reporting 46 as the correct human chromosome number, using fetal cell cultures with hypotonic shock and colchicine treatment
2. **Painter TS (1923)** — [no DOI; J Exp Zool 37:291–336] — The original paper that established (incorrectly) 48 as the human chromosome number and dominated the field for three decades
3. **Lejeune J, Gautier M & Turpin R (1959)** — [no DOI; Compt Rend 248:1721–1722] — First identification of trisomy 21 as the chromosomal basis of Down syndrome, enabled by the Tjio-Levan framework
4. **Nowell PC & Hungerford DA (1960)** — https://doi.org/10.1126/science.132.3438.1497 — Discovery of the Philadelphia chromosome (t(9;22)) in chronic myelogenous leukemia, the first specific chromosomal abnormality linked to cancer
5. **Caspersson T, Zech L & Johansson J (1970)** — https://doi.org/10.1016/0014-4827(70)90523-9 — Introduction of chromosome banding, enabling unambiguous identification of individual chromosomes and structural rearrangements
6. **Kottler MJ (1974)** — [no DOI; Bull Hist Med 48:465–502] — Historical analysis of the counting error from 48 to 46, emphasizing preconception as a major impediment
7. **Ford CE & Hamerton JL (1956)** — https://doi.org/10.1038/1781020a0 — Independent rapid confirmation of the 46 count using testicular samples with hypotonic shock, published the same year as Tjio and Levan

## Future Research Directions

- Understanding the molecular mechanisms of chromosomal instability in cancer, building on the Philadelphia chromosome discovery and subsequent cytogenetic oncology
- Expanding prenatal chromosomal diagnosis beyond karyotyping to whole-genome sequencing, including copy-number variants below the resolution of cytogenetic banding
- Investigating the population genetics of chromosomal polymorphisms (heterochromatin variants, fragile sites) that fall below the threshold of pathogenicity but may influence phenotype
- Applying chromosome-level structural data to understand how three-dimensional genome organization (chromosome territories, topologically associating domains) relates to gene expression and disease
- Examining the history of scientific bias and preconception in other foundational measurements in biology, to develop better institutional mechanisms for challenging established figures
