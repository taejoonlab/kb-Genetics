# Chromosome territories

## Citation (NLM)
Meaburn KJ, Misteli T. Chromosome territories. Nature. 2007;445(7126):379-381. doi:10.1038/445379a

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/445379a](https://doi.org/10.1038/445379a)

---

## Summary

This Q&A article from Nature provides an accessible yet authoritative overview of chromosome territories — the fundamental principle that each chromosome occupies a spatially restricted, roughly spherical volume within the interphase nucleus. The concept dates to Carl Rabl and Theodor Boveri at the turn of the twentieth century, but its experimental demonstration came in the early 1980s when Thomas and Christoph Cremer used a microlaser to induce localized DNA damage and showed that only a subset of chromosomes was affected, consistent with territorial rather than intermixed organization.

Each chromosome territory is approximately 2 micrometers in diameter and is permeated by an interconnected network of nucleoplasmic channels that allow regulatory factors to access sequences deep within the territory. Internally, the territory is non-random: chromosome arms are largely kept apart, and gene-rich regions are spatially separated from gene-poor regions. The authors describe three non-exclusive models for how the chromatin fiber folds to create the territory: large loops anchored at their bases (seen in plants), interconnected megabase-sized domains (predominant in mammals), and loops emanating from a central chromosome core.

Chromosome territories are arranged non-randomly within the nucleus. Each chromosome has a preferred radial position — some chromosomes favor the nuclear interior while others prefer the periphery. Gene-rich chromosomes tend to occupy internal positions, whereas gene-poor chromosomes are often peripheral, though the relationship depends on cell type and gene density versus chromosome size. These positioning patterns are probabilistic rather than deterministic; the two homologs of the same chromosome often occupy different positions in the same nucleus. Importantly, the radial positioning patterns are tissue-specific, change during differentiation (e.g., mouse chromosome 6 moves from internal to peripheral during T cell differentiation), and are evolutionarily conserved (human and Old World monkey lymphocytes share the peripheral/internal positions of chromosomes 18 and 19, respectively).

The authors discuss the functional consequences of nuclear position. Gene activation is correlated with movement of loci toward the nuclear interior in some cases, but the rule is not universal — active genes are found both at the surface and deep within chromosome territories. More significant is the relative positioning of loci to each other: bringing regulatory regions from different chromosomes into proximity can control gene activity, as seen most dramatically in odorant receptor gene selection in olfactory neurons, where a regulatory element on one chromosome associates with a single receptor gene on a different chromosome. Nuclear position also influences genome stability: chromosomes that are preferentially close to each other more readily undergo translocations after double-strand breaks.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Establishes chromosome territories as the highest level of nuclear genome organization, providing the spatial context within which all finer-scale chromatin structures (TADs, loops) operate
- Explains how the probabilistic, non-random radial positioning of chromosomes generates a structured nuclear environment that contributes to compartmentalization of active and inactive chromatin
- Demonstrates evolutionary conservation of chromosome positioning, suggesting functional constraints on nuclear architecture beyond mere compaction
- Introduces the concept that the relative positioning of genomic loci — not just their linear sequence — is functionally relevant for gene regulation, imprinting, and genome stability
- Provides clinical implications: aberrant chromosome positioning in diseased cells could serve as a diagnostic marker, detectable in intact tissue without cell culturing

## Future Research Directions

- Identifying the molecular mechanisms (nuclear scaffold associations, self-organization via gene activity) that establish and maintain specific chromosome radial positions during and after cell division
- Determining whether chromosome repositioning during differentiation is a cause or consequence of gene expression changes, using inducible tethering systems
- Using super-resolution imaging to resolve the internal structure of chromosome territories and understand how chromatin fibers from neighboring chromosomes intermingle at territory boundaries
- Exploiting chromosome positioning as a diagnostic biomarker for detecting aberrant gene expression in diseased tissue, including solid tumors that are refractory to standard cytogenetic analysis
- Exploring how the 3D genome reorganization that accompanies chromosome territory repositioning during development contributes to cell fate specification
