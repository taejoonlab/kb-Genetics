# The Self-Organizing Genome: Principles of Genome Architecture and Function

## Citation (NLM)
Misteli T. The self-organizing genome: principles of genome architecture and function. Cell. 2020;183(1):28-45. doi:10.1016/j.cell.2020.09.014

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2020.09.014](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2020.09.014)

## Summary

This Cell review by Tom Misteli synthesizes converging evidence from genetics, biochemistry, biophysics, and cell biology to argue that the major structural features of the genome — chromatin loops, topologically associating domains (TADs), A/B compartments, chromosome territories, and the location of genes and nuclear bodies — are emergent properties of a self-organizing system. Rather than being hardwired by specific deterministic mechanisms (genetic zip codes, adaptor proteins, site-specific tethers), these features arise from the integrated action of drivers and constraints that together produce a system characterized by both overall stability and pervasive heterogeneity.

The review first describes the architectural hierarchy of genomes: the chromatin fiber (5–24 nm, irregularly folded), chromatin loops (kb to Mb, mediating enhancer-promoter proximity and gene cluster regulation), TADs (hundreds of kb, formed by cohesin-driven loop extrusion and defined by CTCF at boundaries), chromatin compartments (A/B, corresponding to euchromatin and heterochromatin), and chromosome territories (2–3 μm diameter, amorphously spherical with internal channels). Misteli then identifies four general principles that drive genome organization.

First, polymer-polymer interactions: the genome behaves as a self-avoiding polymer, and computational models that assume only local diffusive motion and homotypic interactions among chromatin regions — without requiring any specific molecular machinery — spontaneously generate loops, TADs, and compartments as thermodynamically stable emergent states. Including CTCF and cohesin binding sites in these models dramatically improves their accuracy.

Second, genome dynamics: chromatin loci undergo locally confined diffusion (~1 μm radius), and chromatin-binding proteins have very short dwell times (transcription factors spend >95% of time diffusing or in non-specific interactions). This transient nature is functionally essential — it generates regulatory plasticity by enabling rapid changes in complex composition without requiring dedicated reversal machinery. Dynamic steady states, where overall structure persists despite rapid component exchange, characterize both nuclear bodies and chromatin domains.

Third, phase separation: proteins with intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs) undergo liquid-liquid phase separation, contributing to the formation of membraneless compartments including heterochromatin (HP1α phase separation), transcriptional hubs (RNA Pol II and Mediator condensates), and superenhancer-associated protein aggregates. Phase separation stabilizes chromatin-chromatin interactions and domain organization.

Fourth, architectural constraints: the nuclear lamina (via lamina-associated domains, LADs) and intranuclear bodies (nucleolus, splicing speckles, PML bodies) physically restrict chromatin location and mobility. LADs are characterized by low gene density, enriched H3K9me2/3 and H3K27me3, and late replication timing. Their association with lamins constrains the 3D positioning of heterochromatic regions and has functional consequences — artificial tethering of internal regions to the periphery leads to repression.

The review argues that genome organization exhibits all hallmarks of self-organization: dynamic steady-state stability, extensive heterogeneity (most chromatin loops occur in only 5–30% of individual cells), positive and negative feedback loops, and emergent structural features. The cell-type-specific gene expression program acts as an attractor state, with developmental transitions between attractors accompanied by alterations in large-scale genome topology.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides a unifying conceptual framework — self-organization — that reconciles the apparent paradox of ordered, non-random genome architecture coexisting with pervasive single-cell heterogeneity
- Identifies four interacting principles (polymer physics, dynamics, phase separation, architectural constraints) as the mechanistic basis for 3D genome organization, replacing earlier gene zip-code models
- Distinguishes structural features of chromatin as modulators rather than binary determinants of genome activity, reframing the field's understanding of TAD boundaries and compartment identity
- Explains how the same genome organization principles can account for both the universal architectural features (loops, TADs, territories) and the tissue-specific and single-cell heterogeneous patterns
- Places nuclear RNA, phase separation condensates, and nuclear architectural elements (lamina, nuclear bodies) into a single integrated model of genome organization

## Future Research Directions

- Developing improved single-cell multi-omics approaches that simultaneously capture chromatin conformation, transcription, and epigenetic state to understand how stochastic genome organization contributes to developmental cell fate decisions
- Testing the causal role of phase separation in genome organization by designing mutations that specifically disrupt IDR-mediated condensate formation without affecting other protein functions
- Quantifying the feedback loops between gene expression and genome organization to determine how changes in transcription drive structural reorganization and vice versa
- Using live-cell imaging with nanometer resolution to directly observe the dynamic formation and dissolution of chromatin loops, TADs, and phase-separated condensates in real time
- Applying the self-organization framework to understand how the genome reorganizes during disease, aging (senescence-associated heterochromatin changes), and reprogramming
