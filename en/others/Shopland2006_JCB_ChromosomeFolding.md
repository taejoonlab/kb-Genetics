# Folding and organization of a contiguous chromosome region according to the gene distribution pattern in primary genomic sequence

## Citation (NLM)
Shopland LS, Lynch CR, Peterson KA, Thornton K, Kepper N, von Hase J, Stein S, Vincent S, Molloy KR, Kreth G, Cremer C, Bult CJ, O'Brien TP. Folding and organization of a contiguous chromosome region according to the gene distribution pattern in primary genomic sequence. J Cell Biol. 2006;174(1):27-38. doi:10.1083/jcb.200603083

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1083/jcb.200603083](https://doi.org/10.1083/jcb.200603083)

## Summary

This study investigates how a series of multiple genes within a megabase-scale chromosomal region are spatially organized in the cell nucleus, addressing the poorly understood problem of "higher-order" chromatin folding above the 30-nm fiber level. The authors focused on a well-annotated 4.3-Mb gene-poor region on mouse chromosome 14 (Mmu14), which contains 19 genes organized into four small clusters separated by four gene deserts of 200 kb to 1 Mb. This gene cluster–desert architecture is conserved from primates to chickens, suggesting functional and structural constraints on gene spacing in the primary sequence.

Using a two-color FISH labeling scheme — gene clusters labeled in red (rhodamine) and gene deserts in green (fluorescein) — across 23 bacterial artificial chromosome (BAC) probes spanning the entire 4.3-Mb region, the authors surveyed the nuclear organization of this region in NIH-3T3 fibroblasts, primary mouse embryo fibroblasts, chondrocytes, embryonic stem cells, and T cells. In G1-phase NIH-3T3 cells, three predominant morphological conformations were identified, together comprising 67% of chromosomes. The first (striped fiber) reflects the linear sequence organization in a compacted state with alternating cluster-desert signals and a packing ratio of 300:1. The second (zigzag) displaces all gene clusters to one side of the deserts (800:1 packing). The third (gene cluster hub) groups all gene clusters together into a central hub with deserts arrayed peripherally (900:1 packing). The remaining 20% showed combinations (transitional states). Homologous chromosomes in the same nucleus almost never share the same conformation (4% of cells), indicating independent, probabilistic folding.

The conformations are directly linked to the gene distribution pattern in primary sequence: shifting the probe labeling scheme 250 kb down the chromosome breaks the color correspondence to clusters and deserts and significantly reduces the frequency of zigzag and hub structures, confirming sequence-specific rather than labeling-artifact-driven organization. Applying the same Mmu14 labeling scheme to a gene-dense region on Mmu15 (lacking deserts) generates mostly extended striped fibers, contrasting with the folded structures seen at Mmu14.

Quantitative analysis of specific cluster-cluster and cluster-desert distances reveals that proximal gene clusters C1 and C2 are significantly closer in 3D space than the flanking deserts D1 and D2, even though C1–C2 and D1–D2 are separated by comparable genomic distances. This contrasts with a computational random folding model, which predicts similar separations for sequence-equidistant loci. The mean-squared nuclear distance versus genomic distance relationship is multiphasic — not a simple random walk — indicating specific subdomains with different folding properties, including a distal region that loops back toward the proximal end.

Gene cluster organization is not simply dependent on ongoing transcription: inhibition of transcription elongation by DRB (5,6-dichlorobenzimidazole riboside) for 60 minutes does not change the frequency of the predominant conformations, and RNA FISH shows that transcribing gene clusters do not preferentially aggregate at a common transcription factory. Similarly, gene clusters do not preferentially associate with splicing factor speckles (only 8% contact frequency). Instead, gene deserts preferentially align with the nuclear periphery in the more folded (zigzag and hub) conformations, suggesting that lamina associations of gene-poor sequences contribute to the higher-order architecture of this region.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the first systematic, morphology-based analysis of higher-order chromatin folding across a multi-gene chromosome region, demonstrating that 3D organization is dictated by the pattern of gene clusters and deserts in primary sequence
- Shows that a chromosome region can exist in multiple discrete, probabilistic 3D conformational states rather than a single defined structure, establishing the concept of stochastic yet non-random genome folding that would later underpin single-cell Hi-C findings
- Demonstrates that gene cluster aggregation does not simply reflect ongoing transcription, distinguishing structural (sequence-based) from functional (transcription-driven) contributions to nuclear organization
- Provides pre-Hi-C morphological evidence for the existence of chromatin domains (the gene cluster hubs and desert-excluded structures resemble what would later be called TADs), grounding the biochemical discovery of TADs in prior cell biological observations
- Links nuclear periphery associations of gene deserts (likely lamina-associated domains, LADs) to higher-order chromatin folding, connecting spatial nuclear architecture to the gene distribution pattern encoded in primary sequence

## Future Research Directions

- Applying super-resolution imaging and single-cell Hi-C to the same Mmu14 region to directly compare the morphological conformations described here with contact frequency maps, and to understand the dynamic transitions between conformations
- Determining the molecular basis for the sequence-specific folding of gene cluster regions — whether conserved regulatory sequences within gene deserts act as boundary elements or nuclear lamina anchors
- Investigating whether the gene cluster hub conformation preferentially forms during specific cell cycle phases or in response to developmental signals, given that the predominant conformations were identified in G1 cells
- Testing whether disruption of nuclear lamina associations of gene deserts (e.g., in lamin A/C knockout cells) alters the frequencies of the three predominant conformations
- Using CRISPR-based deletion of gene deserts to determine whether these sequences are required for the formation of gene cluster hubs and whether their removal affects the coordinated expression of genes within the clusters
