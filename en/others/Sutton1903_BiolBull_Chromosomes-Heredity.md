# The Chromosomes in Heredity

## Citation (NLM)
Sutton WS. The chromosomes in heredity. Biol Bull. 1903;4:231-251.

**DOI:** Not available (Biological Bulletin, 1903; classic paper)

---

## Summary

This paper by Walter S. Sutton, a graduate student in Edmund B. Wilson's laboratory at Columbia University, is the foundational document establishing the chromosomal basis of Mendelian heredity. Sutton based his analysis on studies of spermatogenesis in the lubber grasshopper Brachystola magna, and he explicitly states that the general conceptions of the paper were derived purely from cytological data before he became familiar with Mendel's principles — making the subsequent convergence of the two bodies of evidence all the more powerful. Together with Theodor Boveri's parallel experimental work, this paper defines what is now called the Sutton-Boveri chromosomal theory of heredity.

Sutton's central argument proceeds from five cytological observations: the presynaptic germ-cell chromosome group consists of two equivalent series (one paternal, one maternal); synapsis involves pairing of homologous chromosomes by size; the first postsynaptic division is equational; the second postsynaptic (reducing) division separates the synapsed pairs into different germ cells; and chromosomes maintain morphological individuality through successive divisions. The critical deduction is that the orientation of each bivalent chromosome pair at the equatorial plate of the reducing division is a matter of pure chance — any chromosome pair may lie with maternal or paternal chromatin toward either pole, independently of other pairs. This directly explains Mendel's law of segregation (one member of each pair goes to each gamete) and the law of independent assortment (different pairs orient independently).

Three additional insights make this paper far more than a restatement of Mendel in chromosomal language. First, citing Boveri's 1902 dispermy experiments, Sutton accepts that individual chromosomes carry qualitatively distinct developmental potentialities — each chromosome represents the physical basis of a specific set of characters. Second, and with remarkable prescience, he predicts that all allelomorphs (genes) associated with the same chromosome must be inherited together — the first theoretical prediction of genetic linkage, which would be confirmed experimentally by Morgan's group a decade later. Third, he calculates that with n chromosome pairs in the reduced series, 2^n different gamete types are possible, demonstrating mathematically that independent assortment of chromosomes generates essentially unlimited heritable variation.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Marks the historical convergence of genetics (Mendel's 1865 laws, rediscovered 1900) and cytology (chromosomal observations from the 1880s onward) into a unified mechanistic theory of heredity — the founding act of what we now call genetics as a unified science.
- The random independent orientation of bivalents and the resulting 2^n gamete diversity calculation provide the foundational logic for understanding Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, population genetic diversity, and the maintenance of genetic variation in sexual populations.
- The prediction of genetic linkage — that genes on the same chromosome are inherited together — is the direct conceptual ancestor of linkage analysis, genetic mapping, GWAS, and SNP-based genomic studies.
- The paper demonstrates "scientific convergence": a student working purely from microscopic cell biology independently arrived at the same laws that a nineteenth-century botanist derived from breeding experiments.
- Sutton's observations on the accessory (sex) chromosome directly anticipate Wilson and Stevens' sex determination work and Bridges' non-disjunction experiments, making this paper the logical starting point for lectures on sex determination.

## Future Research Directions

- The molecular basis of Sutton's independent assortment — random orientation of bivalents at meiosis I due to spindle-kinetochore interactions — is now understood at the level of kinetochore attachment, tension sensing, and the spindle assembly checkpoint.
- Sutton's predicted genetic linkage became the empirical foundation for recombination-based genetic mapping; modern extensions include linkage disequilibrium (LD) analysis, haplotype phasing, and recombination landscape mapping.
- The 2^n gamete diversity calculation underpins modern estimates of heterozygosity, nucleotide diversity, and the theoretical basis for genetic uniqueness of individuals.
- The prediction that genes on the same chromosome are co-inherited, disrupted by crossing over, was the conceptual seed for Morgan's genetic maps and modern GWAS fine-mapping.
- Sutton's X chromosome observations initiated the entire field of sex chromosome biology — including the evolution of sex-determination systems and the study of sex-chromosome aneuploidy in clinical genetics.
