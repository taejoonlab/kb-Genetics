# Y-chromosome evolution: emerging insights into processes of Y-chromosome degeneration

## Citation (NLM)
Bachtrog D. Y-chromosome evolution: emerging insights into processes of Y-chromosome degeneration. Nat Rev Genet. 2013;14(2):113-124. doi:10.1038/nrg3366

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg3366](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg3366)

---

## Summary

The human Y chromosome is one of the most unusual structures in the genome — it began as an ordinary autosome, yet has shed the vast majority of its ancestral genes over hundreds of millions of years of evolution. Bachtrog's 2013 review synthesizes the genomic revolution's contributions to understanding how and why this degeneration occurs. The review begins with a survey of what whole-genome and transcriptome analyses have revealed about the current gene content of old Y chromosomes in primates and Drosophila. In humans, the euchromatic Y contains only 78 protein-coding genes compared to roughly 800 on the X; in Drosophila melanogaster, the entirely heterochromatic Y harbors just 13 known protein-coding genes. Despite these differences in absolute numbers, both systems converge on a common theme: surviving Y-linked genes are strongly enriched for male-specific reproductive functions.

The review provides a detailed mechanistic account of Y chromosome degeneration. Sex chromosomes originate when one member of an autosome pair acquires a male-determining gene. Sexually antagonistic mutations — beneficial to males but harmful to females — are thought to drive the subsequent suppression of recombination between the proto-X and proto-Y, often through chromosomal inversions. Once recombination is suppressed across increasingly large regions (forming evolutionary strata), the Y becomes subject to three classic population-genetic forces: Muller's ratchet (irreversible accumulation of deleterious mutations in a finite non-recombining population), genetic hitchhiking (deleterious mutations dragged to fixation alongside beneficial ones), and the ruby-in-the-rubbish model (beneficial mutations lost because they are linked to deleterious alleles).

A major contribution of this review is its examination of Y degeneration in "real time" using Drosophila neo-sex chromosomes. By comparing three Drosophila species with neo-Y chromosomes of different ages — D. albomicans (~0.1 Mya), D. miranda (~1 Mya), and D. pseudoobscura (~15 Mya) — Bachtrog traces the progressive stages of degeneration. Strikingly, in D. albomicans, about 30% of neo-Y genes are already transcriptionally downregulated despite showing almost no protein-coding sequence decay. This finding suggests that transcriptional silencing via epigenetic changes precedes the accumulation of nonsense and frameshift mutations — a mechanistic insight that challenges the simple view that coding-sequence degeneration drives silencing.

Plant Y chromosomes offer a complementary perspective. In Silene latifolia, elevated rates of protein evolution and reduced expression at Y-linked genes signal the onset of degeneration, yet more than 80% of Y-linked genes remain functional — consistent with slower degeneration compared to animals, possibly because pollen expresses many Y-linked genes in the haploid state, providing direct selection against deleterious mutations. Similarly, papaya sex chromosomes reveal large-scale inversions creating evolutionary strata ~7 and ~2 million years ago, with dramatic retrotransposon expansion on the Y.

The review concludes by addressing the question of Y chromosome survival. Theoretical modeling shows that the rate of gene decay should slow dramatically as the Y loses genes and interference among remaining loci decreases. Comparative data from Drosophila neo-Y chromosomes support a non-linear (decelerating) model of gene loss rather than the constant-rate extinction scenario sometimes portrayed in popular accounts. The ancient Drosophila Y chromosome, despite being gene-poor, gains genes at a net positive rate. Human Y chromosome gene content has been stable for the past 25 million years, strongly arguing against imminent Y chromosome loss.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Establishes the canonical framework for understanding Y chromosome evolution — from autosomal origin through recombination suppression to gene degeneration — providing essential conceptual vocabulary for the field
- Introduces Drosophila neo-sex chromosome systems as powerful in vivo models for studying degeneration "in progress," showing that evolutionary strata, transposable element accumulation, and gene silencing all occur rapidly after recombination suppression
- Demonstrates that transcriptional downregulation can precede coding-sequence mutation, reframing the mechanistic sequence of Y chromosome decay
- Contrasts animal and plant Y chromosome evolution to reveal how haploid selection in pollen can decelerate degeneration — illustrating how life-history differences modulate universal evolutionary forces
- Directly refutes the popular claim that the human Y chromosome is headed for extinction, providing quantitative evolutionary arguments for long-term Y stability

## Future Research Directions

- Deciphering the molecular mechanism by which transcriptional silencing initiates on neo-Y chromosomes before coding-sequence decay — is it driven by transposable element accumulation, promoter mutation, or chromatin remodeling?
- Determining the quantitative contribution of each population-genetic force (Muller's ratchet, hitchhiking, ruby-in-the-rubbish) to observed degeneration rates using realistic parameter estimates from natural populations
- Characterizing the gene regulatory networks regulated by Y-linked heterochromatin in D. melanogaster and the fitness consequences of Y-linked epigenetic variation on autosomal gene expression
- Investigating why plant Y chromosomes degenerate more slowly than animal Y chromosomes, and whether haploid purifying selection is the dominant mechanism or whether effective population size, generation time, and recombination rates play equally important roles
- Expanding comparative Y chromosome studies to non-model organisms across the tree of life to test whether the observed deceleration of gene loss is a universal feature of Y chromosome evolution
