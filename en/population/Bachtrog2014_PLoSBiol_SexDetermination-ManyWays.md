# Sex determination: why so many ways of doing it?

## Citation (NLM)
Bachtrog D, Mank JE, Peichel CL, Kirkpatrick M, Otto SP, Ashman TL, et al. Sex determination: why so many ways of doing it? PLoS Biol. 2014;12(7):e1001899. doi:10.1371/journal.pbio.1001899

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.1001899](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pbio.1001899)

---

## Summary

This landmark essay by the Tree of Sex Consortium systematically dismantles three pervasive myths about sex determination that have calcified in the biological literature due to the disproportionate influence of a small set of model organisms. By surveying sex determination across the breadth of animals and plants, the authors show that the familiar XY chromosomal sex determination of mammals and Drosophila is far from the norm, and that evolution explores a remarkable diversity of mechanisms to accomplish the same ultimate goal: partitioning individuals into male and female reproductive roles.

The paper first addresses the evolution of separate sexes, noting that while hermaphroditism is rare among insects (which dominate animal species counts), it is common in mollusks, corals, fish, and most flowering plants. Separate sexes evolve when trade-offs between male and female reproductive investment favor specialization, or when inbreeding depression makes self-fertilization costly. These transitions can occur through multiple evolutionary pathways — from hermaphrodites to females plus hermaphrodites (gynodioecy), or through rapid acquisition of male or female sterility mutations.

Myth 1 — that sex chromosomes are universal and stable — is demolished by a broad phylogenetic survey. Temperature-dependent sex determination characterizes all crocodiles, most turtles, and many fish; social cues determine sex in reef fish; Wolbachia bacteria override chromosomal sex determination in many arthropods. Even among species with genotypic sex determination (GSD), the chromosomes involved change frequently. Houseflies, for instance, normally have XY chromosomes but some populations harbor dominant autosomal masculinizing or feminizing alleles that override this system. Genomic conflict — between sex-ratio-distorting elements and suppressors — is identified as a major driver of transitions between sex-determining mechanisms.

Myth 2 — the single master-switch gene — is challenged by multiple lines of evidence. While master-switch genes like SRY in mammals do exist and are conserved, the diversity of master-switch genes across taxa is striking. The primary sex-determination signal differs across fish species in the genus Oryzias, where GSDF, AMH-R, and other genes have been independently co-opted. In zebrafish, sex appears to be a polygenic threshold trait with no single master regulator. The silkworm uses a W-derived piRNA (Fem) to suppress the Z-linked gene Masc. Crucially, while primary sex-determination switches are diverse, downstream pathway components — particularly the DM (doublesex/mab3) family transcription factors — are widely conserved across invertebrates and vertebrates, suggesting a conserved "toolkit" that has been rewired multiple times.

Myth 3 — that sex chromosome differentiation and degeneration is inevitable — is refuted by evidence that ancient homomorphic sex chromosomes exist in pythons (~140 Mya) and ratite birds (~120 Mya). These sex chromosomes are almost as old as the heteromorphic XY system of therian mammals (~180 Mya) yet remain morphologically indistinguishable. Mechanisms proposed for avoiding degeneration include rare XY recombination (possibly via sex reversal), a paucity of genes under sexually antagonistic selection, and resolution of sexual antagonism through sex-biased expression rather than recombination suppression. Furthermore, the Y chromosome's fate is not simply linear decay: theory predicts deceleration of gene loss, and comparative data show the primate Y has been stable for 25 million years.

The essay concludes with a forward-looking research agenda, emphasizing the need to characterize sex-determination mechanisms across a broader taxonomic range and to study sex-chromosome evolution in taxa with rapid turnover — such as lizards, fish, and plants — where evolutionary transitions can be caught in progress.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides a comprehensive, phylogenetically grounded survey of sex-determination mechanisms across all major animal and plant groups, correcting model-organism bias that distorts student intuitions about what is "normal"
- Identifies genomic conflict (meiotic drive, endosymbionts, cyto-nuclear conflict) as a major, underappreciated force driving transitions between sex-determination systems — connecting sex chromosome evolution to broader conflicts in genetic inheritance
- Distinguishes the remarkable diversity of primary sex-determination signals from the conservation of downstream developmental pathways, setting up a mechanistic framework for understanding how evolution rewires gene regulatory networks
- Introduces polygenic sex determination and environmental sex determination as legitimate and widespread alternatives to single-locus chromosomal models, relevant to contemporary concerns about climate change and temperature-sensitive species
- Frames sex chromosome evolution as potentially cyclical rather than linear, with sex chromosome "birth-death" dynamics driven by turnover events — a conceptual reorientation that motivates the search for drivers of turnover

## Future Research Directions

- Systematic characterization of sex-determination mechanisms across currently unsampled taxonomic groups using genomic approaches, to test whether observed turnover rates and mechanisms are representative of broader eukaryotic diversity
- Molecular identification of sexually antagonistic loci to empirically test whether sexual conflict actually drives recombination suppression and sex chromosome differentiation, as theory predicts
- Experimental study of how environmental sex determination responds to climate change: can species with temperature-dependent sex determination adapt to maintain balanced sex ratios under novel temperature regimes?
- Investigation of the molecular pathways linking primary sex-determination signals (which are diverse) to downstream DM-family transcription factors (which are conserved) to understand how regulatory rewiring occurs during sex-determination evolution
- Population genetics and functional genomics studies in taxa with ongoing sex chromosome turnover to determine whether new sex chromosomes are truly more fit than old degenerated ones, and what determines whether a turnover event goes to fixation
