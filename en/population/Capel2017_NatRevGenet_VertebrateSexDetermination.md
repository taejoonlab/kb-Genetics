# Vertebrate sex determination: evolutionary plasticity of a fundamental switch

## Citation (NLM)
Capel B. Vertebrate sex determination: evolutionary plasticity of a fundamental switch. Nat Rev Genet. 2017;18(11):675-689. doi:10.1038/nrg.2017.60

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg.2017.60](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg.2017.60)

## Summary

Sex determination is the process by which a sexually reproducing organism initiates differentiation as male or female. Unlike most developmental programs — such as body axis formation or eye specification — which are governed by highly conserved gene networks, sex-determining mechanisms are remarkably variable among vertebrates. No single gene initiates sex determination across all species, and even when the same molecular players are involved in multiple lineages, they often act in different orders within the cascade. A key structural feature enabling this plasticity is the bipotential primordium: the early gonad, the primordia for internal sex ducts, and the external genitalia all arise with equal capacity to develop along either male or female pathways. Alfred Jost's classic gonadectomy experiments in rabbits established that primary sex determination resides in the decision to form a testis or an ovary, which then drives downstream differentiation of the secondary sex characteristics through hormonal signals such as testosterone and anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH).

In mammals, primary sex determination is anchored to the Y-linked gene *Sry*, which encodes a transcription factor that tips the bipotential gonad toward testis fate by activating *Sox9*. A feedforward loop between SOX9 and FGF9 reinforces testis fate while actively repressing the female pathway centered on WNT4, RSPO1, and FOXL2. This mutual antagonism — in which male and female sub-networks simultaneously compete until one surpasses a bistability threshold — is a unifying principle across vertebrate sex determination, even when the upstream triggers differ entirely. Exceptions within mammals, such as species that have lost the Y chromosome (Tokudaia osimensis) or in which epigenetic silencing of *Sry* generates XY females (Akodon azarae), illustrate the resilience and evolvability of the underlying network.

Beyond mammals, vertebrates display a continuum of genetic (GSD) and environmental sex determination (ESD) strategies. Birds use a ZZ/ZW dosage system controlled by *DMRT1*, while many reptiles and fish exhibit temperature-dependent sex determination (TSD) in which incubation temperature during a critical developmental window biases sex ratios. RAD-seq surveys of gecko species identified at least 25 transitions between XX/XY, ZZ/ZW, and TSD systems — a strikingly low evolutionary barrier between mechanisms. In ectotherms such as the bearded dragon *Pogona vitticeps*, a single generation of high-temperature exposure was sufficient to eliminate the W chromosome and convert a GSD population to TSD, illustrating how climate change could rapidly reshape sex ratio and reproductive biology.

Fish exhibit the greatest diversity in sex-determining mechanisms, including sex reversal in adult life. Sequential hermaphroditism — switching functional sex in response to social or environmental cues — has evolved independently in at least 27 teleost families. Transcriptome analyses during sex change reveal that shutdown of the dominant transcriptional network is required before the opposing network can be activated, mirroring the bistability framework seen in mammalian gonad determination. Master regulators in fish range from transcription factors (*Dmy*, *Sox3*) to TGFβ ligands (*gsdf*, *amhY*, *gdf6*), demonstrating that any signaling component capable of shifting the male/female network balance can be recruited to the top of the sex determination cascade.

Epigenetic mechanisms add a further layer of control, particularly in maintaining cell-fate commitment in adult tissues. Loss of *Dmrt1* in adult Sertoli cells derepresses *Foxl2*, while loss of *Foxl2* in granulosa cells allows *Sox9* re-expression and partial ovary-to-testis transdifferentiation. These findings demonstrate that gonadal cell identity is not permanently fixed but requires active, ongoing repression of the alternative fate — a molecular logic consistent with the bistable network model and consistent with the evolutionary plasticity of the system across vertebrate phylogeny.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Establishes that sex determination is an unusually plastic developmental switch: unlike conserved processes such as body axis specification, sex-determining mechanisms evolve rapidly and repeatedly, providing a model system for studying how developmental networks evolve.
- Introduces the bipotential primordium and bistability framework as unifying concepts: the simultaneous activity of opposing male and female sub-networks, resolved by a threshold-crossing event, explains both the conserved logic and the variable triggers of sex determination across vertebrates.
- Demonstrates that genetic and environmental mechanisms are not mutually exclusive categories but co-exist along a continuum, with many species using both GSD and ESD simultaneously — a critical conceptual update over the classical binary classification.
- Highlights the ecological and evolutionary relevance: temperature-dependent sex reversal in *Pogona vitticeps* shows that climate change can eliminate entire sex chromosome systems in a single generation, making sex determination a pressing topic in conservation biology.
- Provides the conceptual foundation for understanding population-level sex ratio dynamics: deviations from the optimal sex ratio create selection pressure that favors novel alleles restoring balance, linking molecular mechanisms of sex determination to Hardy-Weinberg-like equilibrium thinking at the population level.

## Future Research Directions

- Determine the molecular thermosensor(s) responsible for temperature-dependent sex determination in reptiles — identifying the protein(s) whose activity is temperature-sensitive and positioned within the male/female antagonistic network.
- Clarify the extent to which epigenetic marks (H3K9me3, H3K27me3, DNA methylation) are causally upstream of sex determination versus downstream consequences of transcription factor activity, particularly during the bistable transition window.
- Investigate how psychological sex (gender identity and partner preference) is established independently of gonadal sex determination, especially in fish where behavioral sex change can precede gonadal remodeling.
- Model the population genetics of GSD-to-TSD transitions under climate change scenarios to predict which vertebrate taxa face sex ratio collapse and to evaluate the evolutionary sufficiency of the "rarer-sex advantage" mechanism in restoring balance.
- Apply single-cell multi-omic approaches to resolve the sequence of cell-fate decisions and intercellular signaling that occurs during the bistable transition in the bipotential gonad across multiple model and non-model species.
