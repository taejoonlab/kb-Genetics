# Effective population size and patterns of molecular evolution and variation

## Citation (NLM)
Charlesworth B. Effective population size and patterns of molecular evolution and variation. Nat Rev Genet. 2009;10(3):195-205. doi:10.1038/nrg2526

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg2526](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg2526)

---

## Summary

Effective population size (Ne) is one of the central concepts in population genetics, introduced by Sewall Wright. Ne is defined as the size of an ideal Wright-Fisher population that would exhibit the same rate of evolutionary change due to genetic drift, or the same level of inbreeding effects, as the actual finite population under study. In the Wright-Fisher model, offspring are formed each generation by random sampling, and the rate of divergence between neutral alleles — or the rate of loss of variation within a population — is proportional to 1/(2N). Ne is the key parameter that simultaneously determines both the equilibrium level of neutral genetic variation and the efficiency of natural selection.

A variety of biological factors cause Ne to fall below the census number of breeding individuals (N). The principal factors include unequal numbers of breeding individuals of the two sexes (as when few males mate with many females), increased variance in offspring number per individual (a non-Poisson distribution arising from sexual selection and similar processes), inbreeding, differences in mode of inheritance (autosomal, X-linked, Y-linked, or maternally inherited cytoplasmic organelles), age structure, and changes in population size. Populations that have experienced a bottleneck are particularly affected, as the harmonic mean effect of past small sizes can dramatically reduce Ne. In humans, Ne estimated from DNA sequence diversity is approximately 10,000–20,000 — far smaller than the current population of billions — reflecting a long history of small population sizes and the recent rapid demographic expansion.

In the interplay between selection and drift, Ne together with the selection coefficient (s) determines the efficiency of selection through the product Nes. When Nes is much less than 0.25, a mutation behaves essentially as if it were neutral; when Nes exceeds 2, the probability that a deleterious mutation becomes fixed by drift is negligibly small. Conversely, for advantageous mutations, fixation proceeds much as in an infinite population when Nes exceeds 1. This principle explains why weak selection operates very effectively in bacterial and Drosophila populations with large Ne, while in humans — where Ne is comparatively small — variants with weak selection coefficients can behave as effectively neutral.

The spatial structure of a population (geographic subdivision) has complex effects on Ne. Under conservative migration, mean genetic diversity within subpopulations behaves as if determined by the total effective size across all subpopulations (NeT), analogous to a single panmictic population. Greater restriction of migration between subpopulations leads to a substantial increase in the metapopulation effective size (NeM), lengthening coalescence times across the species as a whole and elevating overall diversity.

Ne is not uniform across the genome. Loci subject to balancing selection show locally elevated Ne around polymorphic sites, generating peaks of diversity, while background selection driven by the removal of deleterious mutations and selective sweeps eliminate linked neutral variation, locally reducing Ne (the Hill-Robertson effect). This spatial variation in Ne within the genome is directly linked to the observation that genetic diversity is low in regions of reduced recombination — a pattern consistently observed in Drosophila, humans, and certain plants.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- **Integrating molecular evolution and population genetics:** Ne functions as the key mediating variable in the debate between neutral theory, nearly neutral theory, and selectionist views. The principle that weak selection is effective when Ne is large, but that weakly deleterious mutations can fix as if neutral when Ne is small, is essential for understanding differences in the rate of protein evolution across species.
- **Explaining patterns of within-genome diversity:** The positive correlation between recombination rate and genetic diversity, diversity peaks around the MHC, and elevated diversity at self-incompatibility (SI) loci are all unified under the framework of spatial variation in Ne within the genome.
- **Applications to conservation and breeding biology:** Estimating Ne is essential for predicting the rate of inbreeding and the maintenance of genetic diversity in small populations. The review clarifies that variance Ne and coalescent Ne can differ under changing population sizes, highlighting the importance of choosing an appropriate estimation method.
- **Providing a benchmark for comparative multi-species genomics:** By presenting Ne estimates for multiple species (humans ~10,400; African Drosophila ~1,150,000; E. coli ~25,000,000), the review establishes reference points for interpreting differences in rates of molecular evolution and levels of genetic variation across species in terms of Ne differences.
- **Theoretical framework for Ne analysis in structured populations:** By mathematically formalising how within-subpopulation and total-population diversity vary with migration patterns in geographically structured populations, the review provides a theoretical foundation for estimating Ne and interpreting molecular evolution in species with real population structure.

7. **Lande R (1988)** — Genetics and demography in biological conservation. — Presented a theoretical framework for applying Ne as a practical tool in conservation biology, emphasising its importance for estimating minimum viable population sizes

## Future Research Directions

- **Refinement of the Ne landscape across the genome:** Combining high-resolution recombination maps with whole-genome sequencing data to more precisely characterise spatial variation in Ne within genomes, and developing analytical methods to separately estimate the relative contributions of background selection and selective sweeps, is needed.
- **Comparative population genomics across diverse taxa:** Systematically analysing the relationship between Ne estimates and genomic complexity across a wide range of taxa to test theoretical hypotheses about the role of Ne in genome architecture evolution.
- **Estimation of temporally varying Ne:** Methods such as PSMC for reconstructing past demographic history from single pairs of genomes are being extended, but improved accuracy in complex populations with structure and migration, along with novel statistical methods, is required.
- **Refining models of the interaction between selection and drift:** Modelling the combined effects of background selection, selective sweeps, and balancing selection acting simultaneously in different genomic regions, and developing statistical methods to disentangle their relative contributions from empirical genome data, is needed.
- **Conservation applications in small population genomics:** Precise estimation of Ne in endangered species, evaluation of Ne-maximisation strategies in conservation breeding programmes, and studies linking the efficiency of purging of deleterious mutations in small populations to Ne are all important priorities.
