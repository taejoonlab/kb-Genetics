# Sex chromosome evolution: so many exceptions to the rules

## Citation (NLM)
Furman BLS, Metzger DCH, Darolti I, Wright AE, Sandkam BA, Almeida P, et al. Sex chromosome evolution: so many exceptions to the rules. Genome Biol Evol. 2020;12(6):750-763. doi:10.1093/gbe/evaa081

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1093/gbe/evaa081](https://doi.org/10.1093/gbe/evaa081)

---

## Summary

The classical model of sex chromosome evolution — from autosome, to homomorphic sex chromosomes, to heteromorphic sex chromosomes, with dosage compensation evolving in step with Y degeneration — is clean, compelling, and frequently wrong. Furman et al. (2020) review how genomic data from a widening array of non-model species have revealed that exceptions to these rules are as informative as the rules themselves. The paper surveys four stages of sex chromosome evolution — origin, recombination suppression, dosage compensation, and the sex chromosome cycle — and at each stage identifies key empirical findings that challenge the canonical framework.

On the origin of sex chromosomes, the classic model assumes sex chromosomes descend from a pair of homologous autosomes. This is often true, but B chromosomes — normally selfish supernumerary chromosomes — have independently evolved as Y chromosomes in psyllids and cichlids, and as W chromosomes in Lepidoptera. In the pillbug Armadillidium vulgare, the W chromosome apparently derived from Wolbachia feminizing factors that were incorporated into the nuclear genome. In plants, the two-locus model (requiring separate male sterility and female sterility loci) is widely invoked but increasingly questioned, with evidence from wild strawberry, persimmon, and Salicaceae suggesting single-locus sex determination.

On recombination suppression, the review scrutinizes inversions as the canonical mechanism. While chromosomal inversions are strongly associated with discrete evolutionary strata on sex chromosomes, direct evidence that inversions cause recombination suppression is sparse. Data from fungi convincingly show that in several mating-type systems, recombination suppression preceded inversions — inversions may instead consolidate pre-existing suppression caused by other means. The paper highlights transposable elements as an underappreciated alternative mechanism: TEs near sex-determining loci can suppress recombination by creating divergence that triggers silencing via host defense pathways, and TE accumulation after suppression reinforces heterochromatin formation. DNA methylation and chromatin modifiers represent yet another category of potential suppression mechanisms, with hypermethylation of Y-linked regions documented in sticklebacks and papaya. Notably, intraspecific variation in the non-recombining region is documented in multiple fish, amphibian, reptile, and mammal taxa, challenging the assumption that sex chromosome states are fixed within species.

The review then re-evaluates dosage compensation, which classical theory treats as an inevitable response to Y degeneration. In reality, complete chromosome-wide dosage compensation is rare — best documented in mammals, some insects, and a handful of other systems. Across birds, snakes, fish, frogs, and plants, incomplete gene-by-gene dosage compensation is the norm, with average sex-chromosomal expression significantly reduced in the heterogametic sex but without the dramatic fitness costs one might expect. Dosage sensitivity is not uniform: genes with haploinsufficiency phenotypes, ohnologs (retained after whole-genome duplication), and highly expressed genes are preferentially compensated. The evolution of global dosage compensation in XY systems may be accelerated relative to ZW systems because higher male mutation rates cause faster Y degeneration and because effective population size is reduced on the Z chromosome.

Finally, the review argues that sex chromosome evolution is better conceived as cyclical than linear. Turnover — the replacement of one sex chromosome pair by another — is documented in lizards, fish, amphibians, insects, and plants, often within narrow taxonomic groups. Turnover can occur through translocation of an existing sex-determining gene to an autosome (homologous turnover) or through de novo evolution of a new sex-determining gene on an autosome (non-homologous turnover). The driving forces of turnover include sexual conflict, genetic drift, and mutation accumulation, but empirical tests remain challenging because identifying sexually antagonistic alleles is technically difficult.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Updates the canonical sex chromosome evolution model with contemporary empirical findings from non-model organisms, demonstrating that the classical linear progression is better understood as a multidimensional landscape of evolutionary outcomes
- Reframes inversions as potentially downstream consequences of recombination suppression rather than its causes — a mechanistic reversal with important implications for how we interpret evolutionary strata
- Highlights epigenetic mechanisms (DNA methylation, transposable element silencing) as underappreciated drivers of both recombination suppression and dosage compensation evolution, connecting sex chromosome research to epigenomics
- Documents intraspecific variation in sex-linked regions across multiple vertebrate groups, showing that sex chromosome states are not fixed within species and that population-level comparisons may be more informative than interspecific ones for testing mechanistic hypotheses
- Establishes the birth-death cycle as a conceptual model for sex chromosome evolution, motivating the study of turnover dynamics and the conditions that stabilize versus destabilize sex-determining systems

## Future Research Directions

- Direct identification of the molecular sequence of events in recombination suppression: do inversions cause suppression, reinforce it, or arise as a neutral consequence of reduced recombination maintaining gene order?
- Population-level genomic studies to characterize intraspecific variation in sex-linked regions across species, testing whether populations with different degrees of sexual conflict show corresponding differences in sex chromosome differentiation
- Experimental manipulation of DNA methylation and TE silencing pathways in species with young sex chromosomes to test whether epigenetic changes can initiate or reverse recombination suppression
- Comprehensive surveys of dosage sensitivity across sex-linked genes using protein quantification and fitness studies of haploinsufficiency, to understand why some genes require compensation and others do not
- Identification of sexually antagonistic loci in species with documented sex chromosome turnover, to empirically test whether sexual conflict is the proximate cause of turnover or whether genetic drift and other forces are more important
