# Methods and Models for Unravelling Human Evolutionary History

## Citation (NLM)
Schraiber JG, Akey JM. Methods and models for unravelling human evolutionary history. Nat Rev Genet. 2015 Dec;16(12):727-740. doi:10.1038/nrg4005

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg4005](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg4005)

## Summary

The genomes of modern human populations carry a record of our species' evolutionary history, encoded in patterns of DNA sequence variation. The broad outline of human prehistory — the emergence of anatomically modern humans in East Africa approximately 200,000 years ago, the out-of-Africa dispersal around 70,000 years ago and its accompanying population bottleneck, and the subsequent colonisation of every habitable region of the globe — is already well established. However, the whole-genome data now generated by massively parallel sequencing technologies afford the opportunity to infer far more complex and detailed models of human history, while simultaneously posing substantial methodological challenges for the correct interpretation of such data.

Quality control (QC) and exploratory data analysis (EDA) on high-quality genotype data constitute indispensable first steps in any population genomic analysis. These encompass read mapping, genotype calling, filtering for departures from Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium, and exclusion of segmental duplication regions. In low-coverage data in particular, heterozygous sites may be miscalled as homozygous, making genotype likelihood-based methods that explicitly model uncertainty preferable. Principal component analysis (PCA) is the primary exploratory tool for visualising population structure, but can yield counterintuitive results under population expansion models and must be interpreted with care.

Methods for inferring population structure and admixture fall broadly into three categories: PCA-based approaches, STRUCTURE-family methods (ADMIXTURE, fastSTRUCTURE, etc.), and chromosome-painting-based approaches. Choosing the number of populations (K) in STRUCTURE-family methods is sensitive to overfitting and requires careful model selection using cross-validation error minimisation or marginal likelihood comparisons. Local ancestry deconvolution — assigning each chromosomal segment in an admixed individual to a source population — relies primarily on HMMs based on the Li-Stephens approximation model; representative tools include HAPMIX, fineSTRUCTURE, and GLOBETROTTER.

Methods for inferring complex demographic history draw on three principal data types. The first is the site frequency spectrum (SFS)-based approach. The SFS tallies the number of sites at which derived alleles occur at each frequency in the sample; population growth is reflected as an excess of rare variants, as is a bottleneck. Within the Poisson random field (PRF) framework, software packages such as dadi, fastsimcoal2, and fastNeutrino use maximum likelihood to estimate demographic parameters. The second approach exploits the length distribution of identity-by-descent (IBD) or identity-by-state (IBS) segments. IBD segments are genomic intervals transmitted from a common ancestor without intervening recombination; their length distribution reflects the history of population size changes and migration. The third relies on whole-genome haplotype data; the Pairwise Sequentially Markovian Coalescent (PSMC) and MSMC are the leading methods, capable of inferring temporally deep changes in effective population size.

Each approach has distinct strengths and limitations. SFS-based methods assume unlinked variable sites, but use of composite likelihoods can produce unbiased yet underestimated standard errors. Haplotype-based methods exploit richer information but are computationally expensive and require phased data. To minimise confounding by selection, it is important to restrict analyses to neutral regions distant from functional sequences; defining such regions using functional genomic data from ENCODE and the Roadmap Epigenomics project is increasingly important.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- By systematically classifying and comparing methods for inferring human evolutionary history from whole-genome sequencing data, the review provides practical guidance for population genomicists in selecting methods appropriate to their research question.
- While emphasising the importance of data quality control, it concretely explains how biases introduced at the pre-analysis stage — technical noise and reference genome mapping bias — propagate into downstream inferences.
- By clarifying both the information content and the limits (statistical identifiability problems) of the site frequency spectrum, the review educates researchers about demographic scenarios that cannot be distinguished from SFS data alone.
- The review stresses the importance of selecting neutral regions and presents strategies to minimise confounding by selection through integration with functional genomic data (e.g., ENCODE).
- In fitting complex demographic models, practical best practices are presented — including optimisation from multiple starting points and the progressive construction of models from simple to complex — to reduce the risk of converging on local optima.

## Future Research Directions

- The development of methods that jointly model selection and demography is urgently needed. Most current approaches treat these two forces separately, which does not resolve the fundamental problem that the presence of selection can bias demographic inference.
- Whole-genome data from geographically diverse populations — particularly the many distinct populations within Africa, as well as Oceanian and indigenous American groups — are essential for filling gaps in the record of human migration history.
- Integrated methodological frameworks that combine ancient DNA and modern population data to perform demographically informed inference with temporal depth are required.
- Computationally efficient methods — approximate Bayesian computation (ABC), deep learning, and summary statistic-based inference — must be advanced to handle extremely complex demographic models involving many populations and intricate migration patterns.
- As fully phased whole-genome data become economically accessible at the individual level, haplotype-based methods should be extended to a broader range of applications, and the temporal resolution they provide should be further enhanced.
