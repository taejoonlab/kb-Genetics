# The Last Universal Common Ancestor Between Ancient Earth Chemistry and the Onset of Genetics

## Citation (NLM)
Weiss MC, Preiner M, Xavier JC, Zimorski V, Martin WF. The last universal common ancestor between ancient Earth chemistry and the onset of genetics. PLoS Genet. 2018 Aug 16;14(8):e1007518. doi:10.1371/journal.pgen.1007518

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1007518](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1007518)

---

## Summary

All known life descends from a single Last Universal Common Ancestor (LUCA). Earth formed 4.5 billion years ago and, following the moon-forming impact, became a magma ocean around 4.4 Ga, with liquid water appearing by approximately 4.2–4.3 Ga. The earliest traces of life are carbon isotope signatures found in rocks dating to 3.95 billion years ago. The universality of the genetic code and amino acid chirality implies that all extant organisms trace back to this evolutionary stage. LUCA is a theoretical construct, but it serves as a conceptual bridge connecting rocks and water to the emergence of cells with heritable information.

Our view of the structure of the tree of life has shifted substantially in recent years. A transition is underway from the traditional three-domain tree — rooted in rRNA phylogenetics and partitioned into Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya — to a two-domain tree in which eukaryotes branch from within the archaeal lineage. This shift has been driven by advances in phylogenetic methodology and by metagenomic data from newly discovered archaeal lineages, most notably the Asgard archaea. The cytoplasmic ribosomes of eukaryotes are archaeal in origin, their mitochondrial ribosomes are bacterial, and nuclear genes of bacterial provenance outnumber those of archaeal provenance by roughly 3:1 — all of which marks eukaryotes as true chimeras. Consequently, genes that can be traced back to the common ancestor of Bacteria and Archaea are inferred to be LUCA's.

The central methodological challenge in reconstructing the gene content of LUCA is lateral gene transfer (LGT). One must distinguish genes that descended vertically from LUCA from those acquired later through LGT. Weiss et al. clustered the 6.1 million proteins encoded by 1,981 prokaryotic genomes (1,847 bacterial, 134 archaeal) at a 25% amino acid identity threshold, yielding 286,514 clusters. Of these, 11,093 were present in both domains, but after applying LGT filters requiring domain monophyly (each domain forming a monophyletic group within the cluster) and representation in at least two phylum-level lineages, only 355 clusters (3%) remained. This means that 97% of the genes shared between Bacteria and Archaea bear signatures of LGT.

The 355 LUCA gene families paint a coherent picture of LUCA's physiology. LUCA was anaerobic, possessing enzymes rich in oxygen-sensitive iron-sulfur (FeS) clusters and proteins dependent on S-adenosyl methionine (SAM)-based radical chemistry. Carbon fixation proceeded via the acetyl-CoA (Wood-Ljungdahl) pathway — the simplest and most ancient of the six known CO2-fixation routes — using CO2 and H2 as substrates. Energetically, LUCA coupled carbon metabolism to ATP synthesis through thioesters and possessed subunits of a rotor-stator ATP synthase. However, LUCA lacked the components of a proton-pumping machinery capable of generating its own ion gradient, suggesting that LUCA instead harvested the naturally occurring pH gradient of alkaline hydrothermal vents to synthesize ATP. LUCA also encoded nitrogenase (nif genes) for N2 fixation and reverse gyrase for adaptation to high temperatures.

New insights into LUCA's genetic system reveal that RNA-modifying enzymes — particularly those that post-transcriptionally modify tRNA — trace back to LUCA. Modifications to tRNA anticodons are essential for codon-anticodon interactions, and 28 conserved modified bases have been documented in both Bacteria and Archaea. This suggests that the genetic code itself originated in the chemically reactive early-Earth environment characteristic of hydrothermal vents, and that the modified bases may preserve chemical imprints of that environment. Taken together, the gene inventory of LUCA, its inferred physiology, laboratory chemistry of relevant reactions, and geochemical evidence all converge on alkaline hydrothermal vents as LUCA's habitat.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Rather than searching for universally conserved genes by the traditional criterion (approximately 30 ribosomal proteins), this work introduces a phylogeny-based gene discovery approach — domain monophyly combined with representation across at least two phyla — that for the first time yields a concrete picture of LUCA's physiology and ecology, making it a field-transforming contribution.
- By quantifying the pervasiveness of LGT (97% of bacteria-archaea shared genes show evidence of transfer), the paper concretely demonstrates how severely conclusions about the tree of life can be distorted when LGT is ignored.
- The hypothesis that LUCA originated at alkaline hydrothermal vents is supported by convergent evidence from laboratory chemistry (spontaneous synthesis of acetyl-CoA pathway intermediates), geochemistry, and phylogenomics, providing a model for multidisciplinary approaches to the origin of life.
- The paper offers a balanced assessment of the current state of the two-domain tree (with eukaryotes arising from within Archaea) and the implications of metagenomic data, honestly acknowledging that the topology of the tree of life remains incompletely resolved.
- The paper clearly articulates the methodological limitation that the LUCA gene list will contract as new genomes are added, and identifies the future challenge of developing filters that remove recent LGT events while retaining ancient vertical inheritance.

## Future Research Directions

- As metagenomic datasets expand explosively, development of more rigorous and automated LGT filtering methodologies is needed to address false-positive and false-negative results arising from mislabeled taxonomic annotations and assembly errors.
- To expand the LUCA gene inventory, flexible filters that accommodate "imperfect domain monophyly" — allowing for a single LGT violation — are required to remove recent LGT events while recovering genes with ancient vertical inheritance.
- Methodological complexities including gene fusions and fissions, the choice of clustering identity thresholds, and structurally similar but poorly sequence-conserved proteins (leveraging structural genomics data) must be addressed to improve the accuracy of LUCA gene discovery.
- The chemical precursor reactions of the acetyl-CoA pathway, tRNA modification, and ATP synthesis should be systematically investigated in laboratory systems that replicate the environmental and chemical conditions of alkaline hydrothermal vents, to further elucidate the chemical processes underlying the origin of life.
- Resolving the two-domain versus three-domain debate and determining the specific scenario for eukaryotic origins will require more complete genome sequences from Asgard archaea and improved phylogenetic methods.
