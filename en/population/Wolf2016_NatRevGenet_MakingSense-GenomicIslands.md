# Making Sense of Genomic Islands of Differentiation in Light of Speciation

## Citation (NLM)
Wolf JBW, Ellegren H. Making sense of genomic islands of differentiation in light of speciation. Nat Rev Genet. 2017 Feb;18(2):87-100. doi:10.1038/nrg.2016.133

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg.2016.133](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg.2016.133)

---

## Summary

Understanding the genetic basis of speciation is one of the central challenges of evolutionary genetics. The genic model of speciation predicts that, under conditions of gene flow, a small number of genetic elements subject to divergent selection initially maintain reproductive isolation between populations, and that "genomic islands" of differentiation form around these loci. The advent of high-throughput sequencing technology has now made genome-wide population surveys possible in a wide range of natural populations, and heterogeneity in the landscape of genomic differentiation is widely observed. The central argument of this review, however, is that genomic islands of differentiation need not be the product of adaptive divergence relevant to speciation — linked selection, specifically genetic draft from selective sweeps and the reduction of diversity associated with purifying selection (background selection), can generate identical patterns.

Drawing on a survey of 67 speciation genomics studies, the review analyses current trends and pitfalls in the field. Taxon sampling is dominated by teleost fishes and insects, with a particular concentration on sticklebacks, Drosophila, and Heliconius butterflies. This bias reflects the availability of genomic resources and prevailing research paradigms, but generalisation beyond these systems requires extension to taxa with a wider range of effective population sizes, recombination rates, and dispersal capacities. A further concern is that the majority of studies (70%) did not explicitly estimate gene flow between populations.

Several statistics are used to characterise the differentiation landscape, each with inherent limitations. FST, the most widely used relative measure of differentiation, can generate false-positive signals: a reduction in within-population diversity (caused by linked selection) artificially inflates apparent differentiation. The absolute differentiation statistic dxy, by contrast, is not confounded by within-population diversity and more directly reflects reduced gene flow. As demonstrated in the meta-analysis by Cruickshank and Hahn, the frequent observation that FST peaks occur in regions with low dxy suggests that these putative islands are explained not by divergent selection but by reductions in within-population diversity driven by linked selection. Researchers should therefore interpret multiple statistics in parallel — FST, dxy, nucleotide diversity (π), Tajima's D, linkage disequilibrium, and haplotype statistics — rather than relying on FST alone.

Inference of demographic history is essential for interpreting speciation genomic studies. Population bottlenecks can generate heterogeneity in genomic differentiation by increasing the variance in coalescence times across the genome. Structural features of the genome — differences between sex chromosomes and autosomes, variation in recombination rate, and supergenes created by chromosomal inversions — also exert important influences on differentiation patterns. Sex chromosomes in particular have lower effective population sizes than autosomes and therefore show faster lineage sorting and elevated FST; there is a real risk of misinterpreting this as evidence for speciation genes. In flycatcher studies, the W chromosome showed near-complete lineage sorting (FST = 0.96–1.00) despite only moderate autosomal differentiation (FST = 0.27–0.40).

The review concludes by presenting best-practice guidelines for speciation genomics research: prioritise reference genome assembly and whole-genome individual resequencing; sample populations across multiple stages of divergence along the "speciation continuum"; use replicated designs with independent population pairs to validate parallel selection; explicitly estimate gene flow (using ABBA-BABA tests, admixture analysis, and isolation-with-migration models); incorporate recombination rate data; and, above all, evaluate alternative explanations from linked selection through neutral null models before designating high-FST regions as "speciation genes."

## Significance in Introduction Context

- The review clearly articulates the key conceptual point that genomic islands of differentiation may be products of linked selection (background selection or selective sweeps) rather than indicators of speciation gene locations — providing a methodological caution against the overuse of FST-based genomic scans.
- It offers practical guidance on distinguishing absolute differentiation statistics (dxy) from relative differentiation statistics (FST) in interpreting differentiation landscapes, and demonstrates that discordance between the two is a signature of linked selection rather than divergent selection.
- The review emphasises how essential the explicit estimation of gene flow is for interpreting speciation genomic studies, and uses the finding that most (70%) existing studies failed to do so as a critique that motivates the need for standardised study designs.
- Through concrete examples, it explains how sampling populations at multiple stages of divergence along the speciation continuum contributes to understanding the temporal dynamics of the evolutionary process.
- The review systematically organises the genomic structural features that must be considered in speciation genetics: sex chromosome effects, variation in recombination rate, inversions and supergenes, and the genetic architecture of barrier loci.

## Future Research Directions

- To overcome the limitations of FST outlier analysis, the development and standardisation of neutral null models that explicitly incorporate diversity reduction by linked selection is urgently needed. At present, most studies interpret FST outliers as evidence of selection without establishing such null models.
- Improved isolation-with-migration models capable of simultaneously inferring the directionality, magnitude, and timing of gene flow — together with integrated inference frameworks that jointly model natural selection, demography, and gene flow — are required.
- Speciation genomics research must be extended beyond its current concentration on teleost fishes, insects, and birds to encompass a broader diversity of taxa with widely varying effective population sizes, recombination rates, and reproductive strategies, in order to derive more general principles.
- Structural variant detection (inversions, translocations) via chromosome-scale genome assemblies and long-read sequencing should be integrated into standard speciation genomics workflows, enabling systematic evaluation of the role of recombination-suppressing structures in speciation.
- Multidisciplinary integration of population genomic data with ecological, behavioural, and developmental data — and in particular functional validation through selection experiments and transgenic confirmation — to establish the causal roles of candidate speciation genes should be expanded.
