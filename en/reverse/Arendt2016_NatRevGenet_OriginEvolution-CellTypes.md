# The origin and evolution of cell types

## Citation (NLM)
Arendt D, Musser JM, Baker CVH, Bergman A, Cepko C, Erwin DH, Pavlicev M, Schlosser G, Widder S, Laubichler MD, Wagner GP. The origin and evolution of cell types. Nat Rev Genet. 2016;17(12):744-757. doi:10.1038/nrg.2016.127

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg.2016.127](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg.2016.127)

## Summary

Cell types are the fundamental building blocks of multicellular organisms and have been classified phenotypically by morphology and function for centuries. In this paper, however, Arendt et al. argue that classification systems based on morphological and molecular similarity are fundamentally problematic from an evolutionary standpoint. Similarity can arise from homology (shared descent from a common ancestor), convergent evolution, or concerted evolution (coordinated change in genetic information shared across multiple cell types). Because these three processes carry different evolutionary meanings, a new definition capable of distinguishing among them is needed to accurately characterize cell type identity.

The authors therefore redefine cell types as sets of cells that evolve more closely with one another than with other cells in the organism — that is, as evolutionary units that co-evolve with some degree of independence from other cells. The conceptual core of this definition is that a cell type constitutes an **evolutionary unit**, made possible by the existence of genomic information used exclusively by that cell type. This cell type-specific genetic information encompasses both coding and non-coding sequences and determines cell type identity. The process by which new cell types arise is termed genetic individuation — the divergence of an initially homogeneous cell population into distinct sister cell types.

The molecular basis of cell type identity resides in the **core regulatory complex (CoRC)**, which comprises terminal selector transcription factors and their interactions. CoRCs enable and maintain cell type-specific gene expression programs. The spinal cord V2a interneuron and motor neuron provide an instructive example: both cell types share the transcription factors Lhx3 and NLI, but motor neurons additionally incorporate Isl1 into a hexameric complex, distinguishing the two fates. Differences in protein-protein interactions thus enable the switch-like repression of alternative cell fates.

During the evolution of new cell types, in addition to CoRC changes, cell type-specific cellular modules called apomeres emerge. Apomeres arise through either module integration or module divergence of pre-existing modules. The evolution of the neuronal synapse exemplifies module integration: adherens junction proteins, the secretory machinery, and receptor machinery were integrated to form the distinct apomeres of the pre- and postsynaptic terminals. In contrast, the vertebrate retinal phototransduction cascades of rod and cone photoreceptors exemplify module divergence from an ancestral pathway, with nearly all subunits duplicated and diverged following vertebrate whole-genome duplication.

Finally, the authors emphasize that developmental lineage and evolutionary lineage need not coincide. Cells of the same cell type may derive from entirely different developmental branches, and conversely a single developmental lineage may produce fundamentally different cell types. In the vertebrate retina, for example, cone and horizontal cells share a common progenitor yet belong to distinct evolutionary lineages. This dissociation of developmental from evolutionary lineage suggests that CoRC comparison is a more reliable criterion for inferring evolutionary relationships among cell types than morphological similarity or developmental lineage.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- **A new evolutionary definition of cell types:** By transcending morphological and functional classification and redefining cell types as evolutionary units, this work provides a conceptual foundation for studying cellular diversity. This definition becomes a key framework for phylogenetically classifying novel cell types discovered through high-throughput technologies such as single-cell RNA sequencing.
- **The importance of the CoRC concept:** As the molecular basis of cell type identity, the core regulatory complex illustrates how profoundly protein-protein interactions among transcription factor complexes govern the independent evolution and fate determination of cell types.
- **Distinguishing convergent evolution from homology:** The paper systematically explains why phenotypically similar cell types need not share a common ancestor, and proposes an evolutionary criterion — CoRC comparison — for making that distinction.
- **Dissociation of developmental and evolutionary lineages:** The argument that a cell type's developmental lineage and evolutionary lineage can differ clarifies the limits of inferring evolutionary relationships among cell types from developmental biology data alone, and underscores the necessity of comparing molecular (regulatory) signatures.
- **A roadmap for the single-cell omics era:** By providing a conceptual framework for interpreting the vast cellular type data generated by rapid advances in single-cell RNA-seq technology from an evolutionary perspective, this work furnishes the theoretical basis for comparative cell atlas studies that trace the origins and divergence of cell types.

## Future Research Directions

- **Construction of cross-species cell atlases:** Large-scale comparative studies using single-cell RNA-seq to profile the transcriptomes of cell types across diverse animal species and systematically map the cross-lineage conservation of CoRCs and regulatory signatures are needed.
- **Mechanistic dissection of CoRC evolution:** How CoRCs evolve during the emergence of new cell types — through transcription factor duplication and divergence, the appearance of novel protein-protein interactions, and related processes — must be elucidated through functional experiments and comparative genomics.
- **Systematic analysis of apomere evolution:** The process of apomere formation through module integration and divergence should be quantitatively analyzed in combination with protein interaction network data, and principles predicting which types of modules are prone to apomerization should be derived.
- **Characterizing general patterns of developmental-evolutionary lineage dissociation:** Systematically investigating how universal the dissociation between developmental and evolutionary lineages is across diverse tissues and animal phyla, and what rules it follows, will contribute to the evolutionary interpretation of stem cell lineage-tracing studies.
- **Evolutionary genetics of cell type number changes:** The genetic basis of not only the birth but also the loss and fusion of cell types should be characterized, and the quantitative relationship between cell type number and organismal complexity and ecological adaptation should be explored.
