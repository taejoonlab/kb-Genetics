# Wound-induced polyploidization is driven by Myc and supports tissue repair in the presence of DNA damage

## Citation (NLM)
Grendler J, Lowgren S, Mills M, Losick VP. Wound-induced polyploidization is driven by Myc and supports tissue repair in the presence of DNA damage. Development. 2019;146(20):dev173005. doi:10.1242/dev.173005

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.173005](https://doi.org/10.1242/dev.173005)

---

## Summary

Many tissues lack resident stem cell populations and must repair injury through alternative mechanisms. This study by Grendler et al. investigates wound-induced polyploidization (WIP) in the adult Drosophila abdominal epithelium, a post-mitotic tissue that permanently lacks the capacity for mitotic cell division. The central question is how injury triggers cells to grow by polyploidy rather than proliferation, and whether polyploid growth is advantageous when cell division is compromised by DNA damage.

The authors demonstrate that injury activates the Hippo pathway transcriptional co-activator Yorkie (Yki), which induces expression of Myc and E2f1. Both genes are necessary and sufficient for epithelial cells to enter S phase and initiate endoreplication (the endocycle) post-injury. Genetic knockdown of either Myc or E2f1 blocks wound-induced S-phase entry, while overexpression of either gene rescues the block caused by Yki knockdown. Strikingly, Myc overexpression alone — even in the absence of injury — is sufficient to drive 96% of adult epithelial nuclei to become polyploid (3C–32C), an effect confirmed under conditionally regulated (temperature-sensitive Gal80ts) expression. In contrast, overexpression of CycE or E2f1 without injury only modestly promotes S-phase entry. Myc is therefore identified as the most potent driver of endocycle induction in post-mitotic cells.

The decision between endoreplication and mitosis is governed by the expression state of the mitotic cell cycle machinery. In the adult fly epithelium, Fizzy-related (Fzr), an E3 ubiquitin ligase that targets mitotic cyclins (CycA, CycB, CycB3) for proteolytic degradation, is constitutively expressed, while CycA and CycB are permanently suppressed. This prevents cells from progressing through mitosis even when Yki-dependent Myc and E2f1 drive S-phase entry, channeling cell cycle re-entry exclusively into the endocycle. To test whether the mitotic cycle can be genetically restored, the authors simultaneously overexpressed the Cdc25 ortholog String (stg) and knocked down fzr. This combination (stg, fzrRNAi) was sufficient to induce CycB protein expression adjacent to the wound and to produce PH3+ (phospho-histone H3-positive) mitotic epithelial cells — demonstrating that switching to proliferation is genetically possible.

However, forcing mitotic cell division severely impairs wound repair. At 3 days post-injury (dpi), 62% of stg, fzrRNAi wounds showed defects in re-epithelialization, compared to 91% complete closure in controls. At 5 dpi, mitotically induced wounds displayed additional hallmarks of mitotic catastrophe: reduced nuclear size (~3.5-fold), micronuclei, and chromatin bridging events (~3 per wound, versus none in controls). The authors explain this by showing that the adult Drosophila epithelium constitutively accumulates low-level DNA damage (62% of epithelial nuclei display γH2Av foci), which can be exacerbated by UV irradiation. When DNA damage is elevated by UV treatment, 73% of WIP-competent control wounds still close by 3 dpi, while 92% of stg, fzrRNAi wounds remain open, with significant cell loss. The polyploid growth mode is therefore adaptive: endoreplication silences p53-responsive DNA damage checkpoint signaling, allowing cells to grow and repair despite pre-existing genomic damage that would trigger mitotic errors.

The study concludes that Myc is a potent and sufficient inducer of the endocycle even in post-mitotic cells, and that the constitutive expression of Fzr in the adult epithelium channels Yki-driven cell cycle re-entry exclusively into endoreplication. Polyploidy is an evolutionarily conserved adaptive wound repair strategy that permits tissue healing precisely when mitotic proliferation would cause further genomic instability.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- This study exemplifies reverse genetics in action: using UAS/Gal4-driven overexpression and RNAi knockdown, the authors systematically identify the sufficiency and necessity of individual cell cycle regulators (Myc, E2f1, CycE, Fzr, stg) in the context of a defined physiological process — wound-induced polyploidization.
- The demonstration that the same upstream signal (Yki) can drive either mitosis or endoreplication depending on the expression state of downstream cell cycle modulators (Fzr) illustrates how tissue-specific gene regulation — a reverse genetics concept — determines cell fate outcomes during repair.
- Wound-induced polyploidization as an adaptive strategy for DNA-damaged tissues reveals a previously unappreciated intersection between genome integrity mechanisms and tissue repair, with direct relevance to understanding post-mitotic tissue regeneration in vertebrates (kidney tubule epithelium, corneal endothelium, cardiomyocytes).
- The conditional Gal80ts system used to temporally control Myc expression demonstrates the power of Drosophila reverse genetics tools for dissecting gene function with precision that is difficult to achieve in mammalian models.
- The conservation of the Hippo-YAP pathway in regulating both proliferation and polyploidization across species (Drosophila Yki / mammalian YAP) suggests that findings from this reverse-genetic Drosophila model may be directly translatable to understanding polyploid repair responses in mammalian tissues.

## Future Research Directions

- Identifying the specific Myc transcriptional targets in post-mitotic epithelial cells that release cell cycle quiescence and initiate endoreplication would reveal how a single oncogene can reprogram differentiated cells into a growth state without triggering apoptosis.
- Determining whether the WIP-associated silencing of p53-responsive genes is a direct consequence of endoreplication-induced chromatin changes (as shown for Drosophila imaginal disc cells by Hassel et al.) or involves an independent mechanism in adult epithelium would clarify the molecular basis of DNA damage tolerance in polyploid cells.
- Investigating whether the mammalian corneal endothelium, kidney tubule cells, and cardiomyocytes use analogous Myc-Hippo-YAP signaling axes to control endoreplication versus proliferation during injury would establish the translational relevance of these findings.
- Assessing the long-term consequences of WIP on tissue homeostasis, including whether persistent polyploidy in repaired epithelium affects susceptibility to subsequent genotoxic or oncogenic insults, is essential before polyploidy can be considered a beneficial intervention target.
- Exploring whether cell fusion (the second mechanism contributing to WIP) is regulated by the same Yki-Myc axis or by a parallel injury-induced signaling pathway would complete the mechanistic picture of polyploidization during wound healing.
