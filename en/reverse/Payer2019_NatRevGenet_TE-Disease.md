# Transposable Elements in Human Genetic Disease

## Citation (NLM)
Payer LM, Burns KH. Transposable elements in human genetic disease. Nat Rev Genet. 2019 Dec;20(12):760-772. doi:10.1038/s41576-019-0165-8

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41576-019-0165-8](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41576-019-0165-8)

## Summary

Transposable elements (TEs) are repetitive sequences comprising approximately half of the human genome, with retrotransposons representing the most prevalent active form. The only currently autonomous retrotransposon active in the human genome is LINE-1 (L1), which accounts for approximately 17% of the genome. A full-length L1 element is 6 kb long and encodes an RNA-binding protein (ORF1p) and ORF2p, which carries endonuclease and reverse transcriptase activities. L1 inserts into new genomic locations via target-primed reverse transcription (TPRT). Non-autonomous retrotransposons — Alu elements (~11% of the genome) and SVA elements — mobilize by co-opting the proteins encoded by L1. Endogenous retroviruses (ERVs) are largely inactivated in contemporary humans, though some insertion polymorphisms continue to segregate in populations.

TE insertions generate a distinctive class of genetic variation. Although similar to copy number variants (CNVs) in size, TE insertions are qualitatively distinct in that they introduce functional elements inherent to the TE sequence itself — including promoters, splicing regulatory sequences, and secondary structures. Over 16,000 polymorphic TEs exist in the human genome, accounting for approximately 24% of known structural variants. More than 6,500 of these are common variants with a minor allele frequency (MAF) ≥ 0.01 and are thus likely to contribute to complex disease risk. TE expression is suppressed in normal somatic cells through heterochromatinization and DNA methylation, but in many cancers L1 promoter hypomethylation, elevated ORF1p protein expression, and somatic retrotransposition are observed.

The mechanisms by which TE insertions cause monogenic disease are diverse. The most direct mechanism is insertion into a coding exon, producing a frameshift mutation or premature stop codon — the paradigmatic example being the first reported de novo L1 insertion causing hemophilia A. TEs can also directly disrupt splice acceptor or donor sequences, alter splicing patterns through intronic insertion (e.g., Alu-mediated exon skipping in the NF1 gene), or donate their own splice signals to drive exonization (e.g., an L1 insertion within RB1). More complex scenarios include TPRT-mediated insertions accompanied by large flanking deletions (an SVA triggering an 867 kb deletion encompassing NF1) and endonuclease-independent retrotransposition (insertion at sites of DNA damage).

At the population level, polymorphic TEs are also significant contributors to disease. An SVA insertion in the 3' UTR of the FKTN gene accounts for 80% of Fukuyama congenital muscular dystrophy alleles in the Japanese population, and an SVA insertion within an intron of TAF1 causes X-linked dystonia-parkinsonism in the Filipino population. Common polymorphic TEs are enriched near GWAS signals, and some function as expression quantitative trait loci (eQTLs) or modulate splicing, thereby modifying complex disease risk. For example, an Alu insertion near the CD58 gene is associated with multiple sclerosis risk.

Ancient, fixed TE insertions can serve as substrates for non-allelic homologous recombination, causing deletions, duplications, and rearrangements. Alu-Alu recombination alone has been implicated in more than 70 monogenic diseases, and L1-L1 recombination has caused deletions of more than 40 kb encompassing COL4A5 and COL4A6 in Alport syndrome. This review emphasizes that TEs contribute to disease not only through the insertions themselves but also, across long evolutionary timescales, by acting as recombination substrates.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Although approximately half of the human genome is composed of TE-derived sequences, the genetic variants they generate have been largely overlooked in conventional SNP-centric analyses; this review repositions TEs as a major source of functional genetic variation.
- TE insertions are qualitatively distinct from generic CNVs in that they introduce functional modules — promoters, splice signals, secondary structures — along with the inserted sequence, and this distinction is made explicit.
- Since the first report in hemophilia A in 1988, the diverse mechanisms by which TE insertions cause monogenic disease (exonic insertion, splice site disruption, exonization, large accompanying deletions) have been systematically catalogued, making this review a key reference for clinical genetics education.
- The activation of L1 expression and somatic retrotransposition in cancer, together with the possibility of linking TE biology to immunotherapy through the "viral mimicry" phenomenon, highlights the interface between TE biology and oncology.
- The review provides a methodological recommendation that TE insertion variants must be considered in GWAS follow-up studies, and offers the prospect that advances in long-read sequencing technology will improve the accuracy of TE variant detection.

## Future Research Directions

- Expanding the clinical application of long-read sequencing technology is needed to more accurately characterize the frequency and spectrum of de novo TE insertions, and to develop technical approaches that integrate TE variant detection modules into current clinical exome analysis pipelines.
- The relationship between L1 expression activation and the "viral mimicry" phenomenon in cancer, and how these connect to immunotherapy response and ADAR1-dependent synthetic lethality, must be elucidated in order to explore strategies for exploiting TEs as novel anti-cancer therapeutic targets.
- Systematic eQTL and sQTL analyses are needed to demonstrate that common polymorphic TEs are functional variants underlying GWAS signals, and to quantify their contribution to complex disease risk.
- Research into the role of altered TE expression in autoimmune, inflammatory, and neurodegenerative diseases is urgently needed, with particular attention to the mechanisms of TE de-repression during normal aging and its physiological consequences.
- Development of single-locus TE expression analysis techniques — methods that exploit unique flanking sequences for locus-specific analysis — is required to accurately measure autonomous transcription at specific loci rather than aggregating expression across entire TE subfamilies.
