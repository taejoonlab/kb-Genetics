# Sex chromosome evolution: historical insights and future perspectives

## Citation (NLM)
Abbott JK, Nordén AK, Hansson B. Sex chromosome evolution: historical insights and future perspectives. Proc R Soc B. 2017;284(1854):20162806. doi:10.1098/rspb.2016.2806

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1098/rspb.2016.2806](https://doi.org/10.1098/rspb.2016.2806)

## Summary

Abbott, Nordén, and Hansson present a comprehensive historical timeline of sex chromosome evolution research, tracing the field from its origins in the late 1800s through the genomic era. The review is structured around a curated chronological table covering more than a century of theoretical and empirical advances, beginning with Henking's 1891 discovery of "odd" chromosomes and culminating in multilocus models and long-read sequencing approaches of the 2010s. By situating modern findings within this historical arc, the authors argue that current understanding has been significantly shaped — and in some respects biased — by early focus on highly diverged mammalian and Drosophila systems.

The standard model of sex chromosome evolution begins with a pair of autosomes acquiring a major sex-determining function, followed by recombination suppression that evolves to keep sex-specific alleles in linkage. As the non-recombining region expands — driven by the accumulation of sexually antagonistic genes — the Y (or W) chromosome degenerates through Muller's ratchet and other mutational mechanisms. The homogametic sex (XX or ZZ) then faces dosage imbalance, which is resolved through dosage compensation mechanisms that vary widely across taxa: near-complete X inactivation in mammals, X hyperexpression in Drosophila, and only partial compensation in birds and snakes. The review emphasizes that Y degeneration is not inevitable; many organisms maintain surprisingly homomorphic sex chromosomes over long evolutionary timescales.

Sex chromosome turnovers — events in which a new autosome pair acquires sex-determining function — are highlighted as far more common than previously appreciated, particularly in fishes and amphibians. These turnovers can occur through autosomal gain of a new sex-determining gene, transposition of a sex-determining locus, or neo-sex chromosome formation via autosome-sex chromosome fusions. Occasional recombination between X and Y chromosomes in sex-reversed individuals can introduce new genetic variation onto the Y and slow differentiation, but this mechanism operates only in species with incompletely differentiated sex chromosomes. The "hot-potato" model proposes that cycling turnovers may be driven in part by the fitness costs that sexually antagonistic variation imposes, creating selective pressure to reset the sex chromosome system.

The two most influential theoretical frameworks in the field are Charlesworth and Charlesworth's (1978) model of dioecy evolution from hermaphroditism and Rice's (1987) model of sexual antagonism as the selective force driving recombination suppression in species with already-separated sexes. Abbott et al. argue that both models remain valid but were developed with a narrow focus that can obscure the diversity of sex chromosome systems in nature. They call for greater crosstalk with speciation biology, which has developed complementary tools for linking short-term ecological processes with long-term chromosomal divergence patterns.

Looking forward, the authors identify three major gaps: insufficient attention to ecological and demographic factors in sex chromosome evolution, the transformative but still underexploited potential of long-read sequencing and chromosome conformation technologies for assembling difficult Y/W regions, and the need for multilocus theoretical models. Drawing on an analogy from the speciation literature, they suggest that sex chromosome divergence may follow nonlinear, threshold dynamics once enough selected loci and sufficient linkage disequilibrium accumulate — fundamentally different from the stepwise divergence assumed in single-locus models.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides a comprehensive historical timeline that contextualizes current models of sex chromosome evolution within over 120 years of theoretical and empirical development
- Demonstrates that field-wide bias toward mammalian XY systems has obscured the true diversity of sex chromosome systems, including homomorphic, UV, and rapidly turning-over systems
- Formalizes the two central theoretical pillars of the field — Charlesworth & Charlesworth (1978) on dioecy and Rice (1987) on sexual antagonism — and explains how they complement rather than compete with each other
- Highlights that Y chromosome degeneration and loss is not universal, which has important implications for interpreting genome-wide sex-biased expression data
- Calls for multilocus models and experimental approaches (including CRISPR-based manipulation of sex-determining loci) as the next frontier in understanding why only some sex chromosomes differentiate extensively

## Future Research Directions

- Incorporate ecological and demographic factors (local adaptation, population structure, range shifts) into models of sex chromosome evolution, which has been largely neglected relative to purely genetic models
- Expand the taxonomic scope of comparative studies using affordable short-read and transformative long-read sequencing to characterize sex chromosomes in non-model organisms across diverse states of differentiation
- Develop multilocus theoretical models of sex chromosome divergence, inspired by speciation literature, to test whether divergence follows stepwise or threshold (nonlinear) dynamics
- Apply CRISPR/Cas9 and experimental evolution to directly test causal steps in sex chromosome formation, including the build-up of sexually antagonistic variation on proto-sex chromosomes in hermaphroditic systems
- Investigate the role of chromosome conformation (Capture Hi-C), full-length isoform sequencing, and epigenomic methods in resolving outstanding questions about dosage compensation mechanisms and sex chromosome–autosome interactions
