# Two Lessons From the Interface of Genetics and Medicine

## Citation (NLM)
Allison AC. Two lessons from the interface of genetics and medicine. Genetics. 2004;166(4):1591-1599.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/166/4/1591](https://genetics.org/content/166/4/1591)

---

## Summary

In this Perspectives essay, Anthony C. Allison recounts, fifty years after his landmark 1954 publications, two lessons drawn from his own career at the interface of genetics and medicine. The first lesson is the discovery that sickle-cell heterozygotes are resistant to malaria — a textbook example of balanced polymorphism maintained by heterozygote advantage. Growing up in Kenya and trained at Oxford after World War II in the then-novel idea that natural selection acts through changes in gene frequencies (the population genetics of Fisher, Haldane, and Wright), Allison investigated inherited blood characteristics in East African tribes. He found strikingly high sickle-cell trait frequencies (20–30%) near the Kenyan coast and Lake Victoria, but only ~1% in the intervening highlands, cutting across linguistic and cultural boundaries. Because homozygous (SS) individuals rarely survived to reproduce, such high frequencies could not be maintained by mutation. Allison's "flash of inspiration" was that intense transmission of *Plasmodium falciparum* in the low-lying regions could give AS heterozygotes a survival advantage, producing a stable polymorphism.

Allison tested this with three complementary strategies, all published in 1954. First, experimental inoculation of *P. falciparum* showed lower parasite rates and densities in AS than in AA individuals. Second, and more compellingly, in a rural Ugandan population without antimalarial drugs, high parasite counts were nearly four times as frequent in AA as in AS children. Third, a theoretical analysis showed the fitness advantage required to maintain the observed frequencies (about 26% at maximal 40% AS, or ~10% at 20% AS). A survey of nearly 5000 East Africans confirmed that high sickle-cell frequencies were confined to hyperendemic malarious areas. Initial skepticism (from Beutler and from Foy) was later overcome by studies in many African populations, culminating in Hill et al.'s well-controlled Gambia data showing ~90% protection of AS children against severe malaria. Allison connects this to the broader picture: other hemoglobin variants (HbC, HbE, β-thalassemia) and G6PD deficiency are also polymorphic in malarious regions and confer protection; haplotype analysis of the β-globin region shows the HbS mutation arose independently at least five times; and the framework of extended haplotype homozygosity (Sabeti et al.) now allows genome-wide scanning for recent positive selection — showing that this 50-year-old finding still informs current genetic problems. The general implication is that disease is a powerful agent of natural selection, and many human polymorphisms are shaped by it, alongside neutral variation (Wright, Kimura).

The second lesson concerns the application of human genetics to define a therapeutic target — a program Allison argues was among the first of its kind. The story begins with inherited enzyme deficiencies of purine metabolism. Eloise Giblett found that children with adenosine deaminase (ADA) deficiency suffered a combined T- and B-lymphocyte immunodeficiency with normal mental development, whereas Lesch-Nyhan patients (lacking HGPRT) had severe neurological disease but essentially normal immune function. This dissociation revealed which purine pathways matter for lymphocytes versus brain, and suggested that an inhibitor of *de novo* guanosine nucleotide synthesis would be selectively immunosuppressive. Allison and Elsie Eugui pursued this at Syntex from 1982, selecting mycophenolic acid (MPA), a reversible inhibitor of inosine-5'-monophosphate dehydrogenase (IMPDH). MPA proved lymphocyte-selective — because proliferating lymphocytes depend on *de novo* synthesis and because the type II IMPDH isoform expressed in activated lymphocytes is more sensitive — and also blocked glycosylation of adhesion molecules. The ester prodrug mycophenolate mofetil (MMF; CellCept) was approved by the FDA in 1995 and became a widely used, effective drug for preventing organ-graft rejection, with annual sales exceeding $1 billion. Allison frames this alongside imatinib (Gleevec) as proof of principle for using genetics to identify molecular targets.

The essay closes with reflective, literary meditations (quoting Shakespeare, Kennedy, and Kipling) on the practice of science: not becoming attached to pet ideas, the pattern by which new ideas are first dismissed ("it isn't true") then reattributed to famous names ("it isn't new" — as the malaria hypothesis was retrospectively credited to Haldane), and the value of an "occasional backward glance." Allison laments the collective amnesia by which few scientists read papers more than five years old, and celebrates that recounting the history of sickle-cell research is now used in science education.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the canonical example of balanced polymorphism / heterozygote advantage (sickle-cell trait and malaria), directly illustrating how selection maintains genetic variation in populations.
- Demonstrates the population-genetics logic (allele frequencies, fitness, Hardy-Weinberg predictions of homozygote frequency) applied to a real human medical problem.
- Shows the molecular basis of a single-gene disorder (the GAG→GTG, Glu→Val substitution in β-globin) and how independent origins of the same mutation can be traced by haplotype analysis.
- Illustrates translational genetics: how understanding inherited enzyme deficiencies can rationally define a drug target (IMPDH → mycophenolate mofetil), bridging Mendelian disease and therapeutics.

## Future Research Directions

- Genome-wide scanning for signatures of recent positive selection (via extended haplotype homozygosity) to identify additional disease-driven human polymorphisms.
- Extending the "genetics-defines-the-target" paradigm to discover new molecular-targeted therapies, building on the successes of mycophenolate mofetil and imatinib.
