# Beyond Mendel: Oligogenic Inheritance in Human Genetic Disease

## Citation (NLM)
Badano JL, Katsanis N. Beyond Mendel: an evolving view of human genetic disease transmission. Nat Rev Genet. 2002;3(10):779-788. doi:10.1038/nrg910

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg910](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg910)

---

## Background

The single-gene Mendelian model has long dominated the understanding of inherited disease. However, clinical genetics increasingly encounters diseases with inheritance patterns that cannot be explained by mutations in a single gene. This review introduces "oligogenic inheritance" — inheritance involving 2 to several interacting loci — as the intermediate territory between monogenic Mendelian diseases and highly polygenic complex diseases. The authors use PKU, cystic fibrosis, retinitis pigmentosa (RP), Bardet-Biedl syndrome (BBS), and Hirschsprung disease (HSCR) as clinical examples to explain digenic inheritance, triallelic inheritance, and modifier gene models.

---

## Key Experiment Methods

1. Clinical pedigree analysis of families showing non-Mendelian inheritance patterns
2. Linkage analysis and candidate gene approaches to molecularly characterize atypical inheritance
3. Loss-of-function experiments in mouse models to validate gene-gene interactions
4. BBS family studies: analysis of how a third mutation in BBS2 or BBS4 affects penetrance when BBS6 carries two mutations
5. Quantitative complementation assays to genetically demonstrate modifier gene effects

---

## Results

- **Digenic inheritance**: RP demonstrates the first clear case — double heterozygosity at ROM1 and RDS/peripherin loci is required for disease phenotype; neither mutation alone is sufficient
- **Triallelic inheritance**: BBS patients carrying two mutations in BBS6 plus a heterozygous mutation in BBS2 or BBS4 show increased penetrance; this 2+1 allele model is unique in human genetics
- **Modifier gene effects**: In HSCR, the primary RET mutation's expressivity is modulated by EDNRB and other loci
- **Dosage model**: Many oligogenic diseases are explained by total functional gene product falling below a threshold
- **Poison model**: In some cases, a mutant product gains toxic function that suppresses other genes, rather than simply reducing dosage

---

## Perspective

This review clearly exposes the limits of the single-gene paradigm in clinical genetics. The oligogenic inheritance framework provides a powerful model for explaining intrafamilial variability and incomplete penetrance. In the era of GWAS and whole-genome sequencing, understanding this oligogenic territory is essential for precision medicine. The modifier gene concept is increasingly important for explaining patients with the same causal mutation but different clinical outcomes.
