# The Sustained Impact of Model Organisms—in Genetics and Epigenetics

## Summary

This commentary by Nancy Bonini and Shelley Berger, published in the journal *Genetics*, argues that model organisms remain indispensable tools in genetics and epigenetics research, even in the era of human genome sequencing.

**Background:** With the advent of inexpensive DNA sequencing, disease-relevant mutations can be identified directly in human patients. This raises the question of whether classical model organisms—Drosophila, yeast, C. elegans, zebrafish—are still necessary. The authors, whose expertise lies in Drosophila and yeast, answer emphatically: yes, they remain essential.

**Historical impact of model organism genetics:** Unbiased genetic screens in model organisms revealed fundamental biological processes that earned Nobel Prizes: cell cycle control (Nasmyth 2001), body plan specification (Lewis 1978; Nüsslein-Volhard and Wieschaus 1980), and programmed cell death (Ellis and Horvitz 1986; Avery and Horvitz 1987). Genome sequencing then demonstrated the breadth of gene conservation, enabling reverse genetics approaches in which human disease gene homologs are studied in simpler systems.

**Expanding genetic approaches:** Genome-scale unbiased screens are now possible. Libraries of RNAi reagents allow knockdown of every C. elegans gene; yeast knockout and overexpression libraries cover the entire genome; and shRNA transgene libraries have been developed for Drosophila. These approaches extend to cell-culture "disease in a dish" models, which complement but do not replace in vivo studies.

**Epigenetics in model organisms:** The concept of epigenetics traces to Waddington (1957), but the modern molecular concept—heritable phenotypic change without DNA sequence alteration—emerged directly from model organism research. Position effect variegation in Drosophila (Muller 1930) and telomere position effect in yeast (Gottschling 1990) were early demonstrations. The major chromatin-modifying enzymes—histone acetyltransferases, deacetylases, ATP-dependent remodelers—were discovered through the combination of biochemistry and genetic screens. Chromatin is now understood as an active regulator of all DNA transactions, not merely a scaffold.

**Future directions proposed:** (1) "Better" models using in situ endogenous gene modification rather than transgenic overexpression; (2) validating candidate variants from human sequencing in model system functional assays; (3) incorporating disease risk factors (aging, environmental toxins, gut microbiome) into in vivo disease models; (4) identifying druggable pathways in animal models. The authors particularly emphasize that epigenetics in disease, behavior, and transgenerational inheritance will require rigorous genetic analysis in model organisms.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the conceptual bridge connecting classical model organism genetics to modern human genomics
- Illustrates the logic of forward genetics: phenotype-first screens in organisms like Drosophila and C. elegans reveal pathways later shown to be relevant to human biology
- Offers motivation for studying genetics: discoveries in "simple" organisms have repeatedly led to Nobel Prizes and medical breakthroughs

## Future Research Directions

- Genetic dissection of gut microbiome–host interactions using Drosophila and C. elegans as tractable in vivo systems
- Rigorous mechanistic demonstration of transgenerational epigenetic inheritance through model organism genetics
- Precision in situ disease modeling using CRISPR to introduce exact human variant alleles into endogenous model organism loci
