## Summary

This Perspectives essay by Sydney Brenner—Nobel Laureate in Physiology or Medicine 2002—is a first-person historical account of the founding of C. elegans genetics, published in *Genetics* as an anecdotal and critical commentary.

**The birth of C. elegans genetics:** In early October 1967, Brenner performed the first mutant hunt using EMS mutagenesis, yielding a grand total of two mutants. The first, E1, was named "dumpy" for its distinctive body shape; the second, E2, was termed "variable abnormal." Brenner quickly improved his phenotype-recognition skills, eventually obtaining 25–30 mutants per screen. He confirms Mendelian segregation within two weeks by backcrossing the first mutant to wild-type N2 males and observing the classic 1:3 ratio in heterozygotes.

**C. elegans as a diploid organism:** The genetics of diploids was novel to most molecular biologists of the time, who were accustomed only to haploid phage and bacterial genetics. The self-fertilizing hermaphrodite, with males used to transport genes between hermaphrodites, made genetic manipulation simple. John Sulston's method for freezing C. elegans was the technical prerequisite that enabled serious long-term genetic research.

**The 1974 paper and its legacy:** The landmark 1974 Genetics paper reported 300 EMS-induced mutants and a map of approximately 100 genes on six linkage groups. Notably, it included a full account of hermaphrodite genetics methods—material that today would be relegated to supplementary information—which explains why it continues to be ritually cited in virtually every C. elegans genetics paper, giving a 35-year-old article an unexpectedly long shelf life. The paper's introduction also outlined a reductionist research program for understanding the C. elegans nervous system.

**Laboratory culture at the MRC:** Brenner vividly describes the work culture at the MRC Laboratory in Cambridge: morning coffee, afternoon seminars, pub visits, and actual experiments that only began after dinner, with the lab filling up with "owls" (night workers). During the six years before the first C. elegans genetics paper, Brenner and colleagues also pursued electron microscopy of muscle mutants, computational approaches to nervous system reconstruction, and molecular biology of myosin—a breadth that explains the delay.

**Leaving C. elegans:** Brenner explains that he left C. elegans research precisely when it was getting interesting: the people who came after him were simply much better. He quotes J. D. Bernal's chess analogy—that in science, as in chess, it is the opening game that is most exciting, and only a few can play the end game. By the time Brenner left, there were already 400 C. elegans laboratories worldwide, growing at a rate of roughly one per week.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the essential historical narrative of how a "joke organism" became one of the most powerful genetic model systems in biology
- Illustrates the criteria for choosing a model organism: small size, rapid life cycle, ease of handling, and genetic tractability
- First-person testimony of how Mendelian genetics, originally demonstrated in plants, was extended to complex multicellular animals
