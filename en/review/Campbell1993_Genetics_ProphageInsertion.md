# Thirty Years Ago in Genetics: Prophage Insertion Into Bacterial Chromosomes

## Citation (NLM)
Campbell AM. Thirty years ago in Genetics: prophage insertion into bacterial chromosomes. Genetics. 1993;133(3):433-438.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/133/3/433](https://genetics.org/content/133/3/433)

---

## Summary

Allan Campbell revisits, thirty years on, his 1961 model for how bacteriophage λ prophage integrates into the *E. coli* chromosome during lysogeny. He developed the model while writing a review on episomes, drawing on Calef and Licciardello's work on prophage gene order, his own studies of λgal specialized transducing phages, and Frank Stahl's ideas about chromosome circularity. The Campbell model proposed that the ends of the linear phage genome join to form a circle, and that a single reciprocal crossover between a phage sequence and a homologous chromosomal site inserts the prophage — neatly explaining the observed cyclic permutation of gene order in the integrated prophage relative to the free phage.

Campbell frames the discovery within a larger question that was genuinely open before 1962: is the chromosome a single unidimensional (linear or circular) molecule, and are added elements like proviruses integral covalent parts of it? At the time, physical studies could not exclude alternative models in which DNA genes were attached laterally to a protein backbone, joined by protein connectors, or arranged as parallel double helices held by protein bonds — the last of which could accommodate side-by-side synapsis of a prophage. Genetic arguments settled the issue before physical proof (restriction mapping, sequencing) was available, and λ was the first element to which such arguments were applied. The goal was to build an integrated genetic map of a chromosome containing an added element; the prerequisites were a phage marked with many mutations and flanking chromosomal markers (the *gal* and *bio* operons). Hfr crosses, P1 transductions, and nested deletions penetrating the prophage all supported linear intercalation.

A key methodological thread is Campbell's serendipitous 1958 discovery of conditional-lethal mutants of λ: two small-plaque mutants formed plaques on strain C600 but not W3350, giving host-specific "suppressible" mutants ideal for mapping. This paralleled Dick Epstein's amber mutants of phage T4 (which plated on some K-12 strains but not *E. coli* B), and Bob Edgar's temperature-sensitive mutants — together establishing conditional-lethal mutations as a general tool. Campbell notes that these rediscoveries of a principle first seen in *Neurospora* were spurred by Benzer's fine-structure work, which taught geneticists how to classify and analyze such mutants constructively.

The essay then traces the model's validation and enrichment over decades. Direct evidence for end-joining by cohesive ("sticky") ends came from Kaiser and Wu (1968); heteroduplex analysis of λgal DNA showed phage and bacterial DNA are covalently joined with no non-DNA linkers; and junction fragments from λgal/λbio phages eventually defined the insertion event at sequence resolution (Landy and Ross 1977). Genetic control proved richer than expected: the crossover does not require RecA (that discovery instead revealed RecA's role in repressor destruction during SOS), extensive homology alone did not drive insertion, and a phage gene *int* (integrase) plus a second gene *xis* (needed with Int for excision), together with host factors IHF and Fis, were required. Enzymology showed Int is a site-specific recombinase acting via a transient tyrosine-DNA bond, generating a Holliday junction with branch migration across a 7-bp overlap; the reaction needs neither external energy nor repair synthesis, using a 21-bp attB and a 235-bp supercoiled attP. Regulation differentially controls *int* (stimulated by CII via the PI promoter, favoring lysogeny) versus coordinate *int-xis* expression from pL (favoring excision), with the *sib* element and RNase III adding further refinement — making λ insertion the first well-characterized programmed DNA rearrangement. Campbell closes by noting the model's broad influence (it inspired the correct idea that gene rearrangement underlies immunoglobulin diversity, and shaped early thinking on transposons and retroviral integration) and the evolutionary questions raised by lambdoid phages with varied insertion specificities, placing λ integrase within a large integrase family of site-specific recombinases spanning prokaryotes and eukaryotes.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- The Campbell model is a textbook cornerstone for teaching lysogeny, site-specific recombination, and the lysis/lysogeny decision in temperate phages.
- Demonstrates how elegant genetic logic (crossover geometry, deletion mapping, marker rescue) established chromosome structure and integration before physical/molecular proof existed.
- Introduces conditional-lethal mutants (amber, temperature-sensitive) — a foundational genetic tool whose parallel discovery in λ and T4 makes a memorable history-of-science case.
- Connects a bacterial-phage phenomenon to major later ideas: V(D)J recombination, transposition, retroviral integration, and the integrase superfamily.

## Future Research Directions

- The λ integrase family remains a model for site-specific recombination and a workhorse of genetic engineering (e.g., Gateway cloning, phage-based genome integration systems).
- Evolutionary questions Campbell raises — how integration specificities change, frequency-dependent selection among lambdoid phages, and the tRNA-insertion origin of the integrase family — continue to inform studies of mobile genetic elements and host-parasite coevolution.
