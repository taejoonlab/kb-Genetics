# The Human Genome Diversity Project: past, present and future

## Citation (NLM)

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg1579](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg1579)

## Summary

The Human Genome Diversity Project (HGDP) is a large-scale resource initiative aimed at systematically studying human genetic diversity worldwide. Cavalli-Sforza reviews its history, current status, and future directions.

**Background and founding**: During the early 1990s, as the Human Genome Project sequenced one representative genome, it became clear that a broader study of worldwide human genetic variation was needed. Prompted by discussions among population geneticists and facilitated by HUGO president Walter Bodmer, Cavalli-Sforza chaired a feasibility committee for what became the HGDP. NIH, NSF, and the US Department of Energy co-sponsored organizing symposia from 1991 to 1994.

**Ethical challenges**: In 1994, concerns arose that DNA from indigenous populations might be exploited commercially ("bio-piracy") or that the data might fuel "scientific racism." The NAS/NRC convened an independent committee that in late 1997 approved the project with conditions emphasizing informed consent and strict ethical oversight. The HGDP has consistently opposed DNA patenting and limited sample access to non-profit laboratories.

**Collection design**: The HGDP chose lymphoblastoid cell lines (LCLs) over raw DNA because LCLs are renewable, replicable, and avoid the errors inherent in repeated PCR amplification. By the time of the April 2002 public announcement, the collection comprised 1,064 cell lines from 52 populations across all continents, stored at CEPH in Paris. All samples represent populations predating the diasporas of the 15th–16th centuries, minimizing admixture from historical mass migrations.

**Key findings from HGDP data**: The first published study using HGDP samples (Rosenberg et al., 2002) genotyped 1,056 individuals from 52 populations for 377 autosomal microsatellite loci. STRUCTURE analysis revealed clear population clustering: K=5 clusters correspond to sub-Saharan Africa, Europe/western Asia, eastern Asia, Oceania, and the Americas — consistent with the out-of-Africa expansion ~50,000 years ago. Critically, Fst analysis showed that only 5–7% of worldwide genetic variation is accounted for by differences among the 52 populations; 93–95% of variation resides within populations. This is larger than the ~85% within-population estimate from earlier protein data, partly due to microsatellite marker properties.

**HGDP and HapMap**: The HapMap Project targets three populations (Utah Europeans, Nigerian Yoruba, East Asians) to characterize linkage disequilibrium (LD) blocks for GWAS of complex diseases. HGDP and HapMap are complementary: HapMap LD patterns vary across world populations (African populations have shorter LD blocks due to longer evolutionary history), and HGDP population sampling can help extend HapMap tags to broader populations. Non-African populations descend from a bottlenecked founding group ~50,000 years ago, resulting in extended LD blocks not found in Africa.

**Biomedical applications**: Beyond evolutionary questions, the HGDP collection provides control samples for association studies. Hardy–Weinberg analysis of 50 individuals per population can estimate the incidence of recessive diseases. The collection can also help disentangle environmental and genetic contributions to complex diseases.

**Future limitations**: The current collection is geographically uneven: India, Polynesia, and northern Asia are unrepresented or underrepresented. The average sample size of ~20 individuals per population limits statistical power. Debate continues on whether future sampling should shift from population-based to individual-based strategies.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Demonstrates how large-scale human population genetics resources are organized and why systematic sampling strategy matters for drawing valid evolutionary conclusions
- Introduces the fundamental finding that most human genetic variation (>93%) exists within populations, not between them — directly refuting genetic definitions of race
- Presents the ethical framework surrounding genetic research on indigenous populations, an essential consideration in any modern genetics curriculum

## Future Research Directions

- Expanding the HGDP collection to include underrepresented regions (India, Polynesia, northern Asia) with larger per-population samples to improve statistical power for both evolutionary and biomedical analyses
- Combining HGDP population structure information with HapMap LD block data to develop globally applicable tag SNP strategies for genome-wide association studies across all world populations
- Integrating crossover history analysis with mutation-based phylogeographic reconstruction to produce a richer, more complete picture of modern human migration history
