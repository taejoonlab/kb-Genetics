# Darwin and Genetics

## Summary

This Perspectives essay by Brian and Deborah Charlesworth (Institute of Evolutionary Biology, University of Edinburgh) critically examines the historical intersection of Darwin's theory of natural selection and the science of genetics, demonstrating both why the synthesis was long delayed and how modern genomics has finally enabled answers to questions Darwin first asked.

**Darwin's fundamental weakness:** Darwin's theory of natural selection was logically incomplete because he lacked an adequate model of inheritance. In a remarkable irony, Darwin had obtained experimental results—in crossing experiments on Primula distyly—that show clear Mendelian 1:1 and 3:1 ratios, but he was unable to interpret them correctly because he had no framework of particulate inheritance. He was also unaware of Mendel's 1866 paper despite its publication two years before Darwin's *Variation of Animals and Plants Under Domestication*.

**The blending inheritance problem:** Darwin, like most of his contemporaries, implicitly assumed blending inheritance. The engineer Fleeming Jenkin pointed out the fatal consequence: under blending, genetic variance decays by half each generation, making it impossible for natural selection to produce permanent change. Darwin's response was his theory of pangenesis—hypothetical "gemmules" carrying information about acquired characters to germ cells—essentially a form of Lamarckian inheritance. Darwin himself recognized its insufficiency: the adaptive characteristics of sterile castes in social insects cannot be explained by inheritance of characters acquired during an individual's lifetime.

**The Mendelian resolution and modern evolutionary genetics:** As Fisher explained in *The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection* (1930), all difficulties posed by blending inheritance vanish with particulate inheritance: variation within a population is conserved rather than lost in the absence of evolutionary forces, a genetic analog of Galileo's law of inertia. The Mendelian basis of quantitative trait variation was established by early 20th-century experiments—Johannsen's pure line experiments, Nilsson-Ehle's and East's F2 segregation experiments—showing that apparent blending in F1 crosses reflects segregation of multiple Mendelian factors.

**Modern tools for answering Darwin's questions:** DNA sequencing technology now enables population-level tests of natural selection across entire genomes. Neutral theory (Kimura) provides the essential null hypothesis. Methods including selective sweep detection, linkage disequilibrium analysis, and comparative genomics are providing answers to questions Darwin first asked: the genetic basis of inbreeding depression, hybrid vigor, reproductive isolation, and mating system evolution. The same experimental designs Darwin used for inbreeding depression studies—paired contrasts of selfed and outcrossed progeny—anticipated the modern paired comparison approach.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Essential historical narrative for understanding why the Darwin-Mendel synthesis was delayed for more than 30 years after Mendel's rediscovery
- Explains the logical flaw in blending inheritance and why particulate genetics was necessary for natural selection theory to be coherent
- Connects classical population genetics (Fisher, Wright, Haldane) to modern genomic approaches—showing that the same framework underlies today's GWAS and molecular evolution studies

## Key References

1. **Fisher RA (1930)** — The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection — Established that Mendelian inheritance is necessary and sufficient for Darwinian evolution
2. **Kimura M (1983)** — The Neutral Theory of Molecular Evolution — Provided the null hypothesis framework for all modern tests of natural selection

## Future Research Directions

- Quantify the relative contributions of heterozygote advantage vs. rare allele advantage to the maintenance of variation in human populations using large-scale GWAS data
- Resolve the genetic architecture of inbreeding depression for complex traits using whole-genome approaches in both model and non-model organisms
- Develop predictive models of heterosis for crop breeding by integrating fine-scale QTL mapping with population-genetic theory of dominance complementation
