# From Mendel to Quantitative Genetics in the Genome Era: The Scientific Legacy of W. G. Hill

## Citation (NLM)
Charlesworth B, Goddard ME, Meyer K, Visscher PM, Weir BS, Wray NR. From Mendel to quantitative genetics in the genome era: the scientific legacy of W. G. Hill. Nat Genet. 2022 Jul;54(7):934–939. doi:10.1038/s41588-022-01103-1

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41588-022-01103-1](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41588-022-01103-1)

## Summary

This tribute Perspective commemorates W. G. (Bill) Hill—recipient of the 2018 Darwin Medal and the 2019 Mendel Medal—who died on 17 December 2021. The authors argue that Hill's diverse theoretical contributions across population genetics, quantitative genetics, and animal breeding all ultimately derive from Mendel's laws of assortment and segregation.

**The r² metric for linkage disequilibrium:** When Mendel documented cosegregation of trait pairs, he studied unlinked genes. Linkage between loci was recognized subsequently, and the concept of linkage disequilibrium (LD)—non-random association of alleles at different loci—became central to population genetics. Hill and Robertson (1968) introduced the r² measure: the squared correlation between the allelic states at two loci, equal to D²/[pA(1−pA)pB(1−pB)]. The r² statistic has the advantages of a known range (0 to 1) and interpretable statistical properties. It has since become the standard LD metric in population genomics and GWAS design, determining how many markers are needed to tag causal variants across the genome.

**The Hill–Robertson effect:** Hill's PhD research on the effects of linkage on selection efficiency showed that linkage between loci under directional selection impedes the response to selection: randomly generated LD causes a beneficial variant to become associated with a harmful nearby variant, reducing the probability of its fixation. Felsenstein (1974) coined the term "Hill–Robertson effect" and used it to explain why recombination is evolutionarily advantageous. The concept has grown in prominence with population genomic studies demonstrating that the ability of populations to incorporate beneficial mutations and purge deleterious ones is impaired in regions of low recombination.

**Genetic architecture of complex traits:** Hill made several theoretical predictions about the structure of genetic variance: (1) dominance variance should be negligible for highly polygenic traits, since it cannot simultaneously coexist with substantial inbreeding depression; (2) additive genetic variance should constitute the majority of total genetic variance; (3) even high-order epistatic interactions do not generate substantial nonadditive variance. Each of these predictions was validated empirically decades later by large-scale GWAS and meta-analyses of twin studies, confirming that most human complex trait variation is indeed additive.

**Mutational variance and long-term selection response:** Hill was the first to quantify why long-term responses to artificial selection continue even after the original standing variation is exhausted—new mutations continuously supply additive genetic variance. This insight underpins modern understanding of the durability of animal and plant breeding programs. Hill championed the development and adoption of restricted maximum likelihood (REML) methods for estimating genetic parameters from pedigree data, and his trainees (including co-authors Visscher, Wray, and Meyer) extended these methods to human genetics and evolutionary ecology.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Demonstrates concretely that Mendel's laws are the foundation of modern population genetics, quantitative genetics, and the genomic era of human complex trait research
- Provides the historical context for r² as the standard LD metric in GWAS—showing that it is not merely a statistical choice but derives from fundamental genetic theory
- Shows how "basic theoretical research" in genetics—often considered abstract—made predictions that were validated by massive empirical datasets decades later
