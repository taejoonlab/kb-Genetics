# Heredity before Genetics: A History

## Citation (NLM)
Cobb M. Heredity before genetics: a history. Nat Rev Genet. 2006 Dec;7(12):953–958. doi:10.1038/nrg1948

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg1948](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg1948)

---

## Summary

This review essay by Matthew Cobb (University of Manchester) traces the approximately 2,000-year intellectual journey toward recognizing that there is such a thing as biological heredity, identifying three converging strands—natural philosophy/science, agricultural practice, and medicine—each of which contributed essential insights that created the context for the breakthroughs of Mendel and Darwin.

**Ancient confusion about "generation":** As recently as 200 years ago, the word "heredity" had no biological meaning. The study of similarities between parents and offspring was subsumed under the broader concept of "generation"—a perplexingly complex phenomenon that fused what we now call reproduction, genetics, and development. Ancient Greek thinkers provided two frameworks: Hippocrates's two-semen blending model and Aristotle's form/matter model (male form, female matter). Neither framework produced a consistent description of parent-offspring resemblance, because the raw phenomena of heredity—children looking sometimes like one parent, sometimes neither, sometimes a grandparent—appeared too contradictory.

**Seventeenth-century discoveries and their limits:** William Harvey, Leeuwenhoek, de Graaf, and others established that "like breeds like" across all organisms and discovered eggs and sperm. Yet scientists spent the following 150 years debating whether ovists or spermists were correct, rather than recognizing that both contribute equally. Leeuwenhoek even interpreted his rabbit cross results—grey male × grey female → grey offspring—as evidence that spermatozoa were the sole source of life, rather than as evidence of hereditary transmission from both parents.

**Eighteenth-century advances—Maupertuis and polydactyly:** Maupertuis and Réaumur independently studied polydactyly across three to four generations in German and Maltese families, demonstrating that the trait re-appeared with consistent patterns—not what preformationist theories predicted. Maupertuis went further, calculating the probability that polydactyly would fail to be transmitted over three consecutive generations: the odds were 8 × 10¹² to 1—essentially the first statistical test of hereditary transmission. This insight had no influence on contemporary thinking and there is no evidence that Mendel knew of it.

**Agricultural practice—Robert Bakewell's revolution:** The decisive practical contribution came from English sheep breeder Robert Bakewell, who in the mid-18th century systematically selected animals against four criteria (beauty, edible/non-edible ratio, meat quality, growth rate) to create the New Leicester breed—castrated rams that could be sold for profit at two years instead of four. The key conceptual innovation was thinking in terms of populations rather than individual animals. Bakewell's success spread through Europe; in Moravia, Brno became a center for both sheep breeding and textile manufacture, and by 1819 local thinkers were outlining "genetic laws." This is precisely the intellectual context in which Mendel worked.

**Medical practice—French physicians name "hérédité":** As Carlos López-Beltrán has shown, the noun "heredity" entered biological language through French physicians studying heritable disease. The Académie de Dijon organized a competition in 1748 asking how hereditary illnesses are transmitted, launching a sustained medical research program on inherited conditions. By the 1830s, "hérédité" was in widespread use in French medical literature, and the English equivalent appeared first in Spencer's writing in 1863—the same time Darwin used it in his notes.

**Conclusion:** The discovery of heredity required not a new instrument or theory, but statistically extraordinary datasets: large, reliable human pedigrees of unusual characters and the large-scale experimental records generated by commercial animal breeders and by Mendel's own systematic plant crosses.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Explains why genetics was not discovered until the 19th century: the conceptual prerequisite (the noun "heredity") did not exist until agriculture and medicine created the data and the language
- Situates Mendel's work in its specific intellectual context: the Brno agricultural and scientific community's decades of work on Mendelian-like "genetic laws" in Merino sheep
- Demonstrates that the history of science is intertwined with economic, social, and linguistic history—not just experimental progress
