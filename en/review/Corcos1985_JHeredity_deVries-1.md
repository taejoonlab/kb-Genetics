# Role of de Vries in the Recovery of Mendel's Work I. Was de Vries Really an Independent Discoverer of Mendel?

## Summary

The traditional account of the rediscovery of Mendel's laws holds that three botanists—Hugo de Vries, Carl Correns, and Erich von Tschermak—independently arrived at Mendel's findings in 1900 without prior knowledge of his 1866 paper. Corcos and Monaghan challenge this narrative, focusing specifically on de Vries's claim to independent discovery.

De Vries published three papers in 1900 reporting his breeding experiments. The first, in French in the Comptes Rendus, did not mention Mendel; the other two did. His failure to credit Mendel in the first paper immediately sparked controversy. Compounding suspicion were de Vries's multiple, inconsistent accounts of how he came across Mendel's citation. In a 1905 letter he credited L. H. Bailey's bibliography; in 1924 he attributed it to Bailey's 1895 Plant Breeding—but that edition contains no bibliography. A third account, from Stomps who succeeded de Vries, described a letter from colleague Beijerinck enclosing a reprint of Mendel's paper just as de Vries was preparing to publish.

The paper's central analysis compares de Vries's writings before and after 1900. The evidence is compelling on several fronts. First, de Vries never reported F2 results as 3:1 ratios before 1900. His 1897 data on Lychnis gave 99 hairy vs. 54 hairless—closer to 2:1—with no interpretive framework, while that same data appeared as a 3:1 ratio only in his post-1900 publications. Second, in 1899 de Vries described F2 offspring of a Lychnis cross as having three phenotypic classes (purple, red, white), yet by 1900, after reading Mendel, the same data were recast as just two classes (colored 73%, white 27%). This reanalysis reflects the Mendelian dominance-recessiveness paradigm being imposed retroactively onto existing data. Third, as late as 1898 de Vries maintained that "all germ cells bear all the hereditary characters of the species"—a pre-Mendelian conception—while after 1900 he adopted language about "antagonistic characters disjoining."

The authors also correct several errors made by de Vries's defenders. Olby cited de Vries finding approximate 3:1 ratios as early as 1894, but the source (Die Mutations Theorie, 1903) postdates 1900. Van der Pas pointed to a large lecture plate used by de Vries before 1900 showing segregation ratios, but Kottler demonstrated the plate's data match the 1903 book, not the 1900 paper—and de Vries was still using terms "active" and "latent" (rather than dominant/recessive) as late as 1904. Sturtevant's mistranslation of a key French sentence—turning "this memoir was not appreciated and has been forgotten" into "this memoir has been known to me and then forgotten"—is also corrected.

The authors conclude that the circumstantial evidence strongly supports the view that de Vries did not develop the law of segregation independently of Mendel. His writings indicate he reached a correct Mendelian interpretation of his data only after reading Mendel's paper, probably between December 1899 and March 1900. If he received the paper by mail from a friend, as Stomps suggests, then he did not even independently discover the paper itself.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Demonstrates that the founding narrative of genetics—"independent rediscovery" in 1900—may be historically inaccurate, teaching students to critically evaluate received scientific history
- Illustrates the difference between observing a numerical ratio and conceptualizing a theoretical law; data alone do not constitute a discovery
- Raises important questions about priority, credit, and scientific honesty that remain relevant in modern science
- Provides a case study in how scientific myths can propagate through textbooks unchallenged for decades
