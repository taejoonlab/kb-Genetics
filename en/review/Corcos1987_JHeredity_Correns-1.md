# Correns, an Independent Discoverer of Mendelism? I. An Historical/Critical Note

## Summary

This brief historical/critical note examines Correns's claim to have independently discovered Mendelism. Corcos and Monaghan focus on a telling inconsistency between Correns's own retrospective accounts and the published record.

When Roberts asked Correns in 1925 to describe the circumstances of his discovery, Correns replied that he experienced a sudden "flash of understanding" one morning in autumn (October) 1899 while lying in bed reviewing his results. He claimed this revelation came a few weeks before he first read Mendel's paper. Yet the very xenia paper he referred to in this account was written in December 1899—and it already cites Mendel. Furthermore, in a second letter to Roberts, Correns explicitly stated: "The memoir of Mendel on his Hieracium hybrids I first read, however, with that on the pea hybrids, in the autumn of 1899."

This creates a fundamental problem. If Correns had already read Mendel before writing the xenia paper, why did that paper offer no quantitative account of his F2 results? Instead, he vaguely described the hybrid cross results as showing "very interesting, but also very complicated relationships into which I cannot enter here." This is a strange omission if he had truly achieved a clear Mendelian understanding weeks earlier. His stated reasons—being busy with other things and not knowing that other investigators were working in the same direction—strike the authors as insufficient to explain withholding what would have been a landmark scientific insight.

The authors propose that Correns was confused when he wrote to Roberts in 1925. The "flash" most likely occurred not before the xenia paper but between that paper and his famous 1900 paper on peas. Crucially, since he had already read Mendel by then, it was not a flash of independent discovery but rather a flash of understanding—specifically, the realization of how his results and Mendel's could be unified under the concept of "anlagen" and how this framework could be linked to the recently discovered process of meiosis.

This paper is the first in a series; Part II evaluates how well Correns actually understood Mendel's paper in his 1900 publication.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Illustrates the distinction between independent discovery and post-hoc understanding of another researcher's framework
- Demonstrates the unreliability of retrospective scientific accounts, especially those given decades after the events in question
- Raises questions about how priority in science is established and why the "independent rediscovery" narrative was so eagerly embraced by the scientific community
- Together with the de Vries papers, contributes to a systematic dismantling of the tripartite rediscovery myth
