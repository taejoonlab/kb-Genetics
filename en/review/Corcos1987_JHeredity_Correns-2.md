# Correns, an Independent Discoverer of Mendelism? II. Was Correns a Real Interpreter of Mendel's Paper?

## Citation (NLM)
Corcos AF, Monaghan FV. Correns, an independent discoverer of Mendelism? II. Was Correns a real interpreter of Mendel's paper? J Hered. 1987;78(6):404-405.

**DOI:** DOI not available in extracted text

## Summary

Having established in previous papers that de Vries was not an independent rediscoverer of Mendel and that Tschermak did not understand Mendel's paper when he read it, Corcos and Monaghan here assess Correns's comprehension of Mendel. Their conclusion is that Correns genuinely understood Mendel's paper—and extended it in three important ways—but committed one significant conceptual error.

**Correns's Understanding of Monohybrid Results**

Correns's 1900 paper used a clearer tabular format than either Mendel or de Vries, presenting phenotypic and genotypic ratios for each generation. He proposed that the expression of each contrasting character is caused by a quasi-particulate entity in the nucleus called an anlage. The dominant anlage suppresses the recessive one without destroying it; during gamete formation, "a complete separation of the two anlagen" occurs so that each gamete receives one or the other. Crucially, Correns was the first to suggest that segregation originates in the process of meiosis ("the reduction division of Weismann"), linking Mendel's abstract rule to a physical cellular process.

**Correns's Extension: The 9:3:3:1 Ratio**

For dihybrid results, Correns compared Mendel's pea data with his own maize data. He pointed out that when two pairs of traits are involved, there are nine distinct genotypic classes that fall into four phenotypic groups—and he asserted as a general proposition that these groups "must occur in a ratio of 9:3:3:1." This is the first explicit statement of this famous ratio in the history of genetics. Furthermore, Correns explicitly assigned one anlage per parental character and gave the percentages of each gamete class for monohybrids, dihybrids, and trihybrids—something Mendel had not done explicitly. He also used Mendel's symbolic notation correctly, which neither de Vries nor Tschermak managed.

**Correns's Error: Conflating the Two Laws**

Correns's principal mistake was lumping the law of segregation and the law of independent assortment into one "Mendel's Law." He declared that this law "includes the 'loi de disjonction' of de Vries, also." By framing Mendel's law in terms of phenotypic groups rather than genotypic classes, Correns made the law appear inapplicable in cases lacking dominance (where three phenotypic classes appear rather than two) or in cases of linkage. In doing so, he failed to see the universality of the law of segregation. As the authors note, Mendel's law operated on character structures (genotypes), not on phenotypic groupings, and was quite general. This confusion between the two laws persisted widely until T. H. Morgan separated them in 1913.

Despite this error, Correns stands out among the three rediscoverers. His post-1900 work included discoveries of the genetic basis of sex determination, cytoplasmic inheritance, selective fertilization, and prophetic suggestions that genes lie on chromosomes. Sturtevant described him as showing "a clear thinking, careful and painstaking, analytic, and experimental approach, worthy of Mendel's."

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Documents the first explicit statement of the 9:3:3:1 ratio and the first mechanistic connection of segregation to meiosis—two cornerstones of introductory genetics
- Shows that even the most capable reader of Mendel in 1900 conflated what we now call the first and second laws, illustrating that conceptual clarity in genetics was hard-won
- Provides historical context for understanding why independent assortment is called the "second law"—the separation of the two laws took over a decade to achieve
- Demonstrates how even important scientists can make conceptual errors that obscure generalizations they are otherwise capable of grasping
