# Role of de Vries in the Rediscovery of Mendel's Paper II. Did de Vries Really Understand Mendel's Paper?

## Citation (NLM)
Corcos AF, Monaghan FV. Role of de Vries in the rediscovery of Mendel's paper. II. Did de Vries really understand Mendel's paper? J Hered. 1987;78(4):275-276.

**DOI:** DOI not available in extracted text

## Summary

Having shown in Part I that de Vries was not an independent rediscoverer of Mendel, Corcos and Monaghan here argue that de Vries failed to fully understand Mendel even after reading the paper. They advance three distinct lines of evidence.

**Point 1: Attributing a Law to Mendel That Is Not in His Paper**

De Vries attributed to Mendel the "law of disjunction of hybrids" in all three of his 1900 papers. Yet the authors point out that no such law exists as an explicit statement in Mendel's paper; Mendel presented it as an assumption underlying a hypothetical mechanism, not a law. More tellingly, de Vries stated this "law" differently in each of his three papers. If he had understood Mendel securely, he would have given a consistent formulation. Instead, de Vries seems to have extracted a clue from Mendel and reframed it within his own theory of intracellular pangenesis—each pangen being an indivisible unit corresponding to a distinct heritable character.

**Point 2: Overgeneralizing Dominance**

De Vries assumed that in all pairs of contrasting traits, one member must always dominate—a claim Correns had already criticized. De Vries tied dominance to his theory of pangenesis: because pangens are indivisible, hybrids cannot show intermediate forms. Mendel, in contrast, explicitly recognized that dominance is not universal (e.g., in flowering time of pea hybrids). His inductive approach—moving from evidence to generalization to proposed mechanism—did not force him to impose dominance on all cases. De Vries's deductive approach, starting from his pangenesis theory and seeking confirmation in data, led him to overgeneralize.

**Point 3: Choosing an Inappropriate Mathematical Model**

De Vries used the binomial expansion (d + r)(d + r) = d² + 2dr + r² to model fertilization. The authors show this is fundamentally inappropriate: fertilization is a process of combining (adding) gametes, not multiplying them. The symbols d² and r² have no biological meaning as phenotypes or genotypes. By contrast, Mendel's combinatorial approach—assigning equal numbers of egg and pollen cells to each type—directly and correctly yields the 1AA:2Aa:1aa ratio with correctly specified phenotypes and genotypes for each class. Compounding the problem, de Vries used the same symbolic notation in two incompatible ways: first as expressions in an algebraic expansion, then substituting 0.5 for each to derive frequencies—an internally inconsistent procedure.

**The Root Misunderstanding**

The authors identify a deeper problem: de Vries approached Mendel with a fundamentally different question. Mendel sought the laws describing hybrid formation and offspring development (an inductive enterprise). De Vries was using hybrids as tools to investigate the inheritance of individual characters (a deductive enterprise from his pangenesis theory). This mismatch of purpose meant de Vries could not appreciate what Mendel had actually accomplished.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Illustrates how theoretical preconceptions can filter and distort the reception of a new idea even when the underlying data are available
- Provides a case study in the importance of using appropriate mathematical models in biology—errors in formalism can mask biological insight
- Helps students understand why Mendel's approach was genuinely revolutionary: his inductive methodology was what enabled him to discover general laws rather than confirm an existing theory
- Together with Parts I and II of the Correns series, completes a systematic reappraisal of the 1900 rediscovery events
