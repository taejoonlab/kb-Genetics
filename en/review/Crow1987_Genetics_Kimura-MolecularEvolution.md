# Twenty-Five Years Ago in Genetics: Motoo Kimura and Molecular Evolution

## Citation (NLM)
Crow JF. Twenty-five years ago in Genetics: Motoo Kimura and molecular evolution. Genetics. 1987;116(2):183-184.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/116/2/183](https://genetics.org/content/116/2/183)

---

## Summary

In this brief *Genetics* "Perspectives" retrospective, James F. Crow revisits Motoo Kimura's June 1962 *Genetics* paper, "On the probability of fixation of mutant genes in a population," and shows how a piece of theory that seemed peripheral at the time turned out to be "pre-adapted" for the study of molecular evolution. Kimura's paper gave a very general treatment — using a diffusion model, for arbitrary dominance and fluctuating selection — of the probability that a mutant allele at a given frequency will eventually replace the existing allele(s). At the time, fixation probability was seen mainly as a tool in animal-breeding theory (selection limits) rather than a central evolutionary question.

Crow places the result in its lineage. As early as 1927 Haldane, using a method of Fisher (1922), showed that a new mutant with selective advantage *s* has an ultimate fixation probability of about 2*s*; Fisher, Wright, and Malécot added refinements. Kimura's diffusion solution generalized these into the compact formula P = (1 − e^(−2s))/(1 − e^(−4Ns)), where N is the breeding population size. This formula recovers Haldane's 2*s* when s → 0 with Ns > 1, and gives P = 1/(2N) for a strictly neutral mutant — the value expected if every gene copy is equally likely to be the eventual ancestor of the population.

The essay's core is how this equation illuminated the neutral theory. In the late 1960s it became clear that molecular evolution (nucleotide and amino-acid substitutions) "marches to a different drummer" than morphological evolution: it is roughly constant per protein across lineages and largely independent of the environment (the molecular clock). Kimura's key insight was that the long-term substitution rate does not depend on how long each mutation takes to fix, but on the *rate of occurrence* of ultimately fixed mutations. Crow works through the algebra: with 2N*μ* new mutations per generation each having fixation probability P, the substitution rate is 2N*μ*P. If substitutions were driven by favorable mutations (P ≈ 2*s*), the rate would be 4N*μs* — proportional to population size N, which is contrary to fact, since carnivores with small populations evolve molecularly as fast as herbivores with large ones. But if substitutions are neutral (P = 1/(2N)), the rate is simply *μ*, the mutation rate — independent of N and matching the data. This is Kimura's neutral theory: most molecular evolution is the random fixation of neutral, or slightly deleterious, mutations.

Crow closes on a personal note, recounting how he first heard of Kimura's mathematics in the early 1950s through his student Newton Morton (then in Hiroshima), and how Kimura — holding only a master's degree in cytogenetics, with self-taught mathematics — joined Crow's group at Wisconsin and earned his Ph.D. two years later, beginning a lifelong collaboration. Crow credits Kimura with carrying the theoretical tradition of Fisher and Wright into the second half of the century, popularizing diffusion equations, and catalyzing the mathematical rigor of modern population genetics, whether or not he is best remembered for the neutral theory itself.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Explains, with the actual formula, the probability of fixation of a new mutation — a cornerstone of population genetics that unifies drift and selection.
- Gives the clearest possible derivation of *why* the neutral substitution rate equals the mutation rate and is independent of population size — the theoretical basis of the molecular clock.
- Distinguishes molecular evolution from morphological evolution, a key conceptual divide for students.
- Offers a human portrait of how the neutral theory arose from a Crow-Kimura mentorship, illustrating how theory and biography intertwine in genetics.

## Key References

1. **Kimura (1962)** — On the probability of fixation of mutant genes in a population — the paper being commemorated; source of the general fixation-probability formula.
2. **Haldane (1927)** — Selection and mutation — derived the classic 2*s* fixation probability for a favorable mutant.
3. **Kimura (1983)** — *The Neutral Theory of Molecular Evolution* — the full statement of the neutral theory and its many supporting arguments.
4. **Kimura and Ohta (1969)** — Average time to fixation ≈ 4N generations — quantified the timescale required for the neutral-rate argument.

## Future Research Directions

- Kimura's neutral theory became the null model for all of molecular evolution, underpinning modern tests of selection (dN/dS, McDonald-Kreitman, site-frequency-spectrum methods) that ask where real data depart from neutrality.
- Crow's remark that "the exact fraction remains to be determined" frames a still-active debate about the relative roles of neutral drift, slightly deleterious mutation, and positive selection in shaping genomes.
