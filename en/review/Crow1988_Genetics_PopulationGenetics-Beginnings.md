# Eighty Years Ago: The Beginnings of Population Genetics

## Citation (NLM)
Crow JF. Eighty years ago: the beginnings of population genetics. Genetics. 1988;119(3):473-476.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/119/3/473](https://genetics.org/content/119/3/473)

---

## Summary

In this GENETICS "Perspectives" essay, James F. Crow marks the eightieth anniversary of the foundational year 1908 and reflects on the origins of population genetics. He opens with the common misconception that, absent counteracting forces, dominant phenotypes should outnumber recessives three to one in a population. It was precisely this error that prompted the mathematician G. H. Hardy to write his famous two-page 1908 paper, opening apologetically that he was reluctant to intrude on matters he expected to be familiar to biologists. The same principle had been published a few months earlier by Wilhelm Weinberg, but his German-language work went largely unnoticed by English-speaking geneticists; only after Curt Stern's 1943 correction did the principle become known as the Hardy-Weinberg law. Crow stresses that although the principle seems almost trivially self-evident—Sewall Wright used it before hearing of either man—it is the foundation of diploid population genetics.

Crow explains the law's two formulations. First, under random mating in a large population without mutation, migration, or selection, genotype proportions remain constant across generations. More usefully, the law predicts genotype frequencies (p², 2pq, q²) from allele frequencies (p, q), letting hypotheses about inheritance be tested against population data. He highlights why natural populations so often fit H-W expectations: unlike most equilibria, this one is reached in a single generation of random mating rather than asymptotically, so a single generation erases prior nonrandom mating. Weinberg went further, generalizing the law to multiple alleles (1908) and multiple loci (1909), recognizing that with more than one locus the equilibrium is approached only gradually at a rate set by recombination. Crow laments that Weinberg, like Mendel, suffered neglect largely because his contemporaries did not read German.

A substantial portion of the essay is a warm biographical portrait of Hardy the pure mathematician—collaborator with Littlewood, discoverer of Ramanujan, and author of *A Mathematician's Apology*—who prized useless, beautiful mathematics and was reportedly embarrassed that his most trivial paper became his most widely known and "distastefully practical." Crow speculates Hardy published in *Science* rather than *Nature* to avoid the Mendelian-biometrician quarrel, or perhaps to keep it from his mathematician colleagues. Weinberg, by contrast, was a busy Stuttgart physician and obstetrician who attended more than 3,500 births yet still made fundamental discoveries: a method to estimate the proportion of monozygotic and dizygotic twins, the recognition and correction of ascertainment bias (founding segregation analysis with his sib and proband methods), and an anticipation of the analysis of variance in his 1910 work on correlations between relatives.

Crow then turns to R. A. Fisher, the greatest statistician of his generation, who struggled to find suitable employment—rejected for military service for poor eyesight, teaching school unhappily—before his blockbuster 1918 paper, "The correlation between relatives on the supposition of Mendelian inheritance." Reviewed by the bitter opponents Pearson and Punnett (neither of whom recommended publication), it appeared only in the Transactions of the Royal Society of Edinburgh with Leonard Darwin's financial help. Ironically its central argument—that quantitative traits are compatible with Mendelism—was moot by publication, yet the paper founded quantitative genetics: it introduced the term "variance," partitioned genetic and environmental components, distinguished dominance and epistatic contributions, and analyzed assortative mating. Crow closes by noting that 1918 also saw Sewall Wright's paper "On the nature of size factors," the forerunner of path analysis, and remarks that the field is still learning about the H-W relationship—citing C. C. Li's demonstration in the same issue that random mating is sufficient but not necessary for H-W ratios.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the historical and conceptual origin of the Hardy-Weinberg principle, the cornerstone equation of any introductory population-genetics unit.
- Clarifies a persistent student misconception (the "3:1 in populations" fallacy) and explains why the H-W equilibrium is reached in a single generation.
- Connects the three founders of theoretical population and quantitative genetics—Hardy, Weinberg, and Fisher—and situates the Mendelian-versus-biometrician debate that the field ultimately resolved.
- Reminds students that segregation analysis, variance partitioning, and path analysis all trace to this founding era.

## Key References

1. **Hardy (1908)** — "Mendelian proportions in a mixed population" (Science) — the two-page paper stating the equilibrium law.
2. **Weinberg (1908, 1909)** — papers deriving the law and extending it to multiple alleles and loci, plus his founding work on ascertainment bias.
3. **Fisher (1918)** — "The correlation between relatives on the supposition of Mendelian inheritance" — reconciled biometry with Mendelism and founded quantitative genetics.
4. **Wright (1918)** — "On the nature of size factors" (Genetics) — forerunner of path analysis.
5. **Stern (1943)** — "The Hardy-Weinberg law" (Science) — set the record straight, giving Weinberg due credit.

## Future Research Directions

- The H-W framework remains the null model against which departures (selection, drift, population structure, assortative mating, inbreeding) are detected in modern GWAS and population-genomic datasets.
- Fisher's 1918 partition of variance underlies today's heritability estimation and the analysis of complex traits via genomic prediction and QTL/GWAS mapping.
