# R. A. Fisher, a Centennial View

## Citation (NLM)
Crow JF. R. A. Fisher, a centennial view. Genetics. 1990;124(2):207-211.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/124/2/207](https://genetics.org/content/124/2/207)

---

## Summary

Written for the centennial of Ronald A. Fisher's birth (February 17, 1890), this *Genetics* "Perspectives" essay by James F. Crow is a personal portrait of a man Crow describes as "an outlier, both scientifically and personally" — an admirer's account written by one who "knew him less well than some but better than most." Rather than a systematic review of Fisher's science, it blends appraisal of his genius with anecdote and candid acknowledgment of his difficult temperament.

Crow emphasizes Fisher's near-occult geometric intuition: he "found the pearl without opening the oyster," arriving at elegant answers with no visible intermediate steps, so that mathematicians often distrusted results they could not follow (one scrawled "Fisher is fishy" in a margin). Crow attributes part of this to Fisher's severe childhood myopia, which forced him to work problems in his head without visual aids, cultivating the geometric insight behind his derivations of significance tests, the *t* distribution, and the analysis of variance. Fisher chose mathematics at Cambridge but always considered his real interest to be biology, reasoning that "a mathematical technique with biological interests is a rather firmer ground than a biological technique with mathematical interests." After miserable years (1913-1919) of office work, teaching, and subsistence farming, he wrote two famous papers — the 1915 transformation for the correlation coefficient and the 1918 paper reconciling biometry with Mendelism that founded quantitative genetics — before joining Rothamsted Experimental Station in 1919, where he created modern experimental design and the analysis of variance (the "plots," "blocks," and factorial designs still bear the farm's imprint).

The essay surveys Fisher's monumental contributions to statistics — small-sample theory, analysis of variance and covariance, experimental design, maximum likelihood, correcting the degrees of freedom in Pearson's χ² test — and notes the underappreciated fact that Fisher was first to use nonparametric permutation tests, regarding randomization as primary and normal-theory tests as labor-saving approximations. Crow speculates that with modern computers Fisher would have relied far more on robust permutation methods. Turning to evolution, Crow calls *The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection* (1930) "arguably the deepest and most influential book on evolution since Darwin," highlighting its emphasis on the variance-conserving property of particulate inheritance, the concepts of reproductive value and the "Malthusian parameter," the Fundamental Theorem of Natural Selection (that the rate of increase of fitness equals the additive genetic variance), and Fisher's explanation of the 1:1 sex ratio via parental expenditure. He also notes that Fisher, despite pioneering the theory of random drift, never regarded drift as evolutionarily important — a lasting disagreement with Wright and later Kimura. The book's final chapters on eugenics, Crow observes, have had no lasting influence.

Crow situates Fisher within the great founding trinity of population genetics alongside Sewall Wright and J. B. S. Haldane, noting that where Haldane and Wright "doggedly ground out the results," Fisher "was more likely to invent a new, neater approach." He recounts Fisher's lifelong genetic experiments (mice, poultry, snails, and more, often kept at home), his methodological contributions to linkage analysis and human genetics (including the three-locus Rhesus hypothesis), and his view that "genetics was transmission genetics" — meiosis was a "black box," famously insisting to Lederberg that his was "a one-strand model." The essay ends with two warm personal anecdotes — a champagne-fueled first meeting in 1946 North Carolina, and Fisher instantly scribbling a generating function for the Luria-Delbrück mutant distribution that Crow then lost — capturing both the charm and the combativeness (notably his bitterness toward Karl Pearson) of a singular scientist.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Introduces the founder of both modern statistics and theoretical population genetics, showing how the two fields grew from the same mind.
- The 1918 paper reconciling Mendelism with continuous (biometric) variation is the historical birth of quantitative genetics — essential background for any course covering complex traits and heritability.
- Presents core Fisherian concepts students meet later: analysis of variance, maximum likelihood, the Fundamental Theorem, reproductive value, and the evolutionary theory of the sex ratio.
- Humanizes the "great trinity" (Fisher, Wright, Haldane) and the drift-vs-selection debate that still structures evolutionary genetics.

## Key References

1. **Fisher (1918)** — The correlation between relatives on the supposition of Mendelian inheritance — reconciled biometry and Mendelism, founding quantitative genetics.
2. **Fisher (1930)** — *The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection* — the foundational work of theoretical evolutionary genetics, including the Fundamental Theorem.
3. **Box (1978)** — *R. A. Fisher, the Life of a Scientist* — the definitive biography (by Fisher's daughter) Crow draws on and recommends.
4. **Crow (1988)** — Fifty years ago: the beginnings of population genetics — Crow's companion essay on the founding of the field.

## Future Research Directions

- Fisher's Fundamental Theorem of Natural Selection continues to generate reinterpretation and formal proof, remaining a touchstone for how additive genetic variance drives adaptation.
- The drift-versus-selection tension Crow highlights — Fisher's dismissal of drift versus Wright's and Kimura's emphasis on it — persists as a central theme in molecular population genetics and genome evolution.
