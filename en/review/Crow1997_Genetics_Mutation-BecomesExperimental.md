# Seventy Years Ago: Mutation Becomes Experimental

## Citation (NLM)
Crow JF, Abrahamson S. Seventy years ago: mutation becomes experimental. Genetics. 1997;147(4):1491-1496.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/147/4/1491](https://genetics.org/content/147/4/1491)

---

## Summary

This Perspectives essay, written by two of Muller's intellectual descendants, commemorates the seventieth anniversary of H. J. Muller's 1927 *Science* paper "Artificial Transmutation of the Gene," which reported the first experimental production of mutations and opened the era of radiation genetics. Crow and Abrahamson explain the paper's curious title: Muller consciously modeled his radiation work on Rutherford's contemporaneous experimental transmutation of chemical elements. Just as physicists had shown that atomic transmutation was not a purely spontaneous, untouchable process, Muller demonstrated that mutation — long thought to arise mysteriously "from within" — could be influenced artificially. This made him an instant celebrity. The 1927 paper was also curious for presenting *no data*: only qualitative results and rough comparisons (a mutation-rate increase of "fifteen thousand percent"), which invited skepticism, notably from T. H. Morgan. Muller silenced the skeptics the following year with a full account delivered at the Fifth International Congress of Genetics in Berlin and published in detail (1928).

The authors emphasize that Muller's radiation discovery was the culmination of a decade of methodological work beginning in 1918, and that his greater contribution was the *toolkit* for studying mutation quantitatively rather than radiation mutagenesis alone. The central problem was the rarity of mutations. Muller recognized that recessive lethals were better material than visible mutations: they were far more numerous and unambiguous (no survivors), eliminating the "personal equation" so that an average technician could score them as reliably as a sharp-eyed Calvin Bridges. He exploited "C factors" (crossover suppressors, later shown to be inversions) to build balanced lethal systems that accumulated mutations over generations, foreshadowing Mukai's later measurement of spontaneous mutation rates of small effect. His most famous stock, the ClB chromosome (C = crossover suppressor, l = recessive lethal, B = Bar duplication), allowed X-linked lethal mutation rates to be measured almost unambiguously — with an F2 culture lacking males signaling a new lethal, detectable with the naked eye or a hand lens. Before turning to radiation, Muller spent years testing whether temperature could alter the mutation rate, reasoning that if mutation behaved like an ordinary chemical reaction it should roughly double or triple per 10° rise; he achieved this despite crude equipment (wet cloths and electric fans in hot Texas summers). Showing the rate could be influenced made mutation "a researchable subject," no longer "an unreachable god." The authors note the historical irony that the nature of the gene was ultimately revealed not by mutation kinetics but by the identification of DNA and the Watson–Crick model; today gene knowledge is used to understand mutation, not the reverse. They also note that convincing chemical mutagenesis waited two decades until Charlotte Auerbach's demonstration with mustard gas, and that Muller wrongly insisted radiation-induced mutations were like spontaneous ones, while Stadler correctly suspected many were deletions.

The second half of the essay is a biographical portrait of Muller's difficult, itinerant life. Born in New York in 1890, he worked his way through Columbia, gravitated to Morgan's fly group but never felt a true insider, and chafed under Morgan's view that ideas mattered less than data. Feeling under-credited, he moved to Rice, then the University of Texas (where Patterson provided support and an X-ray machine but Muller again felt exploited). His leftist politics and idealism led him to Germany (1932, arriving just before Hitler), then the Soviet Union, where the rise of Lysenko destroyed genetics and imperiled his colleagues (including Vavilov, who died imprisoned). Muller escaped via a stint in the Spanish Civil War, endured hard years at Edinburgh (where he supervised Auerbach), and finally, after repeated job insecurity, secured a permanent position at Indiana University in 1945 at age fifty-five; the Nobel Prize followed a year later. The essay closes with personal memories from both authors — Abrahamson as Muller's graduate "son" and Crow as a "grandson" — recalling his relentless work ethic, piercing criticism, prophetic vision (including his 1922 speculation that bacteriophage might be gene-like), and the contrast between his great scientific intellect and his prickly personality.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Marks the origin of experimental mutagenesis and radiation genetics, a turning point that made mutation a measurable, manipulable process rather than an untouchable mystery.
- Highlights the primacy of *method*: balanced lethals, crossover suppressors, and the ClB technique are foundational tools still taught in elementary genetics.
- Establishes the dose–response (linear) relationship between radiation and mutation, with lasting public-health implications and Muller's lifelong crusade against indiscriminate radiation exposure.
- Offers students a vivid case study in how personality, politics, and historical circumstance shape a scientific career and the reception of discoveries.

## Future Research Directions

- Understanding the molecular spectrum of radiation- versus chemically-induced mutations (point mutations vs. deletions), a question Muller and Stadler debated and that modern sequencing can now resolve directly.
- Applying accumulation and quantitative-mutation frameworks to genome-wide measurement of spontaneous mutation rates and mutational load in diverse organisms.
