# H. J. Muller and the "Competition Hoax"

## Citation (NLM)

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.106.059956](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.106.059956)

## Summary

Written by James Crow for the Genetics Perspectives series, this essay honors Hermann Joseph Muller (1890–1967) and focuses on his theoretical contributions to understanding sexual reproduction and evolution, centering on a memorable personal episode dubbed the "competition hoax."

**Muller's Breadth**

Though best known for demonstrating X-ray mutagenesis (earning him the 1946 Nobel Prize), Muller's intellectual range was extraordinary. Joshua Lederberg wrote that "it is not easy to find an original thought in biological theory that has not, in some way, been anticipated here." Evelyn Witkin noted that Muller "laid the conceptual foundation of molecular biology to an extent that is not generally recognized."

**The Fisher-Muller Argument for Sex**

Muller (1932), along with R. A. Fisher, argued that the primary evolutionary advantage of sexual reproduction is that recombination allows favorable mutations arising in different individuals to be combined in a single lineage. In asexual populations, a second favorable mutation must await a descendant of the first mutant individual; in sexual populations, independently arisen beneficial mutations can be combined immediately through mating and recombination. Crow and Kimura (1965) worked out the quantitative consequences, deriving a formula for R, the ratio of mutant allele substitution rate in sexual vs. asexual populations. Their initial formulation overestimated the advantage because it did not account for stochastic effects; Felsenstein (1974) provided the definitive treatment.

**Muller's Ratchet**

In a single sentence in his 1964 paper, Muller tossed out an idea of lasting influence: in asexual populations, "the least-loaded class" of individuals—those with the fewest deleterious mutations—can never be restored once lost except by improbable back mutation, creating a one-way ratchet. Sexual recombination readily generates individuals with fewer mutations than either parent. First emphasized by Felsenstein (1974), this "Muller's Ratchet" became one of the major arguments for sexual reproduction.

**The Competition Hoax**

Muller argued that in asexual populations, multiple favorable mutations compete with each other, each slowing the others' rate of increase, and that this competition effect is absent in sexual populations where recombination leads to "cooperation" rather than competition. Crow and Kimura disputed this. They showed via a simple model—an infinite haploid population with two loci and two alleles, fitnesses xy, x, y, 1—that such a population evolves at exactly the same rate with or without recombination. Competition operates equally in both sexual and asexual populations. Muller accepted this argument and sent Crow a postcard conceding the point: "You're so right! But first, after reading your exposé of the competition hoax, I had the half-baked thought... Then I realized that (it) wouldn't matter... I'm so glad you found this error before sending the article for publication, as I shouldn't have wanted to carry a double tail between my legs, one's enough."

The paper's conclusion, however, is nuanced: clonal interference (the competition effect) is real and significant in asexual populations—it just is not absent in sexual ones. The competition effect may in fact be greater in sexual species if there is additional competition for mates. Subsequent work by Gerrish and Lenski (1998) and others has extensively characterized clonal interference.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Introduces the Fisher-Muller theory and Muller's Ratchet—two of the principal theoretical explanations for why sexual reproduction evolved and is maintained
- Provides a vivid example of scientific error correction: a leading scientist publicly acknowledging a conceptual mistake via a personal note
- Contextualizes the concept of clonal interference, now an active area of experimental evolution research
- Muller's life also illustrates the intersection of genetics with radiation biology and public policy—his work on radiation carcinogenesis parallels Ed Lewis's (see CrowBender2014)

## Future Research Directions

- Quantifying clonal interference in diverse biological systems—from RNA viruses to tumor evolution—and testing whether sexual reproduction quantitatively reduces it
- Investigating whether Muller's Ratchet is relevant to the evolution of mitochondrial genomes and other non-recombining genetic elements
- Integrating the Fisher-Muller model with modern genomic data from experimental evolution studies to test whether sex does indeed accelerate adaptation in real populations
