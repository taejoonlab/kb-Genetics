## Summary

This memorial essay honors Edward B. Lewis (1918–2004), whose Drosophila genetics work over six decades at Caltech culminated in the 1995 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine, shared with Christine Nüsslein-Volhard and Eric Wieschaus. James Crow provides the scientific biography, Welcome Bender covers the molecular biology of the bithorax complex, and several colleagues and students contribute personal remembrances.

**Early Life and Career**

Lewis was born in Wilkes-Barre, Pennsylvania, and began experimenting with Drosophila in high school. He entered Bucknell on a music scholarship (flute) and later transferred to the University of Minnesota, then joined Sturtevant's laboratory at Caltech in 1939, completing his Ph.D. in 1942. After four years as a meteorology officer in World War II, he returned to Caltech as an instructor in 1946, where he remained until retirement in 1988.

**Pseudoalleles and the Position Effect**

Lewis's early work centered on the Star (S) locus and a related mutation he named asteroid (ast). He showed through clever genetic crosses—including use of attached-X chromosomes to prove reciprocal exchange—that these were two separable loci, coinig the term "pseudoalleles." He demonstrated a position effect: trans-heterozygotes (one mutation on each chromosome) showed a more extreme phenotype than cis-heterozygotes (both mutations on the same chromosome). This discovery extended the concept of position effects and became conceptually foundational for the complementation test.

**Transvection**

While testing bx and Ubx in a cis-trans configuration, Lewis noticed that preventing close synapsis of the two chromosomes (via a strategic chromosomal rearrangement) made the trans-heterozygote phenotype more extreme. He interpreted this as gene products moving from one chromosome to the homolog. This "transvection" phenomenon later became a powerful method for detecting chromosome rearrangements within one generation.

**The Bithorax Complex and Hox Colinearity**

Lewis's defining contribution was the decades-long dissection of the bithorax complex (BX-C). He accumulated hundreds of alleles and demonstrated that the BX-C contains multiple functional units (genes), each specifying a "substance" required for identity of a particular thoracic or abdominal segment. He proposed that these units arose by tandem duplication of an ancestral gene, and that their chromosomal order is colinear with the body segments they affect—a rule now known as the colinearity principle.

His four-winged fly (deletion of BX-C transforming the third thoracic into a second thoracic segment) became one of the most iconic images in biology, demonstrating that simple genetic changes could produce logically comprehensible alterations in body plan. In 1985, molecular work by two groups cloning the BX-C loci revealed the homeobox and the stunning conservation of Hox gene clusters between flies and vertebrates. Lewis's rule of colinearity proved universal across the animal kingdom.

**Radiation and Cancer**

In a less-celebrated but equally impactful effort, Lewis challenged the prevailing view that radiation carcinogenesis had a threshold. His 1957 Science paper, using data from Hiroshima, Nagasaki, and other sources, argued for a linear, no-threshold relationship between radiation dose and leukemia risk. Despite fierce opposition—including from Caltech administrators and the Atomic Energy Commission—Lewis's position eventually became the accepted framework for radiation protection standards. He estimated the average leukemia risk as 1.0–2.0 per million persons per rad per year, a figure that "has stood up remarkably well."

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A paradigm example of forward genetics: phenotype-first screens with Drosophila revealing the genetic program of segment development
- Historical origin of the complementation test (cis-trans test) and its conceptual basis
- Demonstrates how model organism research can uncover universal biological principles—Hox colinearity extends to all bilaterally symmetric animals
- Illustrates the role of geneticists in shaping public health policy through rigorous data analysis

## Future Research Directions

- Characterizing the function of non-coding RNAs across the BX-C region, recent findings from Lewis's own lab
- Testing Lewis's tandem duplication model for Hox gene evolution using comparative genomic data from diverse animal lineages
- Reassessing the linear no-threshold hypothesis in light of modern epidemiological data and mechanistic understanding of radiation-induced DNA damage
