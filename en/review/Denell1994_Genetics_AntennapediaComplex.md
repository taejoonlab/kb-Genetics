# Discovery and Genetic Definition of the Drosophila Antennapedia Complex

## Citation (NLM)
Denell R. Discovery and genetic definition of the Drosophila Antennapedia complex. Genetics. 1994;138(3):549-552.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/138/3/549](https://genetics.org/content/138/3/549)

---

## Summary

In this GENETICS "Perspectives" recollection, Rob Denell recounts how the *Drosophila* Antennapedia complex (ANT-C) was discovered and genetically defined, largely through the work of Thom Kaufman and colleagues in the 1970s and 1980s. The story opens in 1976 when Kaufman, excited by his first crosses, became convinced that a group of homeotic genes near the third-chromosome centromere formed a complex controlling anterior developmental commitments—an anterior counterpart to Ed Lewis's bithorax complex (BX-C), which governed posterior fates. Denell and Kaufman had been graduate students together in Burke Judd's lab, where Judd's "saturation mutagenesis" of the *zeste-white* region—identifying every zygotically active function in a chromosomal interval—provided the methodological template that would later be applied to the ANT-C.

Denell reviews the confused starting picture. Several homeotic loci were known near the third-chromosome centromere (*proboscipedia*, *Antennapedia*, *Extra sex combs*, *Multiple sex combs*, *Nasobemia*, and others), but centromeric inhibition of recombination and uncertain allelic relationships left their number and arrangement in doubt. Most known variants were dominant "neomorphs"—for example, many *Antennapedia* alleles shared a dominant antenna-to-leg transformation. Using Lifschytz and Falk's approach of reverting dominant gain-of-function alleles with loss-of-function mutations, Denell (1973) showed that *Nasobemia* was actually an *Antennapedia* allele whose namesake phenotype was neomorphic, and that *Extra sex combs* likewise appeared to be *Antennapedia*-related—work paralleled independently by Kaufman and Ian Duncan. Meanwhile Lewis's elegant BX-C analysis had established that loss-of-function variants cause anterior transformations while gain-of-function variants cause posterior ones, that the genes map colinearly with their body-axis effects, and that a segment's identity reflects how many BX-C genes it expresses—ideas that framed thinking about the ANT-C.

The key strategic insight was Kaufman's recognition that the existing collection of adult-phenotype mutants was biased (nearly all neomorphs); null alleles and additional functionally related genes were needed. He therefore applied Judd's saturation-mutagenesis concept to the region, screening for X-ray- and EMS-induced mutations failing to complement deficiencies of the interval. This work (Lewis, Kaufman, Denell, Wakimoto and others, 1980) defined the complementation groups of polytene region 84A-B, confirmed *Sex combs reduced* as a locus, and revealed that the complex contained more than just homeotic genes. The real payoff came from examining the phenotypes of embryos and larvae homozygous for recessive lethal alleles: this uncovered *fushi tarazu* (ftz)—a segmentation gene named for a Japanese term describing a shortening by segments, later among the most intensively studied genes in *Drosophila*—and *zerknüllt* (zen), involved in dorsal-ventral patterning, interspersed among the homeotic genes.

Denell emphasizes that Kaufman's argument for a genuine, functionally coherent complex—rather than a chance clustering—was later vindicated by molecular biology. The homeotic genes (and the interspersed nonhomeotic ones) share homeoboxes encoding sequence-specific DNA-binding domains, marking them as transcription factors. A clustered homeobox complex regulating development turned out to be an ancient metazoan feature: Beeman's mapping in the flour beetle *Tribolium* suggested that a single ancestral homeotic complex (HOM-C), later split into ANT-C and BX-C in flies, is conserved across insects, and homeobox studies revealed the deep evolutionary conservation of these clusters across the animal kingdom. Denell closes by noting the limits of zygotic saturation screens (they missed the maternal gene *bicoid* and some molecularly discovered genes) and reflects on the satisfaction that these "bizarre" fly mutations led to a gene complex now central to understanding mammalian development—even proposed as a criterion defining membership in the animal kingdom.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A model example of forward genetics in development: how systematic mutant isolation (saturation mutagenesis) and complementation analysis define a functional gene complex.
- Illustrates key genetic concepts—neomorphic (gain-of-function) versus amorphic (loss-of-function) alleles, reversion analysis, complementation groups, and ascertainment bias in mutant collections.
- Introduces homeotic genes, the homeobox, and the colinearity of gene order with body-axis expression—foundational to evo-devo and to understanding conserved Hox clusters.
- Shows how classical *Drosophila* genetics fed directly into molecular developmental biology and comparative genomics.

## Future Research Directions

- Hox gene clusters and their colinear, conserved organization remain central to evo-devo, regeneration, and the study of body-plan evolution across animals.
- The nonhomeotic members of the complex (e.g., the rapidly diverging *fushi tarazu*, and the enigmatic cuticle-gene cluster) pose ongoing questions about how gene clusters gain, lose, and repurpose members over evolutionary time.
