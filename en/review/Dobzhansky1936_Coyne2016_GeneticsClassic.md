## Summary

This article is a *GENETICS* "Genetics Classics" commentary in which Jerry Coyne reflects on the historical significance of Theodosius Dobzhansky's classic 1936 paper — "Studies on Hybrid Sterility II: Localization of Sterility Factors in *Drosophila pseudoobscura* Hybrids."

**Background: the problem Darwin left unsolved**

Dobzhansky's 1937 book *Genetics and the Origin of Species* is credited with launching the Modern Evolutionary Synthesis. Its title deliberately echoed Darwin's *On the Origin of Species* and confronted head-on the problem Darwin never resolved — the discontinuity of organic variation, that is, the reality of species. Despite its title, Darwin's book actually dealt only with genetic change within species; it did not directly explain how new species form.

**The contribution of Dobzhansky's 1936 paper**

The 1936 paper was the first genetic analysis of reproductive isolation, the central concept of the 1937 book. Dobzhansky crossed *Drosophila pseudoobscura* and *D. persimilis* (then called "race A" and "race B"), then backcrossed the hybrid females to males of each pure species. Because hybrid males were sterile, the backcross males descended from hybrid females could carry varied combinations of the two species' genes. He measured testis size in each male as an index of sterility.

The result was clear: hybrid male sterility involved at least seven genes located across all four major chromosomes, with the largest effect arising from interactions between the X chromosome of one species and the autosomes of the other. This was direct evidence for Dobzhansky-Muller incompatibilities (DMIs).

**The Dobzhansky-Muller model**

The core of the DMI hypothesis is that when two geographically isolated populations each accumulate allelic changes at different loci, these changes can cause sterility or lethality only in hybrids when brought back together. Because the sterility never arises within either population, it cannot be removed by natural selection. This model provided the first evolutionarily coherent explanation for how reproductive isolation can evolve.

**Historical impact**

After the 1936 paper, speciation genetics stagnated for nearly half a century, then revived with the introduction of molecular markers. When Orr (1987) and Noor et al. (2001) reanalyzed the same two species with more precise markers, the number of sterility genes rose to at least ten. Heterogametic-hybrid sterility (Haldane's rule) and the over-representation of the X chromosome (faster-X evolution) are now recognized as "rules" of speciation, and both phenomena are explained by mechanisms linked to DMIs.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Conveys the meaning of the first genetic analysis of speciation, providing the starting point for the modern species concept: "evolution = allele-frequency change within populations + reproductive isolation."
- Illustrates the classic methodology of backcross experiments and chromosome-marker gene mapping in evolutionary genetics.
- Dobzhansky-Muller incompatibility remains a core concept of speciation research; this piece introduces it in historical context.
- Provides a framework for understanding the origins of Haldane's rule and the "faster-X" effect.

## Future Research Directions

- Develop a general theory unifying why the X chromosome is over-represented in reproductive isolation — faster-X evolution, meiotic drive, recessive DMIs
- Test experimentally whether DMIs can form without geographic isolation (sympatric speciation)
