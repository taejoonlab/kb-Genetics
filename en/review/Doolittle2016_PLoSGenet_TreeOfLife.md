# What Is the Tree of Life?

## Citation (NLM)
Doolittle WF, Brunet TDP. What is the tree of life? PLoS Genet. 2016;12(4):e1005912. doi:10.1371/journal.pgen.1005912

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1005912](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1005912)

---

## Summary

Doolittle and Brunet ask a deceptively simple question — what exactly do we mean by "the Tree of Life" (TOL) — and reveal it to be philosophically and empirically far more fraught than textbook treatments suggest. The essay distinguishes Darwin's original TOL Hypothesis (that the hierarchical pattern of organismal relationships reflects an underlying tree-like evolutionary process) from the molecular phylogenetic enterprise that was supposed to provide independent proof of this hypothesis. When Zuckerkandl and Pauling proposed in 1965 that molecular sequence trees could verify organismal trees, the assumption was that gene replication and mutation are inherently tree-like. For any single gene, this is approximately true — absent recombination. The problem is that different genes, especially in prokaryotes, often give different trees, because lateral (horizontal) gene transfer (LGT) is far more frequent than anyone imagined a generation ago.

The core challenge is quantifying LGT and its impact on genome composition. Only approximately 100 genes are found in all or nearly all prokaryotic genomes — the Nearly Universal Trees (NUTs) identified by Puigbo et al. — and these are predominantly ribosomal and transcriptional genes. The "complexity hypothesis" (Jain et al. 1999) explains their relative resistance to LGT: proteins embedded in large multi-subunit complexes have so many coevolved molecular dependencies that they cannot easily function in a foreign cellular context. For most other genes, the genome is better described as a "pangenome" — a distributed genomic resource in which genes are lost when superfluous and regained by LGT when needed. E. coli exemplifies this: individual strains carry roughly 5,000 genes, but the pangenome of the species approaches 100,000 gene families. Even the NUTs show some topological incongruence, so the "Statistical Tree of Life" (STOL) — the central tendency among NUT topologies — is the best approximation of a universal TOL, but it remains a statistical construct, not a direct record of ancestry.

The question of LUCA's (Last Universal Common Ancestor's) genome is correspondingly uncertain. Two contrasting models emerge. Under a "large LUCA" model, LUCA had a complex genome and descendant lineages have differentially lost gene families; some widely distributed gene families evolved after LUCA and are not detectably homologous to their functional replacements. Under a "uniformitarian" small LUCA model, only ~100 NUT genes were continuously transmitted vertically from LUCA to all contemporary descendants; the vast majority of genes in any contemporary prokaryote arrived by LGT, often from lineages that have since gone extinct. Under this second model, some gene families have coalescent roots that pre-date LUCA, meaning LUCA is the last universal common cellular ancestor, but not necessarily the last universal common genomic ancestor.

Moving from genes to cells, the authors ask whether a tree of cellular lineages is conceptually simpler. Cell theory (all cells from cells) implies a tree-like history of cellular descent in principle. LGT introduces only a little genetic material per event and need not compromise cell-level tree topology — but when LGT events are numerous and involve suites of genes (as in the acquisition of bacterial respiration genes by anaerobic methanogenic Archaea, or the endosymbiotic fusions that gave rise to plastids and mitochondria), even the cellular tree becomes reticulate at key nodes. The origin of eukaryotes, however it is reconstructed, required a fusion of archaeal and bacterial cellular lineages — a reticulation so fundamental that it defines the deepest split in the eukaryotic tree as a chimeric event, not a simple bifurcation.

The essay concludes that what we call "the Tree of Life" is best understood as a heuristic device — valuable as a summary of life's history, imperfect as a literal depiction of ancestry. The STOL constructed from NUTs is probably the best available proxy for a tree of cellular lineages, but it is neither guaranteed to be topologically correct nor representative of the majority of genomic history. More sophisticated conceptual frameworks are needed for microbial evolution, and the public communication of evolutionary biology should reflect this complexity rather than presenting the TOL as a settled structure.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- The paper directly challenges one of the central organizing principles of evolutionary biology — the Tree of Life — by showing that LGT renders any single gene tree an unreliable proxy for organismal history, forcing a rethinking of what "phylogeny" means for prokaryotes.
- The pangenome concept reframes microbial genomics: bacterial and archaeal genomes are not fixed entities but distributed, fluid repertoires shaped as much by horizontal gain and gene loss as by vertical inheritance, fundamentally altering how we interpret sequence-based phylogenies.
- The distinction between a tree of genes, a tree of cells, and a tree of species is conceptually critical for population genetics courses, since population genetics classically assumes vertical inheritance and tree-like divergence — assumptions that are routinely violated in prokaryotic evolution and increasingly recognized as problematic even in eukaryotes (introgression, incomplete lineage sorting).
- The "complexity hypothesis" provides a mechanistic explanation for why core informational genes are more resistant to LGT than metabolic genes, illustrating how network connectivity — not functional category per se — constrains horizontal transfer and shapes what we can confidently place in a universal TOL.
- The endosymbiotic events that produced plastids and mitochondria, and the fusion origin of eukaryotes, are explicitly reticulating events in the cellular tree, underscoring that major evolutionary innovations often involve lineage mergers rather than bifurcations.

## Key References

1. **Doolittle WF (1999)** — https://doi.org/10.1126/science.284.5423.2124 — Foundational critique arguing that LGT is so pervasive in prokaryotes that "there really can be no universal phylogenetic tree of organisms based on such a reduction to genes"
2. **Woese CR, Fox GE (1977)** — https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.74.11.5088 — Discovery of Archaea as a third domain of life using ssu rRNA, which revolutionized systematics by creating the modern three-domain TOL
3. **Jain R, Rivera MC, Lake JA (1999)** — https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.96.7.3801 — Proposed the complexity hypothesis: highly connected genes resist LGT because they have too many coevolved dependencies to function in a foreign cellular environment
4. **Puigbo P, Wolf YI, Koonin EV (2013)** — https://doi.org/10.1186/1741-7007-11-46 — Forest of Life / Nearly Universal Trees analysis showing that NUT topologies are far more congruent than expected by chance, supporting the concept of a Statistical Tree of Life
5. **Nelson KE et al. (1999)** — https://doi.org/10.1038/22154 — Genome sequence of Thermotoga maritima showing ~25% of genes likely acquired from Archaea, the first major genomic evidence that LGT is widespread and not merely disease-related
6. **Lapierre P, Gogarten JP (2009)** — https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tig.2008.12.004 — Pan-genome analysis across 573 bacterial genomes showing that the universal core is only ~8% of a typical bacterial genome, quantifying the scale of non-vertical inheritance
7. **Doolittle WF, Bapteste E (2007)** — https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0610493104 — "Pattern pluralism and the Tree of Life hypothesis" — argues for accepting multiple, non-congruent patterns of evolutionary relationship rather than forcing all data into a single tree

## Future Research Directions

- Developing graph-theoretic or network-based representations of microbial evolutionary history that can integrate both vertical and horizontal inheritance signals without privileging a single tree topology, potentially replacing the TOL metaphor for prokaryotic systematics.
- Improving methods to reconstruct the genome content of LUCA with greater precision, distinguishing between genes lost differentially in descendant lineages, genes that evolved convergently, and genes acquired by LGT from extinct lineages with coalescents predating LUCA.
- Quantifying the rate and directionality of LGT in natural environments using long-read metagenomic approaches that can resolve chromosomal context — necessary for determining what fraction of any contemporary prokaryote's genome is truly vertically inherited.
- Investigating the evolutionary and cellular conditions that allow whole-genome or multi-gene LGT events (as in the archaeal–bacterial merger at the origin of eukaryotes) to be tolerated and fixed, since single-gene LGT theory does not readily explain such large-scale genomic fusions.
- Reassessing TOL-dependent assumptions built into population genetics models for prokaryotes — such as the coalescent and demographic inference frameworks — to produce tools appropriate for organisms with distributed pangenomes and high LGT rates.
