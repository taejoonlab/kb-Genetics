# Molecular Genetics of *Mus musculus*: Point Mutagenesis and Millimorgans

## Citation (NLM)
Dove WF. Molecular genetics of *Mus musculus*: point mutagenesis and millimorgans. Genetics. 1987;116(1):5-8.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/116/1/5](https://genetics.org/content/116/1/5)

---

## Summary

In this *Genetics* "Perspectives" commentary, William F. Dove frames the state of mouse molecular genetics in the mid-1980s using the memorable metaphor of an "intellectual food chain," in which approaches developed in phage, yeast, and *Drosophila* genetics flow "upward" into the more complex mouse system. His central problem is a practical one: how can one connect a mutant phenotype in the mouse to a specific stretch of cloned DNA, given the enormous gap between genetic map distances (measured in centimorgans) and physical distances (measured in kilobases and cosmid-sized clones)?

Dove first surveys classical mouse genetics, which rested heavily on natural variants — often "patent lethals" (Haldane's term) such as *Agouti-yellow*, *Steel*, and dominant *White-spotting*, alleles that show a heterozygous visible phenotype and a homozygous lethal one. The recessive *t* haplotypes of chromosome 17 are presented as an especially intricate case: a set of shared inversions suppresses recombination with wild type, genetically isolating the *t* complex so that it accumulates a "plethora" of polymorphisms (about 0.007 substitutions per base pair). These natural variants are a rich lode of developmental defects but an embarrassing molecular tangle — motivating a shift from depending on natural variation to *inducing* mutations on a clean genetic background.

The heart of the piece contrasts two experimental strategies. The first is insertional mutagenesis with cloned DNA elements (retroviruses, transgenes), which gives direct molecular access to the disrupted locus but suffers from two problems: retroviral insertions exert position effects over kilobase distances, blurring which sequence is really responsible for gene function, and the insertion frequency per locus is very low (10⁻⁵ or less), requiring impractically large pedigree screens. The second strategy, imported directly from *Drosophila* genetics via Muller and Haldane's breeding schemes, is chemical point mutagenesis with ethylnitrosourea (ENU). ENU is a potent alkylating agent that needs no metabolic activation, produces predominantly GC→AT transitions, and — as Russell and colleagues showed at Oak Ridge — yields forward mutation frequencies of about 0.0007 per locus in spermatogonia, high enough for a laboratory that can only handle a few hundred pedigrees per year.

Dove then reports the first attempts to "saturate" a defined region of the mouse genome (the *t*/*T-H-2* region) with induced recessive lethals, work from his own Wisconsin laboratory with Guénet at the Institut Pasteur, which uncovered perhaps 50-100 lethal complementation groups. This leads to a quantitative discussion of gene density: extrapolation suggests roughly 5,000-10,000 single-copy vital genes in the whole genome, implying a spacing no closer than ~250 kb between vital loci, so that assigning a point mutation to a cosmid-sized (~50 kb) segment would require mapping to about 0.02 cM — far beyond then-current resolution. He closes by noting the paradox that the frequency of vital loci is up to tenfold lower than the frequency of transcribed regions or CpG (HTF) islands, and, drawing on Goebl and Petes' yeast work, speculates that genetic redundancy hides much of the functional genome — "the rest of the iceberg is invisible to us."

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Crystallizes the core tension in reverse-vs-forward genetics: whether to start from a cloned DNA element (insertional mutagenesis) or from an induced phenotype (chemical mutagenesis), and the trade-offs of each.
- Introduces foundational concepts an intro student should own: centimorgans vs. physical distance, the genetic-to-physical map gap, complementation groups, saturation mutagenesis, and gene density estimates.
- Explains why ENU became the workhorse point mutagen of mouse genetics and how *Drosophila* breeding logic (balancers, recessive markers) was transplanted to mammals.
- Shows how quantitative reasoning (mutation frequency, target size, map resolution) turns a vague ambition ("clone the gene") into a tractable experimental design.

## Key References

1. **Russell et al. (1979)** — Specific-locus test showing ENU to be the most potent mouse mutagen — established the germline mutation frequencies that made saturation screens feasible.
2. **Shedlovsky et al. (1986)** — ENU induction of recessive lethals in the *T/t-H-2* region — the first regional saturation mutagenesis in the mouse and source of the 50-100 complementation-group estimate.
3. **Haldane (1956)** — Detection of autosomal lethals in mice — worked out the breeding systems for whole-genome recessive-lethal screening.
4. **Goebl and Petes (1986)** — Most yeast single-copy sequences are dispensable — the basis for Dove's argument that genetic redundancy conceals much of the functional genome.

---

## Future Research Directions

- The genetic-to-physical mapping gap Dove describes was ultimately closed by positional cloning, dense marker maps, and whole-genome sequencing; his saturation-mutagenesis vision matured into large-scale ENU phenotype-driven screens and, later, systematic knockout consortia (e.g., IMPC).
- His closing puzzle — why so many transcribed genes give no mutant phenotype — remains alive today as the study of genetic redundancy, buffering, and gene essentiality now probed genome-wide by CRISPR screens.
