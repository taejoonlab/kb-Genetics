# The Year of the Fly

## Citation (NLM)
Dove WF. The year of the fly. Genetics. 1988;118(2):169-171.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/118/2/169](https://genetics.org/content/118/2/169)

---

## Summary

In this *Genetics* "Perspectives" essay, William F. Dove celebrates a remarkable year (marked, playfully, from one Chinese New Year to the next) in which molecular biology revealed deep evolutionary conservation between *Drosophila* developmental regulators and vertebrate proteins involved in cancer. Just three years after the discovery of the near-perfect homeodomain identities between fly and vertebrate proteins, a series of 1987 papers extended such relationships beyond short domains to substantial stretches — sometimes entire genes — linking normal developmental control in the fly to oncogenes in vertebrates. Dove asks whether the "sentences" of metazoan development share universal "syllables" (domains) or even complete "words" (genes), echoing the universality of the genetic code.

He catalogs the striking sequence relationships. The *Drosophila* homolog of the Abelson (*abl*) proto-oncogene shares 75% amino-acid identity over a 469-residue segment with mammalian c-Abl, and both products autophosphorylate tyrosine — strong evidence of common descent and shared function, though their carboxy-terminal segments diverge. The fly segment-polarity gene *wingless* proved identical to *int-1*, the integration site activated by mouse mammary tumor virus in mammary carcinomas, with 54% amino-acid similarity and conservation of 23 of 25 cysteines. Further relationships joined the list: the *dorsal* maternal-effect gene product resembles the v-*rel* oncogene; the *sevenless* product carries a tyrosine-kinase domain like the Ros oncogene. Comparisons were becoming two-way, as the *Drosophila* genome itself contains perhaps 25 loci that mutate to recessive alleles producing transplantable embryonic hyperplasias (tumors). Dove adds a cautionary note about over-reading computer-detected sequence resemblances: for *lethal(2)giant larvae*, one group found "cell-adhesion" motif similarities, yet another showed the gene retains full function when most of those residues are deleted — "Beware computers bearing gifts!"

The intellectual heart of the essay asks whether "the fly can talk with the mouse about neoplasia." Dove combines two questions: are there universal developmental "words," and can understanding normal proto-oncogene function illuminate the transforming oncogenes of cancer? He notes that all known *Drosophila* transplantable-tumor mutations are recessive and none map to vertebrate-oncogene homologs — raising whether these recessive fly loci are counterparts to vertebrate "recessive oncogenes" (tumor suppressors) such as retinoblastoma and Wilms' tumor, which act recessively at the cellular level through somatic loss of heterozygosity despite dominant inheritance. He reasons about a "dominant-lethal filter" that may remove such genes from populations, and proposes an experimental challenge shared by fly and mouse geneticists: to find genetic-background or environmental conditions that modulate the dominant somatic penetrance of recessive neoplasm-inducing alleles.

Dove closes by envisioning an "extended genetical dialog" made possible by two technical advances he saw converging: improved chemical (ENU) germline mutagenesis in the mouse, and gene targeting by homologous recombination in embryo-derived stem cells (Thomas and Capecchi 1987; Doetschman et al. 1987). Together these would let concrete molecular entities discovered in *Drosophila* by selectional genetics be tested in vertebrates by making null alleles of their mouse counterparts. With characteristic wit — paraphrasing Adlai Stevenson, "Metaphor is all right, if you don't inhale" — he anticipates the coming Year of the Dragon.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A vivid, contemporaneous snapshot of the discovery of deep homology between invertebrate and vertebrate genes — a founding idea of evolutionary developmental biology (evo-devo).
- Introduces proto-oncogenes vs. oncogenes, tumor suppressors, loss of heterozygosity, and dominant vs. recessive gene action at organism vs. cellular level.
- Captures the exact historical moment when gene targeting in ES cells (later a Nobel-winning technology) and ENU mutagenesis promised to unite fly forward genetics with mouse reverse genetics.
- Models scientific caution: sequence similarity is a hypothesis, not proof of function ("Beware computers bearing gifts").

## Key References

1. **Rijsewijk et al. (1987)** — *Drosophila wingless* is identical to the mouse *int-1* oncogene — a landmark demonstration that a developmental gene and a cancer gene are the same.
2. **Thomas and Capecchi (1987)** — Site-directed mutagenesis by gene targeting in ES cells — the reverse-genetics technique Dove foresaw uniting fly and mouse work.
3. **Cavenee, Koufos, Hansen (1986)** — Recessive mutant genes predisposing to human cancer — framed the tumor-suppressor / loss-of-heterozygosity concept central to the essay.
4. **Gateff (1982)** — Cancer, genes, and development: the *Drosophila* case — catalogued the recessive fly tumor loci Dove discusses.

## Future Research Directions

- The fly-to-mouse dialog Dove envisioned became routine: genes discovered in *Drosophila* screens are now validated by targeted knockouts and CRISPR in mice, and Wnt (wingless/int-1) signaling is central to both development and cancer.
- His question about modifiers of the "dominant somatic penetrance" of recessive cancer alleles prefigures modern work on genetic modifiers, penetrance, and tumor-suppressor haploinsufficiency.
