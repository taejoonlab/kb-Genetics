# Weaving a Tapestry from Threads Spun by Geneticists: The Series Perspectives on Genetics, 1987–2008

## Citation (NLM)
Dove WF. Weaving a tapestry from threads spun by geneticists: the series Perspectives on Genetics, 1987–2008. Genetics. 2016;203(3):1011-1022. doi:10.1534/genetics.116.191155.

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.191155](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.191155)

## Summary

William F. Dove reflects on the *Perspectives* column of the journal *Genetics* — "Anecdotal, Historical, and Critical Commentaries" — which he co-edited with James Crow from its 1987 founding (at the invitation of editor-in-chief Jan Drake) through 2008. Rather than a history or textbook, the series became a "tapestry" of personal stories told by geneticists themselves, woven across the whole span of 20th-century genetics. Dove explains the editors' working method: each year they scanned the tables of contents of *Genetics* from 5, 10, 15, 25, or 50 years earlier, and consulted Sturtevant's history, to identify anniversaries worth commemorating and to invite the geneticists who had been present at the birth of a topic and had watched it mature. Crow, born in 1916 (the year *Genetics* was founded), personally knew many population and evolutionary geneticists; Dove brought a molecular and developmental network — a complementarity that let the pair reach authors worldwide.

The essay is organized around three recurrent themes. The first is *how geneticists have functioned* — as solitary investigators, in pairs, or in cooperative groups. Solitary pioneers include Gustave Malécot (whose profound population-genetics dissertation went long unrecognized), Nikolai Vavilov (whose seed-collection program and life were destroyed by Stalin's embrace of Lysenko's Lamarckism), Leo Szilard the "lone wolf," and Julius Adler (who chose *E. coli* for chemotaxis precisely for its genetic tractability). Crow's final article, "Just and Unjust," used the case of the African-American biologist Ernest Everett Just to illuminate the institutional prejudice that isolated an exceptional scientist. The "Power of Two" section celebrates partnerships — Watson and Crick, Meselson and Stahl, Jacob and Monod, Crow and Kimura — and more nuanced pairs, including husband-and-wife teams (the Lederbergs, the Perkinses in *Neurospora*, Silflow and Lefebvre in *Chlamydomonas*), the "Mom and Pop Editorial Shop" of Jan and Pamela Drake, and complementary microbial systems (Shapiro's *Caulobacter* and Kaiser's *Myxobacter*). Cooperative groups trace back to the maize geneticists' remarkably open sharing of materials and unpublished data (Emerson, 1932), a paradigm replicated by the *C. elegans* community around Sydney Brenner and by other organism-specific groups.

A second theme is *how geneticists work hard yet have fun*. Dove recounts Crow's lifelong devotion to the viola (and the classic prank in which lab mates hid anesthetized fruit flies inside it, so they fluttered out of the F-holes mid-concert), Guido Pontecorvo's passion for alpine plants, and Hitoshi Kihara's athletics and skiing — humanizing portraits that show the creative life outside the laboratory.

The third theme is *how public and private institutions sustained genetics in the United States*. Dove surveys the Bussey Institution at Harvard (whose students seeded the early leadership of the Genetics Society of America before it closed in 1938), Caltech (Morgan's *Drosophila* mecca, reinvented under Beadle and Delbrück's phage "church"), the marine stations at Woods Hole and Pacific Grove (where C. B. van Niel indirectly shaped the microbial choices of Adler, Shapiro, and Kaiser), and the independent laboratories at Jackson (JAX, which survived its 1947 fire through gifts of mice from geneticists worldwide) and Cold Spring Harbor (CSHL, home to Demerec and to the influential Phage and Yeast Genetics courses). He also treats the research "substratum" — NIH/NSF peer review and the culture of study sections. Dove closes by reflecting on genetics' 21st-century trajectory: the rise of "Security Council" model organisms and organism-specific diaspora, offset by unifying tools that transcend species (ENU mutagenesis, CRISPR/Cas genome editing, genomics). He argues that mutational analysis and genetic perturbation remain the core strategy of the discipline, and that the GSA and *Perspectives* have a continuing role in sustaining that shared core science.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Offers a panoramic, human-centered map of 20th-century genetics — the people, partnerships, institutions, and communities behind the concepts students learn.
- Shows that scientific progress is social: solitary genius is the exception, and cooperation (sharing strains, open data, communities) has been decisive.
- Connects historical figures (Morgan, Muller, McClintock, Brenner, Beadle, Delbrück) to the institutions and model organisms that anchor modern genetics.
- Serves as an annotated gateway to the entire Crow–Dove *Perspectives* collection, a rich resource for deeper reading in genetics history.

## Key References

1. **Crow (1993, 1994, 2008)** — *Perspectives* essays on Vavilov, Kihara, and E. E. Just — exemplify the series' blend of science, politics, and biography.
2. **Hodgkin (1989)** — "Early Worms" — chronicles the *C. elegans* community around Brenner as a model cooperative group.
3. **Sturtevant (1965)** — *A History of Genetics* — the reference the editors used to identify anniversaries worth commemorating.
4. **Fink (1998)** — the "Security Council of model genetic organisms" framing — captures the late-century shift to organism-centered genetics.
5. **Brenner, Dove, Herskowitz & Thomas (1990)** — "Genes and development: molecular and logical themes," *Genetics* — illustrates cross-organism synthesis at the core of the science.

## Future Research Directions

- Species-transcending tools — efficient random mutagenesis (ENU), targeted genome editing (CRISPR/Cas), and network-level clustering of gene function — extend classical mutational analysis into non-model organisms, one of Dove's explicit hopes for the discipline's future.
- The integration of reductionist 20th-century genetics with systems- and population-level genomics remains an open program for sustaining genetics as a unified science.
