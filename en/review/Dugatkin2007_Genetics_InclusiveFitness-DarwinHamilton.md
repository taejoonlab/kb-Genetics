# Inclusive Fitness Theory from Darwin to Hamilton

## Citation (NLM)
Dugatkin LA. Inclusive fitness theory from Darwin to Hamilton. Genetics. 2007;176(3):1375-1380.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/176/3/1375](https://genetics.org/content/176/3/1375)

---

## Summary

This Perspectives essay traces the century-long path from Darwin's puzzlement over altruism to William D. Hamilton's formalization of inclusive fitness theory. Dugatkin begins with the central problem: if reproduction is the currency of natural selection, altruists — organisms that sacrifice their own fitness for others — should vanish quickly. Sterile worker bees that risk their lives for the hive troubled Darwin so deeply that he called the problem "one special difficulty, which at first appeared to me to be insuperable, and actually fatal to the whole theory." Darwin's own resolution, in a section of *On the Origin of Species*, was that selection "may be applied to the family, as well as the individual" — blood kinship was the key to explaining both sterile insect castes and altruism.

The essay then follows the idea through a series of figures who came close but stopped short. The Huxley–Kropotkin debate was more philosophical than scientific: Thomas Henry Huxley (Darwin's "Bulldog") saw nature as a gladiatorial "war of each against all," with altruism confined to blood relatives, while Peter Kropotkin, the anarchist naturalist, saw "mutual aid" everywhere and divorced from kinship. Neither formalized a theory or ran experiments. The three founders of mathematical population genetics — Haldane, Fisher, and Wright — each approached the problem but never built the general model. Haldane's famous pub quip (he would jump into a river to save two brothers or eight cousins) and his 1955 population-genetics paper captured the gene-counting logic precisely, yet he never wrote the general cost–benefit equation. Fisher, in "The evolution of distastefulness," reasoned that noxious flavors in insect larvae could evolve because a predator, having tasted one distasteful sibling, would spare its relatives — but he too dropped the matter. Wright, who invented *r* as the coefficient of relatedness, built a group-selection model of altruism but never linked it to relatedness.

Dugatkin considers why the "grand triumvirate" never took the final step. Explanations include: they may have thought the subject uninteresting (unlikely, since each wrote about it); "mathematical arrogance" (also unlikely, given their competitive drive to publish); lack of field natural-history experience (Reeve and Seeley's suggestion); and, most persuasively, Alan Grafen's point that the economic, optimization-based framework needed to model cost, benefit, and relatedness as variables was not yet in place. Whatever the reason, when William Hamilton published his seminal models in 1963 and 1964, he explicitly acknowledged his debt to Haldane, Wright, and Fisher.

Hamilton's own story is one of intellectual isolation. As a Cambridge undergraduate he found many biologists skeptical of natural selection; when he sought to combine genetics, evolution, and human altruism through a social-anthropology course, both the anthropologist Edmund Leach and the geneticists rebuffed him. Undeterred, he did his doctoral work jointly at University College London and the London School of Economics, where exposure to economic cost–benefit analysis proved decisive. His 1963 *American Naturalist* paper introduced the first "inclusive" model of altruism, and his 1964 *Journal of Theoretical Biology* papers gave the full mathematical treatment. Using Wright's *r* together with the cost (*c*) and benefit (*b*) of altruism, Hamilton showed that selection favors an altruism gene whenever *rb > c* — now known as Hamilton's Rule: the cost of altruism can be offset by benefits to relatives, weighted by the probability *r* that they share the gene. The essay closes with the tragic story of George Price, who extended Hamilton's models with covariance analysis (deriving the Price equation and a model of spite), underwent a religious conversion, and took his own life in 1975; and with Hamilton's own death in 2000 from malaria contracted in the Congo. Hamilton's 1963–1964 articles are among the most cited in behavioral evolution and are widely taken to mark the birth of sociobiology.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Introduces Hamilton's Rule (*rb > c*) and the concept of inclusive fitness, a cornerstone linking genetics, natural selection, and social behavior.
- Shows how the coefficient of relatedness *r* — a population-genetics quantity — becomes the bridge between gene-level selection and the evolution of altruism and cooperation.
- Demonstrates how a major conceptual advance can lie dormant for decades even when the leading minds have all the pieces, illustrating the role of intellectual frameworks (here, economic optimization) in scientific discovery.
- Connects abstract theory to vivid natural-history examples (worker bees, distasteful larvae, drowning relatives) that make selection at the level of kin intuitive for students.

## Key References

1. **Darwin (1859)** — *On the Origin of Species* — framed the paradox of sterile castes and proposed selection at the level of the family.
2. **Haldane (1955)** — "Population genetics," *New Biol.* 18:34-51 — the drowning-relative scenario capturing the gene-counting logic of kin selection.
3. **Hamilton (1964)** — "The genetical evolution of social behaviour I and II," *J. Theor. Biol.* 7:1-52 — the full mathematical foundation of inclusive fitness theory.
4. **Fisher (1958)** — *The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection*, 2nd ed. — the "evolution of distastefulness" argument that nearly reached a kinship model.
5. **Price (1970)** — "Selection and covariance," *Nature* 227:520-521 — the covariance approach that generalized Hamilton's models and yielded a theory of spite.

## Future Research Directions
