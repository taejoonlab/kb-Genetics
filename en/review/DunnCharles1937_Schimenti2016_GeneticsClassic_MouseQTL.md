# L. C. Dunn and Donald Charles on Quantitative Traits in the Mouse

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.187534](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.187534)

## Summary

This brief Classic appreciation by John Schimenti (Cornell University) introduces the 1937 paper by Leslie C. Dunn and Donald R. Charles, "Studies on Spotting Patterns I: Analysis of Quantitative Variations in the Pied Spotting of the House Mouse," published in the first volume of *Genetics*.

**The biological problem:** Mice homozygous for the "piebald" mutation (gene s) showed an extreme range of coat patterns from nearly all-white to nearly fully pigmented. Dunn—founder of the Genetics Society of America and a legend in mouse genetics for his work on coat color and t haplotypes—became fascinated by the question of whether this variation was genetic, stochastic, or both, and how many genes might be involved.

**The experimental design:** Dunn and his graduate student Donald Charles constructed new inbred strains selected for extreme phenotypes, developed a quantitative piebald grading scale, and performed F1 crosses and backcrosses. This design reads remarkably like a modern QTL study—minus the actual gene mapping, which was molecularly impossible at the time. By applying their grading scale to large offspring cohorts, they characterized the inheritance patterns of s modifier loci in terms of transmission modes, number of modifier genes, and the contribution of individual unlinked alleles.

**Findings:** The phenotypic variation in s homozygotes was influenced by multiple modifier loci. Some of these modifiers were themselves capable of causing pigmentation abnormalities on their own. The observation that inbreeding led to more consistent phenotypes confirmed that the variation had a heritable genetic component. Subsequent papers in the series (five total) extended the analysis to the W (Kit) locus and examined epistatic interactions.

**Historical significance:** The s gene is now known as Ednrb (endothelin receptor type B), a gene implicated in Hirschsprung disease and Waardenburg syndrome in humans. Dunn and Charles's 1937 analysis of modifier loci influencing a single-gene pigmentation defect is a precursor to modern genetic modifier screens and QTL mapping studies of human disease phenotype variability.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Shows that quantitative trait variation in mammals—even in 1937, without molecular tools—could be analyzed through careful crossing designs and phenotypic scoring, anticipating modern QTL mapping
- Demonstrates that single-gene mutations can produce variable phenotypes depending on genetic background—the concept of modifier loci is fundamental to understanding disease penetrance and expressivity
- Provides historical context for why mouse genetics became a powerful system for studying mammalian quantitative traits

- Apply Dunn-Charles-style quantitative genetic analysis to other developmentally variable single-gene disease models to identify genetic background effects relevant to human disease penetrance
