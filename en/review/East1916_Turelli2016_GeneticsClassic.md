## Summary

This Classic appreciation by Michael Turelli (UC Davis) contextualizes East's 1916 *Nicotiana* paper within the broader intellectual conflict between Mendelism and Galton's biometrics, and critically assesses both the achievement and the limitations of East's interpretation.

**The Mendel-Galton synthesis problem:** Mendel studied discontinuous traits with clear ratios. Galton studied continuous traits—seed-pod length, human height—and found normal distributions and "regression toward mediocrity." When Mendel's principles were rediscovered in 1900, reconciling them with Galton's biometrical observations became the central challenge. The resolution required two key elements: recognizing that continuous variation often results from multiple Mendelian factors acting additively, and that environmental effects contribute additional variance. Both appear in Mendel's own 1866 paper, but were not synthesized until the early 20th century.

**The Johannsen–Nilsson-Ehle–East trio:** Turelli credits three plant geneticists with demonstrating the Mendelian basis of continuous variation. Johannsen (1903) demonstrated that phenotypic differences between pure lines of Phaseolus vulgaris were heritable, while variation within pure lines was not. Nilsson-Ehle (1909) showed that wheat kernel color variation was controlled by roughly equal, additive effects at three loci. East (1916) provided the most integrated demonstration, combining environmental effects and polygenic inheritance in a single experiment using Nicotiana longiflora corolla length.

**The Wright-Castle estimator:** East's data became a standard teaching example for the Wright-Castle estimator—a formula for estimating the minimum number of loci contributing to a quantitative parental difference. As a graduate student, Sewall Wright derived the formula n = (P₁−P₂)² / [8(VF₂ − VE)], where VE is estimated as the average parental variance. Applied to East's data, n ≈ 9.6. Castle (1921) published the result without acknowledging Wright's contribution—a notable example of academic credit attribution issues.

**A cautionary note on interpretation:** Turelli points out an irony: East himself viewed his study as evidence that "Galtonian regression in the original sense is now entirely discredited," when in fact his data beautifully demonstrated the reconciliation of Mendel and Galton—precisely what quantitative genetics would formalize. East's misinterpretation stemmed partly from the relatively small sample sizes that produced high-variance regression estimates. Turelli also notes East's troubling role in promoting racial anxiety in the U.S. eugenics movement through his status as an eminent Harvard geneticist—a reminder that scientific authority carries ethical responsibilities.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Explains the intellectual stakes of the Mendel-Galton conflict and why its resolution by the Johannsen-Nilsson-Ehle-East trio mattered for all of modern genetics
- Introduces the Wright-Castle estimator for minimum number of QTLs—a key pedagogical tool for understanding quantitative trait genetics
- Illustrates how the same data can be correctly interpreted (as supporting Mendel-Galton reconciliation) or incorrectly interpreted (as refuting Galton), depending on analytical framework—a lesson in scientific inference
