# R. A. Fisher's 1943 Unravelling of the Rhesus Blood-Group System

## Citation (NLM)
Edwards AWF. R. A. Fisher's 1943 unravelling of the Rhesus blood-group system. Genetics. 2007;175(2):471-476.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/175/2/471](https://genetics.org/content/175/2/471)

---

## Summary

This GENETICS "Perspectives" piece by A. W. F. Edwards recounts how R. A. Fisher, in 1943, deduced the genetic structure of the Rhesus (Rh) blood-group system through pure analytical reasoning. Where the serological data were an intimidating tangle of antigens and antibodies, Fisher proposed that they could be explained by three closely linked loci—which he designated C, D, and E—each carrying a pair of allelic antigens (C/c, D/d, E/e). He represented the eight heritable antigen complexes geometrically as the corners of a cube and the six elementary antigens as its faces, an elegant device that turned a confusing array of reactions into an ordered system. Edwards notes that although the story is often told, his purpose here is to gather, for the first time in one place, five illustrations belonging to it—including Fisher's own solution scribbled on the back of a piece of Caius College notepaper and a beer-stained sheet on which he later sketched his crossing-over hypothesis.

The setting was wartime Cambridge, where in 1943 Fisher was reunited with his former London colleague R. R. Race. Their discussions took place partly in The Bun Shop, a Cambridge public house, over pints of beer. Race, then registered for a Cambridge Ph.D., published the pivotal note in Nature ("An 'incomplete' antibody in human serum," 1944) in which the C, c, D, d, E, e notation—Fisher's idea—first appeared; with characteristic generosity Fisher declined co-authorship. Race famously recalled that the initial reaction to Fisher's cube diagram was "puzzlement and lack of understanding," but that once understood, "thereafter it was impossible to believe anything else." Fisher further predicted haplotypes and antibodies not yet observed (such as the anti-d antibody, still undiscovered), and later reasoned that rare Rh chromosomes could arise by crossing-over in heterozygotes, leading him to propose the chromosomal order DCE with C and E close together and D more distant.

Edwards emphasizes Fisher's combinatorial genius as the background to this achievement. Already in his landmark 1918 paper on the correlation between relatives, Fisher had shown how a two-locus, two-allele system maps onto a single four-allele locus—the two-locus precursor of the three-locus CDE scheme. This same combinatorial insight was what allowed him to see that the Rhesus data could be represented either as a single multiallelic locus (the notation favored by co-discoverer A. S. Wiener) or as three fully linked diallelic loci (Fisher's solution). Fisher also acknowledged the influence of Bernstein's earlier triallelic analysis of the ABO system and of Ernst's interpretation of the Primula incompatibility system as a complex of linked loci.

The article also documents the long and increasingly bitter dispute between Fisher and Wiener, who never accepted the CDE notation and repeatedly criticized it; Edwards recalls witnessing Fisher ostentatiously walk out of Wiener's lecture at the 1961 human genetics conference in Rome. In a personal coda, Edwards—sent as a student by Fisher to learn blood-grouping in Race and Sanger's laboratory—describes his own later work representing Rhesus haplotype frequencies as a metrical (area-proportional) Venn diagram, which visually confirms Fisher's observation that the chromosomes are not in linkage equilibrium and fall into common, rare, and effectively absent classes. Modern molecular biology has substantially vindicated Fisher: D is now known to be one gene, while C and E correspond to alternative splice forms of a second gene—"Fisher's solution is recognizable beneath the modern molecular detail."

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A vivid case study of how linkage, allelism, and haplotype structure can be inferred from phenotypic (serological) data alone, before any molecular tools existed—illustrating the deductive power of transmission genetics.
- Shows the conceptual equivalence between "one multiallelic locus" and "several tightly linked diallelic loci," a subtlety that recurs throughout genetics and is beautifully concrete here.
- Introduces crossing-over and linkage disequilibrium as explanations for the relative frequencies of haplotypes, connecting recombination theory to real human genetic data.
- Students should read it as an example of scientific method (Fisher's own phrase) and of how a bold, testable model that predicts unobserved entities later confirmed becomes compelling.

## Future Research Directions

- The molecular refinement of the RHD/RHCE gene pair continues to inform transfusion medicine and prevention of hemolytic disease of the newborn; Fisher's linkage framework still frames how RHD variants and haplotypes are catalogued.
- Fisher's crossing-over versus mutation explanation for rare haplotypes (e.g., the extremely rare CdE) remains a template for reasoning about the origin of rare recombinant haplotypes in modern population and medical genetics.
