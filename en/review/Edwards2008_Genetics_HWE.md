# G. H. Hardy (1908) and Hardy–Weinberg Equilibrium

## Citation (NLM)

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.104.92940](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.104.92940)

## Summary

This Perspectives article by A. W. F. Edwards (Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge) provides a careful historical reconstruction of the Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium, establishing that Weinberg's derivation preceded Hardy's and that both were delayed by the unnecessary biometrician-Mendelian conflict in Britain.

**Weinberg's prior derivation:** On January 13, 1908, Wilhelm Weinberg addressed an evening meeting in Stuttgart and derived the equilibrium principle for a single locus with two alleles, starting from arbitrary homozygote frequencies m and n (m+n=1): after one generation of random mating, the genotype frequencies stabilize at m²AA : 2mn Aa : n²aa and remain unchanged. He used the binomial theorem directly. Edwards emphasizes that Weinberg's solution was both earlier and more straightforward than Hardy's.

**The British delay:** In England, the bitter animosity between Pearson's biometricians and Bateson's Mendelians so clouded scientific discourse that the simple question—why do dominant phenotypes not increase in frequency over time in a random-mating population?—was not answered for several years. Both Yule (1902) and Pearson (1904) had come close: Yule established the result for gene frequency 1/2; Pearson did so for the same special case while developing a "generalized theory of alternative inheritance," but both failed to generalize because they assumed the gene frequency was necessarily 1/2.

**Hardy's involvement:** On February 28, 1908, R. C. Punnett gave a lecture on "Mendelism in relation to disease" to the Epidemiological Section of the Royal Society of Medicine. Unable to answer an audience question about why dominant traits do not increase in frequency, Punnett took the question to his mathematician friend G. H. Hardy (then a Trinity College mathematician; later famous for number theory). Hardy immediately derived the answer—the answer is a direct consequence of Mendel's law of segregation—and published a short letter in Science in July 1908.

**Hardy's key insight:** Hardy recognized that the problem reduces to the observation that, under Mendelian inheritance, expected allele frequencies among offspring equal those among parents, regardless of dominance, provided both sexes are equally represented and mating is random. After one generation of random mating, genotype frequencies reach p²AA : 2pq Aa : q²aa and remain stable.

**Priority:** Edwards concludes that while "Hardy-Weinberg" is an appropriate name, Weinberg independently derived the general case before Hardy, and the naming should acknowledge both. The key intellectual connection is simple: HWE is a direct consequence of Mendel's first law; the problem arose only because the biometrician-Mendelian divide prevented people from reading Mendel's paper carefully.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides historical context for population genetics' most fundamental law—HWE is not an independent discovery but a direct consequence of Mendel's first law
- Shows how scientific tribalism (biometricians vs. Mendelians) can delay solutions to problems that are, in retrospect, trivially simple
- Illustrates that a pure mathematician (Hardy) with no biological intuition could immediately solve a "biological" problem that biologists had struggled with for years—by simply doing the algebra
