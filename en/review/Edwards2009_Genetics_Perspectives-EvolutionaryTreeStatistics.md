# Statistical Methods for Evolutionary Trees

## Citation (NLM)
Edwards AWF. Statistical methods for evolutionary trees. Genetics. 2009 Sep;183(1):5–12. doi:10.1534/genetics.109.107847

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.109.107847](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.109.107847)

---

## Summary

This Perspectives article by A. W. F. Edwards (Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge) is a first-person historical account of how he and L. L. Cavalli-Sforza developed the three foundational methods for constructing evolutionary trees from genetic data during an intense period of collaboration at the University of Pavia, Italy, in 1963–64.

**Fisher's intellectual legacy:** Edwards frames the entire account against the background of R. A. Fisher's influence. Fisher established quantitative statistical genetics at Cambridge and in 1935 created a Serological Unit at the Galton Laboratory to study human blood groups as genetic markers. One of Fisher's Cambridge recruits was the young Italian bacteriologist Luca Cavalli (later Cavalli-Sforza), who joined Fisher's lab in 1948 and later recalled that "the place was saturated with mathematical theorizing" and that it was here he conceived the idea of reconstructing where human populations originated.

**The Pavia collaboration:** In 1961, Cavalli-Sforza moved to a professorship at the University of Pavia with a new computer (Olivetti Elea 6001) and research funds. He returned to Cambridge and recruited Edwards—the last undergraduate Fisher admitted before retirement—because Edwards had trained himself to use the Cambridge EDSAC 2 computer. In 1963 they relocated to Pavia, where they developed three methods for constructing evolutionary trees from blood-group gene frequency data:

1. **Least-squares additive tree:** Compute pairwise population distances from allele frequencies; fit a tree that minimizes the squared deviations between observed and tree-predicted distances
2. **Minimum evolution / maximum parsimony:** Select the tree topology that minimizes total evolutionary change (branch lengths); equivalent to maximum parsimony for certain models
3. **Maximum likelihood:** Find the tree structure and branch lengths that maximize the probability of generating the observed data under a stochastic evolutionary model—the first application of maximum likelihood to phylogenetic reconstruction

These methods were developed before DNA sequences were available; the initial data were human blood-group gene frequencies. Nevertheless, they directly underlie modern molecular phylogenetics.

**Historiographic caveat:** Edwards openly acknowledges the difficulty of writing history in which one was a participant: memory is fallible and potentially self-serving. He quotes historian K. O. May: "the hope of finding a 'first' comes to grief because of the historically dynamic character of ideas." Every scientific advance is an intersection of preceding lines of thought.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the origin story of maximum likelihood and parsimony in phylogenetics—methods that are now standard in every molecular evolution course and software package (PAUP, RAxML, BEAST)
- Shows how Fisher's statistical framework, applied to human genetic data, directly enabled the development of phylogenetic tree methods
- Demonstrates that the key ingredients for a scientific breakthrough—the right idea, the right person, and the right technology (computers)—must converge: Cavalli's idea from Fisher's Cambridge lab needed Pavia's computer to be realized
