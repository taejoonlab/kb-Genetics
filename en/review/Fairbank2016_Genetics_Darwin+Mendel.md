# Darwin's Influence on Mendel: Evidence from a New Translation of Mendel's Paper

## Citation (NLM)
Fairbanks DJ, Abbott S. Darwin's influence on Mendel: evidence from a new translation of Mendel's paper. Genetics. 2016 Oct;204(2):401–405. doi:10.1534/genetics.116.194613

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.194613](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.194613)

---

## Summary

This Perspectives article by Daniel Fairbanks and Scott Abbott (Utah Valley University) presents a new "Darwinized" English translation of Mendel's 1866 paper *Versuche über Pflanzen-Hybriden* and provides evidence that Darwin's *Origin of Species* directly influenced the language and framing of Mendel's paper—particularly in its final two sections.

**The overlooked direction:** Much historical discussion has focused on the lament that Darwin never read Mendel's paper. This article redirects attention to the reverse relationship: Mendel read Darwin. Mendel owned a personal copy of the German translation of *Origin of Species* (Bronn translation, published 1863), and it contains his own marginalia. The publication date is critical: 1863 was the final year of Mendel's pea experiments, meaning he obtained the book just as he was beginning to write up his results (1863–1865).

**Evidence from the marginalia and distribution of Darwinian terms:** The marginalia show that Mendel read and engaged with specific passages in *Origin*. More tellingly, the authors found that the density of Darwinian terminology in Mendel's paper is substantially higher in the final two sections (sections 10 and 11)—the sections where Mendel reflects on the evolutionary implications of his findings—compared to the experimental sections. One key paragraph in particular shows a clustering of Darwinian terms, providing strong linguistic evidence that Darwin's writing influenced Mendel's interpretive framing.

**The Darwinized translation:** The authors prepared a new translation by comparing German terms in Mendel's paper with the same terms in the German *Origin* he owned, then using Darwin's counterpart English words wherever possible. This produces a translation that reveals the Darwin-influenced character of Mendel's final sections more clearly than previous translations.

**Problems with existing translations:** The Druery-Bateson translation (1901)—the most widely used English version, now freely available online—is described as seriously flawed. The Sherwood-Stern translation (1966) is more accurate but remains under copyright and is difficult to access. Both fail to capture the Darwinian linguistic character of Mendel's final sections. The new Darwinized translation is freely available in open-access format.

**The Fisher-Mendel controversy connection:** Fisher's 1936 paper ("Has Mendel's work been rediscovered?") applied chi-square tests to Mendel's data and concluded that the results were "too good" statistically, suggesting data manipulation. Fisher worked exclusively from the Druery-Bateson translation. The authors suggest that some aspects of the long-running Mendel-Fisher controversy may stem from Fisher's reliance on this flawed translation.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Revises the standard Darwin-Mendel narrative: not just "Darwin didn't know Mendel" but "Mendel knew Darwin and was influenced by him"—suggesting that Mendel consciously framed his particulate inheritance theory within a Darwinian evolutionary context
- Demonstrates that translation is not a neutral act—the choice of words in a translation shapes scientific interpretation for over a century
- Provides evidence that Mendel himself understood the evolutionary implications of his work, contradicting the view of Mendel as an isolated monk unaware of evolutionary theory
