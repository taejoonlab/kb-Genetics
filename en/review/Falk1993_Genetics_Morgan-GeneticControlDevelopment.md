# Morgan's Hypothesis of the Genetic Control of Development

## Citation (NLM)
Falk R, Schwartz S. Morgan's hypothesis of the genetic control of development. Genetics. 1993;134(3):671-674.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/134/3/671](https://genetics.org/content/134/3/671)

---

## Summary

This *Genetics* Perspectives essay by Raphael Falk and Sara Schwartz (Hebrew University of Jerusalem) uncovers a hidden developmental hypothesis buried in the unusual genetic notation Thomas Hunt Morgan and his students used around 1912–1913. The essay opens with a puzzle familiar to anyone who has read Sturtevant's classic 1913 linkage paper: the wing mutants *miniature* and *rudimentary* were written with a counterintuitive notation—the normal wing is "RM," miniature is "rM," rudimentary is "Rm." Why assign the mutant gene *r* to the *miniature* phenotype and *m* to *rudimentary*, rather than the other way around? Falk and Schwartz argue this oddity was no slip but the expression of a sophisticated hypothesis about how genes control embryological development.

They trace Morgan's deliberate, epistemologically self-conscious use of symbols. In his first Mendelian paper (1910) on the white-eyed mutant, Morgan avoided Mendel's convention (R for red, r for white) and instead used "O" for white to signal Bateson's "presence and absence" idea—that a trait like color is due to the *presence* of a factor and its absence (white) to the factor's loss. For wings, Morgan reasoned that R is needed to convert the miniature phenotype to normal, and M is needed to convert the rudimentary phenotype to normal. In other words, each gene controlled a specific, complementary developmental *step*: when M is lost, development proceeds only as far as the rudimentary form; when R is lost, only the miniature form results. The notation was designed to divert attention from the missing gene to the *residuum*—the phenotype produced by all the remaining intact factors. Morgan explicitly stated there might be "hundreds of factors that enter into the production of wings," so the loss of one factor did not abolish wings but left a phenotype built by the remainder.

This scheme encoded two profound ideas that the essay emphasizes. First, Morgan already grasped the *many–many relationship* between genotype and phenotype: many genes affect each trait, and (as he wrote in 1913) a change in one factor may have "far-reaching consequences," affecting many traits—an early statement of pleiotropy. Second, the notation embodied Morgan's resistance to a reductionist, Weismannian view that pairs each character with its own dedicated determinant. However, the essay notes what the model *lacked*: any notion of epistatic, sequential developmental pathways. The nomenclature also proved impractical. As soon as a third mutation affecting the same organ (e.g., *vestigial* for wings, or *eosin* among eye colors) had to be accommodated, the whole system required cumbersome revision each time—it was "not sufficiently elastic." By 1913 Morgan reluctantly abandoned the functional notation in favor of the now-standard convention (capital for the wild-type/dominant factor, lowercase for its mutant correlative), explicitly calling the change "a practical necessity."

Falk and Schwartz set this against the wider intellectual context. William E. Castle pushed for an even more austere, purely descriptive terminology that "commits us to no physiological theory," warning that a notation loaded with assumptions could lead investigators to conclusions based on terminology rather than facts. Morgan resisted going that far, and geneticists have ever since embedded some functional information in their nomenclature. Strikingly, the authors show that Morgan's abandoned multi-developmental-step idea was effectively *resurrected* fifteen years later by Beadle and Ephrussi (1936) in their transplantation study of *Drosophila* eye pigments—except that Beadle and Ephrussi added the crucial concept of *sequential* metabolic/developmental pathways (and did not use Morgan's notation). The essay closes by situating Morgan the embryologist, initially hostile to Mendelism as "preformationist," being won over by the white-eye experiments and by Johannsen's 1911 Columbia lectures distinguishing genotype from phenotype—after which Morgan reluctantly adopted the heuristic of separating discussions of gene *function* from gene *transmission*. (A footnote observes that Morgan himself never published a research paper in *Genetics*, only an obituary of Bridges.)

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Shows how **genetic notation itself carries theory**—a vivid lesson in how symbols encode (or hide) hypotheses, relevant to how students read allele nomenclature today.
- Reveals early, sophisticated understanding of **pleiotropy** and the many-to-many genotype–phenotype relationship, well before molecular mechanisms were known.
- Traces the historical tension between genetics-as-transmission and genetics-as-development, and why the field temporarily separated them.
- Connects Morgan's complementary-step idea to the later Beadle–Ephrussi pathway concept, foreshadowing biochemical genetics and the one gene–one enzyme era.

## Future Research Directions

- Morgan's intuition that many genes contribute to each trait and each gene affects many traits anticipates modern work on pleiotropy, gene regulatory networks, and the genotype–phenotype map.
- The essay's theme—how developmental logic can be embedded in genetic models—resonates with contemporary efforts to link classical genetics with developmental and systems biology.
