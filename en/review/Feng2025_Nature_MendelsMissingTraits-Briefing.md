# Answers to a 160-Year-Old Riddle about the Genetics of Mendel's Pea Traits (Research Briefing)

## Citation (NLM)
Feng C et al. Genomic and genetic insights into Mendel's pea genes. Nature. 2025. doi:10.1038/s41586-025-08891-6

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-025-08891-6](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-025-08891-6)

## Summary

This document is the official Research Briefing for Feng et al. (2025) in *Nature*, authored by first author Shifeng Cheng, providing a concise lay summary of the study's question, discovery, and implications.

Of the seven pea traits Mendel investigated in 1865, the genetic bases of only four had been determined (seed shape, cotyledon color, flower color, stem length); the other three (pod color, pod shape, flower position) had remained unresolved for 160 years.

The team analyzed a panel of ~700 pea varieties by coupling a high-resolution genomic variation map with extensive trait investigation. They identified variants and mechanisms governing the three unresolved traits, and also analyzed 72 additional crop traits.

Key discoveries: Pod color is controlled by a gene disrupting chlorophyll biosynthesis; pod form is explained by a mutation preventing formation of a signaling peptide and by disruption of a transcription factor gene involved in cell-wall thickening; flower position variation results from a deletion in a specific gene causing changes in flower clustering (fasciation). A remarkable diversity of mutational types was observed: point mutations, copy-and-paste insertions, cut-and-paste insertions, and both large and small deletions.

Implications: Mendel's experimental design was sound — he selected traits ideally suited to studying inheritance patterns, with dominant alleles being fully functional and recessive mutated alleles being non-functional. The concept of dominance, whereby a dominant variant masks a recessive one, makes sense at the molecular level. Limitations include the challenges of dissecting complex multi-gene traits and the current lack of efficient gene-editing tools for pea.

## Future Research Directions

- Developing efficient gene-editing systems (e.g., CRISPR) for pea to enable stronger functional validation of candidate genes
- Applying long-read sequencing and single-cell omics to further investigate genetic bases of complex traits in pea
- Exploiting the publicly available dataset from this study to characterize additional crop traits and translate findings to breeding applications in pea and other legumes
