# Century-Old Genetics Mystery of Mendel's Peas Finally Solved (News & Views)

## Summary

This is a News in Focus article in *Nature* by science writer Amanda Heidt, written for a general scientific audience to explain the significance of Feng et al. (2025).

Mendel famously cross-bred some 28,000 pea plants to uncover the principles of genetic inheritance more than 160 years ago. Although the concept of genes did not yet exist, Mendel concluded that plants pass along hereditary "factors" to offspring that determine whether they inherit dominant or recessive versions of traits. Despite this historical foundation, the genes driving three of Mendel's seven original pea traits — pod color, pod shape, and flower position — remained unknown until this study.

JIC co-author Noam Chayut notes that the team was intrigued by the enduring mystery and concluded that "sequencing and computational tools had advanced enough to tackle the final three." Using the JIC Germplasm Resource Unit (housing over 3,500 pea variants) and publicly available genomic datasets, the team deep-sequenced nearly 700 pea genomes, identifying roughly 155 million SNPs. Combining genome-wide association studies and selective breeding, they identified the genes linked to the three remaining traits: pod color (a gene disrupting chlorophyll biosynthesis), pod shape (two genes impairing cell-wall thickening), and flower position (a deletion in a specific gene causing fasciation).

The work took six years and was made possible by an interdisciplinary team where each member contributed a necessary skill. External commentators: plant breeder Jenna Hershberger (Clemson University) praised the work for "solving a historical puzzle" and building "a rich dataset that will be useful not just for future pea-genetics studies, but also for applied pea breeding." The fast-growing market for pea protein underscores the applied relevance of the findings.
