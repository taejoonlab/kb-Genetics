# What Is Mutation? Making Sense of Random and Non-Random Mutation

## Citation (NLM)
Fitzgerald DM, Rosenberg SM. What Is Mutation? Making Sense of Random and Non-Random Mutation. PLoS Genet. 2019 Apr 1;15(4):e1007995. doi:10.1371/journal.pgen.1007995.

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1007995](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1007995)

---

## Summary

This review revisits the fundamental concept of mutation, challenging the classical view that all mutations are strictly random. The authors systematically examine stress-induced mutagenesis (SIM)—the phenomenon whereby mutation rates increase in a non-random, regulated manner under specific environmental stress conditions.

The central mechanism is **mutagenic break repair (MBR)**: when DNA double-strand breaks (DSBs) arise, cells can activate error-prone repair pathways instead of high-fidelity mechanisms. In nutrient-starved *E. coli*, the SOS response is triggered—a transcriptional program induced by DNA damage that upregulates error-prone polymerases (Pol IV and Pol V), elevating mutation rates tens to hundreds of fold. Regulation occurs through RecA protein forming filaments on single-stranded DNA, promoting self-cleavage of the LexA repressor and derepressing SOS genes. In the SIM pathway, RecA filaments, error-prone polymerases, and transient mismatch repair (MMR) inhibition collaborate to concentrate mutations specifically around DSB sites—creating mutation clusters analogous to kataegis patterns observed in cancer genomes.

The review documents the breadth of this phenomenon across the tree of life: SIM mechanisms are conserved from bacteria (*E. coli*, *Bacillus subtilis*) to yeast, plants, and mammalian cells. Cancer cells under chemotherapy stress appear to activate MBR pathways, potentially accelerating the acquisition of resistance mutations. This reframes cancer as an evolutionary dynamic process driven in part by regulated mutagenesis.

The authors carefully distinguish two senses of "random": (1) which specific nucleotide changes occur at any given site (still random/unpredictable) versus (2) the overall mutation rate itself (which is regulated by cellular physiology). Their key argument is that mutation rate is an evolvable, stress-responsive trait—challenging the neo-Darwinian assumption that mutation is entirely decoupled from selection pressures.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides conceptual grounding for the definition and randomness of mutation, a prerequisite for understanding natural selection and evolution
- Introduces a modern challenge to the classical neo-Darwinian paradigm (mutations as purely random, selection-independent events)
- Connects bacterial genetics to cancer biology, illustrating how conserved molecular mechanisms span domains of life
- Essential reading for this course because it forces students to critically re-examine the word "random" in genetics and connects mechanistic molecular biology to evolutionary theory

## Future Research Directions

- Determine whether SIM operates during differentiation and development in multicellular eukaryotes
- Explore MBR pathway inhibitors as strategies to overcome chemotherapy resistance in cancer
- Quantify the contribution of SIM to evolutionary rates and trajectories in natural populations
