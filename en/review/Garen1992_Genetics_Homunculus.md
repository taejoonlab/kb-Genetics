# Looking for the Homunculus in Drosophila

## Citation (NLM)
Garen A. Looking for the Homunculus in Drosophila. Genetics. 1992;131(1):5-7.

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1093/genetics/131.1.5](https://doi.org/10.1093/genetics/131.1.5)

---

## Summary

This Perspectives essay by Alan Garen, published in Genetics, recounts his scientific journey into Drosophila developmental genetics beginning in 1965. The title's "Homunculus" references the medieval concept of a miniature preformed organism within the embryo, used here as a metaphor for the positional information blueprint encoded in early development.

The author traces his entry into the field to Ernst Hadorn's 1965 lecture at Yale. Hadorn had established that Drosophila development proceeds via two separate pathways — one for larval structures and one for imaginal (adult) structures via imaginal discs — and that imaginal cell determination is a stable, heritable state that persists through many cell divisions in culture without being lost. This operationally separated "determination" (the programming of cells for specific fates) from "differentiation" (the expression of that program).

Building on this conceptual framework, Garen and Allen Shearn in 1967 launched a screen for lethal mutants affecting imaginal disc development. They examined zygotic-effect mutants dying as late third-instar larvae and found that ~50% showed defective imaginal discs, estimating that as many as 1000 zygotically active genes specifically control the imaginal pathway.

Experiments by Hadorn, Schubiger, and later Gehring in Garen's lab demonstrated that autonomous imaginal cell determination occurs simultaneously with cellular blastoderm formation — approximately 2–3 hours post-fertilization. This meant that positional information must be established even earlier, during oogenesis. A maternal-effect mutant screen was undertaken by graduate student Thomas Rice to identify maternal genes providing this positional information. No mutants with the predicted phenotype (normal to blastoderm, then late larval lethal) were found, suggesting larval and imaginal pathways initially share maternally derived positional cues. The 10 maternal-effect lethals recovered instead involved defects in cell membrane synthesis or gastrulation (dorsal/ventral polarity), the latter later linked to genes like the dorsal-group genes characterized by Anderson and Nüsslein-Volhard.

The essay concludes by framing the homunculus not as an object but as a dynamic process: maternal genes sketch a rough positional blueprint, refined by zygotically active genes after blastoderm formation. Nüsslein-Volhard, Gehring, Hogness, and collaborators were then revealing the molecular basis of these interactions, bringing the elusive homunculus into view.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Establishes the conceptual framework for imaginal disc determination and differentiates it from differentiation, foundational for developmental genetics
- Illustrates the forward genetics approach: using lethal mutant screens phenotyped at the level of tissue/developmental pathway defects to identify genes
- Relevant for courses covering Drosophila as a model organism, maternal-effect genes, and the historical development of developmental genetics
