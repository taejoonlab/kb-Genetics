# Meuwissen et al. on Genomic Selection

## Summary

This Genetics Classics appreciation piece, authored by Dirk-Jan de Koning, evaluates the landmark 2001 paper by Meuwissen, Hayes, and Goddard — "Prediction of Total Genetic Value Using Genome-Wide Dense Marker Maps" — which first proposed the concept of genomic selection and subsequently transformed animal and plant breeding.

In 2001, most agricultural genomics research focused on detecting QTL via experimental crosses or pedigree relationships, and Marker-Assisted Selection (MAS) used only a handful of individually validated markers. Meuwissen et al. proposed a fundamentally different approach: use a marker density high enough that every QTL affecting a relevant trait would be in linkage disequilibrium (LD) with at least one marker, and make selection decisions based on the joint merit of all markers across the entire genome simultaneously.

The original paper tested this concept using a simulated dataset with 1010 genetic markers and 1000 putative QTL. Four statistical approaches were compared: linear regression, Best Linear Unbiased Prediction (BLUP), and two Bayesian methods dubbed BayesA and BayesB. Despite many subsequent improvements to the Bayesian approaches (giving rise to the "Bayesian Alphabet"), BayesB remains a gold standard for benchmarking new methods. A modified BLUP implementation, GBLUP, is the workhorse of practical genomic selection applications.

At publication the required tools — dense SNP chips — did not yet exist, so genomic selection remained a proposal. After 2006, when medium-density SNP chips became routinely available for major livestock species, the field exploded. In dairy cattle breeding, genomic selection has all but replaced traditional progeny testing, doubling genetic improvement per generation. Other livestock species are following suit, and maize and wheat breeding through international efforts like CIMMYT are also at the forefront. The principles of genomic selection are increasingly being applied to human disease research as well.

De Koning notes that Meuwissen et al. changed selective breeding for many agriculturally important species within 15 years of publication — a rare example of a theoretical proposal being so comprehensively validated and adopted at commercial scale within the lifetime of its authors.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Introduces the historical proposal and practical realization of genomic selection, the foundation of modern quantitative breeding
- Sits at the intersection of population genetics (LD), quantitative trait genetics, and Bayesian statistics, illustrating how statistical innovation drives biological progress
- Essential for understanding the real-world impact of statistical genetics on agriculture and human health genomics

## Future Research Directions

- Improving genomic selection accuracy using whole-genome sequence data rather than SNP arrays
- Development of multi-environment genomic selection models accounting for genotype-by-environment interactions
- Assessing the contribution of rare variants and structural variants to genomic prediction accuracy
