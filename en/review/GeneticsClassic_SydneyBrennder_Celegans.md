## Summary

This Genetics Classics piece, authored by Bob Goldstein, evaluates and contextualizes Sydney Brenner's 1974 paper "The Genetics of Caenorhabditis elegans" — the founding paper that formally established C. elegans as a genetic model organism.

Brenner's intellectual path to C. elegans began with a formative moment in April 1953: as a 26-year-old Oxford graduate student, he traveled to Cambridge to see the Watson-Crick double helix model before it was publicly announced. The future of biology became clear: "We could now find out how genes worked." Over the next twelve years Brenner, Crick, and colleagues built foundational components of the central dogma — demonstrating the existence of messenger RNA (1961), establishing the triplet genetic code (1961), and identifying stop codons (1965). By 1962, however, the basic questions of molecular biology were becoming "inevitable," and Brenner looked toward harder problems: neurobiology and development.

In a 1963 letter, Brenner articulated his vision: "I would like to tame a small metazoan organism to study development directly." He sought an animal small enough to fit in a transmission electron microscope's field of view, with fewer neurons than a fruit fly, and easy to grow in the lab. Microscopic nematodes fit the bill. After writing to Ellsworth Dougherty in Berkeley for worms and advice, Brenner launched C. elegans mutant screens in 1967. The 1974 Genetics paper reported these efforts.

The paper detailed how to perform genetic analysis in a self-fertilizing hermaphrodite that also has cross-fertilizing males, and reported hundreds of mutants — long worms, rolling worms, dumpy worms, uncoordinated worms, blistered worms, and worms with notched or bent heads. Complementation analysis and genetic mapping identified 96 loci on 6 linkage groups, matching the known chromosome number. The paper ended with a preview of preliminary functional analyses and a call for a larger systematic effort.

Brenner's conclusion explicitly proposed "look[ing] for mutants in all of the genes to try to discover how they participate in the development and functioning of a simple multicellular organism." Thousands of researchers pursuing their own interests along the trail Brenner blazed subsequently completed the hermaphrodite cell lineage, mapped the full neuronal connectome, discovered programmed cell death (apoptosis), and identified RNA interference — discoveries recognized with multiple Nobel Prizes. Brenner shared the 2002 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the paradigmatic example of forward genetics in a simple metazoan: starting from phenotype (behavioral/morphological mutants), discovering genes controlling development and function
- Documents the historical shift from molecular biology to developmental genetics/neurobiology as the field matured
- Essential for courses covering model organism choice, forward genetic screen design, complementation testing, and the history of genetics

## Future Research Directions

- Single-cell transcriptomics of all C. elegans cell types to complete the molecular atlas of the organism
- New behavioral mutant screens using automated tracking and machine learning for precise phenotyping
- Leveraging C. elegans as an aging model to dissect longevity gene networks and their conservation in humans
