# What Is a Genome?

## Citation (NLM)
Goldman AD, Landweber LF. What Is a Genome? PLoS Genet. 2016;12(7):e1006181. doi:10.1371/journal.pgen.1006181

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1006181](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1006181)

---

## Summary

This review, part of the PLoS Genetics "What is...?" perspective series, critically re-examines the foundational question of what a genome actually is. The authors argue that the standard NIH definition — "an organism's complete set of DNA, including all of the genes... all of the information needed to build and maintain that organism" — is both oversimplified and internally contradictory.

The paper presents two major challenges to standard genome definitions.

**Challenges to physical permanence:** Several diverse genetic systems challenge defining the genome as a fixed physical DNA entity. Retroviruses convert their ssRNA genomes into dsDNA, integrate into the host genome, and cease to exist as separate physical entities. A more striking example is the microbial eukaryote Oxytricha — a ciliate distantly related to Tetrahymena. Oxytricha maintains two distinct versions of its genome: a germline (~1 Gb, containing ~250,000 scrambled and inverted gene segments) and a somatic version (the expressed genome). After sexual recombination, germline DNA segments are rearranged into correct order using two types of RNA templates derived from the previous generation's somatic genome: long RNAs that template chromosome rearrangement order, and 27-nt piRNAs that mark which DNA regions to retain. The resulting somatic genome contains over 16,000 "nanochromosomes" encoding just 5-10% of the germline sequence. In this system, genomic information flows DNA → RNA → DNA across generations, making it impossible to identify "the genome" as a single physical molecule at any one time. Synthetic biology further demonstrates that information content is more important than physical form: the chemically synthesized Mycoplasma genome existed first as a computer file (non-physical nucleotide sequence) before being re-instantiated as functional DNA.

**Challenges to informational completeness:** The claim that the genome contains "all of the information needed to build and maintain" an organism is an oversimplification. Epigenetic regulation — DNA methylation, histone modification, non-coding RNAs including Oxytricha's ncRNAs — provide platforms for heritable information transfer that appears to bypass the DNA genome. GWAS studies revealing "missing heritability" demonstrate that genomic variation alone cannot account for full phenotypic variance; gene-environment interactions contribute additional information not encoded in the genome itself.

The authors propose a more expansive definition: the genome is "an informational entity, often but not always manifest as DNA, encoding a broad set of functional possibilities that, together with other sources of information, produce and maintain the organism."

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Probes the limits of the foundational concept of "genome," connecting to modern complexity in epigenetics, RNA biology, and synthetic genomics
- Integrates multiple key topics — retroviruses, ciliate genome rearrangement, epigenetic inheritance, GWAS missing heritability — into a single conceptual argument
- Stimulates critical thinking about information, heredity, and the boundaries of what we call a genome
