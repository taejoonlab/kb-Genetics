# Reinventing the Ames Test as a Quantitative Lab That Connects Classical and Molecular Genetics

## Citation (NLM)
Goodson-Gregg N, De Stasio EA. Reinventing the Ames Test as a Quantitative Lab That Connects Classical and Molecular Genetics. Genetics. 2009;181(1):23-31. doi:10.1534/genetics.108.095588

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.108.095588](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.108.095588)

---

## Summary

Published in the Genetics Education section of Genetics, this paper describes the redesign of the classical Ames mutagenicity test into a multi-week, inquiry-based undergraduate genetics laboratory that adds quantitative analysis and molecular genetic investigation to the traditional qualitative screen.

The Ames test, developed by Bruce Ames and colleagues, uses Salmonella typhimurium strains carrying mutations in the his operon to test whether substances induce mutations. His⁻ bacteria plated on histidine-deficient medium cannot grow unless a reversion mutation restores histidine biosynthesis. The number of His⁺ revertant colonies indicates mutagen potency. Traditional teaching labs stopped at simply counting colonies — a qualitative comparison.

The authors extended the lab in two key dimensions:

**Quantitative extension:** Students determine colony-forming units (CFU) per milliliter of their overnight cultures through self-designed serial dilutions, then calculate reversion frequency (revertants per CFU) rather than simply counting colonies. This forces engagement with quantitative experimental design. Two strains were selected: TA1535 (missense mutation in hisG, hisG46) and TA1538 (frameshift mutation in hisD, hisD3052). Positive controls NaN₃ (induces missense transitions) and 4NOP (induces frameshifts/deletions) demonstrate strain-specific mutagen effects — NaN₃ significantly reverts only TA1535, and 4NOP only TA1538 — giving students a direct empirical example of mutation spectrum differences between mutagens.

**Molecular genetic extension:** Each student selects a revertant colony, extracts genomic DNA, amplifies the relevant his gene region by PCR, and submits the product for Sanger sequencing. Analysis of the resulting sequences reveals the specific molecular lesion conferring the His⁺ phenotype. In TA1535 revertants, primarily C→T transitions (wild-type reversion) or C→A transversions (histidine substitution) were found within a 2-bp region. TA1538 revertants showed greater diversity of indel mutations spanning a 30-bp region, all restoring the reading frame without reverting to wild-type sequence. Students see firsthand that many different DNA sequences can encode a functional enzyme conferring the same phenotype.

Pre- and post-test evaluation showed significant improvements in both quantitative skills (dilutions, frequency calculations) and conceptual understanding (reading frame, reversion mechanisms) with p < 0.001. Students rated the lab highly, particularly appreciating that they could test their own chosen potential mutagens (common items like Carmex lip balm, coffee, tobacco, and hair dye). Carmex was found to be mutagenic in both strains. The authors provide all protocols and maintain a revertant sequence database for future classes to contribute to.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Exemplifies connecting classical genetics (reversion phenotype, complementation) to molecular genetics (DNA sequence, reading frame, mutation mechanisms) within a single experiment
- Directly teaches mutation types (missense, frameshift), reversion, and suppressor concepts through hands-on investigation
- Serves as a model for inquiry-based lab design in genetics education, emphasizing student-driven experimental design and quantitative reasoning
