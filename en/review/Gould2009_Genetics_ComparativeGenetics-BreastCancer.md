# The Utility of Comparative Genetics to Inform Breast Cancer Prevention Strategies

## Citation (NLM)
Gould MN. The utility of comparative genetics to inform breast cancer prevention strategies. Genetics. 2009;183(2):409-412. doi:10.1534/genetics.109.108480

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.109.108480](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.109.108480)

## Summary

In this Perspectives essay, Michael Gould recounts how a cancer biologist focused on prevention "morphed into a practicing geneticist," and reviews a decade of his lab's comparative-genomics program aimed at discovering breast-cancer risk-modifying alleles. His early work sought chemopreventive compounds — nontoxic monoterpenes from fruit essential oils such as limonene (orange peel) and perillyl alcohol (POH, from lavender), the latter entering FDA-approved trials and later shown to downregulate NF-κB via a calcium-channel interaction. Because such empirically screened agents tend to have low efficacy and poor target specificity, Gould sought a non-empirical, genetics-driven route to prevention that would identify both drug targets and high-risk populations.

The strategy targets *moderately* penetrant, *common* susceptibility alleles — not the rare, highly penetrant genes like *BRCA1/2*, which account for under 25% of inherited risk. Because such common alleles were hard to find directly in humans a decade earlier (candidate-gene association studies rarely succeeded), Gould adopted a comparative-genomics approach: identify mammary cancer risk loci by unbiased linkage mapping in a rat carcinogenesis model, then test the human orthologs in breast-cancer case-control association studies. The rat was chosen because, like humans and unlike mice, it develops a spectrum of hormonally responsive and nonresponsive, ductal-origin breast cancers.

The first genome-wide study (Shepel et al. 1998, in *Genetics*) crossed the DMBA-susceptible Wistar-Furth (WF) strain with the resistant Copenhagen (COP) strain and, via backcrosses/intercrosses, identified the Mammary carcinoma susceptibility loci *Mcs1–4*, which acted additively; strikingly, even the highly resistant COP strain carried a susceptibility-increasing allele at *Mcs4*. A second cross (WF × Wistar-Kyoto, Lan et al. 2001) revealed four more loci, including modifier loci with no main effect (*Mcsm1*) that could fully negate the resistance of another QTL — evidence for a large web of interacting risk loci. Because genetically mapped QTL are statistical constructs, Gould's lab confirmed them by building congenic strains: six of eight candidate loci were validated on the WF background. Fine mapping then resolved *Mcs1* and *Mcs5* into multiple subloci with complex epistasis (e.g., the resistant WKy *Mcs5a* allele completely neutralizing the sensitive *Mcs5b* allele).

Ultrafine mapping of *Mcs5a* to >100-kb resolution revealed it to be a *compound* QTL of two synthetically interacting, noncoding elements (*Mcs5a1* and *Mcs5a2*) that act only in *cis* — one of the first compound QTL identified in mammals; chromosome-conformation-capture data suggest a CTCF-mediated loop brings the two elements into physical proximity. Translating to humans, Gould's team restricted their search to the ~100-kb orthologous region (vastly reducing the multiple-testing penalty relative to GWAS) and tested SNPs in 12,000 women, finding significant breast-cancer associations at both *MCS5A1* (rs56476643, recessive, +19% risk in homozygotes) and *MCS5A2* (rs2182317, dominant, −14% risk). The human study also sharpened the localization achieved in the rat. Unlike GWAS-only hits, comparative-genomics alleles come with an in vivo model for functional characterization — showing, for instance, that noncoding *Mcs5a* differentially regulates the neighboring *FBXO10* gene in immune, not mammary, tissue. Gould concludes that all four finely mapped *Mcs* loci are noncoding, consistent with the emerging view that most low-penetrance, high-frequency risk-modifier alleles are noncoding and likely act by modulating gene expression — and that integrating comparative genomics with GWAS offers a systematic route to mechanistically annotate disease-modifier loci and identify prevention drug targets.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A clear, worked example of quantitative-trait-locus (QTL) mapping, congenic validation, and fine mapping — core methods for teaching how complex, polygenic traits are dissected.
- Illustrates the logic of comparative genomics: using an experimentally tractable model organism (rat) to home in on human disease-risk loci, drastically reducing the statistical burden of genome-wide testing.
- Introduces key concepts — moderate vs. high penetrance, common vs. rare alleles, epistasis, compound QTL, cis-regulatory and noncoding risk variants — in a concrete disease context.
- Connects basic genetics to translational medicine, showing how mapping risk alleles can suggest drug targets for individualized cancer prevention.

## Key References

1. **Shepel et al. (1998)** — genetic identification of multiple rat breast-cancer susceptibility loci (*Genetics*) — the foundational genome-wide mapping study.
2. **Lan et al. (2001)** — loci controlling breast-cancer susceptibility in the Wistar-Kyoto rat (*Genetics*) — revealed additional and modifier loci.
3. **Samuelson et al. (2007)** — rat *Mcs5a* as a compound QTL with human orthologs associated with breast-cancer risk (PNAS) — the key rat-to-human translation.
4. **Pharoah et al. (2007, 2008)** — limits of candidate-gene studies; polygenes and targeted prevention — the human-genetics backdrop motivating the approach.
5. **Zan et al. (2003)** — production of knockout rats via ENU mutagenesis — a technology Gould's lab developed for functional analysis.

## Future Research Directions

- Elucidating the molecular, cellular, and organismal mechanisms by which noncoding *Mcs* loci modify risk — including long-range and intergenic regulatory action — to convert risk alleles into druggable prevention targets.
- Integrating comparative-genomics functional models with GWAS hits to mechanistically annotate the many noncoding risk-modifier alleles across common diseases, and to test the evolutionary origins of clustered susceptibility alleles.
