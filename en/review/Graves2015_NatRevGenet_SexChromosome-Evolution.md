# Evolution of vertebrate sex chromosomes and dosage compensation

## Citation (NLM)
Graves JAM. Evolution of vertebrate sex chromosomes and dosage compensation. Nat Rev Genet. 2016;17(1):33-46. doi:10.1038/nrg.2015.2

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg.2015.2](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg.2015.2)

---

## Summary

Jennifer Graves opens this review with a characteristically frank observation: vertebrate sex chromosomes illustrate "dumb design" — systems that make no functional sense but are explicable through evolutionary logic. The differentiation of sex chromosomes creates dosage imbalances for hundreds of genes between the sexes and between sex chromosomes and autosomes, imposing costs that evolution is compelled to address. The review synthesizes how sex chromosomes arose independently across vertebrates, how Y and W chromosomes have degenerated at different rates, and how the resulting dosage problems have been solved by a remarkable diversity of mechanisms that share molecular ancestry but differ in extent and implementation.

The raw material of vertebrate sex chromosomes has been diverse and independently acquired. Comparative mapping reveals that human XY genes are autosomal in birds, reptiles, and even monotremes. The short arm of the human X is autosomal in marsupials, showing it was added to the ancestral sex chromosomes only in placental mammals. The bird ZW pair is autosomal in mammals, and different snake lineages use different chromosomal regions for sex determination. Sex-determining genes themselves reflect this independence: SRY evolved from the highly conserved SOX3 gene on the X chromosome; DMRT1 on the bird Z chromosome is autosomal in other vertebrates; AMH is the leading candidate in monotremes. Remarkably, SOX3, DMRT1, and AMH have independently been press-ganged into sex determination in multiple lineages — suggesting that some genes are particularly apt for co-option as sex-determining factors.

Dosage compensation mechanisms are surprisingly diverse. In eutherian (placental) mammals, the XIST lncRNA coats the X chromosome to be inactivated and recruits a silencing domain built from DNA methylation, variant histones, and histone modifications, ultimately creating a two-megadomain structure visible by Hi-C. This system is nearly chromosome-wide and nearly complete, maintaining female/male expression ratios close to 1.0 for most genes. Marsupials also have X inactivation, but it is always paternal, tissue-specific, partial, and lacks XIST — instead using the independently evolved RSX lncRNA that performs analogous functions. Monotremes have five X chromosomes and show little global dosage compensation, though partial stochastic silencing is observed.

Birds offer a striking contrast. The chicken Z chromosome contains ~1,000 genes absent from the W chromosome, yet dosage compensation is incomplete and gene-by-gene. ZZ males express Z-linked genes 30-40% more strongly than ZW females — a dosage valley exists around the MHM (Male HyperMethylated) locus where the sex-determining DMRT1 gene resides and which produces a long ncRNA in females. Different bird species show different dosage-compensated subsets, and the MHM dosage valley appears specific to galliform birds. Snakes illustrate gradients of W chromosome degeneration from pythons (practically homomorphic) to vipers (highly differentiated), with corresponding gradients in partial, gene-by-gene dosage compensation. Fish present early-stage dosage compensation: in stickleback, genes on the oldest X stratum show signs of male-biased upregulation; in tongue sole, a 4 Mb region of the Z chromosome shows strong dosage compensation correlated with DNA methylation.

The review addresses a central theoretical controversy: whether X chromosome upregulation preceded X inactivation, as Ohno hypothesized. Evidence is equivocal. Some analyses in males of opossum and platypus support upregulation of X-linked genes relative to autosomes, but comprehensive RNA-seq studies in placental mammals fail to detect consistent upregulation. The review concludes that upregulation likely occurs for some genes in some lineages but is not a universal first step. The evolution of mammalian X inactivation is instead reconstructed as a superimposition of chromosome-wide control on an ancestral, leaky gene-by-gene system — the stochastic nature of escaper gene expression in both humans and marsupials supports this view.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Frames vertebrate sex chromosome evolution through a comparative lens spanning mammals, birds, reptiles, and fish, revealing that independent origins are the rule and convergent solutions to dosage problems are striking but imperfect
- Introduces the molecular machinery of X inactivation (XIST lncRNA, chromatin silencing domains, Hi-C topology) as a paradigm for epigenetic gene silencing that extends far beyond sex chromosomes
- Highlights the marsupial RSX lncRNA as a convergent functional analog of XIST with no sequence similarity — a powerful illustration of how the same problem (coating the inactive X) can be solved by different molecular means
- Documents the spectrum of dosage compensation strategies from near-complete (eutherian mammals) to partial gene-by-gene (birds, snakes, fish) to near-absent (monotremes) — providing empirical grounding for the theoretical debate about whether dosage compensation is obligate
- Provides evidence bearing on Ohno's classic hypothesis that X upregulation preceded X inactivation, situating this debate in the context of contemporary RNA-seq data and alternative evolutionary models

## Future Research Directions

- Molecular characterization of how RSX lncRNA in marsupials recruits chromatin-silencing factors and whether it interacts with the same protein partners as XIST — to test whether convergent X inactivation uses common or divergent molecular machinery
- Systematic proteomics studies across vertebrates to determine whether female/male protein ratios for sex-linked genes match mRNA ratios, testing whether post-transcriptional compensation operates where transcriptional compensation is incomplete
- Understanding the mechanistic basis of the MHM locus in chickens and its role in gene-by-gene dosage compensation: does it function as a local silencer, a boundary element, or a recruiter of dosage-compensating factors?
- Testing Ohno's hypothesis directly by identifying genes that are X-linked in one species and autosomal in a closely related species, then measuring expression changes in males relative to autosomes
- Evolutionary and functional analysis of why monotremes — with five X chromosomes — show so little global dosage compensation despite the substantial X-linked gene dosage imbalance between males and females
