# 2010: A century of Drosophila genetics through the prism of the white gene

## Summary

M. M. Green's "Perspectives" essay marks the centenary of Drosophila genetics by tracing how a single gene — *white* — has served as a window onto fundamental genetic principles for a hundred years. In January 1910, Thomas Hunt Morgan found his first Drosophila mutant, a white-eyed male, named the gene *white*, and soon showed it resided on the X chromosome — the first localization of a specific gene to a particular chromosome, and the first concrete evidence for the chromosome theory of heredity suggested by Sutton and Boveri. Green recounts that Morgan, an embryologist skeptical of what he saw as the "superior jugglery" of Mendelian factor-invoking, turned to breeding Drosophila to test Mendelism for himself; his success helped transform Mendel's abstract "factors" into concrete "genes."

The essay then follows *white* through successive landmarks of classical genetics. By 1913 Morgan had found several sex-linked mutations; A. H. Sturtevant, as an undergraduate, used recombination frequency as a measure of genetic distance to construct the first linear genetic map of six sex-linked factors. Volume 1 of the journal *Genetics* (1916) opened with C. B. Bridges' "Non-disjunction as proof of the chromosome theory of heredity," in which the rare exceptional offspring of white crosses (white females, red males) were explained by X-chromosome nondisjunction and confirmed cytologically — establishing, with Morgan and Sturtevant's work, the foundations of classical genetics.

Green devotes the middle sections to major genetic phenomena revealed at the *white* locus. **Position-effect variegation and heterochromatin:** Muller (1930) found that X-ray-induced white-variegated (mottled) phenotypes were always associated with inversions or translocations placing the normal *white* allele next to centromeric heterochromatin; experiments by Griffen and Stone, Judd, and Novitski showed that gene function was altered by heterochromatin *proximity* without any change to the gene's physical integrity. **Dosage compensation:** Muller (1932) coined the term for the equalized expression of sex-linked genes between XX females and XY males; pigmented *white* alleles provided an instructive exception, with compensated (e.g., white-apricot) and uncompensated (white-eosin) mutants mapping to separate intragenic sites. **Mobile DNA elements:** paralleling McClintock's controlling elements in maize, Green (1967) discovered an unstable mutable allele (white-crimson) that generated deletions and transpositions like McClintock's; molecular work by Collins and Rubin (1982) proved a foldback DNA element was inserted at white-ivory, confirming a transposon origin of the instability.

The essay culminates in **tandem duplication and unequal crossing over**, tying together classical and molecular genetics. The Bar (B) mutation, studied by Sturtevant (1925), mutated only in homozygous B females (where crossing over occurs), leading Sturtevant to conclude that Bar was not a point mutation but a tandem duplication (and ultra-Bar a triplication) arising via unequal crossing over — later confirmed cytologically by Bridges (1936) and Muller et al. (1936). Green recounts his own parallel discovery: unequal crossing over between pigmented white alleles (wa/wa4) produced reciprocal duplications and deficiencies. In collaboration with Walter Gehring's group, the mobile element *Belshazzar (bel)* was found lying between the duplicated parts, providing the mechanism; Tsubota et al. (1989) later showed the element B104 lies at the junction of the Bar duplication, explaining Bar's extraordinarily rare, unique origin (pairing across the vast 16A1–16A7 distance). Green also notes that wa, tightly linked to the mobile element *copia*, became the first *D. melanogaster* gene cloned (Bingham et al. 1981). He closes with a tribute to Lilian V. Morgan — discoverer of the first attached-X and ring-X chromosomes, tools central to genetic mapping and mosaic analysis — and a reflection that the solitary 1910 white-eyed male inaugurated a century of groundbreaking genetics.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Uses one iconic gene to narrate the entire arc of genetics — from the chromosome theory and the first genetic map to transposons and molecular cloning — an ideal integrative story for an introductory course.
- Demonstrates foundational concepts concretely at a single locus: sex linkage, nondisjunction, genetic mapping, position-effect variegation/heterochromatin, dosage compensation, mobile elements, and unequal crossing over.
- Highlights how classical genetic inference (Sturtevant on Bar) was later confirmed molecularly, illustrating the continuity from breeding experiments to DNA-level mechanism.
- Embodies the Batesonian maxim "treasure your exceptions" — nearly every advance came from a puzzling exceptional fly — a valuable methodological lesson.

## Future Research Directions

- The white/Bar work established unequal crossing over and mobile-element-mediated rearrangement as engines of genome structural variation — themes central to modern studies of copy-number variation and transposon biology.
- Position-effect variegation at white remains a model for chromatin-based (epigenetic) gene regulation, informing ongoing research on heterochromatin spreading and gene silencing.
