# Cyril Dean Darlington: the man who 'invented' the chromosome

## Citation (NLM)
Harman O. Cyril Dean Darlington: the man who 'invented' the chromosome. Nat Rev Genet. 2005;6(1):79-85. doi:10.1038/nrg1504

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg1504](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg1504)

---

## Summary

Cyril Dean Darlington (1903–1981) rose from an unpromising agricultural education to become the most celebrated cytologist of the pre-molecular era. Working largely in isolation at the John Innes Horticultural Institution under William Bateson — who himself rejected the chromosome theory of heredity — Darlington spent the years 1923 to 1932 observing meiosis across a wide variety of organisms. His landmark 1932 book *Recent Advances in Cytology* distilled these observations into a bold, deductive framework: that attraction between homologous chromosomes is always in pairs and side-by-side (parasynapsis); that chiasmata are the physical condition for pairing and later segregation; and that chiasmata are always the consequence of crossing over. These "universal laws of chromosome behaviour" cut through what Darlington described as a confusing jumble of cytological observation and transformed cytology from a descriptive discipline into a largely deductive science.

Darlington's greatest contribution, however, was extending chromosome mechanics into evolutionary theory. In his 1939 work *Evolution of Genetic Systems*, he argued that hereditary mechanisms — what he called "genetic systems," encompassing genes, chromosomes, the reproductive process, and breeding habit — were themselves subject to natural selection. Crossing over, recombination, polyploidy, apomixis, and chromosome ring formation were not merely cellular curiosities but adaptive mechanisms that shaped the amount of heritable variation available to each generation. Ernst Mayr later wrote that "no one made a greater contribution to the understanding of recombination and its evolutionary importance than Darlington." These ideas placed the chromosome, rather than the individual gene, at the centre of the evolutionary process.

The reception of Darlington's work was deeply troubled. The cytological community, which had drifted toward cautious descriptiveness under the shadow of Morgan's dominant Drosophila genetics program, viewed Darlington's bold deductions as dangerous conjecture. John Belling called his 1932 book "propaganda," and graduate students were warned away from it. Evolutionary biologists were equally skeptical: Ledyard Stebbins dismissed *Evolution of Genetic Systems* as a "masterpiece of mythogenesis." Paradoxically, Theodosius Dobzhansky cited Darlington more than almost any other researcher in *Genetics and the Origin of Species* (1937), demonstrating that Darlington's ideas were intellectually indispensable even when formally unacknowledged. Resistance gradually waned: cytological work presented at American Genetics Society meetings rose from 13% of papers in 1929 to 52% by 1940.

After his scientific heyday, the molecular revolution of the 1950s sidelined Darlington's cytocentric worldview. His later career turned toward sociobiology and biological determinism applied to human history and culture, work that attracted accusations of racism and further obscured his scientific legacy. He also became a prominent and early critic of Lysenkoism in the Soviet Union, opposing Trofim Lysenko's rejection of Mendelian genetics at a time when much of the British scientific left was unwilling to criticize a wartime ally. This principled stand, though historically significant, cost him further goodwill in certain circles.

Harman's retrospective argues that Darlington's career yields enduring methodological lessons. First, bold deductive theorizing — even when over-generalised — is essential for breaking out of descriptive stagnation; subsequent careful study refined rather than invalidated his framework. Second, artificial disciplinary boundaries hamper scientific progress; Darlington's willingness to bridge cytology, genetics, and evolutionary theory anticipated the integrative approaches that define modern genomics and evo-devo. His critique of the static "beads-on-a-string" gene model, his concept of genetic systems as units of selection, and his insistence on the evolutionary significance of chromosome mechanics remain relevant starting points for contemporary research on genome evolution and the determinants of recombination rate.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Darlington's work is a foundational case study in the history of cytogenetics, showing how a single figure can synthesise disparate fields (cytology, genetics, evolution) before institutional structures support such integration.
- His deductive approach — inferring universal laws of chromosome mechanics from genetic first principles rather than accumulating descriptive observations — illustrates a powerful but controversial style of scientific reasoning.
- The troubled reception of *Recent Advances in Cytology* and *Evolution of Genetic Systems* demonstrates how disciplinary boundaries and sociological factors in science can delay the acceptance of correct or productive ideas.
- The concept of "genetic systems" — that the machinery of heredity (chromosomes, meiosis, recombination, breeding habit) is itself subject to selection — prefigures modern research questions about the evolution of recombination rates, sex, and genome architecture.
- Darlington's eventual partial rehabilitation, aided by Dobzhansky's citation network and Stebbins's subsequent work, illustrates how synthetic ideas often gain acceptance only after being translated into the dominant empirical language of the time.

## Key References

1. **Darlington CD (1932)** — https://doi.org/10.5962/bhl.title.60597 — *Recent Advances in Cytology*: the foundational text presenting universal laws of chromosome behaviour and the parasynaptic model of meiosis.
2. **Darlington CD (1939)** — *Evolution of Genetic Systems* (Cambridge Univ. Press) — extended chromosome mechanics into evolutionary theory; proposed that genetic systems are themselves units of selection.
3. **Morgan TH et al. (1915)** — *The Mechanism of Mendelian Heredity* (Holt, New York) — established the chromosome theory of the gene and linear gene mapping; the dominant framework against which Darlington's work was measured.
4. **Dobzhansky T (1937)** — *Genetics and the Origin of Species* (Columbia Univ. Press) — the key text of the Modern Synthesis; cited Darlington more than almost any other author, indirectly legitimising chromosome-based evolutionary thinking.
5. **Creighton H & McClintock B (1931)** — https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.17.8.492 — cytological proof of genetic crossing over in *Zea mays*; provided the physical evidence for Janssens's chiasmatype theory that Darlington's framework required.
6. **Janssens FA (1909)** — *Cellule* 25:387–411 — proposed the chiasmatype theory, observing chiasmata as the cytological basis of crossing over; a direct precursor to Darlington's mechanistic interpretation.
7. **Harman OS (2004)** — *The Man Who Invented the Chromosome: A Life of Cyril Darlington* (Harvard Univ. Press) — full-length scientific biography providing the historical context for the 2005 review article.

## Future Research Directions

- The evolution of recombination rate and crossover interference remains an active problem; Darlington's insight that recombination machinery is subject to selection motivates current population-genetic and molecular studies of meiotic genes.
- The "achiasmatic" male *Drosophila* meiosis — long cited as the major exception to Darlington's universal laws — continues to prompt investigation into alternative chromosome pairing mechanisms and their evolutionary origins.
- Modern evo-devo approaches to the evolution of reproductive systems (selfing, apomixis, polyploidy) directly extend Darlington's genetic-systems framework with molecular tools he could not have imagined.
- Advances in chromosome conformation capture and cryo-EM now make it feasible to revisit the structural basis of synapsis and chiasma formation at molecular resolution, addressing the mechanistic questions Darlington posed from cytological first principles.
- The history of science perspective raised in this article calls for further study of how disciplinary boundaries and sociological factors accelerate or retard paradigm shifts, particularly relevant as genetics, genomics, and developmental biology continue to converge.
