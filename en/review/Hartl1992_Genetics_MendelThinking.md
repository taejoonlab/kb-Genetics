# What Did GREGOR MENDEL Think He Discovered?

**DOI:** (not available — pre-DOI publication)

## Summary

This paper revisits the historical question of what Gregor Mendel (1822–1884) actually believed he had discovered through his pea hybridization experiments, and how later generations reinterpreted his findings. Authors Daniel Hartl and Vitezslav Orel analyze Mendel's original publications and correspondence to assess competing "orthodox" and "revisionist" interpretations of his work.

The orthodox view holds that Mendel discovered the laws of particulate inheritance—segregation and independent assortment—and thereby proved a universal mechanism of heredity. The revisionist view situates Mendel within the 19th-century tradition of plant hybridization research, where the central questions concerned the stability of species and the mechanisms of hybrid formation, not the fundamental basis of inheritance.

A central controversy addressed is R. A. Fisher's (1936) statistical analysis, which claimed Mendel's experimental data fit theoretical ratios too closely—the results were "too good to be true"—raising the possibility of data manipulation or selective reporting. Hartl and Orel analyze this claim carefully, discussing whether Mendel may have used confirmatory preliminary experiments to select which data to report, introducing what we now call confirmation bias or reporting bias, without necessarily constituting fraud.

The authors argue that what Mendel himself thought he discovered was the **"law of combination of differing traits"**: he demonstrated that seven pairs of traits segregated in predictable numerical ratios in hybrids. Mendel did not claim to have identified the material basis of hereditary units; rather, he described mathematical regularities in hybrid offspring. The posthumous elevation of his work into foundational laws of genetics was largely the contribution of early 20th-century geneticists following the 1900 "rediscovery."

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the historical and intellectual context for the founding experiments of genetics, essential background before studying Mendelian ratios mechanistically
- Illustrates how scientific discoveries are shaped and reshaped by historical context—a case study in the sociology of science
- Forces engagement with the gap between what Mendel intended and what genetics textbooks now attribute to him
- Essential for this course because understanding Mendel's actual reasoning, rather than the idealized textbook narrative, builds critical thinking about how genetics as a discipline was constructed
