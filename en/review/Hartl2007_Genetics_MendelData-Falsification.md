# Mud Sticks: On the Alleged Falsification of Mendel's Data

## Citation (NLM)
Hartl DL, Fairbanks DJ. Mud sticks: on the alleged falsification of Mendel's data. Genetics. 2007;175(3):975-979.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/175/3/975](https://genetics.org/content/175/3/975)

---

## Summary

Daniel Hartl and Daniel Fairbanks confront one of the most persistent controversies in the history of genetics: R. A. Fisher's 1936 allegation that Gregor Mendel's data were "falsified so as to agree closely with Mendel's expectations." Fisher — who had meticulously reconstructed the timeline and scale of Mendel's experiments and otherwise defended their authenticity — nonetheless concluded that the results fit *too well*, a discovery he privately called "abominable" and "shocking." The authors' thesis is that this charge, though it has "stuck to Mendel like dirt sticks to a candidate after a mud-slinging political campaign," is not justified by Mendel's own words or by the data in his 1866 paper; Fisher, they argue, was "intemperate if not reckless" in alleging deliberate falsification.

The controversy centers on two series of *progeny tests*, in which Mendel grew offspring of F2 dominant-phenotype plants to determine which parents were heterozygous and which homozygous. Fisher's statistical objection turns on a subtle bias: if a parent is diagnosed as heterozygous by the appearance of one or more recessive offspring among *i* progeny, then with only *i* = 10 progeny per parent (as Mendel stated), a small fraction of true heterozygotes will be misclassified as homozygous. This shifts the true expected ratio from Mendel's 2:1 to Fisher's corrected 1.7:1. Mendel's pooled data (720 heterozygous : 353 homozygous) fit his own 2:1 expectation almost perfectly (P = 0.76) but deviate significantly from Fisher's 1.7:1 (P = 0.0045) — the statistical heart of the falsification charge.

The authors examine and reject two proposed reconciliations before offering their own. E. Novitski (2004) suggested Mendel used a biased counting rule requiring a full set of 10 dominant progeny to score a parent as homozygous; but this only works with ~98% seed survivorship, whereas Hartl and Fairbanks show from Mendel's own reported numbers that survivorship averaged only ~94%, which would produce an even larger discrepancy in the *opposite* direction, making Novitski's proposal untenable. Their central argument, drawing on Fairbanks and Rytting (2001), is that Fisher misidentified the trait actually scored. In the trifactorial cross, the third trait was almost certainly *not* flower color (which requires growing F3 plants) but *axillary (leaf-axil) pigmentation* — a pleiotropic effect of the same anthocyanin mutation that is visible in seedlings just 2–3 weeks after germination and is perfectly correlated with flower and seed-coat color. Because a superb gardener like Mendel could easily raise more than 10 seedlings and score them all (or deliberately retain any showing the recessive trait), the effective number of progeny examined was greater than 10, which erases Fisher's expected bias. A second, complementary explanation from Sewall Wright (1966) is that occasional low-level expression of a "recessive" trait in heterozygotes (a penetrance of just p = 0.02) would similarly eliminate the discrepancy.

For the first series of progeny tests, Hartl and Fairbanks show that the data (399 het : 201 hom) do not differ significantly from Fisher's expectation (P = 0.069) once analyzed properly, and that traits scorable in seedlings (seed-coat color, stem length) again imply >10 effective progeny. Tellingly, Experiment 5 — which showed the greatest departure from 2:1 and which *Mendel himself distrusted and repeated* — gave pooled results (125:75) that fit Fisher's expectation almost exactly (P = 0.90). As Wright noted, such candor in reporting a repeated, unsatisfying experiment is hardly what one expects from a fraud. The authors conclude there is no factual basis for the falsification charge and express the hope that Fisher's allegation "can finally be put to rest."

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Directly addresses the "too good to be true" critique of Mendel that every genetics student eventually encounters, defending the integrity of the discipline's founding data.
- Teaches the biology of progeny testing and how sampling with only 10 offspring introduces a real, quantifiable classification bias — a concrete lesson in genetic and statistical reasoning.
- Illustrates the role of pleiotropy (one anthocyanin mutation affecting flower, seed-coat, and axil pigmentation) and the practical realities of botanical experimentation.
- A model of how careful re-reading of primary sources and attention to experimental practice can overturn a famous statistical indictment.

## Key References

1. **Mendel (1866)** — *Versuche über Pflanzen-Hybriden* — the primary source; the raw progeny-test data at the center of the dispute.
2. **Fisher (1936)** — "Has Mendel's work been rediscovered?" *Ann. Sci.* — origin of the falsification allegation and the statistical analysis being rebutted.
3. **Fairbanks & Rytting (2001)** — "Mendelian controversies: a botanical and historical review," *Am. J. Bot.* — the botanical argument (axillary pigmentation, seedling scoring) underpinning the rebuttal.
4. **Wright (1966)** — "Mendel's ratios" — the penetrance explanation and the observation that Mendel's candor argues against fraud.
5. **Novitski, E. & Novitski, C. E. (2004)** — *Genetics* — the counting-rule proposal the authors examine and reject.
