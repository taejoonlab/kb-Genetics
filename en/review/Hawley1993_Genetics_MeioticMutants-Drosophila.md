# Meiosis as an "M" Thing: Twenty-Five Years of Meiotic Mutants in Drosophila

## Citation (NLM)
Hawley RS. Meiosis as an "M" thing: twenty-five years of meiotic mutants in Drosophila. Genetics. 1993;135(3):613-618.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/135/3/613](https://genetics.org/content/135/3/613)

---

## Summary

This *Genetics* Perspectives essay by R. Scott Hawley marks the 25th anniversary of Sandler, Lindsley, Nicoletti and Trippa (1968), the paper that became the cornerstone of the genetic analysis of meiosis in *Drosophila melanogaster*. Hawley celebrates it alongside its intellectual companion, Baker and Carpenter (1972), arguing that together these two works reframed how genetics was done in higher eukaryotes. Before 1968, only three recessive mutations affecting meiosis were known (c(3)G, *ca* and *eq*), each studied as single alleles found by chance. What made Sandler *et al.* revolutionary was not a single discovery but a *systematic* approach: the first deliberate screen designed to isolate meiotic mutants and to characterize them in a standardized way. It stands as one of the earliest examples—alongside Salome Waelsch's *t*-locus lethal screens in the mouse and Ed Lewis's work on the bithorax complex—of systematically hunting mutations that disrupt a complex regulatory process in a higher eukaryote.

The essay recounts the memorable methodology with warmth. Because EMS mutagenesis was not yet available to fly geneticists, Sandler and Lindsley reasoned that recessive meiotic mutations would exist in natural populations as heterozygotes at a frequency around the square root of the mutation rate—higher than could be obtained with X-rays or gamma rays. So they collected flies from wild populations in and around Rome (including a winery and the city's wholesale fruit market), extracting lethal-free second- and third-chromosome complements and testing homozygotes for effects on segregation and recombination. Of 118 complements tested in females, 11 significantly increased nondisjunction; screens in males yielded additional segregation mutants plus a new Segregation Distorter chromosome. In parallel, Baker and Carpenter—then graduate students in Sandler's lab—screened 209 EMS-treated X chromosomes and recovered a set of mutants (*mei-9, mei-41, mei-218, mei-352, nod*) that would drive two decades of meiosis research.

Hawley explains what these mutants taught. The screens directly tested a standing hypothesis: that *Drosophila* females possess two segregation systems, a chiasmate system and a "distributive" (achiasmate) system, each governed by distinct but overlapping sets of genes. Mutations such as *nod* and *mei-S51* confirmed that achiasmate disjunction is genetically separate from chiasmate segregation, and later work on *mei-S51* revealed there are in fact two distinct achiasmate processes. Other mutants (*mei-S332, ord*) showed that sister-chromatid cohesion at meiosis II is under common control in both sexes, while male-specific, chromosome-specific mutants (e.g., the *mei-S8* alleles affecting only chromosome 4) suggested a fundamental difference between male and female meiosis. Crucially, analysis of the recovered mutants let Sandler *et al.* draw pathway diagrams describing wild-type gene functions in terms of "landmarks" and "control points"—language that presaged modern discussions of the cell cycle.

Hawley closes with reflection. The recombination-defective mutants overlapped with mutagen-sensitive/repair-defective mutants, and many also showed mitotic chromosome defects, interlocking meiosis with the emerging fields of DNA repair and mitotic control. By the late 1980s interest in fly meiosis had waned, eclipsed by developmental genetics, but the field then experienced a renaissance driven by new alleles, confocal microscopy, and molecular cloning (e.g., *nod* encodes a kinesin-like protein). Yet Hawley's "strongest perception" is unease that the legacy of mutants from 1968 and 1972 is nearly exhausted—new large-scale screens will be needed. His highest praise is that these two founding papers still serve as the best guideposts for that renewed effort. (The piece is dedicated to the memory of Larry Sandler, and includes the endearing anecdote of Figure 3 having been printed upside down.)

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Illustrates the pivotal shift from studying mutations found "by chance" to conducting *systematic genetic screens*—a foundational concept in forward genetics that students meet early.
- Shows how mutant phenotypes are used to dissect a complex biological process (meiosis) into discrete, genetically separable steps, and how genetic pathways/flow charts are deduced.
- Demonstrates the enduring value of well-characterized mutant collections as community resources, and how classical genetics feeds into later molecular cloning.
- A readable, human account that connects Hardy-Weinberg reasoning (recessives as heterozygotes in wild populations) to experimental screen design.

## Key References

1. **Sandler, Lindsley, Nicoletti & Trippa (1968)** — "Mutants affecting meiosis in natural populations of Drosophila melanogaster," *Genetics* — the founding systematic meiotic-mutant screen this essay celebrates.
2. **Baker & Carpenter (1972)** — "Genetic analysis of sex chromosome meiotic mutants," *Genetics* — the EMS-based companion screen yielding *mei-9, mei-41, mei-218, nod*.
3. **Carpenter (1973)** — mutant defective in distributive disjunction — confirmed achiasmate disjunction is a separate process.
4. **Zhang et al. (1990)** — *nod* encodes a kinesin-like protein — landmark molecular characterization of a meiotic mutant.
5. **Baker & Hall (1976)** — "Meiotic mutants: genic control of recombination and segregation" — synthesized the mutant analyses into pathway road maps.

## Future Research Directions

- Hawley's call for new large-scale screens using higher-tech mutagens (enhancer traps) with molecular readouts foreshadows modern genome-wide and CRISPR-based approaches to meiosis.
- Molecular characterization of the cohesion and segregation genes (*mei-S332, ord*) connects classical fly genetics to today's mechanistic understanding of chromosome cohesion, checkpoints, and aneuploidy in human disease.
