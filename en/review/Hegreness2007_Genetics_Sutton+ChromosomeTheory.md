# What Did Sutton See?: Thirty Years of Confusion Over the Chromosomal Basis of Mendelism

## Summary

This historical paper reexamines Walter Sutton's 1902–1903 cytological studies on grasshopper (*Brachystola magna*) chromosomes, and traces a significant conceptual error in his work that propagated through the genetics literature for three decades.

Sutton's pivotal papers—"On the Morphology of the Chromosome Group in Brachystola magna" (1902) and "The Chromosomes in Heredity" (1903)—proposed the parallel between chromosomal behavior during meiosis and Mendel's laws of heredity, laying the foundation for the chromosome theory of inheritance. In the 1903 paper, Sutton argued that homologous chromosomes pair and segregate across generations, thereby providing a physical mechanism for Mendel's law of segregation.

However, Sutton made a critical error: he believed that the **second meiotic division** was the reductive division (halving chromosome number), when in fact it is the **first meiotic division** (homolog separation) that is reductive. This mistake occurred because Sutton could not reliably track individual chromosome pairs through the complete sequence of meiotic divisions in his grasshopper material. He correctly observed the parallel between chromosome behavior and Mendel's ratios, but built his argument on a misidentified division.

Eleanor Carothers corrected this error in 1913 by tracking heteromorphic (non-identical) bivalent chromosomes through both meiotic divisions, unambiguously demonstrating that the first division is reductive. Despite this correction, Hegreness and Meselson document through careful literature analysis that the confusion persisted in textbooks and scientific papers through the 1930s, with many authors either repeating Sutton's error or failing to clearly resolve it.

The paper's historical message is that the chromosome theory of heredity was not established through a clean, linear sequence of insights—it involved significant misconceptions, slow correction, and lingering ambiguity that took decades to fully resolve.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the historical foundation for the chromosome theory of heredity, which connects Mendel's abstract factors to physical chromosomes
- Demonstrates that the first meiotic division (not the second) is the reductive division—a conceptual anchor for understanding meiosis
- Illustrates how even foundational scientific concepts can harbor errors that persist for decades in the published literature
- Essential for this course because understanding the chromosome theory requires knowing what was actually shown, when, and how the errors were corrected—not just the idealized textbook narrative
