# The Impact of Whole Genome Sequencing on Model System Genetics: Get Ready for the Ride

## Citation (NLM)
Hobert O. The impact of whole genome sequencing on model system genetics: get ready for the ride. Genetics. 2010;184(2):317-319. doi:10.1534/genetics.109.112938

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.109.112938](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.109.112938)

---

## Summary

In this Perspectives essay, Oliver Hobert argues that next-generation whole genome sequencing (WGS) is about to transform how forward genetics is practiced in model organisms. He opens by celebrating the classic forward-genetic screen — isolating mutants in which a specific biological process is disrupted — as an unbiased, assumption-free, and conceptually beautiful approach whose unpredictability (he cites the discovery of miRNAs and Ed Lewis's homeotic mutants) rewards the adventurous. But he stresses that phenotypic analysis alone is not enough: the key mechanistic insight comes from identifying the actual molecular lesion in the mutant, and that identification step has traditionally been the bottleneck.

Hobert reviews the two classical mutagen types and their tradeoffs. Biological mutagens (transposons, viruses, plasmids) leave an easily locatable DNA "footprint," which is why many geneticists prefer them, but they carry positional bias. Chemical mutagens such as EMS and ENU are in many ways superior — high mutation frequency, a rich spectrum of allele types (hypomorphs, hypermorphs, amorphs, neomorphs), no positional bias, and point mutations useful for dissecting protein domains — but their lesions must be located by tedious, time-consuming positional cloning. This difficulty, he notes, is precisely why RNAi-based screens gained popularity in *C. elegans*, even though RNAi suffers from variable penetrance, off-target effects, and a limited range of gene-activity effects compared to true mutations.

The core argument is that deep sequencing (on platforms like Illumina's Genome Analyzer or ABI's SOLiD) lets researchers exploit chemical mutagens without their main drawback, by directly reading out mutagen-induced sequence changes relative to a reference genome. Proof-of-concept studies had already appeared within the previous year in bacteria, yeast, plants, worms, and flies, and Hobert's own lab had used WGS (starting with Sarin et al. 2008) to identify the molecular basis of more than ten *C. elegans* strains defective in neuronal development. He emphasizes the practical advantages: sequencing a *C. elegans* genome at 10× coverage cost under ~$2,000 and took only about five days, with sequencing capacity following Moore's-law-like doubling — putting the "$1,000 genome" for worm and fly "just around the corner." To pinpoint the causal variant among many, background variants can be outcrossed or removed by rough mapping, and candidates validated by transformation rescue, RNAi phenocopy, or — most powerfully — resequencing multiple independent alleles of the same locus.

Hobert then lays out the broader impact. Freed from the "wastelands of genetic mapping," experimenters can process whole mutant collections in parallel, approaching the "holy grail" of near-complete mutational saturation and the deciphering of entire pathways; the new limiting step becomes building mutant collections, spurring automated, machine-based mutant selection. WGS also opens problems previously hard to tackle: subtle or low-penetrance phenotypes, background-sensitive behavioral traits, and even multigenic traits and QTL. It can bring previously intractable organisms into the model-system arena and, as human cancer-genome sequencing was already showing, informs human genetics — though model systems remain essential because their experimental accessibility lets researchers prove which variant is truly causal. His closing message to model-system geneticists is characteristically upbeat: "Get access to a deep sequencer, buckle up, and get ready for the ride."

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Bridges classical forward genetics with the genomics era, showing students how a decades-old strategy (mutagenize, screen, clone the gene) was revolutionized by a single technological advance.
- Clearly explains the conceptual distinction and tradeoffs between chemical and biological mutagens, and between forward-genetic and RNAi-based screens — core vocabulary for an introductory course.
- Illustrates how technology reshapes what questions become tractable (multigenic traits, QTL, subtle phenotypes) and shifts the rate-limiting step of research.
- A vivid, forward-looking primer that helps students appreciate why genome sequencing is now routine in genetics.

## Key References

1. **Sarin et al. (2008)** — *C. elegans* mutant allele identification by whole-genome sequencing (Nat. Methods) — the proof-of-principle study from Hobert's lab.
2. **Lewis (1978)** — the bithorax complex controlling *Drosophila* segmentation — cited as a triumph of phenotype-driven forward genetics.
3. **Lee, Feinbaum & Ambros (1993)** — *lin-4* encodes small RNAs (the first miRNA) — an example of the unpredictable discoveries forward screens yield.
4. **Kamath & Ahringer (2003)** — genome-wide RNAi screening in *C. elegans* — the RNAi alternative WGS improves upon.
5. **Bigelow et al. (2009)** — MAQGene software for *C. elegans* mutant genome analysis — bioinformatic tooling that makes WGS analysis fast.

## Future Research Directions

- WGS-based mutant identification has since become standard, enabling mutational saturation of pathways and the molecular dissection of quantitative and complex traits — exactly as Hobert predicted.
- Automated, high-throughput mutant selection coupled to sequencing points toward the "industrial-scale" genetic screening now common, and toward bringing non-traditional organisms into experimental genetics.
