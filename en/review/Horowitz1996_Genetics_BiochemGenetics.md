## Summary

This short Perspectives piece by N. H. Horowitz—himself a member of Beadle's laboratory—commemorates the 60th anniversary of the Beadle-Ephrussi 1936 *Drosophila* eye disc transplantation experiment, and traces the path from that experiment to the one-gene-one-enzyme hypothesis.

**The 1936 Beadle-Ephrussi experiment**: Beadle and Ephrussi developed a technique to transplant eye imaginal discs from *Drosophila* larvae into the body cavities of host larvae, then observe the resulting eye color in adults. Testing eye color mutants, they found that two mutations—**vermilion (v)** and **cinnabar (cn)**—produced non-autonomous phenotypes: the eye color of the transplanted disc depended on the host genotype. This indicated that diffusible chemical substances produced by genes in the host could rescue (or fail to rescue) the transplanted disc's pigment pathway, making these the first genetically characterized metabolic intermediates.

**Biochemical identification**: The v+ substance was identified as **kynurenine**—initially by Butenandt's German team in 1940, then confirmed by Tatum in Beadle's lab. The cn+ substance was identified as **3-hydroxy-L-kynurenine**. Together, these results mapped the biosynthetic pathway: tryptophan → kynurenine → 3-hydroxy-L-kynurenine → xanthommatin (eye pigment). Each step corresponded to a gene, providing the first clear demonstration that genes control specific biochemical reactions.

**Pathway to the one-gene-one-enzyme hypothesis**: These findings led directly to Beadle and Tatum's landmark work with *Neurospora crassa*, where they demonstrated systematically that each gene specifies a single enzyme—the one-gene-one-enzyme hypothesis. This became the central paradigm connecting genetics to biochemistry and earned Beadle and Tatum the 1958 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine.

Horowitz also reflects on the 1951 Cold Spring Harbor Symposium, where Barbara McClintock presented her discovery of transposable elements in maize—a finding that was not readily accepted by the genetics community at the time, illustrating how paradigm resistance operates even among experts.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the historical origin of the fundamental question "how do genes control phenotype?" and its first experimental resolution
- The Beadle-Ephrussi-Tatum lineage is the conceptual foundation for understanding genotype-phenotype relationships through biochemical pathways
- Illustrates the classic forward genetics logic: phenotype-based mutant selection → rescue experiments → biochemical identification of the diffusible factor
- Essential for this course because the one-gene-one-enzyme concept anchors modern understanding of how genes function, before students encounter more complex gene-function relationships

## Future Research Directions

- Investigate evolutionary conservation of the kynurenine pathway and its links to human disease (neurodegeneration, psychiatric disorders)

- Analyze Cold Spring Harbor Symposium records from the 1940s–50s to reconstruct how paradigm competition and acceptance operated in early molecular genetics
