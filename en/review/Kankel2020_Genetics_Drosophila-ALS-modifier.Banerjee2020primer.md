## Summary

This educational primer provides background for reading Kankel et al. (2020), an ALS modifier screen paper. Authors Banerjee, Benji, Liberow, and Steinhauer cover the following key topics at an introductory level.

***Drosophila melanogaster* as a model organism**: The fly offers short generation time (~2 weeks), feasibility of large-scale genetic screens, powerful genetic tools (GAL4-UAS system, Exelixis deletion collection), and high conservation with human disease genes (~75% of human disease genes have a *Drosophila* ortholog), making it ideal for dissecting human disease mechanisms.

**Forward genetic screens**: Starting from a disease-relevant phenotype (here, ALS-related neurodegeneration) and systematically searching for mutations or deletions that modify that phenotype. **Dominant modifiers** are genetic variants that alter the phenotype in a heterozygous state, providing entry points into gene dosage effects and genetic interaction networks.

**The GAL4-UAS system**: The yeast-derived GAL4 transcription factor and its upstream activation sequence (UAS) allow tissue-specific gene overexpression or knockdown (via RNAi). In the ALS context, human ALS-associated genes (FUS, TDP-43, C9orf72, SOD1) are expressed in fly neurons to recapitulate neurodegeneration phenotypes that can then be screened for modifiers.

**ALS biology**: FUS and TDP-43 are RNA-binding proteins (RBPs) that normally function in the nucleus in RNA splicing and transport. In ALS, these proteins form cytoplasmic aggregates that drive motor neuron death. G4C2 repeat expansion in the C9orf72 gene is the most common cause of familial ALS.

**The Exelixis collection**: A genome-wide collection of deletions and insertions covering much of the *Drosophila* genome, enabling systematic screens. Kankel et al. used this collection to identify dominant modifiers of FUS-overexpression neurodegeneration.

The paper includes guided reading questions to help students critically engage with the original Kankel et al. paper.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Introduces modern applications of forward genetic screens—using *Drosophila* to discover genes relevant to human neurodegeneration
- The GAL4-UAS system is a foundational tool in reverse genetics and functional genomics; understanding it is essential for interpreting many contemporary genetics papers
- ALS is a genetically tractable neurodegenerative disease sitting at the intersection of simple Mendelian genetics and complex disease genetics
- Essential for this course because it teaches how forward and reverse genetics approaches are integrated in practice, using an immediately medically relevant disease system
