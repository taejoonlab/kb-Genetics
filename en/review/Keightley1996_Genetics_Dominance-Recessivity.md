# A Metabolic Basis for Dominance and Recessivity

## Citation (NLM)
Keightley PD. A metabolic basis for dominance and recessivity. Genetics. 1996;143(2):621-625.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/143/2/621](https://genetics.org/content/143/2/621)

---

## Summary

This *Genetics* Perspectives essay by Peter D. Keightley revisits one of the oldest questions in genetics—why is the wild type usually dominant over mutant alleles?—and celebrates the 1981 *Genetics* paper by Henrik Kacser and Jim Burns that gave it a compelling physiological answer. Keightley frames the problem around three patterns any theory must explain: (1) Mendel's observation that heterozygotes often look like one of the homozygotes, with the wild type dominant over the mutant in the vast majority of cases; (2) for mutations of small effect, heterozygotes are on average roughly intermediate; and (3) in allelic series (e.g., the albino series in rodents), heterozygotes between "lower" mutant alleles are intermediate, while the wild type is dominant over all of them. The essay is also a personal tribute to Kacser, who died in March 1995 after a 40-year career focused on the kinetic properties of living systems.

Keightley traces the historical debate. R. A. Fisher (1928) proposed an *evolutionary* explanation: because heterozygotes far outnumber mutant homozygotes, selection at modifier loci would push heterozygotes toward the wild-type phenotype, making wild-type alleles dominant. Sewall Wright (1934) challenged this, showing the relevant selection coefficients would be as small as the mutation rate and thus swamped by mutation. Wright instead pointed to physiology, considering the relationship between the activity of a single enzyme in a metabolic pathway and the steady-state flux of the pathway. He predicted a hyperbolic (diminishing-returns) relationship: if wild-type enzyme activity sits on the plateau of the flux–activity curve, then halving activity (as in a heterozygote for a null allele) barely reduces flux, so null mutants are recessive. Wright, however, still invoked evolution—a selected "safety factor"—to explain why activity lies on the plateau.

The core of the essay explains how Kacser and Burns made Wright's insight rigorous and, crucially, showed the evolutionary safety-factor argument to be unnecessary. Their work rested on metabolic control analysis, developed in their landmark 1973 paper (and independently by Heinrich and Rapoport in Berlin). They defined a *control coefficient* as the fractional change in a systemic property (such as flux) resulting from a fractional change in one enzyme's activity, and proved the **summation theorem**: the control coefficients of all enzymes for a given flux sum to unity. Because a pathway contains many enzymes, most control coefficients must be small; combined with the hyperbolic flux–activity relationship, recessivity of null mutants becomes an *automatic, inevitable consequence* of the kinetic structure of metabolism—no selection for dominance is needed. The framework also explains the intermediate phenotypes seen among lower members of allelic series (which have low activity, hence control coefficients approaching unity and near-linear behavior), and it extends, with more care, to branched pathways.

Keightley then marshals further evidence against selection theories, notably Charlesworth (1979)—whose model predicts no relationship between an allele's fitness effect and its dominance, contrary to observation—and Orr (1991), who found mutations are as often recessive in the predominantly haploid alga *Chlamydomonas* as in *Drosophila*, where selection on the rare diploid phase could not have shaped dominance. Finally, the essay is careful about the theory's limits: it applies to enzyme-encoding genes and "metabolic traits," but not to genes controlling timing or tissue specificity of expression, hormone/receptor systems, or cases producing *dominant* mutations. Dominant mutations arise instead through mechanisms outside the Kacser–Burns framework—haploinsufficiency (e.g., collagen), gain of function (e.g., the CAG/polyglutamine expansion of Huntington's disease), or toxic products—as reviewed by Wilkie (1994). Keightley closes by noting how metabolic control theory also illuminates epistasis, since simultaneous changes in several enzymes can produce synergistic effects on flux.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides a mechanistic, quantitative answer to the classic textbook question "why are most mutations recessive?"—moving beyond the bare Mendelian statement of dominance.
- Contrasts an *evolutionary* explanation (Fisher) with a *physiological/metabolic* one (Wright, Kacser–Burns), teaching students how competing hypotheses are tested and discriminated.
- Introduces metabolic control analysis, the summation theorem, and control coefficients—concepts linking genetics to biochemistry and systems biology.
- Clarifies why dominant mutations (haploinsufficiency, gain of function) require different explanations, reinforcing that "dominance" is a phenotypic, not intrinsic, property of alleles.

## Key References

1. **Kacser & Burns (1981)** — "The molecular basis of dominance," *Genetics* — the paper this essay celebrates; recessivity as an inevitable consequence of metabolic kinetics.
2. **Kacser & Burns (1973)** — "The control of flux" — foundational paper of metabolic control analysis and the summation theorem.
3. **Fisher (1928)** — evolutionary theory of dominance via modifier selection — the hypothesis the essay ultimately argues against.
4. **Wright (1934)** — "Molecular and evolutionary theories of dominance" — introduced the hyperbolic flux–enzyme relationship.
5. **Orr (1991)** — test of Fisher's theory in *Chlamydomonas* — strong empirical evidence against the evolution-of-dominance theory.

## Future Research Directions

- Determining what fraction of genes (and of classically known mutations) encode enzymes would clarify how broadly the metabolic theory applies genome-wide.
- Metabolic control theory's predictions about synergistic epistasis and multi-enzyme modulation connect to modern metabolic engineering and to understanding the genetic architecture of complex traits and mutation load.
