# Hugo de Vries and the Rediscovery of Mendel's Laws

## Citation (NLM)
Kottler MJ. Hugo de Vries and the rediscovery of Mendel's laws. Ann Sci. 1979;36(5):517-538. doi:10.1080/00033797900200351

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1080/00033797900200351](https://doi.org/10.1080/00033797900200351)

---

## Summary

This history-of-science article by Malcolm J. Kottler examines one of the central controversies in the history of genetics: whether Hugo de Vries independently discovered Mendel's laws before reading Mendel's paper in 1900. In that year, de Vries, Carl Correns, and Erich Tschermak each published papers announcing rediscovery of both Mendel's laws and Mendel's original paper, and each claimed to have found the laws for himself first. Historians generally agree that Tschermak was not an independent rediscoverer (his 1900 paper shows he did not fully understand Mendel's laws even after reading Mendel and de Vries), while Correns's claim has attracted little scrutiny. De Vries's claim, however, has been genuinely controversial. Kottler's central evidence is a set of *changing ratios*: de Vries's first published second-generation (F2) ratios, from an 1897 Dutch paper, were 2/3 : 1/3 and 80% : 20% — not 3 : 1. By 1900 both had become 3 : 1. Since de Vries dated his discovery of the laws to 1896, these shifting ratios suggest that as late as 1897 he had not yet grasped the 3 : 1 segregation ratio.

Kottler builds the argument from de Vries's actual experiments. In the 1897 paper "Erfelijke Monstrositeiten" ("Hereditary Monstrosities") — a source overlooked in the literature — de Vries reported crosses framed in terms of his own theory of intracellular pangenesis (1889), which held that each character corresponds to a material unit, the "pangene," transferable between forms by hybridization. In a *Lychnis diurna* × *L. vespertina glabra* cross for hairlessness, he described the F2 as "2/3 pubescent and 1/3 deprived of hair." In a *Linaria* cross for flower color, he described the F2 (about 600 plants) as "about 80% orange, about 20% yellow." Neither was reported as 3 : 1. When de Vries gave a fuller account of the Lychnis cross at the 1899 International Conference on Hybridization (published 1900), he reported "only about three-fourths were hairy," giving raw counts of 99 hairy : 54 hairless. C. Zirkle noted that 99 : 54 fits a 2 : 1 ratio far better than 3 : 1; Kottler argues that de Vries originally saw only the obvious 2 : 1 in his data and was able to "see" the 3 : 1 in the same raw numbers only after learning the true ratio from another source. The 1903 account in volume two of *Die Mutationstheorie* gives the raw Linaria data as 482 : 135 (78.1% : 21.9%), which de Vries in 1897 read as 80% : 20% but in 1903 interpreted as a close approximation to the Mendelian 25% recessive.

A key section concerns "xenia" — the direct, immediate effect of foreign pollen on the seed (endosperm), well documented in maize. De Vries had grown corn since 1876 and returned to xenia by hybridization in 1898, crossing starchy and sweet corn. His clever experimental design left some tassels on the sweet corn so that hybrid (starchy) and self-fertilized (sweet) kernels appeared on the same ear, making the hybrid origin visibly obvious. Kottler carefully corrects a common error: the numbers 180 starchy : 66 sweet often cited as a 3 : 1 F2 ratio were *not* an F2 ratio at all — the 180 starchy kernels were F1 hybrids and the 66 sweet were self-fertilization products. The genuine F2 (from self-fertilizing the starchy F1 plants) gave "about three-fourths starchy, one-fourth sweet." De Vries's two xenia papers (both submitted December 1899) contain his first *published* 3 : 1 F2 ratio; the shorter one appeared before he could have read Correns's xenia paper, so de Vries had obtained at least one 3 : 1 ratio independently of Correns.

The dating matters for the independence question. Zirkle had argued that de Vries did not know the 3 : 1 ratio in July 1899, learned it from Mendel's paper (which he read only afterward), and was led to Mendel by Correns's xenia paper — in which Correns's final sentence referenced both Darwin and Mendel on pea seed color. Kottler shows this specific route is untenable, because de Vries's shorter xenia paper (independent of Correns) already reported a 3 : 1 ratio; if Correns's paper played any role, it was at most to inform de Vries that others already knew of Mendel, removing any incentive to suppress the reference. Correns himself had discovered the Mendelian laws by December 1899 but discussed only the F1 in his xenia paper. Kottler also documents numerous discrepancies between de Vries's 1900 and 1903 accounts (in recessive percentages and in the dates assigned to generations), and notes that a large, technically detailed section on de Vries's Mendelian experiments in *Die Mutationstheorie* was entirely omitted from the 1909–1910 English translation — which is why the article includes an appendix reconstructing these experiments. The cumulative case is that de Vries's claim to have found the 3 : 1 ratio (and the laws) by 1896 is not supported by his own published ratios before late 1899.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Illuminates the 1900 "rediscovery of Mendel" — a foundational moment in genetics — showing that the history is far messier than the textbook narrative of three independent rediscoverers.
- Demonstrates how the interpretation of data (2 : 1 vs. 3 : 1, 80% vs. 75%) depends on the theoretical framework one already holds — a valuable lesson in how ratios only become meaningful once the segregation model is understood.
- Introduces de Vries's competing theory of intracellular pangenesis and the phenomenon of xenia (pollen effect on endosperm), useful context for the conceptual landscape before Mendelism.
- Provides a case study in historical detective work: using changing numbers, publication dates, and translation gaps to test a scientist's priority claim.

## Key References

1. **de Vries (1897)** — "Erfelijke Monstrositeiten" — the neglected first publication of de Vries's F2 ratios (2/3 : 1/3 and 80% : 20%), central to Kottler's argument.
2. **de Vries (1900)** — the three rediscovery papers ("Das Spaltungsgesetz der Bastarde" and the French papers) announcing 3 : 1 ratios and Mendel's laws.
3. **de Vries (1889)** — *Intracellular Pangenesis* — his theory of the "pangene," the framework guiding his hybridization experiments.
4. **Zirkle (1968)** — argued de Vries learned the 3 : 1 ratio from Mendel via Correns; the interpretation Kottler critiques and partly refutes.
5. **Correns (1899)** — "Untersuchungen über die Xenien bei Zea Mays" — the xenia paper whose Mendel reference Zirkle thought led de Vries to Mendel.

## Future Research Directions

- The broader question of how independent the several rediscoverers truly were, and how priority disputes should be adjudicated from documentary evidence, remains a live topic in the history and philosophy of science.
- Kottler's appendix (reconstructing experiments omitted from the English *Mutationstheorie*) invites fuller scholarly recovery of de Vries's original Mendelian data and its bearing on the reconstruction of segregation.
