## Summary

This Genetics Classic commentary by Gary Churchill summarizes the historical significance of Lander and Botstein (1989), the landmark paper that established the modern methodology for mapping quantitative trait loci (QTL) and directly enabled the genomic dissection of complex traits.

**Historical context**: Although the polygenic basis of continuous trait variation had been theorized since the early 20th century, and early mapping studies by Sax (1923) had shown in principle that quantitative traits could be linked to genetic markers, no systematic method existed for genome-wide QTL detection. Botstein et al. (1980) proposed RFLP markers as genome-wide genetic markers, enabling dense genetic linkage maps; Lander and Botstein then provided the analytical framework to exploit those maps for QTL mapping.

**Interval mapping**: The central methodological innovation is **interval mapping**. Traditional single-marker analysis systematically underestimates QTL effects when the QTL does not lie at the marker locus, cannot distinguish tight linkage to a weak QTL from loose linkage to a strong one, and does not define QTL position. Interval mapping uses flanking markers to compute LOD scores at every position along the genome via maximum likelihood, providing unbiased effect estimates, QTL positions with one-LOD support intervals, and a theoretically justified genome-wide significance threshold. The paper also derived the appropriate LOD score threshold for genome-wide searches (typically 2–3) by connecting the LOD score to the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck diffusion process.

**Practical design tools**: The paper provides cross-design optimization using Wright's formula (estimating the number of effective QTL factors to choose crosses with large-effect QTLs), and **selective genotyping**—genotyping only phenotypic extremes—which can reduce the number of individuals requiring genotyping by up to 7-fold with only modest increase in population size.

**Legacy**: Lander-Botstein interval mapping enabled hundreds of QTL studies in agriculture (disease resistance, yield), biomedical research (blood pressure, diabetes, cancer predisposition), and evolutionary biology. The paper is also the conceptual predecessor of modern GWAS methodology.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Establishes the foundational methodology of QTL mapping—the entry point for understanding the genetics of complex, continuously varying traits
- Introduces LOD scores, interval mapping, and multiple testing correction in a genetic mapping context—key statistical concepts for quantitative genetics
- Provides the historical bridge between classical linkage analysis and modern GWAS
- Essential for this course because understanding how Mendelian factors underlying quantitative traits are mapped is prerequisite for appreciating complex trait genetics
