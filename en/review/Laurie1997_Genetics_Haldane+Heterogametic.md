# The Weaker Sex Is Heterogametic: 75 Years of HALDANE'S Rule

## Summary

This Perspectives article by Cathy Laurie marks the 75th anniversary of Haldane's Rule, reviewing current understanding of the rule's generality and the competing mechanistic hypotheses advanced to explain it.

**Haldane's Rule (1922)**: "When in the F1 offspring of two different animal races one sex is absent, rare, or sterile, that sex is the heterogametic sex." In mammals and *Drosophila* (XY males), it is hybrid males that are affected; in birds and Lepidoptera (ZW females), hybrid females are more often inviable or sterile.

**Generality**: Data from mammals, birds, Lepidoptera, *Drosophila*, reptiles (*Lacerta*), amphibians (*Triturus*), nematodes (*Caenorhabditis*), and Orthoptera all support the rule. The pattern cuts across phylogenetic boundaries and is unrelated to maleness per se—it tracks heterogamety in both directions (male or female). Since heterogamety has evolved independently multiple times across taxa, the association with hybrid dysfunction appears causally related to heterogamety itself.

**Major hypotheses**:

1. **The dominance theory**: Proposed verbally by Muller (1942) and formalized mathematically by Orr and Turelli (1995). If X-linked incompatibility interactions are **partially recessive** on average, the heterogametic sex (hemizygous for one X) suffers more than the homogametic sex (heterozygous at both X chromosomes, with each partner allele buffering the incompatibility). The mathematical model shows that partial recessivity is sufficient to produce Haldane's Rule, and that if incompatibility factors evolve faster in the heterogametic sex (r > 1), the rule can hold even under additivity or partial dominance.

2. **Rapid evolution of hybrid male sterility**: Proposed by Wu and Davis (1993). Genetic dissection using attached-X experiments, backcross analysis, and introgression lines in *Drosophila* shows that autosomal factors causing male sterility are far more numerous and evolve much faster (~10× more sterility factors per genomic segment) than those causing female sterility. This is likely driven by sexual selection on male reproductive characters and/or special features of spermatogenesis (lack of post-meiotic transcription, precocious X inactivation).

3. **Y chromosome incompatibilities**: Y-X and Y-autosome incompatibilities contribute to hybrid male sterility in some *Drosophila* species pairs, but this cannot explain inviability and is not a general mechanism.

4. **Meiotic drive**: Competing sex chromosome drive systems between hybridizing species could produce heterogametic dysfunction, but direct empirical evidence is lacking.

Laurie concludes that the dominance theory and rapid evolution of hybrid male sterility are likely complementary mechanisms, explaining hybrid inviability and sterility respectively.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides a population genetics framework for understanding sex chromosome evolution and postzygotic reproductive isolation—the genetic basis of speciation
- Connects Dobzhansky-Muller incompatibilities (the general framework for hybrid dysfunction) to the specific pattern of Haldane's Rule
- Demonstrates how a verbal evolutionary observation (1922) was transformed into a quantitative mathematical theory (1995) through decades of experimental and theoretical work
- Essential for this course because it integrates sex determination, population genetics theory, and speciation genetics in a single well-documented empirical pattern

## Future Research Directions

- Collect additional hybridization data in reptiles and amphibians (taxa with multiple independent transitions in which sex is heterogametic) to further test the generality of Haldane's Rule
- Identify the molecular identity and evolutionary origin of male sterility factors (sexual selection vs. spermatogenesis-specific vulnerability)
- Molecular analysis of exceptions to Haldane's Rule to determine whether they represent mechanistically distinct processes
