# Gene recombination and linked segregations in Escherichia coli

## Citation (NLM)
Lederberg J. Gene recombination and linked segregations in Escherichia coli. Genetics. 1987;117(1):1-4.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/117/1/1](https://genetics.org/content/117/1/1)

---

## Summary

In this "Perspectives" reminiscence, Joshua Lederberg looks back forty years to his landmark 1947 Genetics paper of the same title, written when he was a young researcher who had just discovered genetic recombination in *Escherichia coli* strain K-12 with E. L. Tatum (Lederberg and Tatum 1946). Lederberg recounts the personal and institutional path of the work: it began in Francis J. Ryan's laboratory at Columbia in 1945, matured in Tatum's laboratory at Yale between March 1946 and June 1947 (using Tatum's hard-won auxotrophic mutants of K-12), and was written up at the Marine Biological Laboratory at Woods Hole. The whole enterprise had been motivated by Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty's 1944 identification of DNA as the transforming principle in pneumococcus — a finding that could not be assimilated as "the chemistry of the gene" without a broader base of bacterial genetics.

The scientific core is the demonstration that bacteria undergo genuine genetic recombination. By mixing different auxotrophic strains and applying stringent selection, Lederberg could detect prototrophic recombinants at frequencies as low as one per million; doubly marked parents ruled out simple reversion. Crucially, unselected markers — including auxotrophies and resistance to phage T1 — segregated among the selected prototrophs. The sharp segregation of T1 resistance into stable sensitive and resistant classes argued against artifacts such as cell mixtures or heterokaryosis (nuclear mixing), since a heterokaryon would not produce two pure, stably breeding categories. These results were first presented at the 1946 Cold Spring Harbor Symposium, where they met notable skepticism (famously from Max Delbrück), prompting Lederberg to perform single-cell isolations (learned from Max Zelle) to close the argument.

Lederberg then describes the drive toward the "Mendelization" of bacteria: determining the range of markers that participate in crossing and whether they fall into linkage groups. Using auxotrophs, virus-resistance markers, and sugar-fermentation mutants (scorable by color on indicator media such as eosin–methylene blue agar), he accumulated eight segregating markers. Their non-random co-segregation statistics implied a *single linkage group* and yielded the first genetic map of *E. coli* — a map broadly consistent with the far more complete modern maps of over a thousand markers (Bachmann 1983). He recalls attempts to graft classical chromosomal concepts (four-strand crossing over, meiotic behavior) onto bacterial recombination, which were later overtaken by molecular perspectives.

The essay is candid about missteps and puzzles. When new markers (xylose, maltose, streptomycin resistance) refused to fit the linear map, Lederberg published a three-branched map in 1951, explicitly warning it was "purely formal," yet it was taken too literally; Watson and Hayes (1953) proposed three chromosomes instead. The confusion was resolved by Wollman and Jacob's kinetic studies of progressive, sometimes-interrupted chromosome transfer during conjugation, which established the now-accepted circular map. Lederberg also reflects on lasting puzzles: the striking scarcity of chromosomal inversions and aberrations in bacteria compared with Drosophila, the role of repetitive DNA in recombination, and the still-incompletely-understood physical mechanism by which the DNA strand passes from cell to cell. He closes noting that even as *E. coli* becomes a technological workhorse and its genome nears complete sequencing, some elementary aspects of conjugation remain unsettled.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A first-person account of one of the foundational experiments of microbial genetics: the proof that bacteria recombine, which opened the door to using *E. coli* as the central model organism of molecular genetics.
- Illustrates core teaching concepts — selection for recombinants, segregation of unselected markers, linkage mapping, and the logic of ruling out artifacts (heterokaryosis vs. true recombination).
- Shows how genetic mapping in bacteria evolved from a puzzling "branched" map to the correct circular chromosome, a vivid lesson in how data, models, and mechanism (conjugation) converge over time.
- Connects the 1944 Avery–MacLeod–McCarty DNA discovery to the experimental program that made bacteria a system for studying the gene chemically.

## Key References

1. **Lederberg (1947)** — the original *Genetics* paper being commemorated; first demonstration of recombination and a genetic map in *E. coli*.
2. **Lederberg & Tatum (1946)** — the initial discovery of genetic recombination in *E. coli* K-12, presented at Cold Spring Harbor.
3. **Avery, MacLeod & McCarty (1944)** — DNA as the transforming principle; the motivation for pursuing bacterial genetics.
4. **Wollman & Jacob (1958); Jacob & Wollman (1961)** — kinetic studies of interrupted conjugation that established the circular chromosome map.
5. **Watson & Hayes (1953)** — proposed three linkage groups, illustrating the mapping controversy the branched map provoked.

## Future Research Directions

- The physical conduit of conjugative DNA transfer and the fate of transferred strands during recombinant formation — questions Lederberg flagged as still unsettled and that continue to inform studies of horizontal gene transfer.
- Systematic study of why chromosomal inversions and rearrangements are so rare in bacteria compared with eukaryotes, linking genome organization, repetitive DNA content, and recombination machinery.
