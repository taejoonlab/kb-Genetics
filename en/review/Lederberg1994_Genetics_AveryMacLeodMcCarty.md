# The Transformation of Genetics by DNA: An Anniversary Celebration of Avery, MacLeod and McCarty (1944)

## Citation (NLM)

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1093/genetics/136.2.423](https://doi.org/10.1093/genetics/136.2.423)

## Summary

This essay is a historical retrospective written by Nobel laureate Joshua Lederberg on the 50th anniversary of Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty (1944). Lederberg argues that this paper opened the contemporary, molecular phase of genetics and describes with first-hand vividness how the discovery was received and debated.

The core claims of Avery et al. (1944) fall into three tiers: (a) pneumococci possess clonally heritable capsular polysaccharides associated with virulence; (b) the genetic material underlying these attributes can be transferred between clones by cell-free extracts — the phenomenon of transformation — faithfully transmitted to successive generations; (c) the chemical structure of the transforming principle is DNA, to the exclusion of protein. From these claims emerged the radical inferences that bacteria possess discrete, autonomous genes analogous to those of Drosophila, and that the gene is DNA.

Lederberg recounts being an undergraduate at Columbia working in Francis Ryan's Neurospora laboratory when reprints of the paper circulated. His personal notes read: "unlimited in its implications… Direct demonstration of the multiplication of transforming factor… Viruses are gene-type compounds." He candidly describes how this discovery vectored his own career toward pioneering bacterial genetics.

At the same time, Lederberg stresses that the paper was not immediately accepted. Alfred Mirsky persistently disputed the claim that DNA alone was the exclusive substance of transforming activity. This skepticism was grounded in the prevailing biochemical view of DNA as a monotonous, tetranucleotide-repeat structure — an unlikely candidate for biological specificity. Lederberg argues, however, that this critical reception exemplified the scientific method at its most functional rather than obstructing science. The debate stimulated Chargaff's base composition work, the Hershey-Chase phage experiment, and ultimately Watson and Crick's double helix model. Rigorous proof was only concluded with enzymatic synthesis of biologically active DNA in vitro.

Lederberg counters the comparison of Avery's fate to Mendel's "prematurity" — burial in an obscure journal and decades of neglect. Avery's paper received nearly 300 citations between 1945 and 1954, was reviewed by Sewall Wright in the Annual Review of Physiology, and was noted by three reviewers in the Annual Review of Biochemistry that same year. True prematurity implies an audience incapable of understanding the challenge; for Avery, there was open controversy and active inquiry. That Avery died in 1955 without winning the Nobel Prize remains a notable gap in the history of recognition. Lederberg devotes his own 1958 Nobel lecture to acknowledging this foundational discovery.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides first-hand historical testimony of how a paradigm-shifting discovery — DNA as genetic material — was received, contested, and ultimately vindicated, demonstrating that paradigm shifts are gradual and contested
- Explores the distinction between legitimate scientific criticism that drives further inquiry and obstruction of science, offering a philosophy-of-science perspective on standards of evidence
- Why this paper must be read in class: to understand the historical foundation of molecular genetics through the transformation experiment and to appreciate how major discoveries are accepted through debate and verification rather than immediate consensus
