# What Is Adaptation by Natural Selection? Perspectives of an Experimental Microbiologist

## Citation (NLM)
Lenski RE. What is adaptation by natural selection? Perspectives of an experimental microbiologist. PLoS Genet. 2017;13(4):e1006668. doi:10.1371/journal.pgen.1006668

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1006668](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1006668)

## Summary

Richard Lenski of Michigan State University presents a perspective on the concept of adaptation by natural selection, tracing its intellectual history through the lens of his long-term evolution experiment (LTEE) with E. coli.

Darwin believed evolution was too slow to observe directly, yet this view was increasingly challenged by microbiologists. Historical milestones include Dallinger's 1880s incubator experiments evolving protozoa to higher temperatures, the 1943 Luria-Delbruck fluctuation test establishing that mutations in bacteria arise spontaneously rather than in response to selection, and the Lederberg replica plating experiment demonstrating the conceptual distinction between mutation origin and selective fate. Experiments in the 1950s by Novick, Szilard, Atwood, and Ryan showed that natural selection drove successive selective sweeps in bacterial chemostats, establishing that microbes were excellent subjects for studying adaptation.

Lenski's own LTEE began in 1988 with six populations of E. coli founded from each of two ancestral strains differing by a neutral marker. All evolution is strictly asexual with no plasmids or functional phages. Populations live in minimal glucose medium with daily 1% serial transfer yielding approximately 6.7 cell generations per day. At time of writing the populations had exceeded 66,000 generations.

Three sets of major findings emerge. First, adaptation dynamics: during the first 2,000 generations, large-effect beneficial mutations produced step-like fitness trajectories; the rate of improvement slowed substantially thereafter but is better fit by a power-law model with no asymptote than by a hyperbolic model, with the power-law accurately predicting future fitness using truncated datasets. Second, repeatability: a typical population increased fitness by approximately 70% over 50,000 generations, while typical pairs of populations differ by only a few percent — a striking balance of predictability and divergence. Whole-genome sequencing of 264 clones revealed that over 50% of nonsynonymous mutations in non-hypermutable lineages concentrated in just 2% of protein-coding genes, a strong signal of natural selection. Third, the most dramatic finding: one population evolved the ability to use citrate aerobically at approximately 31,000 generations — a key innovation requiring a promoter-capture rearrangement and specific background mutations, demonstrating historical contingency in evolution.

The latter portion surveys the explosion of experimental evolution, including ecological diversification of Pseudomonas fluorescens in static flasks, stepwise evolution of antibiotic resistance, evolution from an obligate cheater to a superior cooperator in Myxococcus, and evolution of multicellularity ("snowflake yeast") by centrifugal selection in Saccharomyces.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the intellectual lineage from Darwin's natural selection through the Luria-Delbruck experiment to modern experimental evolution, explaining why microbiologists historically resisted Darwinism and how that resistance was overcome
- Reframes the Luria-Delbruck fluctuation test as foundational not only for molecular genetics but for establishing that natural selection operates in microorganisms
- Why this paper must be read in class: it demonstrates that adaptation by natural selection can be observed, quantified, and dissected experimentally, while illustrating core population genetics concepts — repeatability, historical contingency, fitness trajectories, and genome-level parallelism — through real data

## Future Research Directions

- Determine whether fitness improvement in LTEE follows the power-law trajectory indefinitely as the experiment approaches 100,000+ generations, or whether an asymptote eventually emerges
- Mechanistically characterize the causal mutations identified in LTEE to understand the biochemical and regulatory basis of adaptation, providing a model for studying pleiotropy and epistasis
