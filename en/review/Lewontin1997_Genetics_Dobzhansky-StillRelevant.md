# Dobzhansky's Genetics and the Origin of Species: Is It Still Relevant?

## Citation (NLM)
Lewontin RC. Dobzhansky's Genetics and the Origin of Species: is it still relevant? Genetics. 1997;147(2):351-355.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/147/2/351](https://genetics.org/content/147/2/351)

---

## Summary

Richard Lewontin marks the 60th anniversary of Theodosius Dobzhansky's *Genetics and the Origin of Species* (1937) by asking whether the book's program still guides evolutionary genetics. He accepts Jeffrey Powell's judgment that it is "the most important and influential book on evolution of the twentieth century," but argues its lasting significance lies less in synthesizing existing knowledge than in its *prospective* aspect — the implicit research agenda it set for the field from 1937 onward. Two features made the book unique: it was written in the observational language of naturalists and experimentalists, and its schema ran from the origin of variation to the formation of species, directly engaging Darwin's outline in a way that the more abstract syntheses of Fisher, Wright, and Haldane had not.

The heart of Dobzhansky's argument, Lewontin explains, is that species evolution is the conversion of variation *within* populations into variation *between* populations in time and space. Reproductive isolation is the final step in the divergence of geographically separated races. Because between-population differences must arise from within-population variation, the study of intrapopulation genetic variation and polymorphism sits at the very center of evolutionary dynamics — which is why, in a 321-page book aiming to explain speciation, 178 pages are devoted to variation within populations. Lewontin sees this emphasis as the source of population geneticists' enduring skepticism of glib adaptationist "just-so" stories, invoking the Glendower–Hotspur exchange about calling monsters "from the vasty deep."

Lewontin then traces how the actual empirical program lagged behind the manifesto and repeatedly ran into technical impasses. In 1937 the evidence for abundant genetic variation was thin — a few human blood groups, Dobzhansky and Epling's inversion polymorphisms in *Drosophila pseudoobscura*, some Mendelian morphological polymorphisms. Later editions could cite Dobzhansky's own "Genetics of Natural Populations" series and Lamotte's work on *Cepaea* shell polymorphism, but the fundamental problem remained: variation could be studied only at the level of whole chromosomes (inversions, lethals), giving no measure of how much genic variation existed. Protein electrophoresis (Hubby and Lewontin 1966) broke this impasse by revealing variation locus-by-locus in any organism — but its very strength was its fatal flaw: by detaching observations from physiology and development, it made it nearly impossible to infer *what forces* acted on the variation. "Now we could describe genetic variation quite generally but seemed barred from explaining it."

The DNA-sequence era, Lewontin argues, at last provided traction, because the many classes of nucleotides (silent sites, introns, replacement sites, flanking DNA) bear different relationships to fitness, so contrasting their patterns yields internal evidence of selection versus neutral forces — from Kreitman's 1983 *Adh* study to detection of selective sweeps (Berry et al. 1991) and the McDonald–Kreitman test. Yet he closes on a critical, even melancholy note. Population genetics, he insists, remains an *observational and statistical* science, not an experimental one: it compares standing patterns of variation against null models and infers past forces, but cannot cash those inferences into actual biological mechanisms. The irony is that Dobzhansky, who came to genetics from the study of morphological diversity in nature, ended by building a science that "speaks the language of gene frequencies," alienated from organismal biology — never publishing on the ecology or life history of *D. pseudoobscura* despite 40 years of study. Finally, by defining species solely through reproductive isolation, Dobzhansky, via a "scientific synecdoche," removed the actual speciation process — how organisms come to occupy distinct ways of making a living — from most population geneticists' concerns, a limitation Lewontin says persists to this day.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A concise intellectual history of the Modern Synthesis and the founding logic of population and evolutionary genetics — essential context for any course covering variation, selection, and speciation.
- Crystallizes the central idea that species differences are the accumulated, sorted end-product of standing genetic variation within populations.
- Traces the methodological arc from morphological polymorphisms to allozymes to DNA sequences, showing how each technique both enabled and constrained what could be inferred about natural selection.
- Offers a candid critique of the limits of an observational science, teaching students to distinguish description of variation from demonstration of the forces that shaped it.

## Key References

1. **Dobzhansky (1937, 1941, 1951)** — *Genetics and the Origin of Species* — the founding document under review.
2. **Hubby & Lewontin (1966)** — molecular (allozyme) approach to genic heterozygosity in natural populations — broke the descriptive impasse but could not identify selective forces.
3. **Kreitman (1983)** — nucleotide polymorphism at the *Adh* locus of *D. melanogaster* — first demonstration of DNA sequence power to detect weak selection.
4. **McDonald & Kreitman (1991)** — adaptive protein evolution at *Adh* — a test for adaptive divergence between species.
5. **Powell (1987)** — 50th-anniversary appreciation of Dobzhansky's book — the assessment Lewontin builds upon.

## Future Research Directions

- Lewontin's challenge — linking statistical signatures of selection to the actual biology and ecology that generate fitness differences — remains central to modern molecular population genetics and genome-wide scans for selection.
- The unresolved "antecedent question" of how organisms come to occupy distinct niches (and why sexual species need isolating mechanisms) still motivates integrating developmental and ecological biology into evolutionary genetics.
