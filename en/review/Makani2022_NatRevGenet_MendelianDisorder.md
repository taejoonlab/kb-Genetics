# From Mendel to a Mendelian Disorder: Towards a Cure for Sickle Cell Disease

## Citation (NLM)
Makani J, Nkya S, Collins F, Luzzatto L. From Mendel to a Mendelian disorder: towards a cure for sickle cell disease. Nat Rev Genet. 2022;23(7):389–390. doi:10.1038/s41576-022-00498-1

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41576-022-00498-1](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41576-022-00498-1)

---

## Summary

Published in Nature Reviews Genetics as part of the bicentenary celebration of Mendel's birth, this Comment traces how Mendel's foundational principles connect to modern gene therapy for sickle cell disease (SCD) — a paradigmatic Mendelian disorder affecting 5 million people globally, mostly in Africa.

**Mendel's foundational contribution:** Through meticulous pea experiments, Mendel established that inheritance is particulate, not blending. Parental characteristics segregate as independent units — now called genes — and can reappear in later generations. This provided quantitative evidence for "units of inheritance" long before their molecular identity was known. The molecular basis became clear when DNA was found to encode genetic information.

**Sickle cell disease as a Mendelian disorder:** A single point mutation in the β-globin gene (HBB^E6V) produces sickle hemoglobin (HbS). SCD is a classic recessive disease in HbS homozygotes (SS). Interestingly, HbS heterozygotes show dominant protection against severe malaria — exemplifying how the same molecule can have different dominance relationships depending on the trait. Modifier genes, notably BCL11A, influence fetal hemoglobin (HbF) levels; increased HbF ameliorates SCD and may have pleiotropic hematological effects. Thus even a classic Mendelian disorder is not strictly monogenic.

**Human Genome Project gaps:** Completed in 2003, the HGP revealed two major gaps: (1) Africa has the greatest human genetic diversity but the least genomic data — addressed by H3Africa and similar initiatives; (2) therapeutic benefits lagged behind diagnostic advances.

**Gene therapy advances:** SCD gene therapy has progressed from mouse models (2000) to successful human trials (2010–2021), achieving sustained correction of anemia and complete relief from vaso-occlusive events. BCL11A inhibition via shRNA or CRISPR-Cas9 knockout to increase HbF also showed encouraging clinical results. These achievements are possible precisely because SCD is a Mendelian disorder with a single causative mutation.

**Vision for Africa:** The authors (collaborators from Tanzania, USA, and Italy) call for equitable application of genomics to SCD control in Africa within 20 years. A 2019 Gates Foundation/NIH partnership aims to develop curative gene therapies applicable in low-resource settings.

**Mendel's legacy:** Mendel could not have imagined his work would one day apply to human disease, but the universality of hereditary mechanisms made it possible. From pea gardens in Brno to gene therapy for millions of African patients — a testament to the power of fundamental science.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Connects Mendel's abstract laws directly to one of the world's most prevalent genetic diseases, providing tangible motivation for studying basic genetics
- Illustrates dominant/recessive concepts, modifier genes, and gene-gene interactions all within a single disease system
- Raises equity issues in genomics: African populations are underrepresented in genomic databases despite carrying the greatest genetic diversity and disease burden
- Used in class to show the historical arc from Mendel → molecular genetics → gene therapy, and to discuss post-Mendelian complexity

## Future Research Directions

- Develop low-cost, low-infrastructure gene therapy delivery systems for resource-limited settings in Africa
- Explore in vivo gene therapy approaches that eliminate the need for ex vivo bone marrow manipulation
- Comprehensively identify SCD modifier genes in African populations to improve personalized prognosis and treatment
