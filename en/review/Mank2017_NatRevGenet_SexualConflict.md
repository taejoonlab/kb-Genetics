# Population genetics of sexual conflict in the genomic era

## Citation (NLM)
Mank JE. Population genetics of sexual conflict in the genomic era. Nat Rev Genet. 2017;18(12):721-730. doi:10.1038/nrg.2017.83

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg.2017.83](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg.2017.83)

---

## Summary

Judith Mank reviews how the explosion of population genomic data has revitalized the study of sexual conflict, enabling genome-wide scans for sexually antagonistic loci that were previously accessible only through candidate gene approaches. Sexual conflict arises when selection acts in opposing directions on males and females, either through opposing effects of alleles at a single shared locus (intra-locus sexual conflict) or through arms-race dynamics between loci in different sexes (inter-locus sexual conflict). Because males and females share the vast majority of their genome, the potential for intra-locus conflict is pervasive whenever male and female reproductive optima diverge — a situation common in species without strict monogamy. The review synthesizes case studies, population genomic methods, and genomic distribution patterns to build a comprehensive picture of sexual conflict's role in maintaining genetic diversity and shaping adaptive potential.

Case studies in Drosophila melanogaster (Cyp6g1/DDT resistance locus), bank voles (Avpr1a and Oxtr microsatellite alleles), and Atlantic salmon (Vgll3 maturation locus) illustrate how sexually antagonistic alleles generate balancing selection, maintaining both alleles in the population because each is favored in one sex. The Drosophila example shows that resistance alleles increasing female fecundity simultaneously reduce male mating success, producing classic balancing selection. The salmon Vgll3 locus provides a particularly elegant example: a dominance reversal mechanism, whereby the late-maturation allele is dominant in females but recessive in males, partially resolves conflict by allowing each sex to express its optimal phenotype even at a shared heterozygous locus. Importantly, not all balancing selection on reproductive loci arises from sexual conflict — the Rxfp2 horn locus in Soay sheep shows elevated diversity driven by male heterozygote advantage, without any fitness cost to females.

Population genomic approaches now allow genome-wide screening for sexual conflict without phenotypic data. Two principal methods are Tajima's D, which detects elevated nucleotide diversity consistent with balancing selection, and inter-sexual FST, which measures allele frequency differences between males and females in a population. Mank shows that these measures are most informative when combined: sexual conflict over viability or survival produces elevated values for both Tajima's D and FST, whereas sex-specific genetic architecture (resolved conflict) elevates FST without increasing Tajima's D, and other sources of balancing selection (e.g., heterozygote advantage, ecological fluctuation) elevate Tajima's D without necessarily elevating FST. Preliminary combined analyses suggest that much of the elevated inter-sexual FST observed in humans reflects sex-specific survival rather than ongoing unresolved conflict.

The genomic distribution of sexual conflict is not random. Regions with asymmetric inheritance between sexes — sex chromosomes, organelle genomes, and the pseudo-autosomal region (PAR) boundary — are predicted to accumulate sexually antagonistic variation more rapidly than autosomes. Mitochondrial genomes, inherited maternally, illustrate "the mother's curse": male-harming mutations persist because selection acts only through female transmission. On sex chromosomes, two positive-feedback loops operate after recombination ceases between X and Y: the partial sex-linkage of the PAR region encourages further expansion of the non-recombining region, and the fully sex-linked region accumulates antagonistic variation faster than autosomes. Evidence from Silene latifolia plants and preliminary guppy data supports elevated Tajima's D near the PAR boundary, consistent with theory.

Looking ahead, Mank identifies two major unresolved issues. First, disentangling the causes of detected balancing selection requires combined use of multiple genomic statistics, and it remains unclear what fraction of the genome is under ongoing sexual conflict versus resolved conflict with sex-specific architecture. Second, the relationship between sexual conflict and adaptive evolution is ambiguous: while balancing selection from conflict maintains genetic diversity that could fuel adaptation (particularly relevant under Anthropocene environmental change), there is also evidence that unresolved conflict can constrain adaptation. Resolving these questions requires integrating genomic approaches with phenotypic fitness data and ecological context across diverse taxa.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Establishes that sexual conflict generates balancing selection — a mechanism that maintains genetic diversity in populations beyond what mutation rates alone would predict, with direct relevance to population genetics theory
- Demonstrates through concrete case studies (Drosophila, bank voles, Atlantic salmon) how intra-locus sexual conflict operates at the molecular level and how partial resolution mechanisms such as dominance reversal can evolve
- Introduces a rigorous population genomic framework — combining Tajima's D and inter-sexual FST — for distinguishing ongoing sexual conflict from resolved conflict and from other sources of balancing selection
- Clarifies that genomic regions with asymmetric sex inheritance (sex chromosomes, mitochondria, PAR boundary) are predicted evolutionary hotspots for sexual antagonism, providing a mechanistic link between sex chromosome biology and population genetics
- Raises unresolved questions about whether sexual conflict is net beneficial or detrimental to adaptive potential under rapid environmental change, connecting classical quantitative genetics to contemporary conservation genomics

## Future Research Directions

- Develop and apply combination population genomic approaches (Tajima's D + inter-sexual FST + additional statistics) across diverse taxa to determine what fraction of the genome is subject to ongoing sexual conflict versus resolved conflict with sex-specific architecture
- Resolve the ambiguity between sexual conflict over reproductive fitness and over viability/survival, which have distinct evolutionary dynamics and different implications for population-level genetic load
- Investigate whether lineages with high levels of sexual conflict (typically arising from specific mating systems) harbor greater genome-wide diversity that translates into greater evolvability under rapid environmental change
- Map the genomic landscape of sexual conflict more precisely by separately sequencing males and females from natural populations across the life cycle, including embryonic stages, to pinpoint when selection differentials arise
- Integrate population genomic evidence for balancing selection with functional genomic data on sex-biased gene expression, sex-specific splicing, and dominance architecture to understand the mechanisms by which conflict is resolved at the molecular level
