# Barbara McClintock on Defining the Unstable Genome

## Summary

This "Classic" commentary in Genetics, written by Marnie Halpern (Carnegie Institution for Science), introduces Barbara McClintock's landmark 1953 paper "Induction of Instability at Selected Loci in Maize" (Genetics 38:579–599), which documented the existence and effects of transposable elements — one of the most revolutionary discoveries in genetics.

**Historical context:** McClintock's 1953 paper was published the same year as the description of the DNA double helix (Watson & Crick 1953). Despite this coincidence of landmark publications, McClintock's transposon work was largely ignored for 30 years until she was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1983 — as the sole recipient that year.

**The Ac-Ds system:** While studying broken chromosome ends during mitosis, McClintock identified a breakage-sensitive site on maize chromosome 9, named Dissociation (Ds). A second independent element, Activator (Ac), is required to induce breakage at Ds. Her 1950 and 1951 papers established this system; the 1953 paper extended it to show the effect was generalizable across many loci.

**Key findings of the 1953 paper:** The study focused on pigmentation of the aleurone (outermost endosperm layer). McClintock screened for transposition of Ds into two genes (A1, A2) required for anthocyanin synthesis. Using tester stocks with endosperm markers, she showed that new Ds mutations behaved similarly regardless of which locus was affected. This led to her prescient conclusion: **mechanisms controlling gene activity can be independent of the genes themselves**. This predated characterization of regulatory genes in eukaryotes by several decades.

**Molecular characterization:** Thirty years later, Nina Fedoroff's group isolated and sequenced Ac and Ds (1983). DNA sequence analysis confirmed that Ac encodes a transposase enzyme mediating transposition, while Ds is a defective version of Ac.

**Broader impact:** McClintock's discovery sparked the search for mobile elements throughout nature. Over half the human genome consists of transposons. Some cause genomic instability and disease; others mediate evolutionary change through gene regulation influence. Recently, the industrial melanism mutation in British peppered moths was shown to be caused by a transposable element — a direct confirmation of McClintock's insights about transposon-driven phenotypic change.

**Continuing relevance:** Even with fully sequenced genomes and many tools available, the dynamic nature of genomes and the impact of transposons on gene function is far from completely understood. The concept of an "unstable genome" — far from being a peculiarity of maize — is a universal feature of life.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- First experimental demonstration that genomes are dynamic, not static information stores — foundational for understanding genome regulation and evolution
- Proposed the concept of gene regulation before DNA structure was fully understood — a remarkable predictive insight
- Connects to modern biology: transposons comprise >50% of the human genome and play roles in disease, development, and evolution
- Used in class to introduce reverse genetics concepts: how insertional disruption of genes (Ds insertion into A1/A2) reveals gene function
