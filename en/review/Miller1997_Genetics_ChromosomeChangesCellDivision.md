# Chromosome Changes in Cell Differentiation

## Citation (NLM)
Miller OJ. Chromosome changes in cell differentiation. Genetics. 1997;146(1):1–8.

**DOI:** No DOI assigned (1997 Genetics Perspectives article)

---

## Summary

This "Perspectives" article in Genetics, by Orlando Miller (Wayne State University), reviews the diverse chromosome changes occurring during cell differentiation that go beyond simple DNA replication. It covers programmed, epigenetic, developmental, and cell-type-specific genome reorganization phenomena.

**Facultative heterochromatinization:** The best-known examples are mammalian X chromosome inactivation (Lyon 1961) and inactivation of the paternally derived chromosome set in coccids. X inactivation is mediated by the cis-acting RNA product of the XIST gene from the single X inactivation center. Imprinting, such as that of a four-gene cluster on mouse chromosome 7 mediated by H19 RNA, also involves cis-acting non-translated RNAs. DNA methylation and histone underacetylation are the molecular mechanisms of gene silencing.

**Programmed DNA loss:** Common in metazoan differentiation, involving whole chromosomes, large segments, or short precise sequences eliminated at specific developmental stages.

1. **Chromatin diminution:** In Ascaris and Parascaris, very large chromosomes fragment at the 3rd–5th cleavage divisions and most chromatin is eliminated at specific chromosome breakage regions (CBRs), followed by telomere addition. In ciliates (Tetrahymena, Stylonychia), extreme chromatin diminution eliminates >90% of DNA, sequentially excising transposon-like Tec elements followed by short internal eliminated sequences (IES).

2. **Chromosome elimination:** In Sciara coprophila (dipteran fungus gnat), L chromosomes are lost from somatic cells at the 5th–6th cleavage. Helen Crouse found that a cis-acting controlling element near the X chromosome centromere governs X nondisjunction in male meiosis.

**V(D)J recombination:** Immunoglobulin and T-cell receptor gene diversity is generated by B/T lymphocyte-specific RAG1/RAG2 recombination of germline-separated gene segments, using evolutionarily conserved heptamer and nonamer signal sequences.

**Endoreduplication and polyploidization:** Extraordinarily widespread in both plant and animal differentiation. Key examples: Drosophila salivary gland polytene chromosomes; mouse megakaryocytes (up to 128C, influenced by multiple QTL); rodent placental trophoblasts (512C or higher); Bombyx mori silk glands (>500,000C). Endoreduplication is achieved by blocking G2-M transition: loss of M-phase cyclins A and B with continued cyclic Cyclin E expression. CDK1/cyclin B1 kinase activity (MPF) failure prevents mitosis after S phase.

**Under-replication:** In Drosophila polytene cells, heterochromatic sequences (satellite DNAs, ribosomal RNA genes, histone genes) are markedly under-represented. Emil Heitz (1934) distinguished α-heterochromatin (under-replicated) from β-heterochromatin. Hypomorphic Cyclin E mutations abolish under-replication of satellite DNA, suggesting oscillating Cyclin E levels control endocycle S-phase length and thereby under-replication.

**Gene amplification:** Some genes amplify beyond the ploidy level. DNA puffs in Sciara (16-fold amplification, ecdysone-induced) and Drosophila chorion genes (16-fold on X, 60-fold on chromosome 3) amplify by the "onion-skin" mechanism: nested unidirectional firing from internal replication origins.

**Unresolved problems:** Molecular mechanisms of under-replication vs. chromatin diminution, genes controlling megakaryocyte ploidy level, and chromosome dynamics during polyploidization remain open questions.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Challenges the "fixed genome" concept by showing that the genome itself undergoes programmed changes during differentiation
- Integrates X inactivation, imprinting, V(D)J recombination, and polyploidization into a unified framework of developmental chromosome biology
- Highlights that cell differentiation involves not just gene expression pattern changes but actual genomic structural changes
- Used in class as an overview connecting epigenetics, cell cycle regulation, and developmental genetics
