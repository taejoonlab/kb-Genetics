# Tschermak: A Non-Discoverer of Mendelism. II. A Critique

## Citation (NLM)
Monaghan FV, Corcos AF. Tschermak: a non-discoverer of Mendelism. II. A critique. J Hered. 1987;78(3):208-210. doi:10.1093/oxfordjournals.jhered.a110344

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordjournals.jhered.a110344](https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordjournals.jhered.a110344)

---

## Summary

This is the second of two papers by Monaghan and Corcos, directly analyzing the text of Tschermak's two 1900 papers to demonstrate that he was not an independent rediscoverer of Mendelism. Their first paper examined historical evidence; this second paper provides close textual analysis of Tschermak's own writings.

**Misunderstanding of dominance**: Tschermak claimed that Mendel's main contribution was the concept of dominance, which the authors identify as itself revealing how little Tschermak understood Mendel. Mendel's real contributions lay in his quantitative laws governing hybrid formation, his use of symbolic notation, and his application of combination theory. Tschermak treated dominance not as a binary property of a character but as a matter of relative "potency" or "prevalence" — an old blending conception. He even asserted that transitional forms could appear in F1 hybrids, directly contradicting Mendel's explicit statement that "transitional forms were not observed in any experiment." This confusion reappeared as late as 1925 in Tschermak's correspondence with Roberts.

**Backcross data misinterpretation**: In describing his backcross results, Tschermak made a mathematically impossible statement asserting that both dominant and recessive characters were reduced in frequency. He showed no recognition that a backcross with the recessive parent must yield a 1:1 ratio. Instead, he treated ratios of 1.2:1 and 1.75:1 as equivalent possibilities among a continuous range of values, demonstrating no theoretical framework for expecting any fixed ratio from the segregation process.

**Absence of independent assortment**: Tschermak obtained numerical data in dihybrid F2 crosses that approximated the 9:3:3:1 ratio, but he did not understand its significance. His "final conclusion" merely observed that dominant characters remain dominant and recessive characters remain recessive when combined in a dihybrid, without grasping that new combinations of characters appear in F2 — the essential implication of independent assortment. Correns clearly extracted the 9:3:3:1 ratio from equivalent data; Tschermak did not.

The authors conclude that by 1900 Tschermak had accumulated numerical data approximating Mendelian ratios but possessed no understanding of why those ratios arise — the mechanisms of segregation and independent assortment. He never should have been included among the independent rediscoverers of Mendelism.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Critically examines the "rediscovery" narrative of Mendel's laws, demonstrating the fundamental distinction between observing numerical ratios and understanding the underlying genetic mechanism
- Illuminates the differential understanding among de Vries, Correns, and Tschermak, deepening appreciation of what Mendel's original ideas actually meant
- Why this paper must be read in class: to understand how the foundational concepts of dominance, segregation, and independent assortment developed historically, and to think critically about how scientific "rediscovery" narratives can be inaccurately simplified
