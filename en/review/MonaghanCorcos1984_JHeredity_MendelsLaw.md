# On the Origins of the Mendelian Laws

## Citation (NLM)
Monaghan F, Corcos A. On the origins of the Mendelian laws. J Hered. 1984;75(1):67-69. doi:10.1093/oxfordjournals.jhered.a110039

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordjournals.jhered.a110039](https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordjournals.jhered.a110039)

---

## Summary

Monaghan and Corcos present a striking historical analysis demonstrating that the two laws attributed to Mendel in modern textbooks — the law of independent segregation and the law of independent assortment — do not appear in Mendel's original paper in their current form.

**The First Law (segregation)**: Close reading of Mendel's text reveals he did not explicitly present this as a "law." He formulated the segregation of hereditary material in germ cells as a hypothesis or assumption — a logical inference from observable patterns, not an empirically established law in its own right. His language was consistently that of hypothesis-testing: "it seems logical to conclude," "it can be shown theoretically that this assumption would be entirely adequate," "in order to test this hypothesis experimentally." It was de Vries (1900) who first equated his observed segregation of hybrid offspring with a statement about germ cell purity, coining the expression "Mendel's law" and converting Mendel's theoretical assumption into a statement of fact. Correns went further by reinterpreting segregation in terms of "anlagen" — material, causative units — thereby creating a formulation close to the modern first law. He explicitly acknowledged he was converting Mendel's assumption into a law.

**The Second Law (independent assortment)**: This law exists only in embryonic form in Mendel's paper. In his section on inheritance of multiple differing traits, Mendel derived as a secondary conclusion — in a single sentence following his primary conclusion — that the behavior of each pair of differing traits is independent of all other differences. He then repeated the primary conclusion in a later summary but not this secondary one, suggesting he considered it of lesser importance. Crucially, the now-familiar 9:3:3:1 ratio does not appear in Mendel's paper, nor in de Vries's. Correns first explicitly stated this ratio but did not formulate it as a separate second law. The clear separation of the two laws and the naming of the second law is attributable to T. H. Morgan, who first used the term "independent assortment" in his 1913 book Heredity and Sex, and in 1919 in The Physical Basis of Heredity explicitly attributed both laws as Mendel's discoveries.

The conclusion is that the so-called "Mendelian Laws" cannot be found in Mendel's paper; they were read back into his work by the rediscoverers and Morgan, and "Mendel's Laws" is something of a misnomer.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Demonstrates through careful textual analysis how foundational laws of genetics were historically constructed, showing that a text's actual content and later interpretations can differ substantially
- Tracks how Mendel carefully distinguished hypothesis from empirical law, and how de Vries and Correns elevated his hypothetical scheme into law, revealing the social and interpretive dimensions of scientific knowledge production
- Why this paper must be read in class: to understand that the laws we teach as "Mendel's" were actually constructed through decades of subsequent work extending through Morgan, developing critical historical literacy about the foundations of genetics
