## Summary

This piece is a brief historical commentary by Andrew Murray (Harvard) introducing Nurse and Thuriaux's landmark 1980 GENETICS paper, "Regulatory genes controlling mitosis in the fission yeast Schizosaccharomyces pombe."

In 1974, Paul Nurse was a postdoc with Murdoch Mitchison in Edinburgh, searching for fission yeast mutants larger than normal, when he instead found one that was smaller — a wee mutant (named for the Scottish word for "small"). Rather than blocking cell division, this mutant divided early, before cells reached their wild-type size. Inspired by Lee Hartwell's isolation and analysis of cell cycle arrest mutants in budding yeast, Nurse recognized that mutations accelerating the cell cycle held the key to understanding how cells coordinate growth and division to maintain constant average size.

The 1980 GENETICS paper systematically isolated 50 additional wee mutants, finding that 51 of 52 total mapped to the wee1 locus and a single exceptional mutant (cdc2-1w) lay within the cdc2 gene. Previously identified cdc2 mutations were temperature-sensitive alleles that blocked mitosis (cdc phenotype). The discovery that a different mutation in the same gene produced the opposite — premature mitosis — pointed to a simple but profound model: Cdc2 is a positive element or activator required for mitotic entry, while Wee1 is a dosage-dependent inhibitor of Cdc2 activity. Halving wee1+ gene dosage in heterozygous diploids reduced division size to an intermediate level, confirming dosage sensitivity. In contrast, reduction of cdc2+ gene dosage had almost no effect on division timing, suggesting that a specific aspect of Cdc2 activity (rather than its total quantity) determines when mitosis occurs — an early hint at the post-translational regulation by phosphorylation.

Decades of subsequent work confirmed this model in full. Cdc2 (CDK1) and Wee1 are conserved throughout eukaryotes. Paul Nurse shared the 2001 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine with Lee Hartwell and Tim Hunt for these discoveries.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- An archetypal example of phenotype-driven genetic screens revealing molecular regulators of a fundamental cellular process — the intellectual template for forward genetic dissection of the cell cycle
- Demonstrates the power of combining loss-of-function (cdc phenotype) and gain-of-function/altered-function (wee phenotype) alleles of the same gene to reveal the nature of its regulatory role
- Why this paper must be read in class: to understand how the molecular basis of cell cycle regulation was discovered through genetic screens in a model organism, and to learn the logic of using wee mutants as a strategy for identifying rate-limiting regulators
