# The Magic and Meaning of Mendel's Miracle

## Citation (NLM)

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41576-022-00497-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41576-022-00497-2)

## Summary

Written to mark the bicentenary of Gregor Mendel's birth in July 2022, this perspective by Kim Nasmyth (University of Oxford) in *Nature Reviews Genetics* retraces Mendel's origins, revisits his famous study, and reflects on the revolutionary implications of his work and its continuing scientific legacy.

Nasmyth places Mendel's contribution as one of five great tributaries forming the vast river of modern biological knowledge: Mendel's genetic analysis, the cytological study of cell division and chromosomes, the study of chemical reactions inside cells, elucidation of macromolecular structure using electromagnetic wave diffraction, and the appreciation that competition between DNA variants drives biodiversity. Notably, Mendel's 1865 lectures coincided with Maxwell's landmark electromagnetic field paper — both forming the twin methodological pillars of modern biology.

Mendel's central insight was that it is possible to measure the transmission of hereditary information without any knowledge of its material basis — measuring the movement of information rather than of substances. This was a uniquely biological type of quantitative analysis, owing nothing to chemistry or physics.

Nasmyth reviews Mendel's biography: born to poor German-speaking peasant farmers in what is now the Czech Republic; educated in physics and combinatorial mathematics at the University of Vienna under Christian Doppler and Franz Unger; admitted to the Augustinian abbey in Brünn; and paradoxically freed for research by failing his teaching certificate exams twice.

The review carefully walks through Mendel's experimental logic: F1 hybrids resemble only one parent (dominant character); F2 self-fertilization produces recessive characters at a ratio close to 1:3; among the F2 dominants, two-thirds again produce variable progeny while one-third breeds true, yielding the 1:2:1 ratio and the A + 2Aa + a developmental series. For two pairs of divergent traits, the 9:3:3:1 ratio emerges from the combination of two 3:1 series, and Mendel generalized this to n pairs of characters giving 3ⁿ classes, 4ⁿ individuals, and 2ⁿ constant combinations.

Nasmyth argues that the Law of Independent Assortment carries a broader implication often overlooked: it revealed that different aspects of a plant's physiology must be specified by independent elements (genes) transmitted by an identical mechanism. This was a direct antecedent to Beadle and Tatum's one gene, one enzyme hypothesis and thence to the central dogma of molecular biology.

Regarding the contested Hieracium experiments, Nasmyth explains that constant hybrids in hawkweed result from apomixis — asexual seed production bypassing meiosis. Mendel's hypothesis, though mechanistically incorrect, attempted to unify variable (sexual) and constant (asexual) reproduction and would not have been seen as a failure by Mendel himself.

The author applies the "subtraction test" to evaluate Mendel's uniqueness: unlike most major scientific discoveries, Mendel's findings were initially ignored and not rediscovered for 35 years — meaning he made the discovery at least 3–4 decades earlier than any other scientist would have done.

## Significance in Introduction Context

Why this paper matters at the introduction to genetics:
- Situates Mendel's work within the broader history of biology and physics (Maxwell, Darwin), clarifying why his quantitative genetic approach was historically unprecedented
- Contrasts Mendel's empirical, hypothesis-testing science with Darwin's encyclopedic natural history approach, illustrating distinct modes of scientific inquiry
- Essential course reading because it explains not just what Mendel discovered but why it was a "miracle" — a genuine intellectual breakthrough decades ahead of its time — and how it connects to today's genomics, gene editing, and medical genetics

## Future Research Directions

Unresolved questions and future research directions from this paper:

- The origin of life from inanimate matter and whether all life in the universe obeys similar principles remain within the realm of speculation
- The societal consequences of CRISPR-based "improvements" to the human germline require careful ethical evaluation — not all unforeseen consequences may be beneficial
- Expanding Mendelian randomization approaches to better disentangle environmental from genetic causes of complex diseases in population-scale genomics studies
