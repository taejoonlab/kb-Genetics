# Charlesworth et al. on Background Selection and Neutral Diversity

## Summary

This is a brief Genetics Classics commentary by Stephen Wright (University of Toronto) introducing the landmark paper by Charlesworth, Morgan, and Charlesworth (1993), "The effect of deleterious mutations on neutral molecular variation."

The historical context is as follows. In 1974, Maynard Smith and Haigh predicted that positive selection would reduce neutral variation linked to a selected locus — the "genetic hitchhiking" effect. They predicted that if positive selection occurred frequently enough, regions of the genome with lower recombination rates should harbor lower genetic diversity. Stephan & Langley (1989) and Begun & Aquadro (1992) then found a positive correlation between recombination rate and neutral diversity in Drosophila, and interpreted this as evidence for frequent positive selection.

Charlesworth et al. (1993) proposed for the first time an alternative mechanism capable of producing the same pattern: "background selection." This is the reduction of neutral variation caused by negative (purifying) selection against deleterious mutations. The key principle: in regions where recombination does not occur, chromosomal copies carrying deleterious mutations are rapidly removed from the population, making those copies unavailable to contribute to neutral diversity. In large regions with low recombination (e.g., pericentromeric regions), this reduction in diversity can be substantial.

The authors combined classical mutation-selection balance theory with computer simulations that relaxed simplifying assumptions, and showed that background selection is sufficient to explain the recombination-diversity correlation observed in Drosophila. Follow-up studies incorporating partial recombination effects (Hudson & Kaplan 1995, Nordborg et al. 1996) clarified how recombination rate enters the theoretical predictions.

Background selection is now recognized as a major force structuring genetic variation in many species. It is estimated to play an important role in reducing variation near functional sites in humans, contributes to low diversity on Y chromosomes and in selfing and asexual lineages, and is thought to accelerate Y chromosome degeneration and inbreeding depression. A key insight from this work is that both positive and negative selection can produce similar effects on neutral diversity, making it a continuing challenge in population genetics to distinguish the two processes from genomic data.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the historical framework for "background selection" as the key concept showing that neutral molecular variation is shaped not only by neutral processes but also by linked selected sites
- Introduces the interpretive challenge arising from positive and negative selection making similar predictions about genetic diversity patterns — a direct illustration of a current open problem in population genetics
- Why this paper must be read in class: to understand that the distribution of neutral diversity across a genome is shaped by the indirect effects of selection, and to learn the concept of background selection needed to interpret genome-wide diversity patterns
