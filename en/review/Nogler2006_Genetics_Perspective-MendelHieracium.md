# The Lesser-Known Mendel: His Experiments on Hieracium

## Citation (NLM)

## Summary

This Genetics Perspectives piece by Gian Nogler (University of Zürich), written for the 140th anniversary of Mendel's landmark paper, recounts the frustrating chapter of Mendel's career when he tried to verify his laws of inheritance using Hieracium (hawkweeds) and obtained results that contradicted everything he had found in Pisum.

From his pea experiments, Mendel had established two patterns: F1 hybrids are uniform, and F2 progeny segregate in definite numerical ratios. When he turned to Hieracium, he found the exact opposite: (1) F1 offspring were not uniform — they showed a continuous series of variations between the two parental forms; (2) the "F2" generations, on the contrary, were completely uniform and identical to the mother plant, showing no segregation whatsoever. Mendel documented these contradictions honestly in his 1869 paper and in letters to Carl Nägeli, writing in July 1870 that the two phenomena must be "the emanation of a higher general law." Nogler emphasizes that Mendel initiated the Hieracium experiments himself — Nägeli did not impose them on him — and that Mendel was driven by the desire to test whether his Pisum laws were universal. His abandonment of the research was due not to frustration but to serious health problems (eye strain from thousands of hand-emasculations) and the overwhelming demands of his role as Abbot from 1868 onwards.

The modern explanation is straightforward: most Hieracium species reproduce by apomixis — seeds develop asexually from unreduced (2n) egg cells without fertilization, yielding clones of the mother plant. This is why the "F2" never segregated. The variable F1 offspring were produced when the pollen parent (e.g., H. aurantiacum) happened to be a genetically heterozygous plant whose heterozygosity was masked by apomictic reproduction — making it appear "true breeding" when it was in fact a genetically fixed hybrid. In rare cases, apomicts can also produce reduced egg cells that, when fertilized, yield sexually derived hybrid offspring — explaining the minority of non-maternal progeny Mendel occasionally observed. The phenomenon of apomixis in seed plants was not firmly established until Juel (1898); Ostenfeld (1904) was the first to interpret Mendel's Hieracium results as a consequence of parthenogenesis. The three rediscoverers — de Vries, Correns, and Bateson — all initially accepted the existence of a distinct "Hieracium type of inheritance" in contrast to the Mendelian "Pisum type."

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Demonstrates that Mendel's laws are universal but their expression depends critically on the reproductive biology of the organism — apomixis is a natural exception that mimics non-Mendelian inheritance without violating the underlying mechanism
- Illustrates how incomplete biological knowledge (the mechanism of apomixis was unknown) can generate apparent violations of a correct law, with important lessons for interpreting unexpected genetic data
- Why this paper must be read in class: to understand the historical context of Mendel's own doubts about the universality of his laws, the concept of apomixis as an exception to Mendelian inheritance, and how scientific understanding is shaped by what is not yet known

## Future Research Directions

- Identify the molecular genetic basis of apomixis and compare it with sexual reproduction in Pisum to understand how one reproductive mode can mask Mendelian patterns
- Explore the engineering of apomixis in crop plants as a strategy for fixing hybrid vigor and producing genetically uniform seed lines without repeated crossing
