# How microbes "jeopardize" the modern synthesis

## Citation (NLM)
Novick A, Doolittle WF. How microbes "jeopardize" the modern synthesis. PLoS Genet. 2019;15(5):e1008166.

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1008166](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1008166)

---

## Summary

This editorial introduces a PLoS Genetics review series provocatively titled "How Microbes 'Jeopardize' the Modern Synthesis." Novick and Doolittle use it to reframe a recurring debate: does the study of microbial evolution (lateral gene transfer, unusual speciation patterns, novel genome concepts) overturn the mid-twentieth-century "modern synthesis" and demand a wholly new, equally general theory of evolution? Rather than staging that confrontation, the authors argue for theoretical integration over theoretical conflict. Their central move is philosophical: evolutionary theory is best understood not as a single true-or-false general theory, but as a *toolkit of explanatory resources* that can be combined to explain particular episodes and patterns in evolutionary history.

The key conceptual distinction is between the explanatory resources themselves (models such as the Stable Ecotype Model, natural selection, genetic drift) and the "scope claims" that biologists attach to them. Resources, the authors say, are neither true nor false in the abstract; the meaningful question is whether they apply well to a given real case. Scope claims — assertions about the range of circumstances in which a resource is applicable — are, by contrast, "ephemeral products of their time," dependent on which problems, which alternative tools, and which organisms are central to biology at a given moment.

Novick and Doolittle argue that the strongest claims that microbes "jeopardize" the synthesis rely on two moves: (a) identifying the synthetic theory with particular scope claims, and (b) stripping those scope claims from their original historical context. Their worked example is lateral gene transfer (LGT). LGT is often cast as a challenge to the gradualism of the synthesis, but they place that gradualism in context: the synthesis primarily studied sexually reproducing populations and often explicitly excluded microbes (Dobzhansky, Huxley), and the period's gradualism debates were really about the adequacy of gradualistic *speciation*. Since LGT is not primarily a speciation mechanism — indeed it complicates the very idea of a bacterial species — debates about LGT are largely not the same debate that animated the synthesis.

Blame, they stress, is shared: proponents of the synthesis also overreached by treating it as a general theory of all evolution. The authors invoke Beatty's "evolutionary contingency thesis" to argue there never was, and never can be, an informative general theory covering all life, because evolutionary processes are constrained by the evolved materials they act on, and those materials themselves diversify into forms subject to different processes. Seen through the toolkit lens, models incorporating LGT *expand* the toolkit while *clarifying and limiting* the scope of older resources — the synthesis is not falsified, but its limits become better understood. Each review in the series performs this twofold work (reticulate evolution, speciation, the genome concept, mutation, adaptation), and the study of microbes can even feed back to reshape our understanding of macrobial evolution, as Shapiro et al.'s speciation review illustrates. The authors close by predicting that in sixty to eighty years, the scope claims of today's reviews will themselves look "jeopardized" — a reminder that all scientists are bound by their own context.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Offers students a clear, non-technical framework for thinking about *what an evolutionary theory is* — a toolkit of models rather than a single decree — useful whenever a course pits "the modern synthesis" against newer findings.
- Introduces the crucial distinction between an explanatory model and the "scope claims" made about it, which helps students avoid the common error of treating any single mechanism (selection, drift, LGT) as the whole of evolution.
- Uses lateral gene transfer as a concrete case showing why "microbes break the rules" headlines need careful historical reading before they are accepted as refutations.
- Encourages intellectual humility: today's confident claims are themselves time-bound, a healthy stance for students entering a fast-moving field.

## Key References

1. **Zuckerkandl & Pauling / synthesis architects — Dobzhansky (1937), Huxley (1942/2010)** — foundational statements of the modern synthesis that, importantly, centered on sexually reproducing populations and often excluded microbes.
2. **Booth, Mariscal & Doolittle (2016)** — "The Modern Synthesis in the Light of Microbial Genomics," the review that frames how microbial genomics bears on synthesis-era theory.
3. **Beatty (1995)** — "The Evolutionary Contingency Thesis," the philosophical basis for arguing no fully general theory of evolution is possible.
4. **Shapiro, Leducq & Mallet (2016)** — "What Is Speciation?" the companion review showing how ecological species concepts can be modified for microbes and then feed back onto macrobial theory.
5. **Lawrence (2002); Doolittle & Zhaxybayeva (2009)** — analyses of how LGT complicates the notion of a bacterial "species," central to the LGT-vs-gradualism argument.

## Future Research Directions

- Building integrated models that combine synthesis-era resources (selection, drift) with reticulate-evolution and LGT-aware frameworks, and testing how well each applies to specific microbial and macrobial cases.
- Refining species and genome concepts so that a single conceptual framework can accommodate both prokaryotic and eukaryotic evolution without forcing a false choice between "old" and "new" theory.
