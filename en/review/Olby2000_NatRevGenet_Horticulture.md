# Horticulture: The Font for the Baptism of Genetics

## Citation (NLM)
Olby RC. Horticulture: the font for the baptism of genetics. Nat Rev Genet. 2000;1(1):65-70. doi:10.1038/35049564

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/35049564](https://doi.org/10.1038/35049564)

---

## Summary

Written by Robert Olby (University of Pittsburgh, Department of the History and Philosophy of Science) to mark the centenary of the 1900 rediscovery of Mendel's laws, this essay explains why the institutionalization of genetics in England owed its early survival to the horticultural community rather than to mainstream academic biology.

The paper opens by revising the standard narrative. We are told that Mendel's work was neglected for 35 years, then simultaneously rediscovered in 1900 by Hugo de Vries (Netherlands), Carl Correns (Germany), and Erich Tschermak (Austria). Historical research now corrects this in four important ways. First, two of the three rediscoverers (de Vries and Correns) had read Mendel's 1865 paper before their own rediscovery. In Correns's case, his actual notes on Mendel's paper, dated 16 April 1896, survive. Second, the story that Bateson first read Mendel while traveling to London to address the Royal Horticultural Society (RHS) on 8 May 1900, and transformed his lecture on the spot, is undermined by the fact that the contemporary report of that lecture (published four days later) makes no mention of Mendel — it frames de Vries's results through the lens of Galton's "Ancestral Law of Inheritance." Third, only de Vries and Correns clearly understood Mendel's distinction between the transmission of a hereditary element and its expression in development — the conceptual heart of Mendelism. Tschermak conceived of both transmission and expression as a unified "hereditary potency," as did many nineteenth-century biologists including Darwin. Fourth, the political interest in human heredity and eugenics, though prominent internationally, was not the principal driver of genetics in England.

The essay's central argument is that Mendelism survived in England because the RHS embraced it. The RHS was producing new flower varieties — doubled flowers, new colors, novel fruit shapes — through hybridization, and these traits appeared to follow simple Mendelian ratios. Bateson, formally a member of the Royal Society's Evolution Committee, approached the RHS scientific committee in 1897; this led to the 1899 International Conference on Plant Hybridization. It was William Wilks, the dedicated RHS Secretary, who arranged for Charles Druery to translate Mendel's paper, which the RHS published in its journal in 1901 with an introduction and notes by Bateson. The RHS hosted the 1906 Third International Conference on Genetics, where Bateson — as president — coined the word "genetics" in his inaugural address. Seven Mendelians presented papers and were awarded the Veitch Gold Medals.

In contrast, academic botanists and zoologists were largely unreceptive. At the 1905 International Congress of Botany in Vienna, only Tschermak spoke on Mendelism; at the 1910 Brussels congress, it was not mentioned at all. Alfred Russel Wallace belittled Mendel's achievement; Edward Poulton (Oxford) and William Thiselton-Dyer (Kew) were hostile. The root of the conflict was that Bateson and de Vries championed discontinuous variation and origin of species by mutation — a view that denied the creative role of natural selection and placed them in direct opposition to Darwinians. This standoff began to dissolve only after R.A. Fisher (1918) demonstrated mathematically the agreement between Mendelian predictions and biometric data, initiating the neo-Darwinian synthesis.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides essential historical context for how the science of genetics got its name and institutional home, and demonstrates that the sociology of science — who funds what, and why — profoundly shapes which ideas survive
- Corrects four persistent myths about the 1900 rediscovery (Bateson on the train, Tschermak's understanding, the 35-year neglect narrative, the role of eugenics) with documented evidence, modeling the critical evaluation of scientific history
- Why this paper must be read in class: to understand that the founding of genetics as a formal discipline was neither inevitable nor purely intellectual — it was shaped by economic interests in horticulture, institutional politics, and ideological conflicts with Darwinian biology
