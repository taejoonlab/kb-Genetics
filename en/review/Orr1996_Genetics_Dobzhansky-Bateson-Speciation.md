# Dobzhansky, Bateson, and the Genetics of Speciation

## Citation (NLM)
Orr HA. Dobzhansky, Bateson, and the genetics of speciation. Genetics. 1996;144(4):1331-1335.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/144/4/1331](https://genetics.org/content/144/4/1331)

---

## Summary

This Perspectives essay commemorates the sixtieth anniversary of Theodosius Dobzhansky's 1936 paper "Studies on hybrid sterility. II," published in *Genetics*, and uses it as an occasion to revisit the intellectual history of the genetics of speciation. Orr argues that Dobzhansky's greatest achievement was to show that species differences — even reproductive isolation itself — could be dissected with the ordinary tools of Mendelian genetics. Before the 1930s, geneticists had largely avoided speciation, partly because there was so much else to do in early Mendelism (mapping mutants, working out chromosome mechanics) and partly because hybrid sterility defeats its own study: sterile flies leave no offspring. The breakthrough came with Donald Lancefield's 1922 discovery in Morgan's lab of two forms — later named *Drosophila pseudoobscura* and *D. persimilis* — whose crosses produced sterile males but fertile females, giving experimenters workable material.

Dobzhansky's approach was a forerunner of QTL analysis. He crossed *D. pseudoobscura* carrying mapped visible markers to *D. persimilis*, backcrossed the fertile F1 females, and scored fertility (by testis size) against which chromosomal regions came from which species. This let him test the two leading rival theories of hybrid sterility. The first, the "cytocentric" view popular among European embryologists, held that species differences were caused not by Mendelian (nuclear) genes but by the cytoplasm. Dobzhansky falsified this by showing that backcross males with only race A chromosomes were fertile regardless of whether their cytoplasm came from race A or race B, and vice versa; an apparent cytoplasmic effect turned out to be a maternal effect governed by the mother's nuclear genotype. The second view attributed hybrid sterility to large chromosome rearrangements disrupting meiotic pairing. Dobzhansky dismantled this too: several inversion differences were X-linked (irrelevant to male sterility), sterility often mapped to non-rearranged regions, and — in one of the cleverest experiments in evolutionary genetics — he showed that Darlington's rule (which holds in plants, where tetraploid hybrids regain fertility because all chromosomes find pairing partners) is *not* obeyed in animals: pairing failed just as often in tetraploid as in diploid hybrid spermatocytes. Hybrid sterility in animals is due to genes, not structural incompatibilities.

The paper is best remembered for providing the strongest evidence yet for what is now called the Dobzhansky–Muller model. This model resolves Darwin's paradox: how can natural selection allow the evolution of something as maladaptive as hybrid sterility? A single-gene model fails, because the intermediate heterozygote (Aa) would itself be the sterile genotype, and the lineage would end. Dobzhansky's solution invokes an interaction (epistasis) between at least two loci: an ancestral *aabb* population splits, one branch fixes *A* (fertile as *AAbb*), the other fixes *B* (fertile as *aaBB*), but *A* and *B* have never been "tested" together, so *AaBb* hybrids may be sterile. Speciation thus occurs without any genotype ever crossing an adaptive valley. Dobzhansky showed that sterility resulted from an *interaction* between chromosomes (and hence loci), not from heterozygote disadvantage or rearrangements.

The most striking historical revelation of the essay is that William Bateson had proposed essentially the same "complementary factors" model in 1909 — a quarter-century before Dobzhansky or Muller — in a forgotten essay, "Heredity and variation in modern lights," in the volume *Darwin and Modern Science*. Bateson laid out, step by step, how two complementary factors must each be acquired separately by distinct breeds, produce no other perceptible effect, and generate sterility only when brought together by crossing. He even proposed a test. Yet Bateson's role was forgotten: neither Dobzhansky nor Muller acknowledged (or likely knew of) the precedent, Bateson published it in an obscure place and never repeated it, and by the 1920s Bateson himself had lapsed into pessimistic agnosticism about the species problem. As a vocal skeptic of natural selection and part of the Mendelian old guard, Bateson was one of the figures the architects of the Modern Synthesis were reacting against. Orr closes by noting that it is a virtue of science that thinkers who disagreed on large issues — Bateson and Dobzhansky — could nonetheless converge on the same, and correct, conclusion.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Illustrates how the core tools of Mendelian and chromosomal genetics (mapped markers, backcrosses, balancer chromosomes) can be turned on a macroevolutionary question — the origin of species — making speciation an experimental science.
- Introduces the Dobzhansky–Muller incompatibility model, a foundational concept linking Mendelian epistasis to reproductive isolation, and shows how it resolves Darwin's paradox about the evolution of sterility.
- Distinguishes rival hypotheses (cytoplasmic inheritance vs. chromosomal rearrangement vs. genic incompatibility) and shows how each was tested and falsified — a case study in experimental reasoning and hypothesis discrimination.
- Students see how priority, communication gaps, and scientific rivalries shape which ideas get credited, a useful lesson in the sociology of science alongside its content.

## Key References

1. **Dobzhansky (1936)** — "Studies on hybrid sterility. II," *Genetics* 21:113-135 — the commemorated paper; mapped hybrid sterility to interacting genes.
2. **Bateson (1909)** — "Heredity and variation in modern lights," in *Darwin and Modern Science* — the forgotten first statement of the complementary-gene (Dobzhansky–Muller) model.
3. **Muller (1942)** — "Isolating mechanisms, evolution, and temperature," *Biol. Symp.* 6:71-125 — refined the model, showed incompatibilities can be complex, and offered an explanation of Haldane's rule.
4. **Darlington (1932)** — showed hybrid sterility in plants involves chromosome rearrangement, the rule Dobzhansky proved does not hold in animals.
5. **Orr (1995)** — "The population genetics of speciation," *Genetics* 139:1805-1813 — modern confirmation that animal hybrid sterility maps to complementary genes.

## Future Research Directions

- Identifying the actual "speciation genes" underlying Dobzhansky–Muller incompatibilities at the molecular level, and understanding whether they typically evolve by positive selection, genetic conflict, or drift.
- Extending the incompatibility framework to explain Haldane's rule and the large role of the X chromosome, and probing the roles of endosymbionts (e.g., *Wolbachia*) and cytoplasmic factors that were peripheral in Dobzhansky's day.
