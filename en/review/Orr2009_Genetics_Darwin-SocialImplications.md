# Darwin and Darwinism: The (Alleged) Social Implications of The Origin of Species

## Citation (NLM)
Orr HA. Darwin and Darwinism: the (alleged) social implications of The Origin of Species. Genetics. 2009;183(3):767-772.

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.109.110445](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.109.110445)

---

## Summary

Written for the 150th anniversary of *The Origin of Species*, this Perspectives essay by evolutionary biologist H. Allen Orr examines the widespread claim that Darwinism carried profound consequences beyond biology — for economics, politics, and religion. Orr's central thesis is that most of these supposed social and cultural implications have been "misconstrued or exaggerated," and, strikingly, that the chain of influence often ran in the *opposite* direction: from economic and political theory *to* Darwinism, rather than from Darwinism to society.

On the economic front, Orr addresses the popular idea that Darwinism demonstrated the efficiency of competition and thereby justified laissez-faire capitalism (and, in its extreme form, Herbert Spencer's Social Darwinism). He notes the genuine analogy — in both biology and markets, order emerges from the unplanned actions of many independent agents rather than a central planner. But he argues the analogy is misleading in two respects. First, following economist Steven Landsburg, Darwinism has no analog of *market prices*, which are the mathematical heart of economics; the resemblance may sustain metaphors but not a theory. Second, and historically more important, the idea of natural selection was itself suggested by Victorian economics: both Darwin and Wallace credited Malthus's *Essay on the Principle of Population*, and Darwin was steeped in the world of capitalist wealth and Scottish economists (Adam Smith, Hume, Dugald Stewart). Citing Lewontin, Orr suggests it would be more historically accurate to call the theory "Biological Competitive Capitalism."

The political section is the essay's most original. Orr traces the surprising link between *gradualism* in Darwinism and gradualism in conservative political philosophy. Darwin argued that adaptive change must be gradual — small "individual differences," not monstrous large changes, are the material of selection — a view later formalized in R. A. Fisher's (1930) geometric model of adaptation, which showed mathematically that in a complex, high-dimensional organism, mutations of small effect are more likely to be beneficial (because large changes have disastrous pleiotropic side effects). Orr draws a close parallel to Edmund Burke's *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790): society, like an organism, is a complex system in which drastic change breeds unintended, deleterious consequences, so reform must proceed "by insensible degrees." Once again the influence likely ran from politics to biology — the idea that complex systems must change gradually "was in the air," especially in conservative circles that Darwin and Fisher inhabited. Orr adds an ironic coda: the gradualist argument may be biologically weaker than it appears, since evo-devo modularity can tame pleiotropy, and (per Kimura) adaptive evolution more often uses mutations of *intermediate* effect than the micromutations Darwin and Fisher favored.

On science and religion, Orr endorses the "complexity thesis" of modern historians of science (Brooke, Numbers, Harrison, Lindberg): the popular image of perpetual "warfare" between science and religion derives largely from discredited 19th-century polemics (Draper 1874; White 1896). In reality science and religion have sometimes conflicted, sometimes cooperated, and often been indifferent. The reception of Darwinism was similarly varied — crisis for some believers, indifference or even relief for others. Orr reviews modern accommodation by major denominations, the intelligent-design movement, and the *Kitzmiller v. Dover* decision, concluding that rejecting biblical literalism is not the same as embracing atheism (contra the New Atheists such as Dawkins). His survey ends by noting that even the most revolutionary scientific theory need not have uniformly revolutionary implications for every aspect of life.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Illustrates the crucial distinction between the *context of discovery* (how an idea arises) and the *context of justification* (whether it is true) — a lesson for how students should reason about scientific theories.
- Connects a foundational genetics/evolution text (*Origin of Species*) to its cultural reception, helping students see that natural selection was both a product of, and a lens for, its historical moment.
- Introduces the gradualism debate and Fisher's geometric model — a bridge from Darwinian natural history to quantitative population genetics.
- Encourages critical thinking about how scientific theories are misused or overextended for political and social agendas.

## Key References

1. **Fisher (1930)** — *The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection* — formalized gradualism with the geometric model of adaptation, linking Darwin's micromutationism to mathematical population genetics.
2. **Malthus (1798)** — *Essay on the Principle of Population* — the economic work that both Darwin and Wallace credited as the trigger for the idea of natural selection.
3. **Burke (1790)** — *Reflections on the Revolution in France* — pre-Darwinian statement of gradualism in complex social systems, echoing later biological arguments.
4. **Kimura (1983)** — *The Neutral Theory of Molecular Evolution* — basis for Orr's caveat that adaptive evolution may favor intermediate-effect mutations, not micromutations.
5. **Brooke (1991)** — *Science and Religion: Some Historical Perspectives* — foundational statement of the "complexity thesis" replacing the warfare model.

## Future Research Directions

- The population genetics of adaptation — testing whether adaptive substitutions are typically of small, intermediate, or large phenotypic effect (extending Orr 1998) — remains an active empirical program relevant to Darwin's gradualism.
- Evo-devo studies of genetic modularity continue to probe how far pleiotropy can be "tamed," bearing directly on the biological validity of the gradualist argument.
