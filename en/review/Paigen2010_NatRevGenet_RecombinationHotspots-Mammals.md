# Mammalian recombination hot spots: properties, control and evolution

## Citation (NLM)
Paigen K, Petkov P. Mammalian recombination hot spots: properties, control and evolution. Nat Rev Genet. 2010;11(3):221-233. doi:10.1038/nrg2712

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg2712](https://doi.org/10.1038/nrg2712)

---

## Summary

This comprehensive review by Paigen and Petkov at the Jackson Laboratory surveys the state of knowledge on mammalian meiotic recombination hotspots as of 2010, integrating findings from human population genetics, large-scale mouse crosses, and molecular sperm-typing experiments. Recombination in eukaryotes is not distributed randomly along chromosomes but is concentrated in discrete 1–2 kb hotspots surrounded by much larger regions essentially devoid of crossovers. The review covers hotspot properties, regulation (cis and trans), chromosomal-scale organization, and evolutionary dynamics.

Methodologically, three complementary approaches have driven the field forward. Statistical analysis of SNP variation data from human populations uses coalescent-based methods to infer historical recombination rates from haplotype block boundaries, producing genome-wide maps at fine scale. Large-scale pedigree analysis in humans and inbred mouse strain crosses provides direct evidence of currently active hotspots. Molecular sperm-typing assays, in which pooled sperm DNA is interrogated by allele-specific PCR and deep cloning, allow precise quantification of crossover (CO) and non-crossover (NCO) gene conversion products at individual hotspots, and have characterized more than 30 human and mouse hotspots.

At the molecular level, recombination at a hotspot is initiated by a DSB catalyzed by SPO11, followed by 5' strand resection to leave 3' overhangs that invade a non-sister chromatid. The resulting Holliday junctions are resolved as either COs (approximately 10% of DSBs) via the double-strand break repair (DSBR) pathway, or as NCO gene conversions (approximately 90%) via synthesis-dependent strand annealing (SDSA). CO exchange points within a hot spot span 500–2,000 bp following a normal distribution around a central point, while NCO conversions are more tightly localized in the central zone. Hotspot activity varies over orders of magnitude between different hotspots, and this variation follows a negative exponential distribution, consistent with a model in which activity reflects DNA-binding affinity at initiation sites.

The discovery of PRDM9 (PR domain-containing 9) as the primary trans-acting regulator of hotspot activation is a central contribution of work around this review period. PRDM9 contains a KRAB domain, a SET domain that trimethylates histone H3K4 (opening chromatin for DSB access), and a large tandem array of zinc-finger DNA-binding domains. PRDM9 binds a degenerate 13-bp consensus sequence (CCNCCNTNNCCNC) enriched at human hotspots and activates DSB formation by locally remodeling chromatin. PRDM9 is the "hybrid sterility" gene Hst1 in mice — allelic incompatibility disrupts proper meiotic recombination and causes sterility in F1 hybrids between Mus musculus subspecies. This dual role links hotspot control directly to speciation. At the evolutionary level, PRDM9 also resolves the "hotspot paradox": because CO-initiating chromatids acquire sequence from their partner, active hotspot sequences are progressively eroded. PRDM9 mutations can simultaneously create whole new populations of hotspots, providing rapid replacement of lost ones. Consistent with this, PRDM9 is under strong positive selection in humans, mice, and many other vertebrates, with variation concentrated in the zinc-finger DNA-binding residues.

Sex-specific differences in recombination are pronounced: female genetic maps are longer than male maps in humans and mice. This is explained by differences in chromatid compaction — female chromatids are less compact, providing more physical length along the synaptonemal complex for crossovers to be spaced, given that crossover interference operates over a fixed physical (micron) rather than sequence distance. Hotspot evolution is rapid: human and chimpanzee hotspots largely do not overlap, and PRDM9 allelic specificity explains why the human but not the chimpanzee allele can bind the 13-bp consensus motif characteristic of human hotspots. Individual hotspots can arise through single nucleotide changes on a timescale of tens of thousands of years and then erode through biased gene conversion.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the definitive 2010 synthesis of mammalian recombination hotspot biology, establishing the conceptual framework connecting DSBs, chromatin state, PRDM9, and the evolutionary dynamics of hotspot turnover
- Introduces PRDM9 as the first molecular regulator identified for mammalian hotspot locations, explaining both cis (local sequence) and trans (zinc-finger binding) determinants of hotspot activity
- Explains the "hotspot paradox" through biased gene conversion — hotspot sequences are self-destructive, yet hotspots are abundant because PRDM9 can generate new hotspot populations through allelic variation
- Clarifies sex-specific differences in crossover rates through the mechanistic model of chromatid compaction and interference distance, applicable broadly to understanding meiotic biology
- Establishes the rapid evolutionary divergence of hotspot locations between species as evidence for positive selection on PRDM9, connecting meiotic recombination directly to reproductive isolation and speciation

## Future Research Directions

- Genome-wide mapping of PRDM9 binding sites using ChIP-seq across multiple human and mouse strains to determine how variation in zinc-finger arrays creates population-specific hotspot distributions
- Understanding the structural basis of sister versus non-sister chromatid recognition during strand invasion, a mechanistically unresolved step in meiotic recombination
- Dissecting the molecular pathway from PRDM9-mediated H3K4 trimethylation to SPO11-catalyzed DSB formation, including what additional chromatin factors are required
- Clarifying whether "crossover homeostasis" — the buffering of CO numbers against reductions in initiating DSBs — reflects a specific regulatory mechanism or an emergent property of competition among DSBs
- Exploring PRDM9 evolution and hotspot divergence in non-mammalian taxa to determine whether PRDM9-independent hotspot specification mechanisms exist and how they relate to SC-dependent versus SC-independent meiosis
