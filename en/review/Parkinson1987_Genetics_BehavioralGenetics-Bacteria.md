# Doing Behavioral Genetics with Bacteria

## Citation (NLM)
Parkinson JS. Doing behavioral genetics with bacteria. Genetics. 1987;116(4):499-500.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/116/4/499](https://genetics.org/content/116/4/499)

---

## Summary

In this brief GENETICS "Perspectives" reminiscence, John S. Parkinson recounts the early days of using genetics to dissect a behavior in bacteria—the chemotaxis of *Escherichia coli*. Motile microorganisms show surprisingly sophisticated sensory behaviors, migrating toward food and fleeing noxious chemicals. Although such behaviors had been described by Engelmann, Pfeffer, and others in the 1880s, they were largely overlooked until Julius Adler revived the field in the mid-1960s. Adler chose *E. coli* precisely for its genetic tractability, reasoning that the flow of sensory information through stimulus-transduction components should be dissectable genetically, much like a conventional biochemical pathway. The first report on motility mutants from Adler's group appeared in GENETICS in 1967 (Armstrong and Adler), twenty years before this essay.

Parkinson describes the biology being studied. *E. coli* swims by rotating flagellar filaments; in the absence of stimuli the flagellar motors reverse roughly once per second, producing a three-dimensional random walk. In chemical gradients, cells bias this pattern of reversals in response to changing attractant or repellent concentrations to achieve net movement. This behavior can be assayed by watching individual cells under the microscope or by measuring population migration on semisolid agar "swarm" plates, on which cells create and chase their own attractant gradients by consuming nutrients as they grow. Because neither motility nor chemotaxis is essential for viability in the lab, isolating defective mutants seemed, at the outset, a straightforward way to reconstruct the sensory "wiring diagram" of the cell.

The heart of the piece is the difficulty of actually obtaining useful mutants—the challenge that dominated the early work. Parkinson, a former phage worker from Bob Edgar's group at Caltech, joined Adler's Madison lab in 1970 alongside two other ex-phage postdocs. Early enrichment schemes that repeatedly selected cells staying at the center of swarming colonies gave very low yields, mostly recovering motility or growth defects, and chemotaxis mutants could arise in multiple steps over many generations of growth. Cleverer schemes using preformed gradients failed too, largely because wild-type cultures always contain "phenocopies"—individuals transiently lacking flagella—whose descendants confounded the selections. An imaginative attempt to perturb bacterial behavior with psychoactive drugs (anesthetics, stimulants, tranquilizers, hallucinogens), kept locked in a safe, likewise came to nothing—Parkinson recalls only that 1 M LSD inhibited motility "a bit."

Having exhausted their tricks, the group resorted to brute-force screening: an assembly line pouring swarm plates and picking individual colonies to test. Mutants began to appear, and a key methodological improvement—embedding single cells in the agar as the plate was poured to create "miniswarms," allowing several hundred colonies to be screened per plate—turned the trickle into a torrent. The mutants isolated in those years were disseminated across the chemotaxis field and came to define most known components of the sensory transduction machinery: receptors, transducers (methyl-accepting chemotaxis proteins), signaling elements, and flagellar switch components. They enabled purification and characterization of the chemotaxis proteins, the cloning and sequencing of their genes, and the study of their interactions—putting the goal of understanding a simple behavior at the molecular level within reach. Parkinson credits Adler's foresight in choosing an organism with good genetics.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A concise, accessible illustration of the forward-genetic logic: define a phenotype (chemotactic behavior), isolate mutants that disrupt it, and use them to enumerate and order the components of a pathway.
- Demonstrates that even "behavior" is amenable to genetic dissection, extending Mendelian/molecular genetics into neuroscience-like questions using a microbe.
- Honestly conveys the practical craft of mutant screening—phenocopies, ascertainment problems, and the eventual triumph of scaled-up brute-force screening—that textbooks often gloss over.
- Shows how a mutant collection becomes a shared community resource that drives an entire field.

## Key References

1. **Armstrong & Adler (1967)** — "Genetics of motility in *Escherichia coli*: complementation of paralyzed mutants" (Genetics) — the first report on motility mutants that opened the field.
2. **Adler (1966)** — "Chemotaxis in bacteria" (Science) — Adler's foundational demonstration that bacterial chemotaxis is genetically tractable.
3. **Armstrong, Adler & Dahl (1967)** — "Non-chemotactic mutants of *Escherichia coli*" — early isolation of chemotaxis mutants by swarm-center cycling.
4. **Parkinson (1981)** — "Genetics of bacterial chemotaxis" (review) — synthesis of the components defined by the mutant collection.
5. **Berg (1975)** — "Chemotaxis in bacteria" (review) — reviews the pioneering nineteenth-century observations and the physics of bacterial swimming.

## Future Research Directions

- Bacterial chemotaxis became the paradigm for two-component signal transduction and for quantitative systems-biology models of adaptation and gain—work that continues in single-cell and synthetic-biology studies.
- The forward-genetic screening logic pioneered here generalizes to modern high-throughput and CRISPR-based screens for dissecting behavioral and signaling pathways in more complex organisms.
