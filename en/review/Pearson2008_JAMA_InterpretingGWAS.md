# How to Interpret a Genome-wide Association Study

## Citation (NLM)
Pearson TA, Manolio TA. How to interpret a genome-wide association study. JAMA. 2008;299(11):1335-1344. doi:10.1001/jama.299.11.1335

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.299.11.1335](https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.299.11.1335)

## Summary

This educational review in JAMA provides a systematic guide for clinicians and scientists to read and critically appraise genome-wide association studies (GWAS). Written at a moment when nearly 100 loci for 40 common diseases had been identified and replicated (by 2008), the paper addresses the urgent need for literacy in interpreting this new class of genetic evidence.

**GWAS Overview:** The NIH defines a GWAS as any study of common genetic variation across the human genome designed to identify genetic associations with observable traits. A typical GWAS proceeds through four phases: (1) selection of large case and control groups; (2) DNA isolation, genotyping, and data quality review; (3) statistical association testing for each SNP; and (4) replication of identified associations in an independent sample.

**Study Design Types:** The paper systematically compares three major GWAS designs:
- **Case-control:** Most common and cost-efficient but susceptible to population stratification and selection biases
- **Trio design:** Includes affected offspring and both parents; immune to population stratification but highly sensitive to genotyping error
- **Cohort design:** More representative and includes environmental covariate data, but expensive and time-consuming

**Genotyping and Quality Control:** Platforms of 500,000–1,000,000 SNPs capture 67–89% of common SNP variation in European and Asian populations. Key QC steps include: sample call rate (typically ≥80–90%), SNP call rate (≥95%), Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium testing, Mendelian error checking in trios, duplicate concordance rates (≥99.5%), and visual inspection of cluster plots for the strongest associations.

**Statistical Concepts:**
- **Multiple testing:** At P=0.05, a 1 million SNP study yields 50,000 false positives by chance. Bonferroni correction sets the threshold at P = 5×10⁻⁸.
- **Winner's curse:** Effect sizes for associations just exceeding the significance threshold are systematically overestimated, leading replication studies to be underpowered when designed based on initial estimates.
- **Effect sizes:** Most GWAS-discovered associations have modest odds ratios of 1.2–1.3.
- **Q-Q plots:** Comparing observed vs. expected test statistics under the null hypothesis reveals population stratification (broad inflation) versus true associations (deviation only in the extreme tail).

**Replication and Functional Studies:** The paper emphasizes replication in independent samples as the primary strategy to separate true from false associations. Consensus replication criteria require the same or very similar phenotype and population, and similar effect size and direction for the same SNP and allele. Functional studies — examining gene expression, genetic manipulation in cell or animal models — are required to move from statistical association to biological mechanism.

**Limitations:** False-positive potential, insensitivity to rare and structural variants, limited gene function information, absence of environmental data, and the current distance from clinical application are highlighted. The paper raises the "missing heritability" problem: discovered variants explain only a small fraction of the estimated total heritability for most conditions.

**Ten Critical Questions:** A practical checklist for appraising any GWAS report is provided, including whether cases are well-defined, whether cases and controls are comparable, whether the genotyping platform has sufficient density, and whether independent replication was performed.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- **GWAS literacy for clinicians:** This paper provides 10 critical appraisal questions that allow clinicians and researchers to directly evaluate any GWAS report, filling a gap between methodologists and end-users of genetic research.
- **The common disease-common variant hypothesis:** The paper clearly articulates that GWAS rests on the assumption that genetic influences on many common diseases are at least partly attributable to a limited number of allelic variants present in more than 1–5% of the population — a hypothesis that has been largely supported but also refined by subsequent work.
- **Why read this in class:** This review provides a comprehensive, accessible introduction to the full GWAS workflow — from study design to genotyping to analysis to replication to clinical application — making it an ideal primer for anyone encountering GWAS literature for the first time.

## Future Research Directions

- **Resolving missing heritability:** Discovered variants explain only a small fraction of estimated heritability. The contributions of rare variants, structural variants, gene-gene interactions, and gene-environment interactions need to be elucidated through larger sequencing studies and biobank-scale resources.
- **Clinical utility validation:** Whether GWAS findings add predictive value beyond established risk factors (age, BMI, family history) needs formal clinical evaluation, and evidence that genetic information improves patient outcomes must be generated before broad clinical implementation.
- **Functional variant identification:** Most GWAS signals lie in intronic or intergenic regions; eQTL mapping, chromatin accessibility studies, and CRISPR-based functional screens are needed to identify causal variants and understand their molecular mechanisms.
- **Ancestral diversity in GWAS:** Early GWAS were predominantly conducted in European-ancestry populations, limiting generalizability; expansion to diverse ancestral groups is essential for equitable application of genomic medicine.
