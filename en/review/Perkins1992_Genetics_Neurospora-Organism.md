# Neurospora: The Organism Behind the Molecular Revolution

## Citation (NLM)
Perkins DD. Neurospora: the organism behind the molecular revolution. Genetics. 1992;130(4):687-701.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/130/4/687](https://genetics.org/content/130/4/687)

---

## Summary

This *Genetics* Perspectives essay by David D. Perkins is a companion to Horowitz's "Fifty Years Ago: The Neurospora Revolution." Where Horowitz celebrated the people and ideas behind Beadle and Tatum's 1941 breakthrough, Perkins focuses on the *research organism itself*—the filamentous fungus *Neurospora*—and argues that its exceptional combination of features made it ideal not only for the original one gene–one enzyme experiments but for a continuing succession of contributions spanning "the entire range from molecules to populations." He traces the organism's story from long before Beadle and Tatum through its rich later history.

Perkins recounts a remarkable pre-history. *Neurospora*'s vegetative phase was described by French microbiologists nearly a century before Beadle and Tatum, when in the hot summer of 1842 an orange mold spoiled Parisian bread and a war-ministry commission investigated the "champignons rouges du pain" (Payen 1843). Pasteur later cited these observations in his work refuting spontaneous generation. In colonial Java, the Dutch plant physiologist F. A. F. C. Went studied the orange fungus used to make the fermented food *oncom*, and the organism's characteristic association with heat and fire is documented worldwide—blooming on burned vegetation after volcanic eruptions, forest burns, and famously after the 1923 Tokyo earthquake and fire. This heat association reflects the biology of *Neurospora*'s heat-activated, dormant ascospores.

The modern genetic history centers on Bernard Dodge, who—like Beadle—grew up on a farm and came to science late. Dodge discovered heat activation of ascospores and mating types in ascomycetes, made *Neurospora crassa* his lifelong passion, and worked out its basic genetics: the first tetrad analysis showing mating types segregate 4:4, the cytological basis of "pseudohomothallism" in *N. tetrasperma*, and, implicitly, an early biological-species concept based on crossing behavior. Dodge tirelessly promoted the fungus, urging T. H. Morgan and the Drosophila group to adopt it. His enthusiasm indirectly recruited Carl Lindegren, who developed fertile wild-type strains, identified marker mutants, discovered the first linkages (confirming that crossing over occurs at the four-strand stage), and built genetic maps—before abandoning *Neurospora* for yeast just as Beadle and Tatum obtained their first biochemical mutant. Perkins also recounts Barbara McClintock's 1944 visit to Stanford, where she used light microscopy to establish that *Neurospora*'s seven chromosomes and their meiotic behavior were typically eukaryotic.

The heart of the essay catalogs why *Neurospora* proved so powerful and the astonishing breadth of discoveries it enabled. Its advantageous features—haploidy, recovery of all four meiotic products with mappable centromeres, heterokaryon formation, simple defined nutritional requirements, rapid growth, abundant plating propagules, and strains preservable in "suspended animation" (conferring effective immortality)—were novel in the 1940s though now widely shared. Perkins lists a vast range of contributions pioneered in *Neurospora*: elucidation of biosynthetic pathways (e.g., seven genes for arginine synthesis, shown to be scattered rather than clustered); conditional and temperature-sensitive mutants; the first definitive proof of **gene conversion** (Mitchell 1955); discovery of *rec* genes controlling recombination frequency and of **repeat-induced point mutation (RIP)**, later exploited for targeted gene inactivation; heterokaryon incompatibility and mating-type genetics; extensive mitochondrial biology (maternal inheritance, mtDNA isolation, self-splicing introns, alternative oxidase, protein import); the first fungal **circadian rhythm** and clock mutants; membrane transport and electrophysiology; and discovery of *Spore killer* meiotic-drive elements analogous to *Segregation distorter* in Drosophila. Perkins also candidly notes frustrations (e.g., no new essential metabolites were found; bacteria displaced *Neurospora* for bioassays) and defends the organism against the notion of its "demise," concluding that *Neurospora* remains a superb research organism precisely because of its happy combination of traits suiting problems from molecules to natural populations.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Complements the one gene–one enzyme story by explaining *why the choice of model organism matters*—a foundational lesson in experimental genetics.
- Surveys the fungal life cycle, tetrad analysis, centromere mapping, and heterokaryons—classical tools students learn when studying recombination and linkage.
- Introduces landmark concepts pioneered in *Neurospora*: gene conversion, RIP, circadian genetics, meiotic drive (*Spore killer*), and mitochondrial (non-Mendelian) inheritance.
- A vivid, wide-ranging history connecting classical, biochemical, molecular, and population genetics through a single organism.

## Key References

1. **Beadle & Tatum (1941)** — first biochemical mutants of *Neurospora* — the founding work this essay contextualizes.
2. **Shear & Dodge (1927)** — first *Neurospora* genetics paper — defined the genus and its species by crossing behavior.
3. **McClintock (1945)** — cytology of *Neurospora* chromosomes — showed fungal chromosomes are typically eukaryotic.
4. **Mitchell (1955)** — first definitive proof of gene conversion — a landmark in recombination mechanism.
5. **Selker (1990)** — repeat-induced point mutation (RIP) — a uniquely *Neurospora* genome-defense process turned into a research tool.

## Future Research Directions

- Perkins's argument that *Neurospora* spans "molecules to populations" anticipates its continued use in circadian biology, epigenetics/gene silencing (RIP, DNA methylation), and fungal population genetics.
- The organism's tractable genetics and heterokaryon/meiotic-drive systems remain relevant models for studying genome defense, selfish genetic elements, and vegetative incompatibility.
