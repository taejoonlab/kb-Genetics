# Suppressors, Screens, and Genes: An Educational Primer for LIN-35 and Reverse Genetics in C. elegans

## Citation (NLM)
De Stasio EA. Suppressors, screens, and genes: an educational primer for use with "A network of genes antagonistic to the LIN-35 retinoblastoma protein of Caenorhabditis elegans." Genetics. 2012;191(4):1031-1035. doi:10.1534/genetics.112.142943

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.112.142943](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.112.142943)

## Summary

This Genetics Primer by Elizabeth De Stasio (Lawrence University) accompanies Polley & Fay (2012), which identified a network of genes antagonistic to the LIN-35 retinoblastoma protein in C. elegans. The primer explains the genetic concepts and experimental logic of the accompanying paper and provides a structured guide for classroom use.

C. elegans is a powerful genetic model organism: a 1-mm soil nematode with an invariant, known cell lineage, a fully sequenced genome, self-fertile hermaphrodite reproduction (rare males not required), and susceptibility to feeding-based RNAi. Gene names are lowercase italic (lin-35); protein names are uppercase non-italic (LIN-35).

LIN-35 is the C. elegans ortholog of the mammalian retinoblastoma protein Rb. Rb binds E2F transcription factors to repress their target genes, producing cell cycle arrest and suppressing carcinogenesis. In worms, LIN-35 has similar functions, but loss of lin-35 alone causes only a weak vulval lineage phenotype. The severe phenotype — early L1 larval arrest due to intestinal failure — only appears when lin-35 is combined with loss of slr-2 (a zinc-finger transcription factor). Because neither single mutant shows L1 arrest, this is a "synthetic phenotype": a synergistic phenotype requiring two independent mutations.

The core experimental strategy of Polley & Fay: they maintained lin-35; slr-2 double-mutant worms by providing a wild-type lin-35 transgene on an extrachromosomal array that also carries GFP. Since extrachromosomal arrays are lost in ~30% of offspring during meiosis, non-green offspring of these worms are lin-35; slr-2 double mutants that normally arrest at L1. By feeding 16,757 different RNAi bacteria (each targeting a different C. elegans gene) to GFP-positive mothers, and looking for non-green offspring that survive past L1, the authors performed a genome-scale suppressor screen: any gene whose knockdown allows survival of the double mutant is a suppressor of the lin-35; slr-2 synthetic lethal phenotype. Suppressors were classified into three functional groups: (1) ribosome biogenesis genes; (2) genes already known as synMuv suppressors; (3) prohibitins (involved in cell cycle regulation and mitochondrial function).

The primer explains four key concepts: (1) reverse genetics (gene-to-phenotype), illustrated by the genome-wide RNAi screen; (2) synthetic phenotypes and their use in identifying pathway components; (3) the logic of extragenic suppressor screens — how a suppressor's mechanism (bypass, parallel pathway, epistasis) can be inferred from its interaction pattern; (4) phenocopy (G418 antibiotic treatment mimicking the ribosome-suppressor effect). Concrete approaches for classroom use are provided, including small-group assignments tied to specific figures.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A direct illustration of how reverse genetic tools (genome-wide RNAi + suppressor screen) can systematically map a biological pathway in a model organism — exemplifying the gene-to-phenotype strategy used in modern genetics
- The concept of "synthetic phenotype" is introduced through a real case: the lin-35; slr-2 lethal interaction reveals that LIN-35 and SLR-2 function in partially redundant intestinal pathways, a finding invisible to single-gene analysis
- Why this paper must be read in class: to understand reverse genetics, RNAi-based suppressor screens, and synthetic phenotypes as an integrated framework for discovering genetic networks, using C. elegans as the model system

## Future Research Directions

- Map the specific transcriptional targets of LIN-35/Rb in intestinal cells using ChIP-seq and RNA-seq, and compare with mammalian Rb target genes to understand conserved vs. divergent functions
- Determine the mechanistic basis for ribosome biogenesis suppressors: how does reducing translation capacity rescue LIN-35/SLR-2-dependent intestinal failure, and does this connect to known Rb interactions with the ribosome biogenesis pathway
