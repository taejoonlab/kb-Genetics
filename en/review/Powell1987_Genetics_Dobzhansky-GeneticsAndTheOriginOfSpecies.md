# "In the Air" — Theodosius Dobzhansky's Genetics and the Origin of Species

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/117/3/363](https://genetics.org/content/117/3/363)

## Summary

This Genetics Perspectives essay by Jeffrey Powell (Yale University), written on the 50th anniversary of Dobzhansky's landmark 1937 book, argues that Genetics and the Origin of Species is "the most important and influential book on evolution of the twentieth century" and explains why its synthesis was both necessary and historically contingent.

Why was such a synthesis needed in 1937? Despite 37 years since the rediscovery of Mendel and 25 years after Morgan's chromosome theory, the integration of Mendelian genetics with Darwinian evolution was far from complete. Two major barriers existed. First, there was deep mutual incomprehension between geneticists and evolutionists (the latter group composed mainly of systematists, natural historians, and paleontologists). Bateson lamented in 1922: "I had expected that genetics would provide at once common ground for the systematist and laboratory worker. This hope has been disappointed." The central dispute concerned speciation: geneticists like de Vries, Bateson, and Goldschmidt believed new species arise by macromutations or "hopeful monsters" — abrupt saltations that immediately produce a recognizably discontinuous new form. But natural historians saw gradual, continuous geographic differentiation. Second, T.H. Morgan, the dominant geneticist of the era, did not believe in species as real units (he considered species designations "a scholastic distinction that arose when species were regarded as specially created"), denied the creative role of natural selection (seeing mutation as the driving force of evolution), and simply ignored the emerging theoretical work of Wright, Fisher, and Haldane — none of their theoretical papers appear in his three books on evolution.

Dobzhansky succeeded where his predecessors failed because he was uniquely trained in both worlds: Russian natural history and systematics first, then Morgan's laboratory. The 1937 book accomplished several things simultaneously: (1) it discredited "soft inheritance" theories (Lamarckism, etc.) while validating the geneticists' emphasis on spontaneous mutation as the source of variation; (2) it reinstated natural selection as the primary agent of adaptive evolution, restoring the Darwinian tradition; (3) it connected the theoretical population genetics of Wright, Fisher, and Haldane to the study of natural populations — Wright's models of population structure (size, isolation, drift) especially resonated with naturalists who saw geographic isolation as a key driver of speciation; (4) it introduced and defended the biological species concept, emphasizing that species are real evolutionary units and that isolating mechanisms are crucial for diversification; (5) it demonstrated experimentally that microevolutionary variation (intraspecific) and macroevolutionary differences (interspecific) are not qualitatively different — in D. pseudoobscura × D. persimilis crosses, the F1 male sterility was shown to be distributed across the genome through Mendelian markers, and intraspecific geographic variation existed for the very traits defining interspecific distinctions.

Powell also narrates the evolution of Dobzhansky's thinking on genetic drift. He began studying D. pseudoobscura inversion polymorphisms expecting to find a neutral trait — ideal for testing Wright's drift theories. When these inversions proved to be under remarkably strong selection, Dobzhansky de-emphasized drift in later editions (a "hardening of the synthesis," in Gould's phrase). He reportedly told Powell in 1974: "I began as a drifter and then became a selectionist. Now, in my old age, I find myself becoming a drifter again" — reflecting the shift brought about by Kimura's neutral theory.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides the historical framework for the neo-Darwinian Modern Synthesis — the intellectual integration of population genetics, Mendelian genetics, systematics, and natural history — that defines the conceptual foundation of modern evolutionary genetics
- Explains why the synthesis required someone uniquely trained in both camp — and identifies the specific conceptual obstacles (macromutation theory, Morgan's anti-species stance, failure to engage population genetics) that had to be overcome
- Why this paper must be read in class: to understand how Mendelian genetics and Darwinian evolution were unified in the 1930s, why this unification was non-trivial, and to place population genetics within the broader history of evolutionary biology

## Key References

2. **Mayr (1942)** — Systematics and the Origin of Species, Columbia University Press — Directly inspired by Dobzhansky; systematized the biological species concept and allopatric speciation
3. **Wright, Fisher, Haldane (1930s)** — The theoretical population genetics that Dobzhansky translated into empirical evolutionary biology
