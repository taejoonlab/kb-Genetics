# Whatever Happened to *Paramecium* Genetics?

## Citation (NLM)
Preer JR Jr. Whatever happened to *Paramecium* genetics? Genetics. 1997;145(2):217-225.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/145/2/217](https://genetics.org/content/145/2/217)

---

## Summary

John R. Preer, Jr., a lifelong *Paramecium* geneticist, opens this *Genetics* "Perspectives" essay with a lament: between 1945 and 1965, *Paramecium* genetics was a staple of every introductory genetics textbook, yet today's texts scarcely mention it. He asks whether the work was truly inconsequential, merely forgotten, and if forgotten, why. His answer, developed across a rich review of the science, is that the facts about *Paramecium* remain valid and the field still offers "very bright opportunities for future discoveries" — its neglect is sociological, not scientific.

Preer roots the story in the 1920s battle between the "Mendelian-Morgan geneticists," who located heredity in chromosomal genes, and the "physiologists," who argued that the truly fundamental traits (membrane permeability, cell division) were controlled by the cytoplasm, not the nucleus, and that mutations in such traits would be lethal and thus inaccessible. H. S. Jennings took protozoa seriously as a testing ground, and Tracy Sonneborn — the "giant figure" whose students built the field — went on to reveal a whole zoo of non-Mendelian phenomena. Preer reviews these in turn, updating each to 1997. The **macronucleus**: ciliates carry diploid micronuclei (germline) and a polyploid, metabolically active macronucleus (soma); at conjugation the macronucleus is rebuilt from the micronucleus with massive DNA amplification *and* programmed rearrangement — chromosome breakage with telomere addition, and precise excision of internal eliminated sequences (IESs), some of which are transposon-derived and even scrambled in the micronucleus. **Amitosis**: macronuclei divide without mitosis, raising the still-unsolved problem of how genic balance is maintained across many divisions (with allelic assortment revealing the phenomenon).

The essay's most striking material concerns epigenetic and cytoplasmic inheritance. **Caryonidal inheritance** and **macronuclear inheritance** show that a character (e.g., mating type, or the *d48* serotype-A defect) can be determined by the state of the old macronucleus and transmitted through the cytoplasm to the newly forming macronucleus, independent of the genes in the micronucleus — dramatically confirmed when *d48*-like states were manufactured at will by micronuclear transplantation, and when injecting the cloned A gene "rescued" the defect. **Killers** (kappa) provided the first example of cytoplasmic inheritance in animals and inspired Sonneborn's "plasmagene theory," until kappa was shown to be a symbiotic bacterium carrying plasmids/phages encoding toxins (R bodies) — teaching that "the borderline between heredity and parasitism can become very blurred." **Serotypes** demonstrated mutually exclusive, environmentally sensitive, cytoplasmically inherited stable states of gene expression, now understood as transcriptional feedback control. **Cytotaxis** (with Beisson) showed that cortical structure itself templates new structure, a form of structural inheritance. **Behavioral genetics** (Ching Kung) exploited *Paramecium*'s excitable membrane to isolate ion-channel mutants — including one in calmodulin — unavailable in higher organisms. *Paramecium* also revealed a deviation from the "universal" genetic code.

Preer closes with eight candidate explanations for the decline, weighing each: autogamy is too complicated (rejected); it is hard to culture (partly true, favoring *Tetrahymena*); molecular genetics crowded it out (no); it is too "queer" and irrelevant (rejected — "Treasure your exceptions," Sonneborn quoting Bateson); funding dried up (important in Britain); Sonneborn's students dispersed to other organisms; and, most tellingly, "Sonneborn is not around any more." He concludes that the towering, infectious enthusiasm of Sonneborn — who drew students worldwide to Bloomington, the "Mecca for ciliate studies" — was irreplaceable, and that the field's eclipse owes more to the loss of its charismatic leader than to any exhaustion of its scientific promise.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A superb case study in non-Mendelian and epigenetic inheritance — cytoplasmic inheritance, structural (cortical) inheritance, and heritable states of gene expression — that complements the chromosome theory of heredity.
- Introduces genome architecture concepts (germline vs. somatic nuclei, programmed DNA elimination, telomere addition, IESs) that recur in immunology and developmental biology.
- Shows the endosymbiosis theme (kappa as symbiotic bacterium; parallels to mitochondria and chloroplasts) and a real exception to the "universal" genetic code.
- Offers a candid meditation on the sociology of science: why fields rise and fall with organisms, funding, technology, and charismatic leaders — valuable perspective for students choosing model systems.

## Key References

1. **Sonneborn (1947)** — Experimental control of cytoplasmic genetic factors in *Paramecium* — the basis of the plasmagene theory and the foundation of the field Preer reviews.
2. **Epstein and Forney (1984)** — Mendelian and non-Mendelian mutations affecting surface antigen in *P. tetraurelia* — the *d48* mutant that opened modern study of macronuclear inheritance.
3. **Prescott (1994)** — The DNA of ciliated protozoa — the key review of macronuclear DNA rearrangement (breakage, IESs, gene scrambling).
4. **Godiska et al. (1987)** — Transformation of *Paramecium* by microinjection of a cloned serotype gene — the technique that made molecular dissection of ciliate inheritance possible.
5. **Preer (1996)** — Tracy M. Sonneborn 1905-1981 — biographical context for the field's dominant figure.

## Future Research Directions

- The programmed DNA rearrangements and epigenetically inherited macronuclear states Preer describes anticipated modern epigenetics and small-RNA-guided genome editing (scan-RNA / Piwi pathways in ciliates), now studied at the molecular level.
- His unanswered questions — how genic balance survives amitosis, how the cortex templates itself, and the molecular basis of macronuclear inheritance — remain genuinely open and connect to contemporary work on non-genetic inheritance and chromosome copy-number control.
