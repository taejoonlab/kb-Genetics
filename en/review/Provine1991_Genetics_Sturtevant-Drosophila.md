# Alfred Henry Sturtevant and crosses between Drosophila melanogaster and Drosophila simulans

## Citation (NLM)
Provine WB. Alfred Henry Sturtevant and crosses between Drosophila melanogaster and Drosophila simulans. Genetics. 1991;129(1):1-5.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/129/1/1](https://genetics.org/content/129/1/1)

---

## Summary

William B. Provine's "Perspectives" essay reconstructs A. H. Sturtevant's pioneering genetic study of the *Drosophila melanogaster*–*Drosophila simulans* species pair, framing Sturtevant not merely as the maker of the first genetic map but as the Morgan group's leading thinker on the interface of genetics, systematics, and speciation. Provine notes that Sewall Wright, who called H. J. Muller perhaps the greatest living geneticist, nevertheless regarded Sturtevant as the group's clear authority when it came to evolution and speciation. From his student days Sturtevant was an avid Drosophila taxonomist (describing 23 new species in 1916) and, Provine argues, was likely the author of the first outline of Dobzhansky's ambitious "Genetics of Natural Populations" series.

The essay's core is Sturtevant's 1919 discovery that "*Drosophila melanogaster*" was really two species. Males from certain wild-caught stocks, when bred to laboratory females, produced only sterile female offspring. Sturtevant obtained more wild flies (from Lakeland, Florida, via C. W. Metz), and C. B. Bridges found a consistent difference in male genitalia. Sturtevant named the new species *Drosophila simulans* and predicted it would be "extensively discussed" — because understanding differences between closely related species offered the deepest insight into speciation, and because, uniquely, simulans could be crossed with melanogaster, the best-studied species. He rapidly produced three 1920–1921 papers, "Genetic studies on Drosophila simulans." The crosses had two disadvantages: all hybrids were sterile (so no F2 or progeny testing), and the two species were so similar that their chromosome groups appeared identical.

Provine details the genetic ingenuity Sturtevant applied despite hybrid sterility. Reciprocal crosses (including XXY females) showed that identical chromosome complements fared differently depending on the cytoplasm of the mother — an early, important demonstration of *cytoplasmic* contributions to species differences. He also documented sexual selection as a barrier to interspecific mating (females preferentially mate with their own species). Unable to do progeny testing, Sturtevant used a clever allelism test: hybrid females carrying similar recessive mutations from each species reveal, by recessive vs. dominant phenotype, whether the two mutations are allelic. Five of six X-linked mutations and two of four third-chromosome mutations were allelic between species, establishing the first demonstration of *parallel mutations* in two distinct species. Notably, in a section on "Complemental Genes," Sturtevant anticipated *random genetic drift* (before the term existed): genes with no significant effect in their species of origin escape natural selection and may spread by chance — precisely the argument Wright would make years later. Sturtevant also inferred a chromosomal *inversion* to explain non-collinear gene orders, proposing that a "buckle" at crossing over could invert a segment.

The essay closes by tracing the long afterlife of Sturtevant's project. He himself abandoned the melanogaster–simulans cross (because total hybrid sterility blocked recombinant analysis) in favor of the more tractable *D. pseudoobscura* races A and B, though he disputed Dobzhansky and Epling's 1944 elevation of those races to species status, insisting there were no clear taxonomic markers as there were between melanogaster and simulans. Later workers (Bonnier's histology; Kerkis and Patau on temperature, gonads, and salivary-chromosome conjugation; Schultz and Dobzhansky's triploid crosses) extended the study. Muller and Pontecorvo used irradiated simulans males and triploid melanogaster females to generate recombinant chromosomes, concluding that hybrid-sterility factors were numerous, interactive, and spread throughout the genome — even minor chromosomes showed many gene differences, implying "cryptic divergence" that need not have had selective value. Modern hybrid-rescue genes (Watanabe 1979; Hutter and Ashburner 1987) and Coyne's speciation genetics have revived the debate about whether one or many loci underlie speciation — a controversy that, Provine notes, Sturtevant's belief has fueled for seventy years.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A vivid case study in how classical genetics illuminated *speciation*: species definition (reproductive isolation, sterile hybrids, morphological markers), allelism tests, and the genetics of species differences.
- Introduces multiple foundational concepts through one story — cytoplasmic (maternal) effects, parallel mutation, sexual selection as a reproductive barrier, chromosomal inversions, and an early anticipation of random genetic drift.
- Shows Sturtevant's experimental cleverness in extracting genetic information despite the severe constraint of complete hybrid sterility, a model of working around limitations.
- Connects a 1919–1921 discovery to a living research program (hybrid-rescue genes, speciation genetics), demonstrating how historical questions persist and drive modern work.

## Future Research Directions

- The one-locus vs. many-loci debate over the genetic basis of speciation (Muller's "many interactive genes" vs. single hybrid-rescue loci) remains open and continues to motivate speciation genomics.
- Sturtevant's early recognition of cytoplasmic contributions to species differences prefigures modern work on cytoplasmic–nuclear incompatibilities (e.g., mitochondrial and endosymbiont effects) in hybrid inviability and sterility.
