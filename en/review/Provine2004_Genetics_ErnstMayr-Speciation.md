# Ernst Mayr: Genetics and Speciation

## Citation (NLM)
Provine WB. Ernst Mayr: genetics and speciation. Genetics. 2004;167(3):1041-1046.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/167/3/1041](https://genetics.org/content/167/3/1041)

---

## Summary

Written to mark Ernst Mayr's 100th birthday (July 5, 2004), this Perspectives essay by historian of biology William B. Provine traces Mayr's long, ambivalent "love/hate relationship with genetics" as he developed his controversial theory of *genetic revolutions* to explain speciation. When Provine interviewed Mayr in 1986 and asked him to name his most important contribution to evolutionary biology, Mayr replied "without hesitation: genetic revolutions." The essay follows how a systematist and champion of the biological species concept became, of necessity, entangled with genetics.

Mayr did not invent the biological species concept — Dobzhansky had argued for it earlier (1935, 1937) — but he crystallized it in *Systematics and the Origin of Species* (1942), defining species as reproductively isolated groups of interbreeding populations. Because isolating mechanisms are largely genetically determined, adopting this concept tied Mayr inextricably to genetics: no theory of speciation could be complete without a genetic account of how isolating mechanisms arise. Yet in 1942 Mayr, by his own later admission, "did not know much recent genetics." His understanding of the genetics of small, isolated populations came secondhand from Dobzhansky's distillation of Sewall Wright's work rather than from Wright's own papers. In this framework he introduced the *founder principle* — the idea that a population started by a few individuals (or a single fertilized female) carries only a small sample of the parent population's variability — as an auxiliary mechanism (less important than random drift) for reducing variability in isolated populations. Notably, in 1942 he did not yet claim the founder principle was a frequent driver of speciation, nor did he explain precisely *how* reduced variability produced new isolating mechanisms.

A key episode is Mayr's rejection of "beanbag genetics." In his 1959 keynote "Where Are We?" (at the Cold Spring Harbor Symposium marking the *Origin* centenary), Mayr dismissed the mathematical population genetics of Fisher, Wright, and Haldane as treating each gene as an independent unit — "as in the adding of certain beans to a beanbag and the withdrawing of others" — a "gross simplification." This provoked Haldane's famous "Defense of Beanbag Genetics" (1964) and rebuttals from Wright, and (later) Crow. Strikingly, Mayr had by then dismissed the very authority (Wright) on whom he had earlier relied, and admitted in interview that he still had not carefully studied Wright's, Haldane's, or Fisher's original works.

The heart of the story is the invention of *genetic revolutions*. During a 1950 recuperation at Cold Spring Harbor, Mayr learned a "new genetics" from Bruce Wallace and James C. King — the idea of the *gene pool* as a cohesive, coadapted, integrated system in which a gene's selective value depends on its genetic environment (Dobzhansky's "gene pool," Lerner's "genetic homeostasis"). On a 1951 return trip from Italy, Mayr conceived that a founder population, suddenly reduced in size, would experience a drastic change in the genetic environment of most loci — a "chain reaction" of shifting selective values that could rapidly reorganize the coadapted gene complex and generate isolating mechanisms in a short time. This "genetic revolution" (published in Mayr 1954) passes a population from one well-integrated equilibrium, through a highly unstable period with great loss of variability, to a new balanced state (Mayr's famous diagram, points A→E). Mere random drift, in Mayr's new view, could no longer by itself produce speciation. Provine emphasizes that Mayr's theory was "vague and devoid of much genetic content"; its value was to challenge geneticists to explain the origin of isolating mechanisms.

Provine concludes that Mayr's genetic revolutions, though vague, gave "a major impetus" to speciation research: after 1970 a series of studies (Carson, Lande, Templeton, Barton and Charlesworth) evaluated and reformulated the idea. Even those who reject the concept still confront the fundamental problem Mayr posed — echoing Darwin, Muller, and Dobzhansky — that isolating mechanisms are not produced directly by natural selection but arise as incidental by-products of the genetic divergence of isolated gene pools. In their 1986 interview Mayr regretted having classed Wright as a beanbagger, acknowledging that Wright's emphasis on gene interaction and the shifting-balance theory were in fact congenial to his own views; and in 1985 Mayr nominated Wright for the Balzan Prize.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A concise history of the biological species concept and the problem of speciation — central topics in any introduction to evolutionary genetics.
- Introduces the founder principle, founder effects, genetic drift, effective population size, and coadapted gene complexes through the biography of the ideas.
- Dramatizes the productive tension between mathematical population genetics ("beanbag genetics") and organismal/naturalist perspectives — a debate that shaped the Modern Synthesis.
- Shows how a conceptually "vague" but provocative theory can set a research agenda for decades.

## Key References

1. **Mayr (1942)** — *Systematics and the Origin of Species* — crystallized the biological species concept and introduced the founder principle.
2. **Mayr (1954)** — "Change of genetic environment and evolution" — the first full statement of the genetic-revolution theory of speciation.
3. **Dobzhansky (1937)** — *Genetics and the Origin of Species* — the genetic foundation (via Wright) on which Mayr initially relied.
4. **Haldane (1964)** — "A defense of beanbag genetics," *Perspect. Biol. Med.* — the classic reply to Mayr's critique of mathematical population genetics.
5. **Provine (1986)** — *Sewall Wright and Evolutionary Biology* — the historical analysis underlying Provine's account of the Mayr–Wright relationship.

## Future Research Directions

- The genetics of speciation — how reproductive isolation arises as an incidental by-product of divergence (e.g., Dobzhansky–Muller incompatibilities) — remains the core empirical program Mayr's challenge helped launch.
- Modern population genomics can now test the founder-effect / genetic-revolution hypotheses directly, quantifying how bottlenecks reshape coadapted gene complexes and drive divergence.
