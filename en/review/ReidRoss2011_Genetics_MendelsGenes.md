# Mendel's Genes: Toward a Full Molecular Characterization

## Citation (NLM)
Reid JB, Ross JJ. Mendel's genes: toward a full molecular characterization. Genetics. 2011;189(1):3–10. doi:10.1534/genetics.111.132118

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.111.132118](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.111.132118)

## Summary

This perspective review by James Reid and John Ross (University of Tasmania) surveys the molecular identities and functions of the seven genes Mendel studied in Pisum sativum. As of 2011, four genes had been cloned and characterized; the remaining three were still uncharacterized.

**Seed shape (R gene, cloned):** The round vs. wrinkled seed difference is determined by Starch-Branching Enzyme 1 (SBE1). In the r allele, SBE1 is disrupted by an 0.8-kb insertion resembling the Ac/Ds transposable elements of maize. Loss of SBE1 impairs starch biosynthesis, elevates sugars and lipids, increases osmotic pressure, and causes immature seeds to accumulate more water. Upon drying, the reduced starch content creates the wrinkled phenotype.

**Stem length (LE gene, cloned):** The tall (Le) vs. dwarf (le) difference is determined by GA 3-oxidase1 — a gibberellin biosynthetic enzyme that converts the inactive precursor GA20 to bioactive GA1. Tall plants carry about 10-fold more GA1 than le plants. The le-1 mutation is a G→A single nucleotide substitution causing an Ala→Thr amino acid change near the enzyme's active site, dramatically reducing 3-oxidase activity.

**Cotyledon color (I gene, cloned):** The yellow (I) vs. green (i, stay-green) difference is determined by the SGR (Stay-Green) gene, which regulates chlorophyll catabolism during senescence. In i mutants, chlorophyll degradation is impaired, leaving cotyledons green even in the mature, dry seed. The main i allele carries a 6-bp insertion encoding a two-amino-acid insertion in a conserved region of the SGR protein.

**Flower and seed coat color (A gene, cloned):** The purple-flowered (A) vs. white-flowered (a) difference is determined by a bHLH transcription factor that spatially regulates chalcone synthase (CHS) gene family expression, controlling anthocyanin biosynthesis. The most common a allele results from a G→A transition at the GT splice donor site of intron 6, causing a frameshift and premature stop codon. This explains Mendel's observation that seed coat color is always co-inherited with flower color — both are pleiotropic effects of the same gene.

**Pod color (GP gene, not cloned):** The green (Gp) vs. yellow (gp) pod difference reflects disruption of chloroplast structure in the pod mesocarp; gp pods have plastids lacking grana with only ~5% of wild-type chlorophyll. Synteny between pea and Medicago genomes was proposed as a route to identify candidate genes.

**Pod form (V? gene, not cloned):** Inflated (V/V) vs. constricted/parchmentless (v/v or p/p) pods differ in the completeness of the sclerenchyma layer on the inner pod wall. Both v and p single mutants lack a complete sclerenchyma layer; double mutants (p v) lack it entirely. The identity of the gene(s) Mendel used is uncertain. Parchmentless pod varieties have been grown for centuries — these are the edible "sugar pod" or snow pea types.

**Flower position (FA gene, not cloned):** The axially-distributed (Fa) vs. terminally-clustered, fasciated (fa) flower position difference is thought to involve a gene related to Clavata signaling, known to maintain shoot apical meristem structure. The "Mummy Pea" variety likely carried Mendel's fa allele.

The authors emphasize that Mendel's seven mutations represent a remarkable diversity of molecular mechanisms — transposon insertion, single base substitutions, splice-site changes, and small insertions — all producing clear dominant/recessive phenotypes. Several have been agriculturally important for centuries (dwarf habit reducing lodging, edible pods, sweet seeds from reduced starch synthesis). The authors argue that the full molecular characterization of Mendel's genes would represent both a triumphant connection between classical and molecular genetics and an outstanding educational resource.

## Significance in Introduction Context

Why this paper matters at the introduction to genetics:
- Bridges classical Mendelian genetics (phenotypic segregation) and modern molecular biology (gene sequences, protein functions, mutation mechanisms) using exactly the traits Mendel himself studied
- Illustrates that dominance/recessivity at the phenotypic level corresponds at the molecular level to functional vs. loss-of-function alleles — making Mendel's abstract concepts tangible
- Essential course reading because it shows how the seven pea traits that launched genetics each correspond to defined biochemical pathways (starch biosynthesis, gibberellin metabolism, chlorophyll degradation, anthocyanin biosynthesis), creating ideal case studies for teaching gene function

## Future Research Directions

Unresolved questions and future research directions from this paper:

- Identifying the molecular basis of GP (pod color), V (pod form), and FA (flower position) — subsequently resolved by Feng et al. (2025) in Nature
- Determining which specific mutant alleles Mendel actually used, by examining historical European pea cultivar collections and tracing the geographic distribution of specific mutations
- Developing a complete molecular genetics course built around Mendel's seven genes, each illustrating a different molecular mechanism and biochemical pathway — gene regulation, enzyme function, transcription factor activity, signal transduction
