# Beyond the Identification of Mendel's Genes

## Citation (NLM)
Ross JJ. Beyond the identification of Mendel's genes. Nat Plants. 2025;11:1474–1475. doi:10.1038/s41477-025-02071-0

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41477-025-02071-0](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41477-025-02071-0)

---

## Summary

This News & Views commentary in *Nature Plants* is written by John J. Ross (University of Tasmania — co-author of the landmark Reid & Ross 2011 review on Mendel's genes) and offers an expert assessment of Feng et al. (2025) that goes beyond celebrating the discovery to examine its deeper conceptual implications.

**Significance beyond gene identification:** Ross emphasizes that the GWAS results in Feng et al. show, for all seven traits, that the strongest peak corresponds to a Mendelian locus — meaning each Mendelian gene makes a greater contribution to phenotypic variation than any other genomic region. This directly counters recent arguments that Mendelian genes are minor players overwhelmed by environmental and genetic context. The striking example: 100% of the 138 r accessions had wrinkled seeds — environment and genetic context were insufficient to restore the round phenotype. On the other hand, the i (green cotyledon) allele shows some environmental penetrance, consistent with Mendel's own notes.

**Conceptual challenge posed by Gp:** Ross highlights that Gp is not a gene in the conventional molecular sense — it is an approximately 106-kb deletion sequence. Gp co-segregates with ChlG (chlorophyll synthase), located only 3.7 kb downstream of the deletion boundary. In gp pods, partial TIR-NBS-LRR transcripts fuse with ChlG transcripts, reducing functional ChlG transcript levels by 94%. The structural sequence of ChlG itself is intact in gp plants.

Ross particularly commends the elegance of the complementation test. In double heterozygotes (Gp/gp and ChlGWT/ChlGW121*), yellow pods were produced because the chromosome carrying wild-type ChlG also carried the gp deletion (preventing normal ChlG transcription), while the other chromosome carried the mutant ChlGW121* allele (preventing functional ChlG protein). Therefore, neither chromosome could produce sufficient ChlG function. Critically, the yellow pods cannot be explained by loss of the genes within the 106-kb deletion (including a candidate 3′-exoribonuclease), because those genes are present as wild-type copies on the other chromosome in the double heterozygote.

Ross notes that the possible phenotypic effects of disruption to the TIR-NBS-LRR gene by the gp deletion were not studied by Feng et al. — such genes are generally involved in disease resistance.

**Brief assessment of P, V, and Fa:** The P gene (PsCLE41) was first molecularly characterized by Liu et al. (2024) and confirmed by Feng et al. For V, the PsMYB26 retrotransposon insertion model remains to be fully confirmed. The fa allele (5-bp exonic deletion in PsCIK2/3) is well-supported.

**Conclusion:** The paper ensures the ongoing relevance of Mendelian genetics but also poses new challenges, particularly regarding the terminology required to accommodate the "gene" Gp, which defies conventional gene-centric description.

## Significance in Introduction Context

Why this paper matters at the introduction to genetics:
- Provides an expert critical reading of Feng et al. (2025), modeling how scientists evaluate evidence beyond the headline findings
- Directly addresses and rebuts the argument that Mendelian genes are minor contributors to phenotypic variation — a conceptually important point for understanding what "a gene" actually does
- Essential course reading because it raises the question of what constitutes "a gene" in the post-genomic era, using the Gp locus as a compelling case where phenotypic dominance maps to a deletion rather than a coding sequence

## Future Research Directions

- Investigating the phenotypic consequences of TIR-NBS-LRR gene disruption in gp plants — particularly regarding disease resistance, which is the canonical function of such genes
- Completing the molecular confirmation of V (PsMYB26 and Ogre retrotransposon insertion) with additional functional evidence, such as direct promoter activity assays
- Exploring whether the Gp locus case — where a deletion affecting a downstream gene's transcription is the genetic basis of a Mendelian trait — has parallels in other organisms, and what this implies for gene definition in the era of structural genomics
