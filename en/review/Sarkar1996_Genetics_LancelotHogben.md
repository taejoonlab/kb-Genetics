# Lancelot Hogben, 1895–1975

## Citation (NLM)
Sarkar S. Lancelot Hogben, 1895-1975. Genetics. 1996;142(3):655-660.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/142/3/655](https://genetics.org/content/142/3/655)

## Summary

This Perspectives essay by Sahotra Sarkar, written for Hogben's birth centennial, portrays Lancelot Hogben as one of the most versatile biologists of his generation — a figure remembered less for any single discovery than for the extraordinary breadth of his contributions across human and medical genetics, comparative physiology, endocrinology, medical statistics, and even linguistics. In genetics he made methodological contributions to human and medical genetics and produced an influential analysis of the nature–nurture dispute. He pioneered comparative (evolutionary) physiology, helped found the Society for Experimental Biology, invented the standard pregnancy test used for fifteen years, contributed to UK medical statistics, and wrote a philosophical critique of the foundations of statistics as well as four books on linguistics. Though often overshadowed by his friend J. B. S. Haldane, Hogben was, like Haldane, a highly successful popularizer of science; his *Mathematics for the Million* (1936) sold over half a million copies, was translated into fifteen languages, and remains in print.

Sarkar emphasizes Hogben's lifelong political and ethical commitments, which are inseparable from his science. Born into an austere, poor, fundamentalist household in Portsmouth, he rose through scholarships to become the first student from a London County Council secondary school to reach Cambridge (Trinity). Influenced by Bertrand Russell, he became a socialist and, during World War I, a conscientious objector who was briefly imprisoned. He was an active feminist under the influence of his first wife, the mathematician Enid Charles. During a professorship in South Africa (1927–30), he fought so vigorously against the racial prejudice he called "chromatocracy" that he felt compelled to leave. His early scientific work included cytological studies showing parasynapsis in cockroach chromosomes (supporting the Morgan school's model of linkage and crossing over) and comparative endocrinology on amphibian metamorphosis and color change; his observation that female *Xenopus laevis* lay eggs after injection with pituitary extract became the "Hogben pregnancy test."

Hogben's most significant genetic work came after 1930, when he took the new Chair of Social Biology at the London School of Economics (chosen over R. A. Fisher). His *Genetic Principles in Medicine and Social Sciences* (1931) offered a critical survey of human genetics: a careful analysis of twin studies and their misinterpretation, a rigorous treatment of segregation analysis, the first systematic English-language account of Haldane's selection work (which Fisher had ignored), and — most importantly — it introduced to an English-speaking audience Bernstein's statistic for detecting linkage from phase-unknown matings, a crucial step in human linkage studies. The book, though its final chapters were explicitly political (targeting the pro-eugenics conclusions of Fisher's *Genetical Theory of Natural Selection*), was well received and became Hogben's most influential work of the 1930s. He extended methods for segregation analysis, rigorously established the single-recessive-gene basis of alkaptonuria that Garrod had suggested, and worked out correlations of relatives for sex-linked inheritance. Although his and Bernstein's linkage methods were later superseded by Fisher's maximum-likelihood and the LOD-score approaches of Haldane, Smith, and Morton, his analysis of the nature–nurture dispute proved more lasting. Using Krafka's data on facet number in Bar-eyed *Drosophila* at different temperatures, Hogben argued for the "relativity" or interdependence of nature and nurture: because genotype–environment interaction is routine, one cannot assign definite quantitative values to the relative importance of heredity and environment independent of a specified environment. This argument, greeted with silence and skepticism (even from Fisher) in the 1930s, eventually became standard, though for human traits the dispute remains unresolved.

The essay follows Hogben's later career — his 1936 election to the Royal Society, moves to Aberdeen and Birmingham, a dramatic wartime escape from Norway across the Soviet Union to America, a Wisconsin professorship that produced *An Introduction to Mathematical Genetics* (1946), and his wartime medical-statistics work (including an early demonstration that indiscriminate prophylactic antibiotic use selects for resistant strains). In his later decades his interests shifted decisively toward linguistics and a skeptical examination of the foundations of statistical inference (doubting Fisher, Neyman-Pearson, and Wald alike, though offering no alternative). Sarkar closes by noting that, like Haldane, Hogben spread himself across so many projects that his contribution to any one was diminished, leaving no single finding by which he is remembered; instead he is memorialized for his breadth, his lifelong commitment to social justice, and his gifts as a popularizer — and, fittingly for a champion of interactions, the totality of his contributions exceeds the sum of its parts.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Introduces the nature–nurture debate through Hogben's rigorous argument about genotype–environment interaction — a concept central to interpreting heritability and quantitative traits.
- Illustrates the early development of human genetics methodology: twin studies, segregation analysis, and the detection of linkage (Bernstein's statistic, later LOD scores).
- Shows the tight historical entanglement of genetics with the eugenics debate, presenting Hogben as a principled anti-eugenics voice countering Fisher's hereditarianism.
- Presents a model of the scientist as public intellectual and popularizer, connecting technical genetics to broader social and ethical concerns.

5. **Garrod (1902)** — "The incidence of alkaptonuria," *Lancet* — the single-recessive-gene hypothesis Hogben later established rigorously.

## Future Research Directions

- The nature–nurture problem for human traits remains unresolved; detecting genotype–environment interactions in human populations is still experimentally difficult and methodologically contested.
- Continued refinement of human linkage and association methods (from LOD scores to modern genome-wide approaches) builds on the foundations laid by Bernstein and Hogben.
