# Shapiro 2009 — Natural Genetic Engineering: Retrospective on E. coli Genome Engineering

## Citation (NLM)
Shapiro JA. Perspective: Letting *Escherichia coli* teach me about genome engineering. Genetics. 2009;183(4):1205-1214. doi:10.1534/genetics.109.110007

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.109.110007](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.109.110007)

## Summary

This Perspectives article is a career retrospective by James A. Shapiro, centered on his discovery of DNA insertion sequences (IS elements) in the *E. coli* gal operon during his PhD at Cambridge University in the 1960s.

Shapiro observed that IS elements could mediate processes analogous to in vivo cloning without restriction enzymes. In 1979, he proposed the "Shapiro intermediate" model to explain the mechanism of replicative transposition in bacteriophage Mu — a model that became a cornerstone reference for transposition research.

He also described adaptive mutation, a phenomenon in which mutation frequency increases by five orders of magnitude under starvation conditions, and reported that the occurrence of transposable elements within bacterial colonies is developmentally regulated. Integrating these observations, Shapiro proposed the concept of "natural genetic engineering" — the central thesis that cells use evolutionarily acquired biochemical systems to restructure their genomes in biologically responsive ways. This perspective connects directly to Barbara McClintock's earlier work on transposable elements in maize.

The paper occupies an important place in the intellectual history of genetics because it challenges the passive view of genomes embedded in the central dogma, arguing instead that cells can be active genome editors.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides a historical first-person account linking IS element discovery to the broader concept of genome plasticity
- The "natural genetic engineering" concept can be viewed as an intellectual precursor to modern genome editing paradigms including CRISPR
- Demonstrates how bacterial genetics insights propagated into eukaryotic genomics
