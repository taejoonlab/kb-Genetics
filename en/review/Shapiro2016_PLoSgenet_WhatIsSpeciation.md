# Shapiro et al. 2016 — What Is Speciation? A Genomic Perspective

## Citation (NLM)
Shapiro BJ, Leducq JB, Mallet J. What Is Speciation? PLoS Genet. 2016;12(3):e1005860. doi:10.1371/journal.pgen.1005860

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1005860](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pgen.1005860)

---

## Summary

This review by B. Jesse Shapiro, Jean-Baptiste Leducq, and James Mallet examines the history of species concepts and the genomic-era understanding of speciation.

The authors compare the Biological Species Concept (BSC), ecological species concept, and the genic view of speciation. A central insight is that microbes challenge traditional species concepts due to horizontal gene transfer (HGT), yet species still exist as identifiable genetic and ecological clusters. Genomic islands of speciation in Vibrio and Sulfolobus are presented as case studies, and the authors draw a distinction between gene sweeps (where only specific gene combinations spread selectively) and genome-wide sweeps.

The conclusion is that natural selection is the primary driver of speciation across the tree of life. The Darwinian Threshold concept is invoked to explain the transition between HGT-dominated microbial regimes and sexually reproducing eukaryotes. The authors argue that "fuzzy species" — entities that are partially but not fully isolated — exist universally across the tree of life, supporting a gradualist view of speciation.

The paper provides a clear illustration of how genomic data offer new resolution to speciation research that was impossible with morphological or single-locus approaches.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A key review demonstrating how classical species concepts are being redefined in the genomics era
- Shows that fundamental population genetics principles apply even in microbial worlds dominated by HGT
- Provides a modern framework for understanding speciation as an interplay between gene flow and selection
