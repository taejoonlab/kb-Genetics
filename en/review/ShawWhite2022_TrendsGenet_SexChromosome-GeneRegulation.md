# The evolution of gene regulation on sex chromosomes

## Citation (NLM)
Shaw DE, White MA. The evolution of gene regulation on sex chromosomes. Trends Genet. 2022;38(6):636-650. doi:10.1016/j.tig.2022.01.010

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tig.2022.01.010](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.tig.2022.01.010)

---

## Summary

Most of what we know about sex chromosome degeneration concerns the loss of coding sequences — stop codons, frameshifts, and deletions that render Y-linked genes nonfunctional. Shaw and White (2022) argue that this focus has left a large and important gap: how regulatory sequences evolve on sex chromosomes, and what role regulatory changes play in the loss and gain of gene expression on the Y chromosome. Their review integrates theoretical models with empirical findings to synthesize a view of sex chromosome evolution in which regulatory evolution is not a secondary effect but a primary driver of gametolog expression divergence.

The review begins by noting that transcriptome studies have consistently shown Y-linked gametologs are downregulated across diverse taxa — in mammals, birds, Drosophila, fish, and plants. Strikingly, studies of young sex chromosomes in Rumex, D. albomicans, and Mercurialis annua reveal that expression differences between X and Y gametologs can emerge before the accumulation of detectable amino acid substitutions in coding regions. This observation — that regulatory divergence precedes coding-sequence degeneration — aligns with a recently proposed model called "degeneration by regulatory evolution" (DRE), developed by Lenormand and Roze. In the DRE model, cis-regulatory mutations accumulate first on the Y chromosome, lowering expression. Reduced expression makes deleterious coding mutations more recessive (because the Y-linked allele is already expressed at low levels), further reducing selection against those mutations. A positive feedback loop ensues: more coding degeneration reduces selection pressure to maintain regulatory function, driving further regulatory decay.

Four mechanisms are identified as contributors to regulatory evolution on sex chromosomes. First, cis-regulatory mutations at promoters and enhancers — changes in transcription factor binding sites that alter chromatin accessibility — can directly reduce transcription of Y-linked gametologs. Second, transposable elements (TEs) accumulate after recombination suppression and can suppress nearby gene expression through the spread of repressive heterochromatin; on the Drosophila miranda neo-Y, TE accumulation is associated with constitutive heterochromatin (marked by H3K9me2/me3) spreading across the chromosome. Third, chromatin accessibility changes — shifts in nucleosome positioning and open chromatin regions — can alter transcription factor access and thereby gene expression without sequence changes. Fourth, DNA methylation at CpG islands in promoters correlates with transcriptional repression; in Silene latifolia, many Y-linked gametologs in the oldest evolutionary strata show higher promoter methylation than their X homologs, often near transposable elements.

The review also addresses gametologs that are retained on the Y chromosome through purifying selection. Genes broadly expressed across multiple tissues — particularly those with haploinsufficiency phenotypes — are independently retained on Y chromosomes across mammals, birds, fish, and Drosophila. These genes are enriched for essential cellular regulatory functions and appear to have maintained regulatory function as well as coding function. An open question is whether ancestral cis-regulatory elements are preserved on these retained Y-linked genes through purifying selection, or whether compensatory evolution of new regulatory elements has occurred. A third fate for Y-linked gametologs is the acquisition of novel, often testis-specific expression patterns — driven either by loss of spermatogenesis function from the X-linked gametolog, or by evolution of new regulatory elements on the Y-linked allele.

Shaw and White highlight the critical bottleneck limiting progress: the paucity of high-quality Y chromosome assemblies. Y chromosomes are highly repetitive, making them resistant to standard short-read assembly approaches. Long-read sequencing combined with Hi-C chromatin contact maps has enabled chromosome-scale Y assemblies in D. miranda and stickleback, and similar approaches are expanding the field. Functional annotation of regulatory regions — using ATAC-seq for chromatin accessibility, ChIP-seq for histone modifications, and Hi-C for enhancer-promoter interactions — will be necessary to move from correlative to causal understanding of regulatory evolution.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Shifts the conceptual focus of sex chromosome degeneration from coding-sequence loss to regulatory evolution, establishing that Y chromosome gene silencing can and does precede coding mutation — a mechanistic priority that reframes how we study early sex chromosome evolution
- Introduces the DRE (degeneration by regulatory evolution) model as a formal theoretical framework for understanding how regulatory changes initiate a cascade leading to Y-gametolog silencing, in the absence of selective interference
- Identifies four concrete molecular mechanisms — cis-regulatory mutations, TE accumulation and heterochromatin spreading, chromatin accessibility changes, and DNA methylation — as contributors to Y-linked gene silencing, connecting sex chromosome research to mainstream epigenomics
- Explains the fate of retained Y-linked genes (purifying selection for haploinsufficient, broadly expressed genes) alongside gained Y-linked genes (testis-specific expression through regulatory neofunctionalization), providing a comprehensive framework for gametolog evolutionary outcomes
- Highlights the sequencing and annotation challenges specific to Y chromosomes and introduces the technological solutions (long-read sequencing, Hi-C, ATAC-seq, ChIP-seq) that are enabling progress

## Future Research Directions

- Direct empirical characterization of regulatory region evolution on Y chromosomes by comparing the rate of substitution in annotated enhancers and promoters on Y versus intergenic neutral regions — to determine whether purifying selection, positive selection for silencing, or neutral drift dominates regulatory evolution
- High-quality Y chromosome assemblies with functional regulatory annotations across diverse taxa (fish, plants, insects) to determine which patterns of regulatory evolution are generalizable versus taxon-specific
- Temporal dissection of regulatory versus coding-sequence degeneration using young sex chromosome systems: can the temporal order of cis-regulatory mutation and amino acid substitution be empirically established?
- Functional validation of specific regulatory elements on retained Y-linked genes using CRISPR editing — do ancestral regulatory elements remain intact, or have compensatory elements evolved to maintain expression?
- Investigation of whether transposable elements introduce novel cis-regulatory elements that rewire Y-linked gene expression patterns, and whether this is a widespread mechanism for testis-specific expression gain on Y chromosomes
