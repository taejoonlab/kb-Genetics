# Shendure & Fields 2016 — Massively Parallel Genetics

## Citation (NLM)
Shendure J, Fields S. Massively parallel genetics. Genetics. 2016;203(2):617-619. doi:10.1534/genetics.115.180562

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.115.180562](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.115.180562)

---

## Summary

This Commentary by Jay Shendure and Stanley Fields argues that observational human genetics must be supplemented with perturbational massively parallel functional assays to interpret the meaning of human genomic variation at scale.

The authors note that GWAS and sequencing alone are insufficient to interpret the functional significance of the vast number of variants discovered in the human genome. They propose a five-step workflow: (1) generate large allelic series, (2) introduce variants into a model system, (3) measure functional effects in multiplexed assays, (4) partition variants by effect size, and (5) calibrate results to human phenotypes.

Concrete applications include interpretation of BRCA1 variants of uncertain significance (VUS) and GWAS fine-mapping. BRCA1 variants are notoriously difficult to classify for pathogenicity without functional data, but massively parallel assays can simultaneously classify thousands of variants. The authors argue this approach can transform variant interpretation from a passive, observational endeavor into an active, experimentally validated one.

This paper served as a conceptual foundation for subsequent approaches such as saturation genome editing, which has been applied to BRCA1 and other disease-associated genes to generate comprehensive variant effect maps.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Introduces a new paradigm for variant interpretation in the post-GWAS era
- Serves as a programmatic call for fusion of functional genomics and human genetics
- Highlights the experimental imperative needed to address the clinical VUS problem
