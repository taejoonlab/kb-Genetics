# Smith & Wood 2016 — Teaching Genetics: Past, Present, and Future

## Citation (NLM)
Smith MK, Wood WB. Teaching genetics: past, present, and future. Genetics. 2016;204(1):5-10. doi:10.1534/genetics.116.187138

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.187138](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.187138)

## Summary

This Commentary by Michelle K. Smith and William B. Wood surveys 100 years of genetics education, examining the history of teaching practices, present-day evidence-based methodologies, and future challenges.

Past genetics instruction was primarily lecture-based, centered on Mendelian inheritance patterns, and is noted to have had early connections to eugenics. Current best practices include active learning techniques (clicker questions with peer discussion), backward course design, and concept inventories. Freeman et al. (2014), a landmark meta-analysis, demonstrated that active learning significantly improves student performance relative to traditional lecturing.

The paper identifies future priorities: measuring and rewarding teaching excellence, developing better approaches to teaching complex quantitative traits, implementing constructed-response assessments, and expanding Course-based Undergraduate Research Experiences (CUREs). The GSA Genetics Learning Framework is also presented as a resource for curriculum alignment.

The authors call on genetics educators to apply evidence-based pedagogy and to continuously update both course content and instructional methods in a rapidly evolving field.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides historical context for understanding the evolution of genetics teaching and the evidence base for current classroom methods
- Introduces the empirical rationale for active learning (Freeman et al. 2014), supporting evidence-based pedagogical choices
- Highlights the positive impact of CUREs on student research engagement and learning outcomes
