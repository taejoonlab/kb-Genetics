# Early days of gel electrophoresis

## Citation (NLM)
Smithies O. Early days of gel electrophoresis. Genetics. 1995;139(1):1-4.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/139/1/1](https://genetics.org/content/139/1/1)

---

## Summary

In this "Perspectives" memoir, Oliver Smithies recounts, on the fortieth anniversary of starch-gel electrophoresis, how he invented the technique in Toronto in 1954. He opens by contrasting the state of the art: modern high-resolution gels detect about 0.2 microgram of silver-stained protein, whereas in the early 1950s the Tiselius moving-boundary apparatus in which he trained (in Jack Williams' physical-chemistry lab at Wisconsin) required roughly a million times more material — 0.2 gram — and gave results too poor to follow protein purification. This experience convinced Smithies that he needed a better, lower-material assay.

Moving to the Connaught Laboratories at the University of Toronto to search (unsuccessfully) for an insulin precursor, Smithies turned to zone electrophoresis, which separates a mixture into discrete zones ([A]+[B]+[C]) rather than the overlapping boundaries of the moving-boundary method, and used far less material. Filter-paper electrophoresis failed for insulin, which absorbed to the paper and smeared. On a visit to Andrew Sass-Kortsak's lab he saw starch-*grain* electrophoresis, free of absorption problems but requiring tedious chemical assays of dozens of starch slices. The decisive insight came from a childhood memory of helping his mother with laundry: the starch used to stiffen shirts was liquid when hot and set to a jelly when cold. Smithies reasoned that a *cooked, cooled starch gel* could let proteins migrate and then be detected by staining. On Saturday, January 23rd, 1954, he cooked "Starch according to Lintner" (~15 g/100 ml), poured a gel, and ran purified insulin at 220 V — his notebook reads "Result v. promising."

Two months later he ran serum "just for rough test" and was astonished to resolve "-11 components!" where the field then spoke of only five serum proteins. Abandoning the insulin project, he spent seven months refining the method, using his own serum and that of graduate-student friends Gordon Dixon and George Connell. The pivotal genetic discovery came on October 26th, when serum from a technician, Beth Wade, showed "extra components." Smithies first suspected a sex difference, then a mix-up ("must have muddled sera"), but finally recognized genuine *hereditary* differences — later shown to reside in the plasma protein haptoglobin. His 1955 paper noted "Hereditary factors may determine the serum groups," launching the field of protein polymorphisms, developed with geneticist Norma Ford Walker.

Smithies closes by tracing the intellectual continuity of his career. Comparing starch-gel and filter-paper runs (with M. David Poulik) revealed that starch gels resolve proteins by molecular sieving — the pore size approaches molecular dimensions — which inspired two-dimensional electrophoresis. Working out the molecular genetics of haptoglobins led him, prompted by Jim Crow's pointer to the Bar locus in Drosophila (unequal crossing over and gene duplication; Sturtevant 1925, Bridges 1936), to explain haptoglobin's "extra components" via homologous and non-homologous recombination and gene duplication. He notes that these same ideas extend to modern gene targeting — including his own then-recent work using homologous recombination to duplicate genes in mice — underscoring a continuity of thought from starch gels to gene targeting.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Chronicles the invention of a technique — gel electrophoresis — that underlies vast swaths of molecular genetics, from protein polymorphisms to DNA analysis, giving students a concrete origin story for a tool they will use.
- Directly links a *method* to a *conceptual discovery*: the technique's resolving power revealed haptoglobin protein polymorphism, effectively founding the study of molecular genetic variation in human populations.
- Models how observation, serendipity (the laundry-starch memory), and careful follow-up ("must have muddled sera" → real hereditary variation) drive discovery.
- Connects electrophoretic detection of variation to enduring genetic concepts — unequal crossing over, gene duplication, and homologous recombination — bridging classical Drosophila genetics and modern gene targeting.

## Key References

1. **Smithies (1955)** — the paper introducing starch-gel electrophoresis and first noting hereditary serum-protein groups.
2. **Smithies & Walker (1955)** — genetic control of serum proteins in humans; foundational for protein polymorphism studies.
3. **Poulik & Smithies (1958)** — comparison of starch-gel and filter-paper methods, revealing the molecular-sieving basis of resolution and prompting 2-D electrophoresis.
4. **Sturtevant (1925); Bridges (1936)** — the Bar-locus work on unequal crossing over and gene duplication that guided Smithies' interpretation of haptoglobin variation.
5. **Smithies & Kim (1994)** — targeted gene duplication in mice, showing the continuity from electrophoretic genetics to gene targeting.

## Future Research Directions

- The line from resolving ~11 serum components to modern two-dimensional gels resolving 750+ spots foreshadows quantitative proteomics and its use in mapping population-level protein variation.
- Smithies' bridge from starch gels to homologous-recombination-based gene targeting anticipates the reverse-genetics toolkit (knockouts, targeted duplications) for dissecting quantitative genetic traits.
