# Spradling 2006 — Learning the Common Language of Genetics

## Citation (NLM)

**DOI:** [https://academic.oup.com/genetics/article/174/1/1/6060957](https://academic.oup.com/genetics/article/174/1/1/6060957)

## Summary

This Perspectives article by Allan C. Spradling was written on the occasion of the 75th anniversary of the Genetics Society of America (GSA) and offers a critical reflection on the state of genetics research and its future direction.

Spradling identifies four revolutionary eras of genetics: classical genetics, molecular genetics, molecular cloning, and current genomics. He critiques the present situation, describing the genetics community as a collection of "tribes" speaking different model organism languages (mouse, Drosophila, C. elegans, etc.) with no common vocabulary.

He particularly criticizes genomicists for focusing on single-cell data while ignoring multicellular biology — a bias he terms "single-cell genomics." He argues that learning the common language of genetics requires understanding supracellular modules — the basic building blocks of tissues such as crypts, islets, follicles, and niches — operating through conserved cell types and multicellular architectures.

Spradling defends individual creativity over centralized research planning, arguing that the most significant future innovations will come from individual investigators pursuing independent ideas. He characterizes the present as "one more garden variety, ho-hum revolution," emphasizing that the vast majority of biological knowledge remains to be discovered.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- An insightful critique of reductionist biases in the genomics era, calling for multilevel biological approaches
- Reaffirms the value of model organisms while emphasizing the need to integrate multicellular biology with genomics
- Provides historical perspective on genetics research direction, contextualizing current research trends
