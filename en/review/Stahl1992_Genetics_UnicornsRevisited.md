# Unicorns Revisited

## Citation (NLM)
Stahl FW. Unicorns revisited. Genetics. 1992;132(4):865-867.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/132/4/865](https://genetics.org/content/132/4/865)

---

## Summary

In this short, witty Perspectives commentary, Franklin W. Stahl revisits the controversy over "directed" (or "adaptive") mutation — the claim by Cairns, Overbaugh, and Miller (1988) that some bacterial mutants arise as a *specific, adaptive response* to the selective environment. The phenomenon appeared to violate the central tenet of neo-Darwinian genetics: that mutations are random with respect to their usefulness. The mutants were "adaptive" because they let the cell grow under selection, and "specific" because irrelevant mutants did not accumulate. Stahl frames the whole debate through his earlier "unicorn" metaphor — a nod to skeptic James Randi's rhetorical challenge ("what would you do if I said 'I keep a unicorn in my back yard'?") — arguing that the right response to an extraordinary but not physically impossible claim is to "climb over the fence and have a look," not to dismiss it outright.

Stahl uses the essay to evaluate Foster and Cairns's (1992) new experiments, which tested (and dismantled) several mechanistic models for directed mutation rather than establishing a definitive mechanism. First to fall was the "anti-Central-Dogma" model, in which a successful protein feeds information back to the gene, immortalizing a propitious transcription error as a DNA mutation. This is neatly refuted by the observation that directed revertants of a *lac* amber mutant include not only true revertants but also tRNA suppressor mutants — which could not have arisen by any protein-to-DNA feedback. A second model, that transcription itself is mutagenic (with lactose inducing *lac* transcription), is undermined by the same tRNA suppressors and by the finding that the gratuitous inducer IPTG does not by itself cause accumulation of Lac+ cells.

Stahl candidly reports the collapse of his own model too. He had proposed (Stahl 1988) that repair synthesis in stationary-phase cells generates mutations, with slow postreplicational mismatch repair eventually erasing irrelevant ones — unless a mutation lets the cell escape its metabolic bind and replicate, fixing the change before repair. Foster and Cairns showed that the mismatch repair (Mut) system, and the alkylation repair pathway, are *not* responsible for the selective disappearance of irrelevant mutations. "I was less pleased to see my model crash," Stahl admits.

The essay then scrutinizes the gene-amplification model that Foster and Cairns offered to accommodate two observations: that reversion is reduced in a *recA* strain, and that reversion depends on DNA replication. Stahl argues that a simple *tandem duplication* model cannot work well, because it produces only a twofold bias toward beneficial mutants (or requires implausibly many tandem copies to yield the observed 20-fold effect, with no selective advantage to build such an array). He instead sketches his own alternative — the "Toe in the Water Model" — based on RecA-dependent stable DNA replication (D-loops): in starving cells, short abortive DNA synthesis is normally expelled and degraded, but if a life-saving mutation arises during that synthesis, it enables the D-loop to mature into a full replication fork, saving the beneficial mutation. Stahl notes this model explains most, though not all, of the data (it cannot account for Hall's 1991 correlated double-reversion, which Stahl suggests is best rejected for lack of controls). He closes by cautioning that the failure to provide a proven or even attractive mechanism means Foster and Cairns's work will not silence blindly skeptical critics.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- A vivid case study in how genetics polices its foundational assumption — that mutation is random with respect to fitness — and how extraordinary claims are tested rather than merely accepted or rejected.
- Models the scientific virtue of intellectual honesty: Stahl publicly reports the refutation of his own hypothesis alongside others'.
- Introduces core molecular-genetics concepts (reversion, suppressors, mismatch repair, RecA/recombination, gene amplification, stable DNA replication) in the context of a real controversy.
- The "adaptive mutation" debate anticipates later, better-understood mechanisms (stress-induced mutagenesis, amplification), showing how a provocative claim can drive productive research.

## Future Research Directions

- The mechanism of stress-induced / adaptive mutagenesis in stationary-phase bacteria — later clarified through amplification, error-prone repair, and the RpoS stress response — remains a model for how environment modulates (without "directing") mutation rates.
- Stahl's emphasis on RecA-dependent replication and recombination foreshadows modern work on how DNA repair and replication fidelity shape the mutational spectrum under stress.
