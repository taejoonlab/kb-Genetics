## Summary

This CLASSIC primer by Rasmus Nielsen reviews the impact of Fumio Tajima's landmark 1983 paper on modern population genetics.

Nielsen describes coalescence theory as arguably one of the most important theoretical discoveries in all of biology over the past 50 years. While Kingman (1982) independently discovered the coalescent, graduate students Tajima and Hudson were simultaneously developing coalescence theory independently.

Tajima (1983) made two key contributions: (1) derived statistical properties of a sample using coalescence theory, including the mean and variance of time to most recent common ancestor (MRCA); and (2) derived the variance of the average number of pairwise differences (π), introducing what became known as "Tajima's estimator" of the mutation-scaled effective population size. The paper also laid the mathematical groundwork for incomplete lineage sorting (ILS), opening the interface between population genetics and phylogenetics.

This paper is recognized as a founding work of modern population genetics and remains required reading for any graduate student entering the field.
