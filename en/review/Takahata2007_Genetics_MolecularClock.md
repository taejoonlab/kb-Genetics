# Molecular clock: an anti-neo-Darwinian legacy

## Citation (NLM)
Takahata N. Molecular clock: an anti-neo-Darwinian legacy. Genetics. 2007;176(1):1-6.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/176/1/1](https://genetics.org/content/176/1/1)

---

## Summary

Naoyuki Takahata's "Perspectives" essay traces the history of the molecular clock and argues that it constitutes an "anti-neo-Darwinian legacy" — evidence that at the molecular level evolution is governed largely by mutation and near-neutrality rather than by the creative power of natural selection central to neo-Darwinism. He begins with early roots: Nuttall's 1904 immunological measurements of serum relatedness across mammals, refined into immunological clocks used to reconstruct primate phylogenies (Goodman 1962). In 1962, Zuckerkandl and Pauling calibrated amino acid substitution rates in mammalian hemoglobins and estimated divergence times, and by 1965 advanced the concept of a molecular evolutionary clock, describing its stochastic nature with a Poisson process. Crucially, they recognized that for a clock to exist, amino acid changes must be almost exclusively near-neutral — an insight that supported the idea of near-neutrality at the molecular level.

Takahata then situates the clock as a *key input* to Motoo Kimura's neutral theory. Contrary to the common belief that electrophoretic polymorphism drove the theory, he argues the high evolutionary rate estimated from hemoglobin was decisive: Kimura's 1968 paper begins with Zuckerkandl and Pauling's substitution rate, extrapolates it genome-wide (using Muller's 1958 base-pair estimate), and finds the resulting rate too high to be accommodated by Haldane's "cost of natural selection." King and Jukes (1969), in their provocatively titled "Non-Darwinian Evolution," reinforced the argument while noting that non-coding DNA might inflate Kimura's estimates. Over the following years Kimura shifted his emphasis: rather than the *high* rate, the remarkable *constancy* of the substitution rate for each protein became, in his view, the strongest evidence for neutrality (Kimura and Ohta 1971a). Selective constraint — "the more stringent the functional requirement, the slower the evolutionary change" — became a vital concept, ultimately confirmed by rapid substitution at synonymous sites and in pseudogenes.

A large portion of the essay concerns the accuracy of the clock and its many complications. Debates over an immunological "hominoid slowdown" (Goodman) versus a recent human–ape ancestor (Sarich and Wilson) led to the invention of the relative-rate test, which avoids reliance on debatable fossil dates. Early protein-clock work showed that the variance of substitution numbers exceeds Poisson expectations (overdispersion), prompting Gillespie's argument for an "episodic clock" with bursts of rapid change, and mechanistic explanations invoking site interdependence or fixation of slightly deleterious mutations (Ohta). Takahata distinguishes *lineage effects* (systematic mutation-rate variation among lineages, strong for silent substitutions and tied to replication-dependent, generation-time effects) from *residual effects* (rate variation among proteins within branches, strong for amino acid substitutions). Wu and Li (1985) first applied the relative-rate test to DNA, finding rodents evolve about twice as fast as humans. Modern genomic analyses (CpG hypervariability, body-size and temperature dependence, human–chimp comparisons) show the clock "ticks erratically," yet it remains widely used because molecule-specific rate differences average out when many loci are combined.

Takahata's deeper thesis concerns the link between molecular and phenotypic evolution — a question raised by Zuckerkandl and Pauling and pursued in different ways. King and Wilson (1975) emphasized regulatory mutations for species differences; Kimura (1983, 1990) instead demanded a *bifurcation* between selection-dominated phenotypic evolution and drift-dominated molecular evolution, proposing a four-stage macroevolution scenario in which neutral variation, released by relaxed constraint, becomes advantageous when environments change. A separate tradition — Masatoshi Nei's "mutationism," reviving Thomas Hunt Morgan — treats mutation (especially gene duplication and birth-and-death) and recombination as the main driving force of both molecular and phenotypic evolution, with selection as editor rather than composer. Takahata judges that mutationism likely prevails over long-term evolution (e.g., the origin of adaptive immunity or tissue mineralization in the vertebrate lineage), while short-term phenotypic change (such as human traits, perhaps at loci like ASPM or via Hsp90 as a "capacitor") is more diverse and opportunistic. He concludes that phenotypic evolution resists a single rule, but that at the DNA and much of the protein level a simpler, mutation-dominated rule prevails — the molecular clock being "a most remarkable manifestation and a tribute from nature" to students of evolution.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides a historically grounded introduction to the neutral theory and the molecular clock, two pillars of molecular population genetics that students must understand to interpret sequence divergence and phylogenetic dating.
- Clarifies the logic connecting rate *constancy* to *neutrality*, and the concept of selective constraint (functionally important molecules evolve more slowly) — core ideas for reading dN/dS and conservation analyses.
- Frames a foundational debate — selectionism (neo-Darwinism) vs. neutralism/mutationism — that recurs throughout modern genomics.
- Introduces essential methodological tools (Poisson clock, relative-rate test, overdispersion/R-statistic, lineage vs. residual effects) that students meet in molecular evolution coursework.

## Key References

1. **Zuckerkandl & Pauling (1962, 1965)** — proposed the molecular evolutionary clock and its stochastic (Poisson) nature; the conceptual origin point.
2. **Kimura (1968); King & Jukes (1969)** — the neutral theory and "Non-Darwinian Evolution," using molecular rates to challenge pan-selectionism.
3. **Kimura & Ohta (1971a, 1973)** — reframed rate *constancy* and selective constraint as the strongest evidence for neutrality.
4. **Sarich & Wilson (1973)** — the relative-rate test, a fossil-independent method for testing clock behavior.
5. **Gillespie (1984, 1991)** — the overdispersed / episodic clock and the R-statistic, defending a selectionist mechanism.
6. **Nei (1983, 1987, 2005)** — mutationism, reviving Morgan and casting mutation (including gene duplication) as the main driving force.

## Future Research Directions

- Connecting the molecular clock to species-specific life-history traits (generation time, body size, temperature) and to direct, sequencing-based measurements of spontaneous mutation rate and spectrum in mutation-accumulation lines.
- Resolving the long-standing link between molecular and phenotypic evolution — testing whether regulatory-mutation, Kimura's four-stage, or Nei-style mutationist scenarios best explain major phenotypic innovations.
