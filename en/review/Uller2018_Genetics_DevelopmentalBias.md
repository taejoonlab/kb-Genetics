# Uller et al. 2018 — Developmental Bias and Evolution: A Regulatory Network Perspective

## Citation (NLM)
Uller T, Moczek AP, Watson RA, Brakefield PM, Laland KN. Developmental bias and evolution: a regulatory network perspective. Genetics. 2018;209(4):949-966. doi:10.1534/genetics.118.300995

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.118.300995](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.118.300995)

---

## Summary

This Perspectives article by Tobias Uller and four co-authors argues, from a regulatory network perspective, that developmental bias is not merely a constraint but can actively contribute to the direction of evolution.

Developmental bias is defined as the tendency of developmental systems to produce some phenotypic variants more readily than others due to the system's structure, composition, or dynamics. The authors move beyond the traditional equation of bias with constraint, demonstrating that developmental bias can facilitate evolutionary adaptation. Examples include the fixed seven cervical vertebrae in mammals, computational modeling of mammalian tooth morphology, and mutation accumulation studies in Drosophila wing shape.

From a regulatory network perspective, the key insight is that neutral genotype networks (sets of topologically different networks with the same phenotype) enable populations to explore phenotype space without fitness cost. Directional correlational selection can bias the M matrix (distribution of mutational effects) toward directions aligned with the fitness landscape, meaning that even undirected mutations can produce phenotypic variation biased toward adaptive outcomes.

The paper concludes that developmental bias influences both the rate and direction of evolution and can explain patterns in biodiversity distribution. This is a central theme of the Extended Evolutionary Synthesis.

## Future Research Directions

- Methodology to track the evolution of developmental regulatory networks using single-cell resolution data
