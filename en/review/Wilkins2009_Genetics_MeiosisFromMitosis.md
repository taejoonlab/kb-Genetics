# Wilkins & Holliday 2009 — The Evolution of Meiosis From Mitosis

## Citation (NLM)
Wilkins AS, Holliday R. The evolution of meiosis from mitosis. Genetics. 2009;181(1):3-12. doi:10.1534/genetics.108.099762

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.108.099762](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.108.099762)

---

## Summary

This Perspectives article by Adam S. Wilkins and Robin Holliday presents a hypothesis for how meiosis evolved from mitosis.

Meiosis differs from mitosis by four distinctive steps: (1) homologous chromosome pairing (synapsis), (2) extensive recombination between non-sister chromatids during pairing, (3) suppression of sister-chromatid separation in meiosis I, and (4) absence of chromosome replication before meiosis II. The simultaneous appearance of all four steps is extremely difficult to explain evolutionarily.

The central argument is that the evolutionary origin of meiosis involved the insertion of a single new step — stable genome-wide homolog synapsis — into the mitotic cycle. The remaining three distinctive features follow as automatic consequences of existing mitotic regulatory mechanisms. More surprisingly, the authors argue that the initial selective pressure for synapsis was not to enhance recombination, but to restrict ectopic recombination (between non-identical sequences at different chromosomal locations), thereby improving the fidelity of genome transmission.

As early eukaryotic genomes grew in size and repetitive sequences accumulated, ectopic recombination became increasingly hazardous. Homolog synapsis provided a solution by ensuring recombination occurs only between fully matched homologous sequences. The proposed hypothesis is experimentally testable in transgenic yeast by inducing rec8 and Dmc1 activities in dividing diploid cells.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Provides a compelling stepwise hypothesis for how the complexity of meiosis could evolve gradually
- Argues that sex and meiosis can be evolutionarily dissociated, with parameiosis preceding true sexual reproduction
- Offers a new framework viewing recombination regulation — rather than recombination enhancement — as the primary selective force
