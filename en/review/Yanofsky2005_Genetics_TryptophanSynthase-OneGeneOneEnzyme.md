# The Favorable Features of Tryptophan Synthase for Proving Beadle and Tatum's One Gene–One Enzyme Hypothesis

## Citation (NLM)
Yanofsky C. The favorable features of tryptophan synthase for proving Beadle and Tatum's one gene–one enzyme hypothesis. Genetics. 2005;169(2):511-516.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/169/2/511](https://genetics.org/content/169/2/511)

---

## Summary

In this GENETICS "Perspectives" memoir, Charles Yanofsky recounts how his study of the enzyme tryptophan synthase became the vehicle for proving—and extending—Beadle and Tatum's one gene–one enzyme hypothesis into the principle of gene–protein colinearity. He begins by explaining why Beadle and Tatum's 1941 proposal, though a major conceptual advance, could not initially be proven: in the 1940s almost nothing was known about the chemical nature of the gene, DNA was widely thought too repetitive to encode diverse proteins, and it was not even firmly established that polypeptides are linear sequences of amino acids. Beadle himself recalled that by the 1951 Cold Spring Harbor Symposium, the faithful "could be counted on the fingers of one hand—with a couple of fingers left over." A cascade of 1950s discoveries changed this: Avery et al. (DNA as transforming principle), Hershey and Chase (phage DNA as genetic material), Watson and Crick (the double helix), Meselson and Stahl (semiconservative replication), Sanger (the linear sequence of insulin), Benzer (fine-structure genetic mapping), and Ingram (the single amino-acid change in sickle-cell hemoglobin). Together these redefined the question as the gene–protein colinearity hypothesis.

Yanofsky traces his own path through this problem. As a graduate student at Yale (1948–1951) under David Bonner, an alumnus of the Beadle/Tatum group, he began working on the gene–enzyme relationship using the mold *Neurospora* and its tryptophan desmolase (later tryptophan synthase). With Suskind and Bonner he found that mutants defective in this enzyme fell into two classes—those producing an immunologically cross-reacting material (CRM), presumably an inactive form of the enzyme protein, and those producing none. The CRM concept was pivotal: it implied that mutations could leave a detectable but inactive protein whose specific amino-acid alteration could in principle be identified. Studies of suppressed mutants further led Yanofsky, before mRNA or tRNA were known, to conclude that suppression permits mistranslation of the unchanged mutant gene rather than altering the gene itself. But his inability to purify the *Neurospora* enzyme convinced him this system was unsuitable for definitive work.

The decisive move was to *E. coli*. After a period at Western Reserve, where he mapped the tryptophan pathway and learned transduction-based fine-structure mapping at Cold Spring Harbor, Yanofsky relocated in 1958 to Stanford—into the very lab space once occupied by Tatum, down the hall from Beadle. There Irving Crawford showed that *E. coli* tryptophan synthase is a complex of two separable polypeptides, TrpA and TrpB, and made the "monumental observation" that each protein's activity in its own partial reaction is enhanced 30- to 100-fold by the other partner. This complementation-in-the-tube provided an enzymatic assay for otherwise inactive missense proteins: a mutant TrpA protein could still be detected by its ability to activate wild-type TrpB. Combined with a system for overproducing mutant TrpA (up to 1–2% of soluble protein) and its acid stability for purification, this made TrpA an ideal test object.

Yanofsky's group then built a fine-structure genetic map of *trpA* using P1kc transduction and deletion mapping (in the manner of Benzer's rII work), while purifying and fingerprinting the corresponding mutant proteins to locate each amino-acid change. By 1962–1964 they had shown that the order of mutational sites on the genetic map matched the order of the altered amino acids in the protein—establishing colinearity—and analyses of paired mutants at a single glycine site demonstrated intracodon recombination, consistent with a multi-nucleotide codon. The complete amino-acid sequence of TrpA was finished in 1967 (then the longest polypeptide sequenced), and the *trpA* nucleotide sequence in 1979 fully confirmed the earlier genetic conclusions. Yanofsky notes that the same colinearity was reached independently and beautifully by Sarabhai, Brenner, Stretton, and Bolle using amber mutants of the phage T4 head protein, and by other groups using alkaline phosphatase and T4 lysozyme—a convergence of imaginative approaches that together nailed down the gene–protein relationship.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- The concrete experimental proof that a gene's linear sequence of sites corresponds, in order, to a protein's linear sequence of amino acids—colinearity—bridging Mendelian genes and molecular protein structure.
- Explains the CRM concept and enzyme complementation, showing how clever assays let geneticists study "invisible" inactive proteins.
- Illustrates the full forward-genetic-to-molecular workflow: mutant isolation, fine-structure mapping, protein fingerprinting, and sequencing, all before routine gene cloning existed.
- A first-person account of the intellectual context (Avery, Hershey-Chase, Watson-Crick, Benzer, Ingram) that turned one gene–one enzyme into one gene–one polypeptide.

## Key References

1. **Beadle & Tatum (1941)** — "Genetic control of biochemical reactions in Neurospora" — the original one gene–one enzyme hypothesis this work sought to prove.
2. **Crawford & Yanofsky (1958)** — separation of *E. coli* tryptophan synthetase into two components — established the two-polypeptide complex and complementation assay.
3. **Yanofsky et al. (1964)** — "On the colinearity of gene structure and protein structure" (PNAS) — the central demonstration of colinearity.
4. **Sarabhai, Stretton, Brenner & Bolle (1964)** — "Co-linearity of the gene with the polypeptide chain" (Nature) — independent proof using T4 head-protein amber mutants.
5. **Benzer (1957, 1959)** — fine-structure genetic mapping — the methodological precedent for mapping mutational sites within a gene.

## Future Research Directions

- Colinearity underpins all modern reverse genetics: predicting protein sequence from DNA sequence, engineering point mutations, and interpreting missense variants in genomic medicine.
- The tryptophan synthase complex remains a model for enzyme structure–function and channeling studies (its 3D structure and intersubunit tunnel), linking classical genetics to structural and mechanistic enzymology.
