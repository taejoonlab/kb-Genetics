# Forty Years Ago: The Discovery of Bacterial Transduction

## Citation (NLM)
Zinder ND. Forty years ago: the discovery of bacterial transduction. Genetics. 1992;132(2):291-294.

**DOI:** [https://genetics.org/content/132/2/291](https://genetics.org/content/132/2/291)

---

## Summary

In this Perspectives commentary, Norton Zinder reconstructs from his laboratory notebooks the discovery of bacterial transduction, published forty years earlier as Zinder and Lederberg (1952). The work was done in Joshua Lederberg's laboratory at the University of Wisconsin, where Zinder — then only 22 years old — set out to extend Lederberg's discovery of bacterial conjugation to *Salmonella typhimurium*. Using the newly developed penicillin enrichment technique for isolating auxotrophic mutants, he marked a panel of phage-typed *S. typhimurium* strains with pairs of non-overlapping double mutations (mostly amino-acid requirements) so that recombination could be scored as the appearance of prototrophic colonies on minimal medium.

All within-strain (self) crosses failed to yield prototrophs. Out of desperation, Zinder began crossing different strains — a 20 × 20 set — and on October 5, 1950 a cross between the Lilleengen strains LT-2 and LT-22 produced recombinants at a frequency even higher than seen in *E. coli* K-12 conjugation. Careful analysis revealed the phenomenon was profoundly different from conjugation: it was asymmetric, transferring only one genetic marker at a time, and all recovered markers were those of the LT-22 parent. A U-tube experiment (patterned on Bernard Davis's test that had ruled out a filterable agent in *E. coli* conjugation) showed the opposite result for *Salmonella*: a filterable agent, dubbed "FA," crossed the sintered-glass filter and converted LT-22 mutants to prototrophy without cell-to-cell contact. The agent was eventually understood to be a temperate phage (PLT-22, now P22) carried by LT-22 that could grow on LT-2 and carry back bacterial genes.

Zinder emphasizes how much serendipity the discovery required: the original LT-22 markers happened to be linked genes in aromatic amino-acid biosynthesis that could cotransduce, and LT-22 happened to carry a lysogenic transducing phage able to propagate on LT-2. Progress was initially confused by the then-popular but mistaken notion of "L-forms" (filterable bacterial forms). The turning point came at the 1951 Cold Spring Harbor Symposium — a meeting Zinder describes as capturing genetics "in a prerevolutionary crisis," with Goldschmidt denying the gene's existence and McClintock's transposition work being discussed — where Harriett Ephrussi-Taylor's suggestion that transduction was due to phage carrying DNA gave Zinder a clear experimental program.

Returning to the bench, Zinder established the genetic nature of transduction: the transducing potential of any phage preparation reflected the genotype (not phenotype) of the last host on which it grew; there was no mutant/wild-type directionality (shown with recessive streptomycin resistance); and flagellar antigens could be interchanged between *Salmonella* species. All evidence pointed to gene *replacement* on a haploid background rather than gene addition. Physical characterization showed FA and phage co-purified, adsorbed to the same strains (correlating with somatic antigen XII), and shared UV, protease, and nuclease sensitivities — establishing FA as bacterial genetic material packaged in a phage particle. Remarkably, this was all before the Hershey-Chase experiment and the Watson-Crick model. Lederberg proposed the name "transduction," and within a few years it became a classical tool for studying operons and gene fine structure.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Transduction is one of the three classical modes of bacterial gene transfer (alongside transformation and conjugation) — a cornerstone of any introductory genetics unit on prokaryotic genetics and horizontal gene transfer.
- The essay is a vivid case study of how discovery actually happens: how serendipity, mistaken interpretations (L-forms), and the intellectual climate of a field (the pre-DNA "crisis" of genetics) shape a landmark result.
- It illustrates the power of well-designed genetic logic — the U-tube experiment, genotype-vs-phenotype tests, and directionality tests — to characterize an invisible agent before its molecular nature was known.
- Students see how transduction became a workhorse for fine-structure mapping and operon analysis, and how the concept generalized to any virus-mediated gene transfer, including modern recombinant-DNA and viral oncogenesis.

## Key References

1. **Zinder & Lederberg (1952)** — "Genetic exchange in Salmonella," J. Bacteriol. 64:679-699 — the original paper reporting transduction.
2. **Lederberg & Zinder (1948)** — penicillin enrichment for concentrating biochemical mutants — the technique that made mutant panels possible.
3. **Davis (1950)** — U-tube demonstration that *E. coli* K-12 conjugation requires cell contact — the experimental template Zinder adapted to reveal a filterable agent.
4. **Stocker, Zinder & Lederberg (1953)** — transduction of flagellar characters in *Salmonella* — first clear example of linked (cotransduced) markers.
5. **Cold Spring Harbor Symposium (1951)** — the meeting that reoriented Zinder's experimental program.

## Future Research Directions

- Transduction and generalized/specialized transducing phages remain central tools in bacterial genetics and synthetic biology; the essay foreshadows the modern understanding of horizontal gene transfer and mobile genetic elements shaping microbial genomes.
- Zinder's closing observation — that viruses can act "as bacterial genes" — anticipates present-day concerns about phage-mediated transfer of antibiotic-resistance and virulence genes across microbial communities.
