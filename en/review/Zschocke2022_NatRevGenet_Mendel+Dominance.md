# Zschocke et al. 2022 — Gregor Mendel and the Concepts of Dominance and Recessiveness

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41576-022-00495-4](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41576-022-00495-4)

## Summary

This Comment by Johannes Zschocke, Peter H. Byers, and Andrew O.M. Wilkie, written on the occasion of Gregor Mendel's 200th birthday, reflects on the history of the terms "dominant" and "recessive" and their application in modern medical genetics.

Mendel defined dominant and recessive in the 1850s through pea crossbreeding experiments focused on discontinuous traits. Crucially, he defined these terms based on the characteristics of hybrids and made no conceptual distinction between heritable material and manifesting traits. After the 1900 rediscovery of Mendel's laws, de Vries, Correns, Bateson, Sutton, and Johannsen variously extended the concepts but used the terminology inconsistently.

Key challenges in modern medical genetics include: most "dominant disorders" actually show semi-dominant (intermediate) inheritance patterns; the same gene can show different inheritance patterns depending on which variant is present; and in cancer predisposition syndromes, a dominant inheritance of genetic risk coexists with a recessive (second-hit, loss-of-heterozygosity) mechanism at the cellular level.

The authors conclude with a refined definition: dominance and recessiveness are not properties of alleles themselves or of specific phenotypes, but represent the functional link between heterozygous genetic variants and the resulting phenotype in the individual.

## Significance in Introduction Context

- Essential historical review explaining how the core Mendelian terms were originally defined and how they have evolved in meaning
- Provides a conceptual framework for correctly applying "dominant" and "recessive" in the molecular era
- Discusses practical implications for variant classification and genetic counseling in medical genetics
