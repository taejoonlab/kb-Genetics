# The Full Breadth of Mendel's Genetics

## Citation (NLM)
van Dijk PJ, Ellis THN. The full breadth of Mendel's genetics. Genetics. 2016;204(4):1327–1336. doi:10.1534/genetics.116.196626

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.196626](https://doi.org/10.1534/genetics.116.196626)

---

## Summary

Published in *Genetics* to mark the 150th anniversary of Mendel's pea paper, this review by Peter J. van Dijk and T.H. Noel Ellis argues that the standard interpretation of Mendel's Hieracium (hawkweed) experiments as a "frustrating failure" is a serious misreading of Mendel's actual aims and results.

The conventional interpretation holds that Mendel chose Hieracium to verify the segregation ratios he had found in Pisum. Because Hieracium species mostly reproduce via apomixis — clonal seed production bypassing meiosis — the expected Mendelian ratios never materialized. Apomixis in Hieracium was not discovered until 1904 (by Ostenfeld, 20 years after Mendel's death), so this has been presented as an unfortunate coincidence that frustrated Mendel's goals.

The authors argue this interpretation stems from a misreading of Mendel's first letter to Carl Nägeli (December 31, 1866). Examining a facsimile of the original handwritten letter, they show that paragraph 4 ends at the bottom of page 2 and paragraph 5 begins at the top of page 3, with evidence suggesting an entire sheet (one or more pages) is missing between them. Paragraph 4 describes a protocol for testing Pisum findings in other genera; paragraph 5 announces the selection of Hieracium, Cirsium, and Geum for "further experiments." Without the missing page, these paragraphs appear logically connected, but they are not.

The authors' alternative interpretation: Mendel deliberately chose Hieracium to study "constant hybrids" — hybrids whose progeny do not segregate — which are "essentially different" from the "variable hybrids" he had studied in peas. Mendel's pea paper itself explicitly discusses constant hybrids and notes their evolutionary significance ("since constant hybrids acquire the status of new species"). Letter I itself contains a passage where Mendel anticipated that Hieracium might produce constant hybrids, similar to Geum urbanum × rivale.

The two phases of Mendel's Hieracium research are reconstructed: Phase 1 (1866–1869) successfully produced artificial constant hybrids that bred true through successive generations — a vindication, not a failure. Phase 2 involved the unexpected discovery that multiple distinct types of constant F1 hybrids could form from the same cross, which puzzled Mendel and demanded more work than his duties as Abbot permitted. His final letter to Nägeli reflects not frustration with failure but regret that administrative duties prevented him from completing the necessary follow-up experiments.

The famous statement "My time will come" was reportedly said by Mendel in his garden, among his Hieracium and Cirsium plants — an entirely appropriate location given the reinterpretation presented here.

This paper was recognized by Nasmyth (2022) as providing a more accurate account of Mendel's Hieracium work. Mendel was above all a geneticist interested in both inheritance mechanisms and species evolution, and his research program was broader than the pea experiments alone.

## Significance in Introduction Context

Why this paper matters at the introduction to genetics:
- Challenges a century-old misconception about Mendel's research, demonstrating that scientific history is always subject to revision when new evidence or careful reanalysis emerges
- Reveals that Mendel's conception of heredity encompassed both variable (sexual/meiotic) and constant (asexual/apomictic) hybrids, giving his work a broader evolutionary context than usually taught
- Essential course reading because understanding what Mendel actually intended — not just what his laws state — clarifies how genetics connects to evolution, speciation, and reproductive biology

## Future Research Directions

Unresolved questions and future research directions from this paper:

- Elucidating the molecular mechanisms of apomixis in Hieracium and related species, including how it is genetically controlled and heritable
- Engineering apomixis into important F1 crop hybrids to fix hybrid vigor in seeds — considered a "holy grail" of plant breeding, still not achieved
- Further recovery and analysis of historical documents from Mendel's era (monastery archives, correspondence fragments) to reconstruct a more complete picture of his scientific thinking and experimental progress
