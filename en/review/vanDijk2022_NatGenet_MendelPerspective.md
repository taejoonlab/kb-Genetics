# How Did Mendel Arrive at His Discoveries?

## Citation (NLM)
van Dijk PJ, Jessop AP, Ellis THN. How did Mendel arrive at his discoveries? Nat Genet. 2022;54(7):926–933. doi:10.1038/s41588-022-01109-9

**DOI:** [https://doi.org/10.1038/s41588-022-01109-9](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41588-022-01109-9)

---

## Summary

Published in *Nature Genetics* for the bicentenary of Mendel's birth, this Perspective by van Dijk, Jessop, and Ellis presents newly discovered historical evidence that offers a fresh picture of how Mendel arrived at his discoveries. The authors critically examine three competing theories: (1) Fisher's claim that Mendel had a fully formed prior theory; (2) Olby's argument that Mendel was not interested in inheritance but rather in speciation from hybrids; and (3) the standard textbook view that Mendel studied inheritance as a fundamental scientific question. They then propose a new scenario supported by recently digitized historical sources.

The key new evidence is a short newspaper article in the Brünn newspaper *Neuigkeiten* from July 1861, which praises Mendel's peas, beans and cucumbers — obtained through artificial fertilization — for their yield and taste. A second local newspaper added that Mendel "approaches the truth in a practical manner." This demonstrates that Mendel simultaneously ran an applied vegetable breeding program and a purely scientific research program.

The proposed new scenario: Beginning in 1854, Mendel ran a vegetable breeding program crossing pea varieties. Seed traits (color and shape) are particularly suited to incidental discovery of 3:1 ratios, because pea seeds represent the F2 generation — the cotyledons of the embryo inside the seed — visible directly on the F1 plant, without requiring an extra growing season. Because roughly 60% of all possible crosses between Mendel's 22 selected varieties would have varied for at least one seed trait, and about 25% for two seed traits simultaneously, it is plausible that Mendel noticed the striking 3:1 ratios in his breeding crosses — a "prepared mind" recognizing a pattern that others, such as Knight, Goss, Seton, Gärtner, Laxton, and Giltay, had all missed.

The role of Mendel's friend Johann Nave (1831–1864) is highlighted as another critical factor. Nave was a civil servant with deep expertise in plant cell biology. In May 1858, Nave lectured to the Scientific Section of the Agricultural Society on reproduction in algae, citing Pringsheim's discovery that fertilization involves "probably one" invading male gamete per egg cell — directly relevant to Mendel's later proposal that a single pollen grain fertilizes each egg cell. Nave instigated the founding of the Natural Science Society in Brünn in 1861 to promote fundamental science, and when he died of tuberculosis in November 1864 (just months before Mendel's famous lectures), his scientific library — containing key works by Pringsheim, Radlkofer, Braun, and Hofmeister on cell theory — was donated to the Society. This cell-biological context provided the intellectual framework for Mendel's gamete hypothesis.

The paper concludes by addressing Fisher's three questions: What did Mendel discover? (The rules of inheritance, emerging from observations made in a plant breeding program.) How did he discover it? (Empirically, triggered by noticing 3:1 ratios in seed traits.) What did he think he had discovered? (The cellular mechanism of how traits are transmitted from parents to offspring, framed in terms of the reproductive cell biology of his time.)

## Significance in Introduction Context

Why this paper matters at the introduction to genetics:
- Demonstrates that applied agricultural research and fundamental scientific discovery are not separate endeavors — Mendel's genetics emerged from a vegetable breeding program
- Illustrates the concept of a "prepared mind" in science: only someone with quantitative training and awareness of cell biology could have recognized and formalized the 3:1 ratios that others had observed but not understood
- Essential course reading because it answers the question of how the foundational principles of genetics were actually discovered — not through armchair theorizing, but through careful empirical observation in a practical context, guided by a deep understanding of both mathematics and biology

## Future Research Directions

Unresolved questions and future research directions from this paper:

- Further discovery and analysis of historical documents (monastery records, Agricultural Society minutes, digitized newspapers) to reconstruct in more detail the timeline linking Mendel's breeding program to his scientific experiments
- Deeper investigation of the intellectual exchange between Mendel and Johann Nave and how cell biological knowledge of the 1850s shaped Mendel's gamete hypothesis
- Analysis of Mendel's annotations to Darwin's The Variation of Animals and Plants Under Domestication — which is known but not fully analyzed — to understand how Mendel engaged with pangenesis theory
