# BME333 / BIO333 — Genetics (UNIST, 2026 Fall)

## Course Objective

Students will learn how genetic information is transmitted, expressed, varied, and analyzed across scales — from single genes to whole genomes and populations — and will be able to apply genetic reasoning and experimental logic (forward and reverse genetics, model organisms, and genomic data) to real-world biological and biomedical problems.

## Course Description

An introduction to the principles of genetics, framed by evolution and illustrated with model organisms and human-genetics applications. The course moves from transmission genetics (Mendelian inheritance and its extensions; the chromosomal basis of heredity and sex determination) through molecular genetics (DNA structure, replication, the central dogma, recombination, mutation, chromosome rearrangement and ploidy, gene regulation and epigenetics), into real-world genetics (population and quantitative genetics, genome annotation and resources). It then covers experimental strategy (forward and reverse genetics, disease modeling), surveys six genetic model organisms (*E. coli*, *C. elegans*, *Drosophila*, mouse, zebrafish, dog), and closes with human genetics (cancer, developmental and metabolic disorders, GWAS, and gene therapy).

**Reference textbooks:** [Genetics: From Genes to Genomes (8e)](ref.Genetics-FromGenesToGenomes.md) · [Evolution: Making Sense of Life (4e)](ref.Evolution-MakeSenseOfLife.md)

## Lecture Details

Objective and description for each lecture; click the title to open the lecture note (learning objectives, ~60-min outline, textbook chapters, and linked vault readings).

### 01 · [Evolutionary Ideas](lec01_Evolutionary-Ideas.md)
*Week 01 Mon (08-31)*

- **Objective:** Understand how evolutionary thinking and the unsolved problem of heredity set the stage for genetics, culminating in the Modern Synthesis.
- **Description:** Pre-Darwinian ideas of heredity (blending, pangenesis, use/disuse); Darwin's descent with modification by natural selection and its missing mechanism of inheritance; modern definitions of adaptation, fitness, and selection; reconciliation of Darwinian selection with Mendelian and population genetics.

### 02 · [Tree of Life, Orthology & Paralogy](lec02_Tree-of-Life-Orthology-Paralogy.md)
*Week 01 Wed (09-02)*

- **Objective:** Read phylogenies and use homology concepts to reconstruct evolutionary history and infer gene function.
- **Description:** Reading and rooting trees and the tree-of-life concept (with horizontal gene transfer and LUCA caveats); homology vs. orthology vs. paralogy; tree building and bootstrap support; dN/dS and branch-site tests of selection; the molecular clock and de novo gene birth.

### 03 · [Mendel's Principles of Heredity](lec03_Mendel-Principles.md)
*Week 02 Mon (09-07)*

- **Objective:** Master the laws of segregation and independent assortment and the particulate theory of inheritance.
- **Description:** Monohybrid and dihybrid crosses (3:1, 9:3:3:1); Punnett squares and probability rules; why Mendel's experimental design was decisive; the historiography (the data-too-good debate, rediscovery); the modern molecular identity of Mendel's pea genes.

### 04 · [Extensions to Mendel's Laws](lec04_Extensions-to-Mendel.md)
*Week 02 Wed (09-09)*

- **Objective:** Explain departures from simple Mendelian ratios through allelic relationships and gene interactions.
- **Description:** Molecular basis of dominance and recessivity; incomplete/co-dominance, multiple and lethal alleles; pleiotropy, penetrance, expressivity, epistasis; gene–gene and gene–environment interactions as a bridge to oligogenic inheritance.

### 05 · [Chromosomes & Sex Determination](lec05_Chromosomes-Sex-Determination.md)
*Week 03 Mon (09-14)*

- **Objective:** Establish the chromosome theory of heredity and survey how sex is genetically determined and how it evolves.
- **Description:** Sutton–Boveri chromosome theory and Bridges' nondisjunction proof; meiosis linked to Mendel's laws; human karyotype and aneuploidy; sex-determination systems (XY, ZW, haplodiploidy, environmental); sex-chromosome origin, degeneration, and dosage compensation.

### 06 · [Central Dogma, DNA Structure & Replication](lec06_Central-Dogma-DNA-Structure-Replication.md)
*Week 03 Wed (09-16)*

- **Objective:** Connect the identity and structure of the genetic material to how it is copied and expressed.
- **Description:** Evidence that DNA is the hereditary material (Avery–MacLeod–McCarty; Hershey–Chase); the double helix and base pairing; semiconservative replication and its machinery; the central dogma (DNA→RNA→protein) and the one gene–one enzyme concept.

### 07 · [Recombination & Linkage](lec07_Recombination-Linkage.md)
*Week 04 Mon (09-21)*

- **Objective:** Use recombination frequency to map genes and understand the molecular basis of crossing over.
- **Description:** Linkage vs. independent assortment; two- and three-point mapping and interference; the double-strand-break-repair mechanism of recombination and hotspots; linkage disequilibrium and association mapping; LOD scores in human pedigrees.

### 08 · [Mutation & Copy Number Variation](lec08_Mutation-CNV.md)
*Week 04 Wed (09-23)*

- **Objective:** Classify mutations and understand their origin, induction, and detection.
- **Description:** Molecular types and functional effects of mutation; the Luria–Delbrück fluctuation test (mutations pre-exist selection); mutagenesis as an experimental tool; the Ames test; copy-number variation and transposable elements as sources of variation and disease.

### 09 · [Rearrangement, Ploidy & Organellar Inheritance](lec09_Rearrangement-Ploidy-Organellar.md)
*Week 05 Wed (09-30)*

- **Objective:** Predict the consequences of chromosomal rearrangements and altered ploidy, and contrast organellar with nuclear inheritance.
- **Description:** Deletions, duplications, inversions, and translocations and their meiotic effects; aneuploidy from nondisjunction; auto- vs. allopolyploidy and the costs and benefits of whole-genome duplication; mitochondrial/chloroplast inheritance (maternal inheritance, heteroplasmy).

### 10 · [Gene Regulation & Epigenetics](lec10_Gene-Regulation-Epigenetics.md)
*Week 06 Wed (10-07)*

- **Objective:** Contrast prokaryotic and eukaryotic gene regulation and understand epigenetic and 3D-genome control.
- **Description:** Operon logic vs. combinatorial enhancer-driven regulation; transcription factors, enhancers, and super-enhancers; 3D genome organization (TADs, chromatin loops, Hi-C); chromatin accessibility, DNA methylation, and transposable-element control in cell identity and disease.

### 11 · [Real World Genetics — Variation in Population](lec11_Population-Variation.md)
*Week 07 Mon (10-12)*

- **Objective:** Apply Hardy–Weinberg and the forces of evolution to measure and interpret population variation.
- **Description:** The Hardy–Weinberg principle and its assumptions; how mutation, migration, drift, and selection perturb allele frequencies; effective population size; heterozygosity, runs of homozygosity, and genomic islands of divergence in real datasets (e.g., the HGDP).

### 12 · [Real World Genetics — Complex Traits & QTL](lec12_Complex-Traits-QTL.md)
*Week 07 Wed (10-14)*

- **Objective:** Explain continuous variation with the polygenic model and map quantitative-trait loci.
- **Description:** The multiple-factor hypothesis reconciling Mendel with continuous variation; partitioning phenotypic variance and heritability; QTL mapping via marker–trait linkage; reading LOD-score QTL scans and the limits of QTL analysis; the bridge to human complex traits.

### 13 · [Real World Genetics — Genome Annotation & Resources](lec13_Genome-Annotation-Resources.md)
*Week 09 Mon (10-26)*

- **Objective:** Understand how genomes are sequenced, assembled, annotated, and searched.
- **Description:** Genome vs. assembly vs. annotation; short-read sequencing and read mapping; ab initio/homology/evidence-based gene annotation; sequence-similarity search (BLAST, dynamic programming, BLOSUM62); public resources (NCBI, Ensembl, UCSC) and the limits of current annotation.

### 14 · [Forward Genetics](lec14_Forward-Genetics.md)
*Week 09 Wed (10-28)*

- **Objective:** Design and interpret phenotype-first genetic screens to discover gene function.
- **Description:** Forward vs. reverse genetics; mutagenesis and screen design; complementation and allelic series; mapping a mutation from classical linkage to whole-genome sequencing; modifier/suppressor screens and the role of model organisms.

### 15 · [Reverse Genetics](lec15_Reverse-Genetics.md)
*Week 10 Mon (11-02)*

- **Objective:** Use gene-first perturbation tools to determine gene function.
- **Description:** Reverse vs. forward genetics; knockouts, knock-ins, RNAi, and CRISPR/Cas; dCas9 fusions for targeted activation and repression; multiplexed/massively parallel screens; interpreting perturbation phenotypes given redundancy and off-target effects.

### 16 · [Genetics in Disease Modeling](lec16_Disease-Modeling.md)
*Week 10 Wed (11-04)*

- **Objective:** Evaluate how model organisms are used to study and dissect human genetic disease.
- **Description:** Why and when model organisms are valid (face, construct, predictive validity); conserved genes and pathways enabling cross-species modeling; modifier screens to find disease genes and therapeutic targets; the strengths and limits of animal models.

### 17 · [Genetic Model — E. coli and Bacteria](lec17_Model-Ecoli-Bacteria.md)
*Week 11 Mon (11-09)*

- **Objective:** See how bacteria and their phages built molecular genetics and the concepts of gene transfer.
- **Description:** Why E. coli became the workhorse; conjugation, transduction, and transformation; interrupted-mating mapping and the circular chromosome; lysogeny and prophage integration (the Campbell model); phage genetics and the fine structure of the gene.

### 18 · [Genetic Model — C. elegans](lec18_Model-Celegans.md)
*Week 11 Wed (11-11)*

- **Objective:** Understand why the worm is a premier metazoan model and what it has revealed.
- **Description:** Brenner's rationale and the invariant cell lineage/connectome; forward screens for development, cell death, and behavior; oocyte maturation and chromosome dynamics; LIN-35/Rb cell-cycle pathways; the worm as a disease-modeling and functional-genomics platform.

### 19 · [Genetic Model — Fruitfly (Drosophila)](lec19_Model-Fruitfly.md)
*Week 12 Mon (11-16)*

- **Objective:** Trace how Drosophila founded chromosomal and developmental genetics.
- **Description:** Morgan's fly room; the white gene and nondisjunction proving the chromosome theory; recombination/linkage maps and meiotic mutants; homeotic genes (Antennapedia/Bithorax) and body-plan control; modern Drosophila disease modeling.

### 20 · [Genetic Model — Mouse](lec20_Model-Mouse.md)
*Week 12 Wed (11-18)*

- **Objective:** Understand the mouse as the premier mammalian model for human biology and disease.
- **Description:** The classical toolkit (inbred strains, coat-color/morphological mutants, linkage/QTL mapping); the molecular revolution (transgenesis, ES cells, gene targeting/knockouts); forward vs. reverse approaches; quantitative-trait and disease-modeling applications.

### 21 · [Genetic Model — Zebrafish](lec21_Model-Zebrafish.md)
*Week 13 Mon (11-23)*

- **Objective:** Understand the zebrafish as a leading vertebrate genetic model for development and disease.
- **Description:** Zebrafish biology (transparent embryos, external fertilization, high fecundity); large-scale ENU and insertional mutagenesis saturation screens; the reference genome and human orthology (~70% of human genes); modern tools and disease modeling. (Note-thin lecture — includes PubMed reading.)

### 22 · [Genetic Model — Dog](lec22_Model-Dog.md)
*Week 13 Wed (11-25)*

- **Objective:** Use the domestic dog as a natural model for the genetics of variation and selection.
- **Description:** Strong artificial selection and breed structure creating long linkage blocks that make GWAS tractable; across-breed mapping; the genetics of body size (a few large-effect loci such as IGF1); relevance to human traits and disease.

### 23 · [Human Genetics — Cancer](lec23_Human-Cancer.md)
*Week 14 Mon (11-30)*

- **Objective:** Understand cancer as a somatic genetic disease driven by mutation accumulation and clonal evolution.
- **Description:** The multistep/clonal-evolution model; oncogenes vs. tumor suppressors and Knudson's two-hit hypothesis; driver vs. passenger mutations and somatic mutation in normal tissue; intratumor heterogeneity as evolution; inherited cancer-predisposition syndromes.

### 24 · [Human Genetics — Developmental & Metabolic Disorder](lec24_Human-Dev-Metabolic-Disorder.md)
*Week 14 Wed (12-02)*

- **Objective:** Connect single-gene defects to metabolic and developmental disease and their clinical management.
- **Description:** One gene–one enzyme from Garrod's inborn errors to modern IEMs; classification of inborn errors and the rationale for newborn screening (PKU); developmental disorders and the role of de novo mutation; modifiers and expressivity; genotype-to-treatment links. (Note-thin lecture — includes PubMed reading.)

### 25 · [Human Genetics — GWAS](lec25_Human-GWAS.md)
*Week 15 Mon (12-07)*

- **Objective:** Understand the logic, interpretation, and limits of genome-wide association studies.
- **Description:** Common-variant association exploiting linkage disequilibrium; Manhattan and QQ plots and genome-wide significance; confounding and QC (population structure); polygenicity; Mendelian randomization for causal inference; polygenic scores, biobanks, and diversity limitations.

### 26 · [Human Genetics — Gene Therapy](lec26_Human-Gene-Therapy.md)
*Week 15 Wed (12-09)*

- **Objective:** Understand gene-therapy modalities, delivery, landmark trials, and challenges.
- **Description:** Gene addition vs. editing, in vivo vs. ex vivo, somatic vs. germline; delivery vehicles (AAV, lentivirus, ex-vivo CRISPR); landmark trials (hemophilia B and RPE65 AAV; sickle-cell/β-thalassemia CRISPR); risks — immunity, insertional mutagenesis, off-target editing, durability, and cost/access. (Note-thin lecture — includes PubMed reading.)
