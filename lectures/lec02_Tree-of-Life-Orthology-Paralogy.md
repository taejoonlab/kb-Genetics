# Tree of Life, Orthology & Paralogy

**Course:** BME333 / BIO333 Genetics (UNIST, 2026 Fall) · Lecture 02 · ~60 min
**Syllabus:** [← Course schedule](2026.BME333-BIO333-Syllabus.md) — Week 01 Wed, 2026-09-02

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:
- Read a phylogenetic tree correctly (nodes, branches, root, topology vs. branch length) and describe the tree of life concept and its limits (horizontal gene transfer, LUCA).
- Distinguish homology, orthology, and paralogy, and explain why the distinction matters for functional inference and genome annotation.
- Explain how trees are built and how node support is assessed with the bootstrap.
- Interpret dN/dS (Ka/Ks) as a signature of purifying, neutral, or positive selection, and describe branch-site tests.
- Explain the molecular clock and the concept of de novo gene birth.

## Lecture Outline (~60 min)
1. **The tree of life** (~10 min) — How Darwin's single figure became a research program; reading trees; why horizontal gene transfer and endosymbiosis make the "tree" a network near its base. LUCA as the inferred root of cellular life.
2. **Homology, orthology, paralogy** (~12 min) — Common ancestry (homology); orthologs (split by speciation) vs. paralogs (split by duplication); the "ortholog conjecture" for transferring functional annotation across genomes (Genetics 8e Ch. 11).
3. **Building trees & node support** (~12 min) — Distance vs. character-based methods overview; the bootstrap as resampling to quantify confidence in a clade; what a bootstrap value does and does not mean.
4. **Selection on sequences: dN/dS** (~12 min) — Synonymous vs. nonsynonymous substitutions; dN/dS < 1 (purifying), ≈ 1 (neutral), > 1 (positive); branch-site tests to detect episodic positive selection at specific codons on specific lineages.
5. **Molecular clock & de novo gene birth** (~10 min) — Rate constancy assumptions and calibration; using the clock to date divergences; how genuinely new genes arise from non-coding sequence.
6. **Synthesis & wrap-up** (~4 min) — Tie phylogenetic thinking to genome annotation and to later population-genetics lectures.
