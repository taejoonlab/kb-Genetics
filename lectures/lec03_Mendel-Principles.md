# Mendel's Principles of Heredity

**Course:** BME333 / BIO333 Genetics (UNIST, 2026 Fall) · Lecture 03 · ~60 min
**Syllabus:** [← Course schedule](2026.BME333-BIO333-Syllabus.md) — Week 02 Mon, 2026-09-07

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:
- State Mendel's Law of Segregation and Law of Independent Assortment and connect them to the particulate theory of inheritance.
- Predict monohybrid and dihybrid cross outcomes (3:1, 9:3:3:1) using Punnett squares and the product/sum rules.
- Explain why Mendel's choice of pea, discrete traits, and quantitative counting made his experiments decisive.
- Discuss the historiography of Mendel: his reasoning, the "too-good-to-be-true" data debate, and his rediscovery.
- Relate Mendel's classical traits to their molecular identities in the modern pea genome.

## Lecture Outline (~60 min)
1. **Why Mendel succeeded** (~10 min) — Garden pea as a model: true-breeding lines, controllable crosses, discrete alternative traits; the decisive move of counting large progeny numbers and reasoning quantitatively (Genetics 8e Ch. 1).
2. **Segregation (first law)** (~12 min) — Monohybrid cross, F1 uniformity, F2 3:1 ratio; dominant vs. recessive alleles; the particulate ("factor") model versus blending inheritance; the testcross.
3. **Independent assortment (second law)** (~12 min) — Dihybrid cross and the 9:3:3:1 ratio; product rule for independent events; when independent assortment holds (and a preview that linkage breaks it).
4. **Probability tools** (~8 min) — Product and sum rules, branch diagrams; predicting genotype and phenotype ratios efficiently.
5. **Mendel in historical & scientific context** (~12 min) — His actual reasoning and hybrid paper; the Fisher "data too good" controversy; Hieracium failure; rediscovery in 1900; Darwin–Mendel contrast.
6. **Mendel's genes today** (~6 min) — Molecular identity of the classic traits and the pea reference genome; Mendel's "missing" traits mapped in 2025.
