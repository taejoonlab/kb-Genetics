# Extensions to Mendel's Laws

**Course:** BME333 / BIO333 Genetics (UNIST, 2026 Fall) · Lecture 04 · ~60 min
**Syllabus:** [← Course schedule](2026.BME333-BIO333-Syllabus.md) — Week 02 Wed, 2026-09-09

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:
- Explain dominance and recessivity at the molecular/biochemical level rather than as intrinsic allele properties.
- Describe incomplete dominance, codominance, multiple alleles, lethal alleles, and how they modify classic Mendelian ratios.
- Define pleiotropy, penetrance, expressivity, and epistasis and give examples of each.
- Explain how gene–gene and gene–environment interactions produce departures from simple Mendelian expectations.
- Connect single-gene "Mendelian" logic to more complex (oligogenic) inheritance as a bridge to human genetics.

## Lecture Outline (~60 min)
1. **Why dominance is not intrinsic** (~12 min) — Dominance/recessivity explained by gene dosage, enzyme kinetics, and haplosufficiency vs. haploinsufficiency; the metabolic-flux view of why most loss-of-function alleles are recessive (Genetics 8e Ch. 2).
2. **Allelic relationships** (~12 min) — Incomplete dominance and codominance (e.g., ABO), multiple allelic series, and lethal alleles; how each alters the observed phenotypic ratio.
3. **One gene, many effects: pleiotropy** (~10 min) — A single gene affecting multiple traits; penetrance and expressivity as sources of variable phenotype among identical genotypes.
4. **Gene interactions: epistasis** (~12 min) — Modified dihybrid ratios (e.g., 9:7, 12:3:1, 9:3:4); interpreting epistasis as pathway logic; complementation.
5. **Environment & real-world traits** (~10 min) — Gene–environment interaction; PTC tasting as a clean example of an allele with an environmental/perceptual readout; beyond single genes toward oligogenic inheritance.
6. **Wrap-up & bridge** (~4 min) — How these extensions still obey Mendel's mechanics, and preview of chromosomes and inheritance.
