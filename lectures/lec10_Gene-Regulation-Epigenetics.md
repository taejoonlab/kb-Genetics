# Gene Regulation & Epigenetics

**Course:** BME333 / BIO333 Genetics (UNIST, 2026 Fall) · Lecture 10 · ~60 min
**Syllabus:** [← Course schedule](2026.BME333-BIO333-Syllabus.md) — Week 06 Wed, 10-07

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:
- Contrast prokaryotic operon logic with the combinatorial, enhancer-driven regulation of eukaryotic genes.
- Explain how transcription factors, enhancers, and super-enhancers control cell-type-specific transcription.
- Describe how 3D genome organization (TADs, chromatin loops, Hi-C) links distant regulatory elements to their target promoters.
- Define epigenetic regulation (chromatin accessibility, DNA methylation, transposable-element control) and explain its role in cell-type identity and disease.

## Lecture Outline (~60 min)
1. **From operons to eukaryotic complexity** (~8 min) — Recap prokaryotic regulation (repressors/activators, inducible vs repressible) as a baseline, then contrast with the modular, multi-input control needed for eukaryotic development.
2. **Transcription factors and enhancers** (~12 min) — How sequence-specific TFs read cis-regulatory DNA; enhancers act at a distance and combinatorially; promoter–enhancer communication.
3. **Super-enhancers and cell identity** (~8 min) — Large enhancer clusters that drive master-regulator and identity genes; why they concentrate regulatory activity.
4. **3D genome architecture** (~13 min) — TADs and chromatin loops constrain enhancer–promoter contacts; Hi-C as the method that revealed genome compartments; the genome as a self-organizing 3D structure.
5. **Chromatin accessibility and methods** (~7 min) — Measuring open chromatin and how accessibility gates TF binding.
6. **Epigenetics: methylation, TEs, and cell types** (~12 min) — Heritable chromatin/methylation states; transposable-element silencing and its failure in disease; how regulatory evolution generates new cell types.

- [Lieberman-Aiden2009_Science_HiC-3DGenome](../en/article/Lieberman-Aiden2009_Science_HiC-3DGenome.md) — The landmark Hi-C paper that made 3D genome folding measurable.
