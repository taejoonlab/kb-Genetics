# Real World Genetics — Complex Traits & QTL

**Course:** BME333 / BIO333 Genetics (UNIST, 2026 Fall) · Lecture 12 · ~60 min
**Syllabus:** [← Course schedule](2026.BME333-BIO333-Syllabus.md) — Week 07 Wed, 10-14

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:
- Explain the polygenic (multiple-factor) model and how it reconciles Mendelian inheritance with continuous variation.
- Partition phenotypic variance into genetic and environmental components and define broad- and narrow-sense heritability.
- Describe the principle of QTL mapping — using marker–trait linkage to localize genes underlying quantitative traits.
- Interpret a LOD-score QTL scan and discuss the strengths and limitations of QTL analysis.

## Lecture Outline (~60 min)
1. **Continuous variation and the multiple-factor hypothesis** (~12 min) — How many genes of small effect plus environment produce a continuous distribution; East's kernel/flower-size experiments as the founding demonstration.
2. **Fisher's reconciliation and heritability** (~12 min) — Fisher 1918 uniting Mendelism with biometry; variance partitioning (VP = VG + VE); broad- vs narrow-sense heritability and why it is a population-specific quantity.
3. **QTL mapping principle** (~14 min) — Crossing divergent lines, genotyping markers, and testing marker–phenotype association; the Lander–Botstein interval-mapping framework and the LOD score.
4. **Reading and interpreting QTL scans** (~12 min) — LOD peaks, confidence intervals, effect sizes, and the "missing" small-effect loci; permutation thresholds.
5. **From QTL to gene, and to humans** (~10 min) — Limitations (linkage resolution, epistasis, GxE); classic mouse QTL and a human single-gene-to-quantitative example (PTC tasting); bridge to GWAS.
