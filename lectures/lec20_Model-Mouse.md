# Genetic Model — Mouse

**Course:** BME333 / BIO333 Genetics (UNIST, 2026 Fall) · Lecture 20 · ~60 min
**Syllabus:** [← Course schedule](2026.BME333-BIO333-Syllabus.md) — Week 12 Wed, 2026-11-18

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:
- Explain why the mouse is the premier mammalian genetic model and its relevance to human biology and disease.
- Describe the classical mouse-genetics toolkit: inbred strains, coat-color and morphological mutants, and early linkage/QTL mapping.
- Explain how molecular genetics transformed the mouse (transgenesis, ES cells, gene targeting/knockouts).
- Distinguish forward (mutagenesis, mapping) from reverse (targeted knockout/knock-in) approaches in the mouse.
- Connect mouse genetics to quantitative-trait and disease-modeling applications.

## Lecture Outline (~60 min)
1. **Why the mouse?** (~8 min) — A mammal close to humans with a deep history of fanciers' inbred strains, defined genetics, and a rich mutant collection.
2. **Classical mouse genetics** (~12 min) — Inbred strains and congenics, coat-color/morphology mutants, early linkage maps, and the first mammalian QTL-style analyses of continuous traits.
3. **The molecular-genetics revolution** (~15 min) — Transgenesis, embryonic stem cells, and homologous-recombination gene targeting; the arrival of knockout and knock-in mice.
4. **Forward vs. reverse genetics in the mouse** (~12 min) — ENU mutagenesis and positional cloning versus targeted alleles and conditional (Cre/lox) systems; strengths and limits of each.
5. **Quantitative traits and disease models** (~10 min) — Mapping complex traits in crosses and recombinant inbred panels; humanized and disease-model mice.
6. **Wrap-up & discussion** (~3 min).
