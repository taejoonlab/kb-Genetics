# Genetic Model — Zebrafish

**Course:** BME333 / BIO333 Genetics (UNIST, 2026 Fall) · Lecture 21 · ~60 min
**Syllabus:** [← Course schedule](2026.BME333-BIO333-Syllabus.md) — Week 13 Mon, 2026-11-23

## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:
- Explain why the zebrafish (*Danio rerio*) became a leading **vertebrate** genetic model — external fertilization, optically transparent embryos, rapid development, and high fecundity.
- Describe how large-scale ENU and insertional mutagenesis screens identified genes essential for vertebrate development, and how they differ in throughput and gene-cloning ease.
- Compare the zebrafish saturation-screen strategy with the invertebrate screens (*C. elegans*, *Drosophila*) covered in prior lectures.
- Summarize the value of the zebrafish reference genome for comparative genomics and human-disease orthology (~70% of human genes have a zebrafish orthologue).
- Evaluate the strengths and limitations of zebrafish as a model of human genetic disease.

## Lecture Outline (~60 min)
1. **Why a vertebrate model? Zebrafish biology** (~10 min) — Life cycle and husbandry: external development, transparent embryos allowing live imaging of organogenesis, ~3-month generation time, hundreds of eggs per clutch. Position zebrafish as filling the gap between invertebrate models and mouse.
2. **Forward genetics: the ENU saturation screens** (~15 min) — The Tübingen and Boston "big screens" of the 1990s: mutagenize males with ENU, breed F2 families, score F3 embryos for visible phenotypes across organ systems. Concept of genetic saturation and complementation to assign mutants to genes.
3. **Insertional mutagenesis & gene cloning** (~12 min) — Retroviral insertional screens: lower mutagenesis efficiency than ENU but the inserted provirus tags the disrupted gene, making molecular cloning fast. Trade-off between mutation frequency and ease of gene identification.
4. **The zebrafish reference genome & human orthology** (~12 min) — A high-quality genome assembly (>26,000 protein-coding genes); ~70% of human genes have at least one zebrafish orthologue. How the genome enables positional cloning, comparative genomics, and modeling of human disease genes.
5. **Modern tools & disease modeling; wrap-up** (~11 min) — Morpholino knockdown, transgenesis, and CRISPR/Cas9 in zebrafish; strengths (imaging, chemical/genetic screens) and limitations (genome duplication/paralogs, some human genes absent). Preview: the dog as a model of variation and selection (Lecture 22).
